12 AI Prompts for Your Shopify Store

Prompt 1: Shopify Product Description

The product description is the single most important copy asset on a Shopify product page. It is the last piece of content a shopper reads before deciding to add to cart or leave. Most Shopify product descriptions fail because they list features instead of selling outcomes, ignore the shopper's real question ("will this solve my problem?"), and end without a clear reason to act. This prompt generates a complete product description that leads with customer benefit, covers features in benefit-first language, handles the primary objection, and closes with a conversion-focused CTA. It also includes a meta description and keyword-rich opening for SEO.

Prompt SH-01: Shopify Product Description
Act as an e-commerce copywriter specializing in Shopify product pages with a track record of improving add-to-cart rates. Write a complete product description for the Shopify product below.

Structure:
— Opening hook (1–2 sentences): Lead with the customer outcome or the problem the product solves. Include the primary keyword naturally.
— Feature/benefit section (5 bullet points): Each bullet = feature + benefit. Format: [Feature] — [what it means for the customer].
— Objection handler (1 short paragraph): Address the most common reason this type of product gets returned or not purchased.
— Social proof bridge (1 sentence): Reference customer results or reviews without making up specific numbers.
— CTA (1 sentence): Benefit-driven call to action. No generic "buy now."
— SEO meta description (155 characters max): Keyword-rich, benefit-focused, includes the product name.

Rules: Write in second person (you/your). No superlatives ("best," "amazing," "incredible") without proof. No passive voice. Every sentence must earn its place — if it does not help the shopper decide, cut it.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY — e.g., "skincare," "kitchen gadgets," "activewear"]
— Primary Keyword: [TARGET SEO KEYWORD]
— Top 5 Features: [LIST YOUR FIVE KEY FEATURES]
— Primary Customer Benefit: [WHAT PROBLEM DOES THIS SOLVE OR WHAT OUTCOME DOES IT CREATE?]
— Target Customer: [WHO IS BUYING THIS — e.g., "busy moms," "home gym owners," "small business owners"]
— Most Common Objection: [THE #1 REASON PEOPLE HESITATE TO BUY — e.g., "will it fit?", "is it durable?", "will I actually use it?"]
— Brand Voice: [TONE — e.g., "warm and encouraging," "direct and no-nonsense," "premium and understated"]

Opening Hook:
You train hard enough without your gear slowing you down. The FitCore Resistance Band Set gives you a full-body workout that adapts to your strength level — at home, at the gym, or anywhere in between.

Features & Benefits:
• 5 resistance levels (10–50 lbs) — progress at your pace without buying new equipment every few months.
• Anti-snap natural latex construction — stretches thousands of times without the snap-back that sidelines other bands.
• Non-slip grip handles — keeps your focus on the workout, not on repositioning slipping bands mid-set.
• Door anchor + ankle straps included — unlocks 30+ exercises without a single machine or weight plate.
• Compact mesh carry bag — fits in a laptop bag, suitcase, or desk drawer so your workout goes wherever you do.

Objection Handler:
Resistance bands that snap mid-workout are a real concern — and a real hazard. FitCore bands are made from natural latex with a double-layer core that we test to 10,000 stretch cycles before they leave our facility. If yours ever breaks, we replace it. No questions, no return shipping label required.

Social Proof Bridge:
Thousands of customers have replaced their gym membership with this set — and kept it off for over a year.

CTA:
Add to cart and get your first workout in today — the setup takes 30 seconds.

Meta Description (148 chars):
FitCore Resistance Band Set: 5 levels, anti-snap latex, handles, door anchor & ankle straps. Full-body home gym that fits in a bag.

Notice how each bullet point completes the sentence "this feature means you get..." rather than just naming the feature. That structure forces benefit-first writing and prevents the spec-sheet format that kills conversion rates on product pages.

Prompt 2: Shopify Collection Page Copy

Collection pages are the most overlooked copy opportunity in Shopify. Most stores use the default theme text or leave the description blank entirely. But collection pages have two critical functions: they orient shoppers who land from a category search query (organic SEO traffic often lands here, not on a product page), and they build brand positioning by communicating why your version of this category is different. This prompt generates a collection page introduction, subcategory navigation text, and an SEO meta description optimized for both conversion and search visibility.

Prompt SH-02: Shopify Collection Page Copy
Act as an e-commerce copywriter and SEO strategist. Write collection page copy for a Shopify store that converts shoppers who land from search and builds brand positioning for shoppers browsing the catalog.

Provide:
— Collection intro (80–120 words): Opens with the primary keyword naturally in the first sentence. Orients the shopper to what they will find in this collection. Communicates the brand's editorial angle on this category — why your selection is different from a generic category page. Ends with a soft guide to action (browse, explore, find).
— 3 subcategory navigation blurbs (20–30 words each): Short, benefit-driven descriptions for the top 3 sub-sections or product types within the collection.
— SEO meta description (155 characters max): Keyword-rich, includes the collection name and a differentiating benefit.

Rules: The intro must include the primary keyword in the first 50 words. No corporate fluff. No "welcome to our collection" openings. Treat the shopper as a customer who already knows what they want — your job is to confirm they are in the right place and point them toward the right product.

Variables:
— Collection Name: [COLLECTION NAME — e.g., "Women's Activewear," "Natural Skincare," "Office Desks"]
— Primary SEO Keyword: [TARGET KEYWORD — e.g., "women's yoga leggings," "natural face moisturizer," "standing desks"]
— Brand Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOUR SELECTION DIFFERENT — e.g., "all products are OEKO-TEX certified," "every desk ships in 2 days," "curated for petite frames"]
— Top 3 Sub-Sections: [NAME THE 3 SUBCATEGORIES OR PRODUCT TYPES IN THIS COLLECTION]
— Target Customer: [WHO IS BROWSING THIS COLLECTION]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

Collection page SEO is underutilized by most Shopify stores. Google indexes collection pages and ranks them for category keywords — "best yoga leggings," "natural face moisturizer," "standing desks for small spaces" — that drive high-intent shopping traffic. A 100-word, keyword-optimized collection intro is one of the highest-ROI copy investments a Shopify merchant can make because it captures traffic that would otherwise land on a competitor's product page.

Prompt 3: Shopify Homepage Sections

The Shopify homepage has one job: move visitors to a product or collection page as fast as possible. The homepage is not a brochure. It is not a brand story. It is a conversion funnel entry point. The copy must communicate what you sell, who it is for, and why you are the right choice — all in the time it takes a new visitor to scroll once. This prompt generates copy for the five core homepage sections: hero, features/benefits, social proof, featured collection intro, and the final CTA block.

Section 5 — CTA Block:
— Closing headline (6–8 words): Urgency or aspiration
— Supporting copy (20 words max): Reinforce the core promise
— CTA button text (3–5 words)

Variables:

The hero section is the highest-leverage copy on your entire Shopify store. Every visitor sees it. Most stores use vague taglines ("Crafted with passion," "Quality you can trust") that communicate nothing specific. Prompt 4: Shopify About Us Page

The About Us page is the second most visited page on most Shopify stores after the homepage. Shoppers visit it when they are considering buying but want to know if they can trust you. Most About Us pages fail because they read like a LinkedIn summary: founding year, mission statement, and a team photo. What actually converts About Us visitors into buyers is a brand story that puts the customer at the center, articulates the founder's specific insight or experience that created this product, and ends with a purchase invitation. This prompt generates a full About Us page using the brand origin story framework.

Prompt SH-04: Shopify About Us Page
Act as a brand storytelling copywriter specializing in e-commerce. Write a complete About Us page for a Shopify store using the brand origin story framework: Founder's Problem → Failed Solutions → The Insight → The Product → The Mission → Customer Invitation.

Structure:
— Opening (2 sentences): Start with the founder's specific problem — not a generic industry observation. Make it visceral and relatable.
— Failed solutions (1 short paragraph): What the founder tried before creating this product. Why those solutions fell short. This is where the reader identifies with the story.
— The insight (1 paragraph): The specific realization or discovery that led to creating the product. What the founder saw that others missed.
— The product (1 paragraph): How the product was developed, what makes it different, and the first results. Include specific details — timeline, iterations, early customers.
— The mission (2–3 sentences): What success looks like for a customer. What the brand is working toward beyond just selling products.
— Customer invitation (1 paragraph): A direct, warm invitation to try the product. Connects the founder's story to the customer's outcome. Ends with a CTA.

Rules: The customer is the hero, not the founder. The founder's story is a bridge to the customer's aspiration. Avoid corporate language. Use first person for the founder's voice. Be specific — specific details are more believable than general claims.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Founder Name(s): [FOUNDER NAME(S)]
— Founder's Original Problem: [THE SPECIFIC PROBLEM THE FOUNDER EXPERIENCED — be specific]
— Failed Alternatives They Tried: [2–3 THINGS THEY TRIED THAT DIDN'T WORK]
— The Key Insight: [WHAT DID THEY REALIZE THAT OTHERS MISSED?]
— What Makes the Product Different: [CORE DIFFERENTIATOR]
— Brand Mission Beyond Sales: [LARGER PURPOSE — e.g., "make sustainable skincare accessible," "bring specialty coffee to suburbs," "help home cooks feel confident"]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

The "customer as hero" principle is the most important rule for About Us copy. The founder's story is compelling only insofar as it mirrors the customer's own experience. When a reader thinks "that was me — I had that exact problem," they stop reading a brand's history and start reading their own story. That emotional recognition is what turns About Us visitors into buyers.

Prompt 5: Shopify Announcement Bar Copy

The announcement bar is the thin strip at the top of most Shopify themes — above the navigation. It is the first thing every visitor sees on every page of your store. Most merchants set it once ("Free shipping on orders over $50") and never update it. But the announcement bar is valuable real estate that should rotate based on your current offer, urgency lever, or seasonal message. This prompt generates a set of five announcement bar variations — one for each use case — so you always have a relevant, high-converting message running.

Prompt SH-05: Shopify Announcement Bar Copy
Act as a Shopify conversion copywriter. Write 5 announcement bar messages optimized for different use cases. Each message should be concise (under 80 characters including the CTA link text), punchy, and create a reason to act now.

Write one variation for each use case:
— Variation 1: Free shipping offer (threshold-based)
— Variation 2: Limited-time sale or discount (with expiry)
— Variation 3: New arrival or product launch
— Variation 4: Social proof / trust signal
— Variation 5: Urgency / scarcity (low stock or final hours)

Format each as: [Message text] — [CTA link text]

Rules: No emoji unless specifically requested. No vague language ("amazing deals," "shop our latest"). Be specific about the offer, deadline, or product. The CTA link text should describe the destination, not just say "click here."

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Free Shipping Threshold: [AMOUNT — e.g., "$50," "$75," "all orders"]
— Current Discount: [OFFER — e.g., "20% off sitewide," "$10 off your first order," "buy 2 get 1 free"]
— Sale Deadline: [DATE OR TIMEFRAME — e.g., "ends Sunday," "48 hours only," "today only"]
— New Arrival: [PRODUCT OR COLLECTION NAME]
— Social Proof Stat: [NUMBER — e.g., "10,000+ orders," "4.9 stars," "trusted by 5,000 customers"]
— Scarcity Detail: [WHAT IS LIMITED — e.g., "only 12 units left," "selling fast," "last chance before restock"]

The announcement bar has a 100% impression rate — every visitor on every page sees it. Rotating it based on your current offer or seasonal moment means you are always communicating the most relevant reason to act. A single well-written announcement bar driving 2% of visitors to add to cart is worth more than a homepage section that most shoppers scroll past.

Prompt 6: Shopify Abandoned Cart Email (Klaviyo/Shopify Email)

Abandoned cart emails recover an average of 5–15% of carts that were left behind. Most Shopify stores use the default abandoned cart email — a plain reminder with a product image and a "complete your purchase" button. That default email recovers some carts. A structured three-email sequence that moves from gentle reminder to objection handling to incentive recovers significantly more. This prompt generates all three emails in a single pass, with subject line variations, body copy, and CTA text for each stage — ready to paste into Klaviyo or Shopify Email.

Prompt SH-06: Shopify Abandoned Cart Email Sequence (3 emails)
Act as an e-commerce email copywriter specializing in Klaviyo flows for Shopify stores. Write a 3-email abandoned cart sequence that recovers shoppers at three different psychological stages.

Email 1 — Sent 1 hour after abandonment (Gentle Reminder):
— Subject line: Conversational, no discount mentioned yet. 2 variations.
— Preview text (40 chars max): Curiosity or benefit hook.
— Body: Short (80–100 words). Remind them of the specific item. Restate the top benefit. Make returning frictionless. No pressure, no guilt.
— CTA button text: Action-focused, not generic.

Email 2 — Sent 24 hours after abandonment (Objection Handler):
— Subject line: Address the most likely objection. 2 variations.
— Preview text (40 chars max): Reinforce confidence.
— Body: 100–130 words. Address the top 2 purchase objections. Include a social proof element (reviews, number of customers, guarantee). No discount yet.
— CTA button text: Trust-building.

Email 3 — Sent 72 hours after abandonment (Last Chance + Incentive):
— Subject line: Urgency + incentive. 2 variations.
— Preview text (40 chars max): Discount or bonus reinforcement.
— Body: 80–100 words. Introduce a time-limited discount or bonus. Create genuine urgency (inventory, offer expiry). Final CTA.
— CTA button text: Urgency-driven.

Rules: Never start an email with "Hi [First Name]." Do not guilt-trip ("You forgot something"). Do not use fake scarcity. Each email must work as a standalone — the reader might only open one.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product Name: [PRODUCT LEFT IN CART]
— Product Benefit: [PRIMARY BENEFIT OF THIS PRODUCT]
— Top 2 Objections: [WHY SHOPPERS HESITATE — e.g., "price, fit uncertainty," "not sure if they need it, shipping time"]
— Social Proof: [REVIEW COUNT, STAR RATING, OR CUSTOMER STAT]
— Guarantee: [RETURN POLICY OR GUARANTEE — e.g., "30-day free returns," "100% satisfaction guarantee"]
— Recovery Discount: [DISCOUNT FOR EMAIL 3 — e.g., "10% off," "$15 off," "free shipping"]
— Discount Code: [CODE — e.g., "COMEBACK10"]
— Discount Expiry: [TIMEFRAME — e.g., "expires in 24 hours," "valid through Sunday"]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

The three-email structure matters because different shoppers abandon for different reasons. Some just got distracted. Some are comparison shopping. Some have a specific objection about fit, quality, or price. Email 1 catches the distracted shopper. Email 2 answers the objector. Email 3 converts the price-sensitive shopper with an incentive. A single abandoned cart email cannot do all three jobs effectively — but a three-email sequence designed for each stage recovers all three types of abandoners.

Prompt 7: Shopify Post-Purchase Email Sequence

The post-purchase period — the 7 days after a customer buys — is the highest-leverage email window in e-commerce. Customer sentiment is at its peak. The purchase decision has already been made. And the customer is primed to be turned into a repeat buyer and brand advocate if you give them the right experience. Most Shopify stores send a single order confirmation and nothing else. This prompt generates a complete post-purchase sequence: order confirmation, shipping update with brand reinforcement, delivery day experience guide, review request, and a cross-sell or reorder prompt.

Prompt SH-07: Shopify Post-Purchase Email Sequence (5 emails)
Act as a Shopify retention email strategist. Write a 5-email post-purchase sequence that transforms a first-time buyer into a repeat customer and brand advocate.

Email 1 — Order Confirmation (immediate):
— Subject: Confirms the order with brand warmth, not just a receipt notice.
— Body (60–80 words): Thank them genuinely. Confirm what they ordered and when to expect it. Give them one "what to do while you wait" tip that adds immediate value.

Email 2 — Shipping Confirmation (when order ships):
— Subject: Shipping update + anticipation builder.
— Body (60–80 words): Confirm shipping. Restate the product's core benefit as anticipation. Include a "get ready" tip or usage preparation idea.

Email 3 — Delivery Day (when order is delivered):
— Subject: Celebratory, experience-focused.
— Body (80–100 words): Acknowledge the delivery. Give the customer 1–2 specific tips for getting the best first experience with the product. Build excitement without overselling.

Email 4 — Review Request (3–5 days after delivery):
— Subject: Personal, conversational ask for feedback.
— Body (60–80 words): Ask for a review in a way that feels like a conversation, not a form request. Explain why reviews help other customers (not just the brand). Include the review link.

Email 5 — Cross-Sell or Reorder (7–10 days after delivery):
— Subject: Based on the product's natural replenishment or complement cycle.
— Body (80–100 words): Reference their original purchase. Introduce the complementary product or replenishment reminder. Give a reason to act now (first refill discount, bundling benefit).

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product Purchased: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY]
— Best First-Use Tip: [ONE SPECIFIC TIP FOR GETTING THE MOST FROM THE PRODUCT ON DAY ONE]
— Complementary Product: [CROSS-SELL PRODUCT NAME]
— Replenishment Timeline: [HOW OFTEN DOES THE PRODUCT NEED REPLACING — e.g., "every 30 days," "after 90 uses," "each season"]
— Review Platform: [WHERE REVIEWS LIVE — e.g., "our site," "Google," "Judge.me"]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

Post-purchase email sequences are the most neglected revenue lever in Shopify. Acquiring a new customer costs 5–7x more than retaining an existing one, but most Shopify stores invest almost nothing in the post-purchase experience. A well-written five-email sequence turns a one-time buyer into a repeat customer, a reviewer, and a potential brand advocate — compounding the value of every acquisition dollar you spend on ads.

Prompt 8: Shopify Facebook/Instagram Ad

Facebook and Instagram ads drive the majority of paid traffic for most direct-to-consumer Shopify brands. But most Shopify ad copy makes the same mistakes: leading with the product instead of the customer's problem, using generic CTAs, and writing for the brand rather than for the scroll. This prompt generates a complete Meta ad set: primary text, headline, description, and three creative hook variations optimized for cold, warm, and retargeting audiences — the three audiences every Shopify brand needs to run simultaneously.

Prompt SH-08: Shopify Facebook/Instagram Ad Copy
Act as a Meta ads copywriter specializing in direct-to-consumer Shopify brands. Write ad copy for three audience segments: cold (never heard of the brand), warm (visited the site or engaged with content), and retargeting (added to cart or viewed product).

For each audience, provide:
— Primary text (125 words max): Scroll-stopping hook in the first line. For cold: problem-first. For warm: benefit + social proof. For retargeting: specific product + urgency/incentive.
— Headline (27 characters max): Direct benefit statement.
— Description (27 characters max): Supporting detail or offer.
— CTA button: Select from Facebook's available options (Shop Now, Learn More, Get Offer, Sign Up, Book Now).

Also provide 3 first-line hook variations for the cold ad (test these as creative hooks):
— Hook 1: Problem/pain point opener
— Hook 2: Unexpected or counterintuitive statement
— Hook 3: Social proof / number-driven opener

Rules: The first line of every ad must work as a standalone hook — it is the only thing the reader sees before clicking "more." No passive voice. No "Introducing our new..." openers. Lead with the customer, not the brand.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product: [PRODUCT NAME AND BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
— Target Audience: [WHO THIS AD REACHES — demographics, interests, or behaviors]
— Core Problem Solved: [WHAT PAIN POINT OR FRUSTRATION DOES THIS PRODUCT ADDRESS?]
— Primary Benefit: [WHAT THE CUSTOMER GETS]
— Social Proof: [REVIEW STAT, CUSTOMER COUNT, OR MEDIA MENTION]
— Current Offer: [DISCOUNT, FREE SHIPPING, OR BUNDLE DEAL]
— Retargeting Incentive: [WHAT YOU OFFER TO BRING CART ABANDONERS BACK — e.g., "10% off code," "free gift with purchase"]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

The three-audience structure is how profitable Shopify brands structure their Meta ad accounts. Cold audiences need a problem-first hook because they do not know you exist yet. Warm audiences need social proof because they are evaluating whether to trust you. Retargeting audiences need a specific incentive because they already want the product and just need a reason to complete the purchase. Running the same ad copy to all three audiences is one of the most common and costly Shopify ad mistakes.

Prompt 9: Shopify Google Shopping Title Optimizer

Google Shopping titles are the primary signal Google uses to match your products to search queries. A product titled "Blue Mug" will never show for "12 oz ceramic coffee mug blue handle" — even if that is exactly what you are selling. This prompt takes your existing Shopify product titles and rewrites them following Google's recommended title structure: Brand + Product Type + Key Attributes. It also identifies the specific search queries each rewritten title will now match that the original was missing.

Prompt SH-09: Shopify Google Shopping Title Optimizer
Act as a Google Shopping specialist who optimizes Shopify product feed titles for maximum impression share and click-through rate. Rewrite the product titles below following Google's recommended title structure and Shopping ads best practices.

Title structure: Brand + Product Type + Key Attributes (color, size, material, gender, use case, compatibility)

Rules:
— Maximum 150 characters per title (Google's limit)
— Front-load the most important keywords (Google truncates after ~70 characters in most placements)
— Include specific attributes that shoppers use to filter: color, size, material, gender, age group, compatibility
— Do not use promotional language ("best," "cheap," "sale") — Google may disapprove
— Use natural language, not keyword stuffing
— Capitalize the first letter of each major word

For each product, provide:
— Original title
— Optimized title (150 characters max)
— 3–5 search queries this title will now match that the original was missing
— What attributes were missing from the original title

Variables:
— Product Titles to Optimize: [LIST YOUR CURRENT SHOPIFY PRODUCT TITLES]
— Brand Name: [YOUR BRAND]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY — e.g., "apparel," "home goods," "beauty," "electronics"]
— Key Attributes to Include: [ATTRIBUTES RELEVANT TO YOUR PRODUCTS — e.g., "material, color, size, gender" or "capacity, compatibility, color"]

Tips for Using AI to Write Better Shopify Copy

The 12 prompts above give you the complete copy toolkit for your Shopify store. These five tips ensure you use that toolkit to get results, not just output.

1. Include Your Brand Voice in Every Prompt

The single biggest upgrade you can make to any AI prompt is adding a specific brand voice description. "Warm and encouraging" produces different copy than "direct and no-nonsense," which produces different copy than "premium and understated." If your brand has a style guide, paste the voice guidelines directly into the prompt. If not, write 2–3 sentences describing how your best customer would describe your brand's personality. That voice consistency across product descriptions, emails, and ads is what makes a Shopify store feel like a brand rather than a catalog.

2. Generate Multiple Variations and Test Them

Do not treat the first AI output as the final version. Ask the AI to regenerate with a different angle, tone, or framework. For high-traffic pages like product descriptions and pop-up copy, run A/B tests using Shopify's native testing or a tool like Intelligems. Even a 15% improvement in product page conversion rate compounds significantly across the lifetime of your store's traffic. AI makes generating test variations fast enough that there is no reason to settle on one version.

3. Use Real Data in Your Prompts

The more specific the variables you fill in, the better the output. "4.9 stars, 2,400 reviews" produces better copy than "great reviews." "Free returns within 45 days, no questions asked" produces better copy than "easy returns." "Our best-selling SKU sold out in 72 hours during our last restock" produces better copy than "popular product." Pull your actual numbers from Shopify Analytics, Google Search Console, and Klaviyo before running any prompt. Specificity is what separates AI copy that sounds generic from AI copy that sounds like it was written by someone who knows your brand.

4. Refresh Copy Seasonally

Product descriptions written in January feel stale by Q4. Announcement bars from last summer's sale are still running in October. Email subject lines that worked during launch lose effectiveness after subscribers have seen your tone for months. Run your core prompts at the start of each quarter with updated variables — new review counts, new offers, new social proof stats, new seasonal angles. Seasonal copy freshness is one of the simplest levers for improving Shopify email open rates and return visitor conversion rates.

5. Match Copy to Traffic Source

A shopper who arrives from a Facebook ad cold audience has different context than a shopper who arrives from an email link or an organic Google search. The Facebook visitor may not know your brand. The email visitor already opted in. The Google visitor typed a specific product query. Your product page copy cannot personalize itself dynamically based on traffic source (without advanced tools), but your ad copy, collection page copy, and email body copy can all be tuned to the context of the audience seeing them. Use the audience-specific prompt variations to build copy that meets each traffic source where it is.
