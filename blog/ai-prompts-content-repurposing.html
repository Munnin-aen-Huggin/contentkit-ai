10 AI Prompts for Content Repurposing Across Platforms

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for content repurposing are structured instructions that transform a single piece of content — a blog post, video, podcast episode, or webinar — into platform-native formats for social media, email, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, and more, without losing the original ideas or voice. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts with variables for every major repurposing workflow, from blog-to-social to a full content matrix that extracts 10 formats from one source piece.

Most content dies the day it is published. A blog post earns traffic for a week, then disappears into the archive. A podcast episode reaches subscribers who already follow you, but never finds the LinkedIn audience that would share it with 10,000 connections. A webinar gets recorded, uploaded, and forgotten — even though it contains 60 minutes of original research, case studies, and expert insight that your blog audience has never seen.

Content repurposing solves this problem. Instead of creating new content for every channel and every week, you take the ideas you have already created — ideas that cost time, research, and creative effort to produce — and adapt them into the formats each platform rewards. A 2,000-word blog post becomes a LinkedIn carousel, a Twitter thread, an email newsletter, and five platform-specific social posts. The ideas are the same. The format, length, and native language of each platform changes.

The bottleneck has always been the reformatting itself. Writing a Twitter thread that hooks in the first tweet is a different skill from writing a LinkedIn carousel that works without captions, which is different from writing an email newsletter that leads with value before the CTA. Each format has its own grammar and engagement patterns. Manually translating content into all of them takes hours per piece.

Structured ai prompts for content repurposing eliminate that bottleneck. The prompts below encode the native rules of each platform directly into the instruction. You paste your source content, fill in the variables, and the AI applies the right structure, length, and format for each destination. What used to take a half-day of formatting work now takes minutes per format.

For a broader library of prompts beyond repurposing — including content calendar planning, social media captions, and email sequences — see our guide to AI prompts for content calendars and the full AI marketing prompts collection.

Why Content Repurposing Is the Highest-ROI Content Activity

Every piece of long-form content you create contains far more value than a single publication extracts. A blog post with 10 key insights can generate 10 individual social posts, each standing on its own. A podcast interview with three expert guests contains enough quotable moments for a month of LinkedIn content. A webinar with an audience Q&A has natural FAQs that belong in a blog post, a Twitter thread, and a product page.

The math is straightforward. If creating original content takes 4 hours and repurposing each format takes 30 minutes without AI (and 5 minutes with AI prompts), then repurposing into 5 formats multiplies your content output by 5x for a fraction of the original creation cost. Consistently, content marketers who build repurposing workflows report 3–5x more content output with the same team size.

Platform-Native Content Outperforms Cross-Posted Content

The key word is "native." Copying a blog post introduction into a LinkedIn caption and calling it repurposing is not repurposing — it is recycling, and the platform's algorithm punishes it. LinkedIn rewards posts that start with a hook, use short paragraphs, and avoid external links in the body. Twitter/X rewards threads that break ideas into punchy, standalone tweets with a clear payoff in the first 280 characters. Email newsletters reward value-first content that gets to the insight before the CTA. Each of the 10 prompts below is built around the native engagement patterns of the destination format, not a generic rewrite of the source content.

Repurposing Compounds Over Time

A piece of evergreen content that gets repurposed into 10 formats has 10 opportunities to drive traffic, generate leads, and build audience — many of which continue working months or years after the original publication. A LinkedIn carousel from a blog post you wrote six months ago can still go viral today. A Twitter thread from a podcast episode can resurface in search results when someone discovers it for the first time. The original creation cost is fixed. The distribution return is ongoing.

The 10 prompts below cover the most valuable repurposing workflows for content marketers and creators: blog-to-social (5 platforms), blog-to-email, video-to-blog, podcast-to-shownotes, long-form-to-thread, blog-to-carousel, webinar-to-blog and email sequence, testimonial-to-multi-format, data-to-infographic, and a full content repurposing matrix. Each prompt includes variables so you can apply it to any content topic, brand voice, or audience.

10 AI Prompts for Content Repurposing

Prompt 1: Blog Post → Social Media Posts (5 Platforms)

The most common repurposing need — and the one most often done badly. Most content teams copy a sentence from the blog post, add a link, and post it identically across every platform. This prompt does the opposite: it reads the source blog post and writes platform-native social posts for LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Instagram, Facebook, and Pinterest, each formatted and toned for how real users engage on that platform. One prompt generates five ready-to-publish posts that each feel original to the platform they are written for.

Prompt CR-01: Blog Post → Social Media Posts (5 Platforms)
Act as a social media strategist who specializes in platform-native content. Read the blog post below and write one social media post for each of the following platforms. Each post must be written in the native style and format of that platform — not a copy-paste of the blog intro.

Platform requirements:
— LinkedIn (1,300 characters max): Start with a bold single-sentence hook on its own line. Use 3–5 short paragraphs. End with a question or CTA. No external links in the body — mention the link is in comments.
— Twitter/X (280 characters max): Write the standalone tweet that captures the single most surprising or counterintuitive insight from the article. Include the blog URL.
— Instagram (caption, 2,200 characters max): Open with a hook line, tell a mini-story or give one actionable tip, end with a call to action, and include 5 relevant hashtags on a new line.
— Facebook (500 characters max): Conversational tone. Ask a question to drive comments. Share one insight and invite the audience to weigh in.
— Pinterest (pin description, 500 characters max): Keyword-rich. Describe the content's value, include the primary keyword naturally, and end with a URL or CTA.

Variables:
— Blog Post Title: [BLOG POST TITLE]
— Blog Post URL: [URL]
— Blog Post Content (paste full text or key sections): [PASTE BLOG CONTENT]
— Brand Voice: [BRAND VOICE — e.g., "professional but approachable," "bold and direct," "educational and empathetic"]
— Target Audience: [WHO READS THIS — e.g., "B2B marketers," "early-stage founders," "freelance designers"]
— Primary Keyword: [TARGET KEYWORD FOR SEO/DISCOVERABILITY]

LinkedIn (example output):
Most content teams repurpose content wrong.

They copy the blog intro, add a link, and post it on every platform. Then wonder why engagement is flat.

The real issue: every platform has its own native language. LinkedIn rewards short paragraphs and hooks. Twitter rewards insight in 280 characters. Instagram rewards story. Copy-pasting ignores all of that.

Here is what a systematic repurposing workflow actually looks like: one blog post becomes 5 platform-native posts, an email newsletter, a Twitter thread, and a LinkedIn carousel — each written in the format that earns engagement on that platform.

What does your current repurposing process look like? Drop it in the comments — link to the full breakdown is there too.

Notice how the LinkedIn post does not quote the blog directly — it takes the central tension of the article and frames it as a LinkedIn-native story. That is the difference between repurposing and recycling.

Prompt 2: Blog Post → Email Newsletter

Your email list is your highest-converting distribution channel. The average email open rate (20–40% for engaged lists) dwarfs social media organic reach (1–5% on most platforms). But blog-to-email repurposing fails when it is simply a summary of the article with a "read more" link. The best email newsletters take the blog's core insight and expand it with a personal angle, original commentary, or an exclusive tip the blog post does not include. This prompt generates an email newsletter from a blog post that gives subscribers a reason to open it even if they have already read the article.

Prompt CR-02: Blog Post → Email Newsletter
Act as an email newsletter writer who turns blog posts into newsletter editions that feel personal, not promotional. Read the blog post below and write a complete email newsletter edition.

Newsletter structure:
— Subject Line (50 characters max): Curiosity-driven. Do not reveal the full answer. A/B test: write 3 subject line options.
— Preview Text (90 characters max): Complements the subject line without repeating it.
— Opening (2–3 sentences): A personal anecdote, observation, or question that connects to the article topic. Sounds like a human, not a brand.
— Core Insight (150–250 words): Distill the single most valuable idea from the blog post. Add one piece of original commentary, a personal experience, or an additional tip not in the article.
— Quick Takeaway (3 bullet points): The three most actionable things from the article. Formatted as "If you [situation], try [action]."
— CTA (1–2 sentences): One clear next step — read the full article, reply with a question, or share with someone who needs it.
— P.S. line: A secondary CTA or teaser for next week's content.

Tone: Conversational, first-person, direct. Write as if emailing a smart colleague, not broadcasting to a list.

Variables:
— Blog Post Title: [BLOG POST TITLE]
— Blog Post URL: [URL]
— Blog Post Content: [PASTE BLOG CONTENT]
— Newsletter Name: [NEWSLETTER NAME — e.g., "The Content Brief," "Growth Weekly," "The Founder's Edge"]
— Sender Name: [SENDER FIRST NAME OR BRAND]
— Audience: [WHO IS ON THIS LIST]
— Brand Voice: [VOICE — e.g., "direct and no-BS," "warm and educational," "tactical and data-driven"]

The subject line A/B test instruction is intentional. Email subject lines are the single highest-leverage variable in email performance, and the difference between a 20% open rate and a 35% open rate is almost always the subject line. Generating three options and testing them is standard practice for any email program with volume.

Prompt 3: Video → Blog Post Transcript

Video content is one of the hardest formats to repurpose because a raw transcript is almost unreadable as a blog post. It is full of verbal filler ("you know," "like," "so"), non-linear trains of thought, and references to visual elements that do not exist in text. This prompt does not just clean up the transcript — it restructures the video's ideas into a fully readable, SEO-optimized blog post with headers, short paragraphs, and proper editorial flow, while preserving the speaker's voice and all substantive ideas from the video.

Prompt CR-03: Video → Blog Post Transcript
Act as a content editor who specializes in converting video and audio content into high-quality blog posts. Read the raw video transcript below and convert it into a fully structured, publish-ready blog post. Do not simply clean up the transcript — restructure it into editorial prose.

Blog post structure:
— Title: SEO-optimized, includes primary keyword, under 60 characters
— Meta Description (150–160 characters): Includes primary keyword and a clear value proposition
— Introduction (150–200 words): Answer-first format. Lead with the main takeaway, then explain what the article covers.
— H2 Sections (3–5 sections): Pull out the major topics from the video. Each section gets a keyword-rich H2 heading and 150–300 words of prose.
— H3 Subsections (as needed): Use for specific examples, steps, or tips within each H2 section.
— Key Quotes (2–3): Pull the most quotable lines from the transcript and format as blockquotes.
— Conclusion (100–150 words): Summarize the main points and include a CTA.

Editorial rules:
— Remove all verbal filler ("um," "uh," "you know," "like," "so basically")
— Remove visual references ("as you can see here," "look at this slide") and replace with prose descriptions
— Preserve the speaker's voice and specific word choices where they add personality
— Add transition sentences between sections so the post reads as a unified article, not a transcript
— Do not add information not present in the transcript

Variables:
— Video Title: [VIDEO TITLE]
— Primary Keyword: [TARGET KEYWORD]
— Raw Transcript: [PASTE FULL VIDEO TRANSCRIPT]
— Speaker Name/Brand: [SPEAKER OR BRAND NAME]
— Target Audience: [WHO READS THIS BLOG]
— Internal Links to Include: [RELATED BLOG POSTS OR PAGES TO LINK TO]

The "do not add information not in the transcript" rule is critical and often overlooked. AI models have a tendency to hallucinate additional details, statistics, or examples that sound plausible but were not in the original video. This instruction keeps the output faithful to the source material while still producing a fully editorial post.

Prompt 4: Podcast → Show Notes + Blog Post

Podcast show notes serve two audiences simultaneously: the listeners who want a summary and reference links after listening, and the search engines that index the show notes page. A great set of show notes includes a scannable episode summary, timestamped highlights, key quotes, and links to everything mentioned. This prompt generates both a publish-ready show notes page and a standalone long-form blog post from the same podcast episode — two pieces of content from one source.

Prompt CR-04: Podcast → Show Notes + Blog Post
Act as a podcast content strategist who turns episode recordings into two pieces of written content: show notes and a blog post. Read the podcast transcript or outline below and produce both outputs.

Output 1 — Show Notes (for episode page):
— Episode Summary (100–150 words): What the episode covers and the main takeaway
— What You Will Learn (3–5 bullet points): Key insights listeners will take away
— Episode Highlights with Timestamps (5–8 highlights): Format as [00:00] — Topic or insight
— Key Quotes (2–3): The most shareable or insightful quotes from the episode
— Resources Mentioned: Format as a bulleted list of everything referenced in the episode
— Guest Bio (if applicable, 75 words max)

Output 2 — Blog Post (800–1,200 words):
— SEO Title: Includes primary keyword and episode guest or topic
— Introduction (150 words): Context for the episode topic and why it matters now
— 3–4 H2 sections expanding on the episode's key themes
— Pull quotes formatted as blockquotes
— Conclusion with episode CTA (subscribe, listen, leave a review)

Variables:
— Podcast Name: [PODCAST NAME]
— Episode Title: [EPISODE TITLE]
— Episode Number: [EPISODE #]
— Guest Name and Title (if applicable): [GUEST NAME — TITLE, COMPANY]
— Transcript or Detailed Outline: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT OR EPISODE OUTLINE]
— Primary Keyword for Blog Post: [TARGET KEYWORD]
— Resources Mentioned in Episode: [LIST ANY TOOLS, BOOKS, OR LINKS MENTIONED]
— Listen Link: [EPISODE URL OR AUDIO PLAYER LINK]

The dual-output structure — show notes and blog post from the same prompt — is the key efficiency. Most podcasters write one or the other, but the show notes page serves listeners and the blog post serves search. Running one prompt generates both in a single pass, then you publish them to different URLs and link them together. This gives every episode two pages of indexed content instead of one.

Prompt 5: Long-Form → Twitter/X Thread

Twitter/X threads are one of the highest-reach content formats on the platform when done correctly. A thread that hooks in the first tweet, delivers genuine insight in each subsequent tweet, and ends with a strong CTA can generate thousands of impressions from an audience that never reads your blog. The mistake most writers make is writing threads as a summary of their article. The best threads reframe the article's insights as a standalone experience — someone who has never read the original should get full value from the thread alone. This prompt generates a 10–15 tweet thread that stands on its own.

Prompt CR-05: Long-Form → Twitter/X Thread
Act as a Twitter/X content strategist who writes high-engagement threads. Read the long-form content below and write a Twitter/X thread of 10–15 tweets. The thread must stand on its own — someone who has never read the original content should get full value from the thread alone.

Thread structure:
— Tweet 1 (hook): A single bold statement, surprising insight, or counterintuitive claim. Under 240 characters. End with a line break and "Thread:" or a down-arrow emoji to signal continuation. This tweet must earn the click to expand on its own.
— Tweets 2–10 (body): Each tweet = one idea, one insight, or one step. Use numbers for listicle threads ("2/", "3/"). Short sentences. Line breaks between thoughts. No fluff. Each tweet should be worth reading independently.
— Second-to-last tweet (CTA setup): Summarize what the thread covered in 1–2 sentences.
— Final tweet (CTA): Include one action — follow for more, link to the full article, or ask the audience a question to drive replies.

Thread rules:
— Each tweet max 280 characters
— No tweet should start with "I" — start with the insight, not the narrator
— Use specific numbers, percentages, and timeframes wherever they exist in the source content
— No corporate language. Write like a person, not a brand account.

Variables:
— Source Content Title: [TITLE]
— Source Content (paste full text): [PASTE CONTENT]
— Author/Brand Name: [NAME]
— Target Audience on Twitter/X: [WHO FOLLOWS THIS ACCOUNT]
— Link to Full Article (for final tweet): [URL]
— Thread Style: [STYLE — e.g., "tactical and numbered," "storytelling," "contrarian and direct"]

Tweet 1 (hook example):
Most content teams work 3x harder than they need to.

They create new content every week instead of repurposing what already works.

Here is the repurposing system that turns 1 piece into 10: Thread:

Tweet 2:
2/ Most "repurposing" is just recycling.

Copy the intro. Add a link. Post on every platform.

Result: flat engagement everywhere because nothing was written for the platform it lives on.

Tweet 3:
3/ Real repurposing is translation.

LinkedIn has its own native language. Twitter has its own. Email has its own.

The same idea, formatted for each one, outperforms the original on every channel.

The "no tweet should start with I" rule is borrowed from the best Twitter/X writers. Starting with "I" makes the tweet about the narrator. Starting with the insight makes it about the reader. That small change consistently improves engagement rates.

Prompt 6: Blog Post → LinkedIn Carousel

LinkedIn carousels — PDF document posts that users swipe through slide by slide — are consistently among the highest-reach organic formats on LinkedIn. They receive 3–5x more impressions than standard text posts because LinkedIn's algorithm treats them as documents and surfaces them differently in feeds. The challenge is that a carousel must communicate visually: each slide is a single, swipeable idea with minimal text, a clear visual hierarchy, and a narrative that rewards swiping to the next slide. This prompt generates the slide-by-slide copy for a LinkedIn carousel adapted from a blog post.

Prompt CR-06: Blog Post → LinkedIn Carousel
Act as a LinkedIn content strategist who creates high-engagement carousel posts. Read the blog post below and write the slide-by-slide copy for a LinkedIn carousel (PDF document post). The carousel should drive swipes, shares, and profile visits.

Carousel structure (8–12 slides):
— Slide 1 (Cover): A bold title that promises a specific outcome. Treat it like a book cover — it must earn the swipe. Include a subtitle that expands the promise. (20 words max total)
— Slides 2–3 (Problem/Context): 2–3 slides that establish the problem or gap this carousel solves. Each slide = 1 idea, 10–20 words max.
— Slides 4–9 (Core Content): One tip, step, insight, or framework per slide. Each slide gets: a headline (under 10 words) + 1–2 supporting sentences (under 25 words each).
— Slide 10–11 (Summary): A summary slide that restates the key point or list. Can be a recap numbered list.
— Final Slide (CTA): One clear action. "Follow [Name] for more like this" + a secondary CTA (read the full article, download the resource, DM for X).

Design notes for each slide (to guide visual design):
— Suggest a background color or visual concept (light/dark, icon type, diagram idea)
— Keep text minimal — the slide is a visual medium, not a page of text

Variables:
— Blog Post Title: [TITLE]
— Blog Post Content: [PASTE BLOG CONTENT]
— Author/Brand Name: [NAME OR BRAND]
— Target LinkedIn Audience: [WHO FOLLOWS THIS PROFILE — e.g., "SaaS founders," "marketing managers," "agency owners"]
— Carousel Goal: [GOAL — e.g., "drive blog traffic," "grow followers," "generate DMs," "build authority"]
— Brand Colors (for design notes): [PRIMARY AND SECONDARY HEX COLORS, OR "not specified"]

The "treat slide 1 like a book cover" instruction is the most important piece of carousel strategy. Carousel reach is determined by how many people swipe past the first slide. If the cover does not promise a specific, desirable outcome, no one swipes. Every other slide is irrelevant if the cover does not earn the first swipe.

Prompt 7: Webinar → Blog Post + Email Sequence

Webinars are among the most content-rich formats in the marketing arsenal — and among the most under-leveraged after the event ends. A 60-minute webinar contains enough material for a 2,000-word blog post, a 3–5 email follow-up sequence, social clips, and a lead magnet. Most teams send one post-event email, upload the recording, and move on. This prompt generates both a long-form blog post and a 3-email follow-up sequence from a single webinar transcript or outline — the minimum viable repurposing stack for any webinar.

Prompt CR-07: Webinar → Blog Post + Email Sequence
Act as a content strategist who maximizes ROI from webinar content. Read the webinar transcript or detailed outline below and produce two outputs: a blog post and a 3-email follow-up sequence.

Output 1 — Blog Post (1,500–2,000 words):
— SEO Title: Includes primary keyword and the webinar's main topic
— Introduction (200 words): Set context for the topic, cite the webinar as source, and preview the post's structure
— 4–5 H2 sections: Each section covers a major webinar segment. Include the most valuable insights, examples, and frameworks from each section.
— At least 2 blockquotes: Pull the most quotable moments from the webinar
— Conclusion with CTA: Watch the full recording, download the slides, or book a call

Output 2 — 3-Email Follow-Up Sequence:
— Email 1 (send immediately after): Thank attendees, deliver the recording link and any promised resources, and preview what they learned. Subject line: "Your [Webinar Name] recording + resources"
— Email 2 (send 2 days later): Highlight one key insight from the webinar they may have missed. Include one actionable next step they can take today. Subject line: curiosity-driven, referencing the specific insight.
— Email 3 (send 5 days later): Softer CTA email. Share a related resource (blog post, template, or guide). Include a product CTA for those who are ready to take the next step.

Variables:
— Webinar Title: [WEBINAR TITLE]
— Webinar Date: [DATE]
— Host Name and Company: [HOST — NAME, COMPANY]
— Guest Speaker(s): [SPEAKER NAME — TITLE, COMPANY (if applicable)]
— Webinar Transcript or Outline: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT OR DETAILED OUTLINE]
— Recording Link: [URL TO FULL RECORDING]
— Resources Promised (slides, templates, etc.): [LIST RESOURCES]
— Product CTA for Email 3: [WHAT YOU WANT THEM TO DO — e.g., "book a demo," "start a free trial," "purchase the course"]
— Primary Keyword for Blog Post: [TARGET KEYWORD]

Email 2's timing — two days after the webinar — is specifically designed to catch registrants who did not attend live and are still within the purchase-consideration window. The "insight they may have missed" framing works for both attendees (reinforcing a key point) and no-shows (offering value they missed), without requiring you to segment the list differently.

Prompt 8: Customer Testimonial → Multi-Format Content

Customer testimonials are one of the most underused content assets in most marketing libraries. Most teams post the testimonial on the website's social proof section and stop there. But a single strong testimonial — especially one with specific results, a compelling before/after, or an interesting customer story — can be adapted into a case study, a LinkedIn post, an ad creative, an email, and a Twitter post. This prompt extracts maximum value from a single customer testimonial by generating five platform-native content pieces, each using the testimonial's core story differently.

Prompt CR-08: Customer Testimonial → Multi-Format Content
Act as a case study and testimonial content specialist. Read the customer testimonial or success story below and generate five content pieces that each use this story differently for a different platform or format.

Output 1 — Mini Case Study (300–400 words): Structure as Problem → Solution → Result. Include the customer's situation before, what they did with your product/service, and the specific measurable result. Write in third person, journalistic style.

Output 2 — LinkedIn Post (800–1,000 characters): Lead with the result. Tell the story in 3–4 short paragraphs. End with a question or CTA. No external links in the body.

Output 3 — Ad Creative Copy (Facebook/Instagram): Short headline (under 40 characters) that leads with the result. Body copy (under 125 characters) that gives context and social proof. CTA button recommendation.

Output 4 — Email Testimonial Story (200–300 words): Frame the customer story as a relatable narrative. Start with their situation (which readers will identify with), describe the turning point, and end with the result. Include a soft CTA.

Output 5 — Tweet/Social Quote Card (under 240 characters): The most quotable line from the testimonial, formatted as a pull quote. Include the customer's name and role if available.

Variables:
— Customer Testimonial or Success Story: [PASTE FULL TESTIMONIAL OR STORY]
— Customer Name: [NAME]
— Customer Title and Company: [TITLE, COMPANY]
— Your Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Key Result (if not in testimonial): [SPECIFIC MEASURABLE OUTCOME — e.g., "reduced content creation time by 60%," "increased email open rates from 18% to 34%"]
— Target Audience for These Pieces: [WHO YOU ARE TRYING TO REACH]

The five-format approach extracts the maximum value from testimonials with strong, specific results. A testimonial that says "it is great!" has limited repurposing potential. A testimonial that says "we reduced our content production time by 60% and grew our email list by 4,000 subscribers in 90 days" contains a story, a before/after, specific numbers, and a clear result — all the raw material needed for every format above. When collecting testimonials, prompt customers for the specific outcome they achieved to make this prompt as effective as possible.

Prompt 9: Data/Report → Infographic Copy

Data reports, survey results, and research findings are highly repurposable because they contain information that is inherently visual — statistics, percentages, rankings, and comparisons that readers want to scan rather than read. An infographic turns dense data into a shareable, embeddable format that earns backlinks, social shares, and platform reach that a text-heavy report cannot. This prompt extracts the most visually compelling data points from a report and writes the copy for a long-form infographic, including the title, section headers, stat call-outs, and explanatory captions.

Prompt CR-09: Data/Report → Infographic Copy
Act as an infographic content strategist who turns data-heavy reports into visually compelling infographic copy. Read the data or report below and write the complete text content for a long-form infographic.

Infographic copy structure:
— Infographic Title (under 60 characters): Bold, specific, includes the primary stat or topic. Example: "The State of Content Marketing in 2026: 15 Key Stats"
— Subtitle (under 100 characters): Provides context for the data — source, year, sample size
— 4–6 Sections: Group related data points into thematic sections. Each section gets:
  — Section Header (under 40 characters): The theme of this section's data
  — 2–4 Stat Call-Outs: Format as "[BIG NUMBER/STAT]" on one line and "[CONTEXT]" on the next. The big number should be immediately scannable (e.g., "73%" not "73 percent of respondents")
  — 1 Insight Line (under 60 characters): A one-line interpretation of what the section's data means
— Key Takeaway (1 sentence): The single most important insight from the entire report
— Source Attribution: Format as "Source: [Source Name], [Year], [Sample Size]"

Design guidance (to include with copy for the designer):
— Recommend which stats deserve the largest visual treatment (biggest numbers, most surprising findings)
— Note which data points could be visualized as charts, progress bars, or icons vs. text call-outs

Variables:
— Report/Data Source: [REPORT NAME, ORGANIZATION, YEAR]
— Full Report or Key Data Points: [PASTE REPORT CONTENT OR LIST OF STATISTICS]
— Primary Topic/Keyword: [TOPIC — e.g., "content marketing," "email marketing ROI," "remote work trends"]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS THIS INFOGRAPHIC FOR]
— Infographic Goal: [GOAL — e.g., "earn backlinks," "social shares," "lead magnet," "press/PR"]
— Brand Name: [YOUR BRAND FOR ATTRIBUTION]

The design guidance section — recommending which stats deserve the largest visual treatment — is a practical addition that most content-to-infographic workflows skip. Designers often default to treating all data points equally, when the most impactful infographics use visual hierarchy to guide the reader's eye to the most surprising or important numbers. Including this guidance in the prompt output means your designer has a content strategy brief, not just raw copy.

Prompt 10: Content Repurposing Matrix (One Piece → 10 Formats)

The content repurposing matrix is the ultimate prompt in this collection. Instead of repurposing one piece of content into one other format, the matrix takes a single source piece and maps it to 10 different formats simultaneously, producing either the full content for each format or a detailed brief for your team to execute. This is the prompt that content teams use to plan an entire month of content from a single cornerstone piece — a pillar blog post, a flagship report, or a hero video. Run this prompt once per month on your most important piece of new content.

Prompt CR-10: Content Repurposing Matrix (One Piece → 10 Formats)
Act as a senior content strategist building a full content repurposing matrix. Read the source content below and produce a complete repurposing plan that maps this single piece of content to 10 different formats. For each format, write either the full content (for shorter formats) or a detailed production brief (for longer formats that require design or video production).

Format 1 — LinkedIn Post: Full copy (800–1,200 characters). Hook-driven, short paragraphs, ends with a question.

Format 2 — Twitter/X Thread: First 5 tweets of the full thread. Indicate how many total tweets the full thread should have.

Format 3 — Email Newsletter: Subject line (3 options), preview text, and full newsletter body (300–400 words).

Format 4 — Instagram Caption: Full caption (up to 2,200 characters) + 5 hashtags.

Format 5 — LinkedIn Carousel: Cover slide + first 5 slide copy blocks. Note total recommended slide count.

Format 6 — Short-Form Video Script (60 seconds): Hook (first 3 seconds), 3 main points (10 seconds each), CTA (last 10 seconds). Written as spoken words, not prose.

Format 7 — Pinterest Pin: Title (100 characters max), description (500 characters max), keyword tags (5 tags).

Format 8 — FAQ Blog Section: 5 FAQs with answers (100–150 words each) derived from the source content. Formatted in H3 + paragraph structure.

Format 9 — Presentation/Slide Deck Outline: Title slide + 6–8 section headers with 2–3 bullet points per section. Brief note on what visual or data goes on each slide.

Format 10 — Podcast Episode Outline: Episode title, 3–5 main talking points with sub-points, suggested guest questions (if interview format), and a recommended episode length.

After all 10 formats, include a Publishing Schedule Recommendation:

5 Tips for Systematic Content Repurposing with AI

The 10 prompts above give you the production capability. These five tips turn that capability into a repeatable, scalable system.

1. Build a Source Content Library First

Before repurposing becomes systematic, you need a source content library — an organized inventory of your best existing content, tagged by topic, format, performance, and repurposing status. Start with your top 10–20 performing blog posts or videos (measured by traffic, shares, or conversions) and treat them as priority repurposing candidates. Content that has already proven its value with one audience is the safest bet for repurposing. A Notion database or simple spreadsheet with columns for "Original URL," "Formats Repurposed Into," and "Performance Notes" is sufficient.

2. Always Adapt Voice, Not Just Format

The most common repurposing failure is format-only adaptation. You take a blog post, run it through the LinkedIn post prompt, get output that is structurally correct for LinkedIn but reads nothing like your actual LinkedIn voice. Before using any prompt, include a voice sample in the "Brand Voice" variable — paste in 2–3 examples of your best-performing content from that specific platform. The AI uses those examples to calibrate its tone, vocabulary, and sentence structure to match your established voice, not a generic version of what that platform sounds like.

3. Repurpose Promptly, Then Evergreen

The optimal repurposing cadence has two phases. First, repurpose new cornerstone content within the first week of publication — this is when organic search and social momentum are highest and your audience is most primed for the topic. Run the Content Repurposing Matrix prompt on publication day so the repurposed formats are ready to deploy over the following two to three weeks. Second, build an evergreen repurposing schedule that revisits your best older content every 90 days with fresh angles, updated statistics, and new platform formats that did not exist when you originally published.

4. Measure Repurposed Content Separately

Most analytics setups do not distinguish between original content and repurposed content, which makes it impossible to measure the ROI of your repurposing effort. Use UTM parameters to tag all traffic from repurposed formats back to the original source URL, and create a separate analytics view or dashboard for repurposed content performance. When you can see that one original blog post generated 500 additional email signups through its repurposed LinkedIn carousel and Twitter thread, you have the business case to invest more in systematic repurposing — and the data to know which formats drive the most return.

5. Repurpose the Best, Create the Rest

Not every piece of content deserves full repurposing treatment. Reserve the Content Repurposing Matrix (Prompt 10) for your highest-performing, most evergreen pieces. Use the single-format prompts for regular content that has a specific distribution need. And continue creating net-new content for topics that have no existing source material. The goal is a 70/20/10 content split: 70% of your distribution comes from repurposing your best existing content, 20% from adapting recent content into one or two additional formats, and 10% from creating new content that seeds the next repurposing cycle.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Content Repurposing

What is content repurposing and why does it matter? +
Content repurposing is the process of taking existing content — a blog post, video, podcast episode, or webinar — and adapting it into new formats for different platforms. It matters because most content dies after its first publication. A blog post gets traffic for a week, then disappears. A video lives on YouTube but never reaches your LinkedIn audience. Content repurposing gives each piece of content 5–10 additional lives: the same ideas reach new audiences, in formats those audiences prefer, on platforms where they already spend time. Marketers who repurpose systematically produce 3–5x more content output with the same creation effort.
How do AI prompts make content repurposing faster? +
AI prompts make content repurposing faster by automating the structural translation between formats. The hardest part of repurposing is not finding the ideas — they are already in your original content — it is reformatting them for the new platform's native style. A blog post must become Twitter threads that hook in the first tweet, LinkedIn carousels that work without captions, and email newsletters that lead with value. Each format has its own grammar, length, and engagement patterns. The AI prompts in this article encode those platform-specific rules directly into the prompt, so you paste your source content, run the prompt, and get platform-ready output in minutes rather than hours.
Which content formats can be repurposed most effectively? +
Long-form content repurposes most effectively because it contains the most raw material. A single 2,000-word blog post contains enough ideas for 5 LinkedIn posts, a 10-tweet thread, an email newsletter, and a short-form video script. Webinars and podcast episodes are especially repurposable because they contain natural dialogue, questions and answers, and real examples that can become written content. The content repurposing matrix prompt at the end of this article maps a single piece of content to 10 different formats — the best starting point for any systematic repurposing workflow.
Do these prompts work with ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini? +
Yes. All 10 prompts in this article are model-agnostic — they work with ChatGPT (GPT-4o), Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or any large language model. The prompts rely on clear format specifications, platform-specific rules, and structural frameworks rather than model-specific features. Paste the prompt into whichever AI tool you already use, fill in the variables with your source content, and generate repurposed output in under five minutes. For best results with long source content like webinar transcripts, use a model with a large context window such as Claude or GPT-4o.
How many formats should I repurpose each piece of content into? +
Start with three to five formats per piece and build from there. The most effective repurposing stack for most content marketers is: (1) the original long-form piece, (2) an email newsletter version, (3) a LinkedIn post or carousel, (4) a Twitter/X thread, and (5) a short-form video script. That stack covers your highest-ROI distribution channels without overwhelming your production workflow. Once you have that rhythm working, use the content repurposing matrix prompt to expand into additional formats — infographic copy, podcast show notes, presentation slides — for your highest-performing original content.
