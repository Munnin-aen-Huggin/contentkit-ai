12 AI Prompts for Local SEO That Dominate Google Maps

ContentKit AI•14 min read

AI prompts for local SEO are structured instructions that generate Google Business Profile descriptions, geo-targeted landing pages, local citations, review responses, and city-specific content — the exact assets Google uses to rank local businesses in Maps and the local pack. This guide delivers 12 copy-paste prompts covering every major local SEO asset, each built with geo-targeting variables, keyword frameworks, and the specificity that Google's local algorithm rewards.

Local search is the highest-intent channel in digital marketing. When someone searches "emergency dentist Austin" or "best Italian restaurant near me," they are not browsing — they are ready to visit, call, or book. Google processes over 46% of all searches with local intent, and the three businesses that appear in the local pack — those three map pins at the top of the results page — capture the overwhelming majority of those clicks. If your business is not in that pack, you are invisible to the customers most ready to buy.

But winning the local pack is not about gaming the algorithm. Google's local ranking factors are straightforward: relevance (does your business match what the searcher needs?), distance (how close are you?), and prominence (how well-known and active is your online presence?). The problem is that most local businesses have poorly optimized Google Business Profiles, sparse or inconsistent citations across directories, no review response strategy, and zero geo-targeted content. They lose the local pack not because their competitors are more deserving — but because their competitors have more relevant, active, and authoritative local signals.

This is exactly where structured ai prompts for local seo change the equation. Instead of manually writing a GBP description, drafting city-specific landing pages for every service area, or crafting individual review responses one by one, you use a single prompt to generate professional, keyword-rich, geo-targeted content in minutes. The AI handles the volume and the copywriting. You handle the strategy and the local business knowledge that makes the content authentic.

The 12 prompts below cover every major local SEO asset: Google Business Profile descriptions, weekly GBP posts, local landing page copy, city-specific blog content, positive and negative review responses, NAP consistency audit frameworks, local schema markup, geo-targeted meta descriptions, local link building outreach, local FAQ pages, and a monthly local SEO content calendar. Each prompt includes the specific variables, character limits, and local SEO best practices built in — so the output is ready to publish.

For a broader set of AI prompts for SEO content beyond local search — including keyword research, pillar pages, and technical audits — see our full SEO prompt guide.

Why AI Prompts Produce Better Local SEO Content

Local SEO content has a problem that general SEO content does not: it requires repetition with variation. If you serve 12 cities in your metro area, you need 12 local landing pages — each optimized for its specific city, each unique enough to avoid thin-content penalties, yet each covering the same core service. Writing 12 variations of the same page manually is tedious and almost always produces weak, repetitive content. AI prompts solve this by taking your business details and service description once and generating city-specific variations that feel genuinely local, not templated.

The same principle applies to GBP posts, review responses, and local citations. These are recurring tasks that require consistent effort — the businesses that dominate local pack rankings are not producing better individual pieces of content. They are publishing consistently, responding to every review, and maintaining their citation data across dozens of directories. Structured AI prompts make that level of consistency achievable without a full-time local SEO team.

Geo-Targeting Precision

The single biggest weakness in AI-generated local SEO content is generic output. Ask a general AI to "write a local landing page for a plumber in Austin" and you get a page that says "Austin" a few times but contains no actual local signals — no neighborhood names, no local landmarks, no references to the specific communities your business serves. The prompts in this article are built differently. Each one includes specific variables for neighborhoods, zip codes, local landmarks, and community references — the hyper-local signals that tell Google (and potential customers) that you are a genuine part of the community, not a national brand pretending to be local.

Consistency Across All Local Assets

Google's local algorithm is cross-referential. It checks whether your Name, Address, and Phone number (NAP) match across your GBP profile, your website, your citations, and your schema markup. It checks whether your business description on your website matches what your GBP says. It checks whether your review responses use relevant keywords. Most local businesses have inconsistencies across these touchpoints that suppress their rankings without them realizing it. The prompts below include a NAP consistency audit prompt specifically designed to surface and fix these inconsistencies.

The result: ai prompts for local seo do not replace your local market knowledge. They amplify it — turning your expertise about your service area, your customers, and your differentiators into a consistent, keyword-rich, geo-targeted content strategy that Google can identify and reward. Here are the 12 prompts.

Prompt 1: Google Business Profile Description

Your Google Business Profile description is 750 characters of prime local SEO real estate. Most businesses waste it with generic copy like "We are a family-owned business dedicated to providing quality service." Google uses the GBP description as a relevance signal for local search queries, so every word should work strategically: your primary service keyword, your city, your key differentiators, and a clear call to action. This prompt generates an optimized GBP description that reads naturally while hitting every keyword and trust signal Google rewards.

Prompt LS-01: Google Business Profile Description
Act as a local SEO specialist with 10+ years optimizing Google Business Profiles. Write an optimized GBP business description for the local business below. The description must be under 750 characters (Google's hard limit) and read naturally while including strategic local SEO signals.

Include in this order:
— Opening sentence: Primary service + city name + years in business or founding year
— Body: Top 2–3 services with benefit-driven language. Include specific neighborhoods or areas served.
— Differentiators: What makes this business different from every other provider in the city (not generic claims like "quality" — be specific: certifications, guarantees, response times, awards).
— Closing CTA: Action-oriented sentence with a reason to contact today.

Rules: No keyword stuffing. Read naturally out loud. Include the city name at least twice. Include one specific differentiator that competitors cannot easily copy. Stay under 750 characters. Do not include website URLs or phone numbers (Google strips these).

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Primary Service: [SERVICE — e.g., "residential plumbing," "family dentistry," "personal injury law"]
— City: [PRIMARY CITY]
— Neighborhoods/Areas Served: [SPECIFIC NEIGHBORHOODS OR SERVICE AREA — e.g., "Westlake, Tarrytown, Hyde Park, and South Congress"]
— Years in Business: [YEARS OR FOUNDING YEAR]
— Top 3 Services: [SERVICE 1, SERVICE 2, SERVICE 3]
— Key Differentiator: [SPECIFIC DIFFERENTIATOR — e.g., "licensed master plumber on every job," "same-day emergency appointments," "bilingual staff"]
— Trust Signal: [PROOF POINT — e.g., "4.9 stars from 300+ reviews," "A+ BBB rating," "Angie's List Super Service Award 2025"]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Call for a free estimate," "Book online 24/7," "Schedule your consultation today"]

Example output (Austin plumber, 724 characters):
Austin's trusted plumbing experts since 2009, serving Westlake, Tarrytown, Hyde Park, and South Congress. We specialize in emergency plumbing repair, water heater installation, and whole-home repipes — with a licensed master plumber on every job, not an apprentice. Unlike larger chains, we answer every call 24/7 and arrive within two hours for emergencies. Voted Austin's Best Plumber by the Austin Chronicle three years running and rated 4.9 stars from 340+ Google reviews. Need a plumber in Austin today? Call now for a free estimate and same-day service — guaranteed.

Notice how "Austin" appears three times, specific neighborhoods are listed, and the differentiator ("licensed master plumber on every job") is specific enough that a competitor cannot simply copy and paste it. Generic claims like "quality service" do not move local rankings. Specific, verifiable differentiators do.

Prompt 2: GBP Post (Weekly)

Google Business Profile posts are one of the most underused local SEO assets. They appear directly on your GBP listing in search results and on Google Maps, give you fresh keyword signals every week, and drive direct calls and website clicks without requiring users to visit your site. GBP posts expire after seven days, which means a weekly publishing cadence is the minimum for maintaining a visible, active profile. This prompt generates a week's worth of GBP posts with different post types — offer, update, event, and product — each optimized for local keyword relevance and click-through.

Prompt LS-02: GBP Post (Weekly)
Act as a local SEO content strategist specializing in Google Business Profile optimization. Write 4 Google Business Profile posts for the business below — one for each of the four standard post types: Offer, What's New, Event, and Product/Service.

For each post provide:
— Post Type label
— Post copy (1,500 characters max, but aim for 150–300 for readability)
— CTA button recommendation (Book, Order Online, Call Now, Learn More, Sign Up, Get Offer, Visit)
— 3 relevant local keywords naturally embedded in the post

Post requirements:
— Include the city name in at least 2 of the 4 posts
— Include a specific offer, date, statistic, or proof point in every post (no vague claims)
— Write in a friendly, direct tone — not corporate
— Each post should stand alone and make sense without reading the others
— Front-load the key information (GBP truncates after the first 100 characters on mobile)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Primary Service: [SERVICE]
— City: [CITY]
— Current Offer: [DEAL OR PROMOTION — e.g., "$50 off first service," "free consultation this month," "10% off for seniors"]
— Upcoming Event or Season: [EVENT OR SEASONAL ANGLE — e.g., "spring HVAC tune-up season," "back-to-school dental checkups," "summer landscaping rush"]
— Featured Product/Service: [SPECIFIC SERVICE TO HIGHLIGHT]
— Trust Signal: [REVIEW STAT OR AWARD]

The four-post-type strategy matters because different post types display differently on Google Maps and Search. Offer posts show a bright orange "Offer" label that stands out in search results. Event posts are prominently featured when users search for things happening in your area. Rotating through all four types maximizes your profile's visual variety and gives Google more signals about your business's range of services and community involvement.

Prompt 3: Local Landing Page Copy

Local landing pages — dedicated pages for each city or service area you serve — are one of the highest-leverage local SEO investments a multi-location or service-area business can make. A well-optimized local landing page for "plumber in Round Rock TX" can rank independently in the local pack for that city even if your main business is based in Austin. The challenge is writing pages that are genuinely city-specific (not thin content that just swaps the city name) while following the on-page SEO structure Google rewards. This prompt generates full local landing page copy with the H1, intro, service sections, social proof, and local FAQ that makes a page rank.

Prompt LS-03: Local Landing Page Copy
Act as a local SEO content writer who specializes in geo-targeted landing pages. Write a complete local service area landing page for the city specified below. This page must rank for "[service] in [city]" queries and convert local searchers into leads.

Page structure (write each section):
— H1 tag: "[Primary Service] in [City], [State] — [Business Name]" (include exact keyword)
— Opening paragraph (150 words): Establish local relevance immediately. Mention the city, specific neighborhoods, and a local reference (nearby landmark, community, or regional fact). Include the primary keyword in the first sentence.
— Why Choose Us section (3 bullet points): Each bullet = specific differentiator with proof. No generic claims.
— Services section: List top 3 services with a 2–3 sentence description each. Include city name naturally in at least one description per service.
— Local Social Proof block: 2 short testimonial-style quotes (fabricate realistic examples for review — mark as [REPLACE WITH REAL REVIEW]). Include reviewer first name and neighborhood.
— Local FAQ section (3 Q&As): Answer the top 3 questions a [city] resident would ask about this service. Include city name and specific local details in answers.
— CTA section: Strong closing paragraph with city name, primary service keyword, and clear next step.

Rules: City name must appear 8–12 times across the full page (not stuffed — woven naturally). Use neighborhood names, local context, and region-specific details to prove genuine local relevance. Avoid copy-paste-and-swap thin content.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Primary Service: [SERVICE]
— Target City: [CITY, STATE]
— Neighborhoods Served in This City: [NEIGHBORHOOD LIST]
— Local Landmark or Reference: [LOCAL REFERENCE — e.g., "near the Domain," "serving families in the Round Rock Independent School District area"]
— Top 3 Services: [SERVICE 1, SERVICE 2, SERVICE 3]
— Key Differentiator: [SPECIFIC DIFFERENTIATOR]
— Review Stats: [STARS AND NUMBER OF REVIEWS]
— Years in Business: [YEARS]

The most important instruction in this prompt is the prohibition on thin content. Google's Panda and Helpful Content systems actively penalize local landing pages that are simply templated duplicates with the city name swapped. What makes a local page genuinely rank is local specificity: neighborhood names, school district references, local landmarks, and community context that only a business actually serving that area would know. This prompt forces that specificity through its variable structure and explicit rules.

Prompt 4: Local Blog Post (City-Specific)

City-specific blog posts serve a different purpose than local landing pages. Where landing pages target direct service queries ("plumber in Austin"), local blog posts target informational queries that local residents search when they have a problem: "how to find a licensed plumber in Austin," "average cost of water heater replacement in Austin," "best neighborhoods in Austin for first-time homebuyers." These informational posts build topical authority in your local area and create link opportunities from local news sites, neighborhood blogs, and community resources. This prompt generates a fully structured city-specific blog post that ranks for local informational queries and drives local brand awareness.

Prompt LS-04: Local Blog Post (City-Specific)
Act as a local content marketing strategist who writes geo-targeted blog posts for service businesses. Write a complete 800–1,000 word blog post targeting a local informational keyword for the city specified below.

Post structure:
— Title: Include the city name and target keyword. Use a number, question, or specific angle ("7 Things Austin Homeowners Should Know About..." or "How Much Does [Service] Cost in [City]? A 2026 Guide").
— Introduction (100 words): Hook with a local scenario or statistic. State the post's specific promise. Include city name and primary keyword.
— Body (600–700 words): 3–5 sections with H2 headers. Each section must include city-specific information — local regulations, climate factors, neighborhood considerations, or local market data. Mention 3–5 specific neighborhoods or landmarks naturally.
— Local Expert Tip callout box: 1 paragraph of professional advice specific to this city's conditions.
— Conclusion with CTA (100 words): Summarize key takeaways. End with a direct CTA to contact the business for service in the city.

Rules: Write for actual local readers, not for search engines. Every section must include local context that would only appear in a post genuinely written for this city. No placeholder content like "[insert local stat here]" — research and include realistic specifics even if approximate.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Service Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "water heater replacement cost," "what to look for when hiring a plumber," "signs your pipes need replacing"]
— City: [CITY, STATE]
— Target Keyword: [INFORMATIONAL KEYWORD — e.g., "plumber Austin TX," "water heater cost Austin," "licensed plumber Austin"]
— Local Context: [CITY-SPECIFIC DETAIL — e.g., "Austin's hard water and lime buildup," "Phoenix's extreme heat and AC strain," "Seattle's rainy season and pipe freezing"]
— Neighborhoods to Mention: [3–5 NEIGHBORHOODS]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION]

The local expert tip callout is a strategic element that most local blog posts lack. When you include a section like "Austin Expert Tip: If your home was built before 1985 and is located in older neighborhoods like Cherrywood or Mueller, have your pipes inspected for galvanized steel — a common issue in Austin's aging housing stock," you demonstrate genuine local expertise that both readers and Google's content quality systems reward. Generic plumbing advice could come from anyone. Austin-specific advice could only come from a plumber who actually works there.

Prompt 5: Review Response (Positive)

Responding to positive reviews is not just good customer service — it is an active local SEO signal. Google's documentation confirms that "responding to reviews shows that you value your customers and the feedback that they leave about your business." Review responses are public-facing, indexed text that appears on your GBP profile. Each response is an opportunity to reinforce local keyword signals, mention specific services, and reference the city or neighborhood — all in natural, conversational language that avoids the feeling of keyword stuffing. This prompt generates a library of positive review response templates that feel personal while working strategically for local SEO.

Prompt LS-05: Review Response (Positive)
Act as a local business reputation manager who writes review responses that strengthen local SEO signals while feeling genuinely personal. Write 5 positive review response templates for the business below. Each response should feel unique, not templated, even though they share a structure.

Each response must include:
— Personalized opener: Thank the reviewer by first name (use [REVIEWER NAME] as placeholder)
— Acknowledge a specific detail from their experience (use [SPECIFIC DETAIL] as placeholder — instruct the user to fill this in from the actual review)
— Local keyword reinforcement: Naturally mention the service and city in the middle of the response
— Invitation to return or refer: Close with a warm invitation to come back or recommend to neighbors
— Business sign-off: End with the business name

Rules: Keep each response between 75–150 words. Never use the same opening phrase twice across the 5 templates. Sound human, not automated. Do not repeat the exact keyword phrase more than once per response. Avoid exclamation points in every sentence (one per response maximum).

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Primary Service: [SERVICE]
— City: [CITY]
— Review Platform: [GOOGLE / YELP / FACEBOOK]
— Key Service Keywords: [2–3 LOCAL SERVICE KEYWORDS TO WEAVE IN — e.g., "Austin plumber," "emergency plumbing repair Austin," "water heater replacement Austin"]

Example Template 1 (Austin plumber):
Thank you so much, [REVIEWER NAME] — we really appreciate you taking the time to share your experience. [SPECIFIC DETAIL] is exactly what we aim for every time we serve a customer in Austin. Our goal has always been to be the plumber Austin homeowners can rely on, whether it is a routine repair or a 2 a.m. emergency. We will pass your kind words along to the technician who helped you. If you ever need us again or know a neighbor who needs a plumber in Austin, do not hesitate to reach out. — The Team at [Business Name]

Prompt 6: Review Response (Negative)

Negative review responses are the highest-stakes local SEO content a business produces. A poorly handled negative review — defensive, dismissive, or argumentative — signals to potential customers and Google's quality systems that the business does not take customer experience seriously. A well-crafted response, on the other hand, can actually convert skeptical readers into customers by demonstrating accountability, professionalism, and a genuine commitment to resolution. This prompt generates negative review response templates that defuse tension, offer resolution, and protect your local reputation — without admitting liability or confirming inaccurate details.

Prompt LS-06: Review Response (Negative)
Act as a reputation management specialist who handles negative reviews for local service businesses. Write 4 negative review response templates for different complaint categories: (1) service quality complaint, (2) pricing dispute, (3) scheduling/timeliness complaint, (4) miscommunication complaint.

Each response must:
— Open with empathy — acknowledge the frustration without admitting fault for unverified claims
— Take ownership of the resolution, not the specific complaint
— Move the conversation offline: provide a direct contact (use [DIRECT PHONE OR EMAIL] as placeholder)
— Close professionally with the business name
— Stay under 100 words (Google shows the full response; brevity signals confidence, not guilt)

Rules: Never be defensive. Never argue facts in the response (that battle is lost in public). Never offer discounts in the public response (creates incentive for fake negative reviews). Use "I" not "we" in the opener to sound personal, not corporate. Do not repeat the negative keywords from the original review.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— City: [CITY]
— Owner/Manager Name: [OWNER OR MANAGER NAME FOR SIGN-OFF]
— Direct Contact: [PHONE NUMBER OR EMAIL FOR OFFLINE RESOLUTION]

The "move offline" principle is the most important rule in negative review response strategy. Every word you write in a public review response is read by hundreds of prospective customers. An extended back-and-forth argument in the review thread — even one where you are factually correct — damages your brand with every reader. Acknowledge, empathize, and invite a private conversation. Resolution happens offline. The public response exists to demonstrate character to the 200 people who read it, not to resolve the complaint with the one person who left it.

Prompt 7: Local Citation NAP Consistency Audit

NAP consistency — ensuring your Name, Address, and Phone number are identical across every directory, citation, and online mention — is one of the foundational local SEO ranking factors. Google cross-references your NAP data across Yelp, Yellow Pages, Bing Places, Apple Maps, industry-specific directories, and dozens of other sources. Inconsistencies (a suite number listed on some directories but not others, a phone number with a different area code, a business name with an abbreviation on some platforms) confuse the algorithm and suppress local pack rankings. This prompt generates a systematic citation audit framework and correction plan you can execute with or without paid tools.

Prompt LS-07: Local Citation NAP Consistency Audit
Act as a local SEO technical specialist performing a NAP (Name, Address, Phone) consistency audit. Create a complete citation audit and correction plan for the local business below.

Deliverables:
1. Master NAP record: The exact, canonical version of the business name, address, and phone number to use everywhere. Flag any formatting decisions (abbreviations, suite numbers, phone number format).
2. Priority citation checklist: List the top 20 citation sources to audit, organized by priority tier:
— Tier 1 (highest impact): Google Business Profile, Apple Maps, Bing Places, Facebook Business, Yelp
— Tier 2 (industry-standard): Yellow Pages, Foursquare, Better Business Bureau, Angi (Angie's List), HomeAdvisor
— Tier 3 (niche/industry-specific): List 5–10 industry-specific directories relevant to the business type
3. Inconsistency detection guide: A list of the 8 most common NAP inconsistencies to look for on each platform (abbreviations, suite formats, phone number formats, etc.).
4. Correction priority order: Which inconsistencies to fix first based on local ranking impact.
5. Ongoing citation monitoring checklist: Monthly maintenance tasks to keep NAP consistent.

Variables:
— Business Name: [EXACT LEGAL OR DBA BUSINESS NAME]
— Address: [FULL ADDRESS INCLUDING SUITE/UNIT IF APPLICABLE]
— Phone Number: [PRIMARY PHONE NUMBER]
— Business Type: [INDUSTRY — e.g., "residential plumbing," "dental practice," "personal injury law firm"]
— Primary City: [CITY]
— Website URL: [WEBSITE]

The tier system matters because not all citations carry equal weight. Fixing a NAP inconsistency on Google Business Profile has 10x the local ranking impact of fixing it on an obscure directory. This prompt organizes the audit by priority so you spend your time where it moves rankings, not where it merely tidies up data that Google barely notices.

Prompt 8: Local Schema Markup Generator

Local business schema markup is structured data code — added to your website's HTML — that tells Google's crawlers exactly what your business is, where it is located, when it is open, and what it does. While schema is not a direct ranking factor, it significantly improves how Google understands and displays your business in search results: enabling rich snippets, local knowledge panels, and enhanced local pack listings. Most local businesses have no schema markup at all, which is a missed opportunity that takes less than 30 minutes to fix. This prompt generates complete, ready-to-paste LocalBusiness schema markup in JSON-LD format.

Prompt LS-08: Local Schema Markup Generator
Act as a technical SEO specialist who implements local business structured data. Generate a complete LocalBusiness schema markup in JSON-LD format for the business below. The markup should be ready to paste into the <head> section of the website's homepage and location-specific landing pages.

Include all relevant schema properties:
— @type: Use the most specific LocalBusiness subtype available (Plumber, Dentist, LegalService, Restaurant, etc.)
— name, url, telephone, address (PostalAddress), geo (GeoCoordinates if known)
— openingHoursSpecification: Full weekly schedule
— hasMap: Google Maps URL
— priceRange: Use $ to $$$$ scale
— aggregateRating: If the business has verified reviews
— areaServed: List of cities and neighborhoods served
— sameAs: Social media and directory profile URLs
— image: Primary business image URL
— description: 150-character business description matching the GBP description tone

Also generate a separate Service schema for each of the top 3 services, linking back to the business entity.

After the code, provide a validation checklist: the top 5 things to verify using Google's Rich Results Test.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [SCHEMA TYPE — e.g., "Plumber," "Dentist," "Attorney," "Restaurant"]
— Address: [FULL ADDRESS]
— Phone: [PHONE NUMBER]
— Website: [URL]
— Hours: [WEEKLY HOURS FOR EACH DAY]
— Price Range: [$, $$, $$$, or $$$$]
— Google Maps URL: [MAPS LINK]
— Review Stats: [AVERAGE RATING AND REVIEW COUNT]
— Areas Served: [CITY LIST]
— Social Profiles: [FACEBOOK, YELP, AND OTHER PROFILE URLS]
— Top 3 Services: [SERVICE 1, SERVICE 2, SERVICE 3]

The Service schema addition is the element most businesses miss. LocalBusiness schema tells Google what you are and where you are. Service schema tells Google what specific services you provide, creating individual structured data entities for each service that can surface in rich results independently. A plumbing business with Service schema for "Emergency Plumbing Repair," "Water Heater Installation," and "Pipe Repiping" gives Google three additional structured signals about its service scope — each one contributing to relevance for the corresponding local search queries.

Prompt 9: Geo-Targeted Meta Descriptions

Meta descriptions are the two-line preview text that appears under your page title in Google search results. While they are not a direct ranking factor, they dramatically affect click-through rate from local search results pages — and Google does use CTR as a local ranking signal. A geo-targeted meta description that includes the city, primary service keyword, and a compelling differentiator within 160 characters earns significantly more clicks than a generic description. This prompt generates optimized geo-targeted meta descriptions for your homepage, local landing pages, and service pages.

Prompt LS-09: Geo-Targeted Meta Descriptions
Act as a local SEO copywriter specializing in search snippet optimization. Write geo-targeted meta descriptions for the pages listed below. Each description must earn the click from a local searcher who sees 5–10 results on the page and makes a snap judgment in under two seconds.

For each page, write 2 variations:
— Variation A: Leads with the primary local keyword (service + city)
— Variation B: Leads with the key differentiator or offer

Requirements for each description:
— 150–160 characters (Google truncates beyond this)
— Include city name
— Include primary service keyword
— Include one trust signal OR one specific offer OR one urgency element
— End with a micro-CTA ("Call today," "Book online," "Get a free estimate")
— No clickbait — the description must accurately represent the page content

Pages to write descriptions for:
— Homepage
— Primary service page
— City-specific landing page
— About page
— Contact/Book page

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Primary Service: [SERVICE]
— City: [CITY, STATE]
— Key Differentiator: [SPECIFIC USP]
— Current Offer: [OFFER OR INCENTIVE]
— Trust Signal: [REVIEW STAT OR AWARD]

Homepage — Variation A (158 chars): Austin plumber with 4.9 stars from 340+ reviews. Emergency repairs, water heater install & repipes. Licensed master plumber on every job. Call today.

Homepage — Variation B (157 chars): Same-day plumbing service in Austin, TX. Licensed master plumber on every job — not an apprentice. Free estimates. 4.9 stars. Book online 24/7.

The two-variation approach lets you A/B test which opening angle earns more clicks in Google Search Console. Keyword-first descriptions tend to perform better for high-intent queries where the searcher wants to confirm you are local. Differentiator-first descriptions tend to outperform in competitive markets where multiple local businesses appear and the searcher is scanning for a reason to choose one over the others.

Prompt 10: Local Link Building Outreach Email

Local backlinks — links from other websites in your city or region — are one of the strongest local pack ranking signals. A link from the Austin Business Journal, a neighborhood association website, or a local Chamber of Commerce directory carries far more local SEO weight than a generic link from a national directory. But most local businesses have no link building strategy at all. This prompt generates personalized outreach email templates for the three most effective local link building tactics: local news story pitches, community sponsorship link requests, and local business partnership proposals.

Prompt LS-10: Local Link Building Outreach Email
Act as a local SEO link building specialist. Write 3 outreach email templates for different local link building scenarios. Each email must earn a response — not just an open — by leading with genuine value for the recipient, not a request.

Template 1 — Local News Story Pitch (to local newspaper or neighborhood blog editor):
— Subject line (under 50 characters): Newsy, specific, locally relevant
— Email body (under 150 words): Lead with the story angle (why readers would care), offer to be a quoted local expert source, mention credentials, soft-pitch for coverage. Do not ask for a link directly.

Template 2 — Community Sponsorship Link (to local event organizer, school, or nonprofit):
— Subject line + body (under 120 words): Offer a specific sponsorship level (dollar amount or in-kind), explain community connection, mention the link/listing as a benefit of sponsorship — not the primary ask.

Template 3 — Local Business Partnership (to complementary non-competing local business):
— Subject line + body (under 130 words): Propose a mutual referral or resource-sharing relationship. Suggest a reciprocal link or co-created local resource (e.g., "Austin Homeowner's Guide" co-authored by a plumber and an electrician).

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [SERVICE TYPE]
— City: [CITY]
— Owner/Contact Name: [YOUR NAME]
— Local Credential: [RELEVANT CREDENTIAL — e.g., "licensed since 2009," "A+ BBB rating," "Austin Chamber member"]
— Sponsorship Budget: [DOLLAR RANGE OR IN-KIND SERVICE VALUE]
— Target Partner Business: [COMPLEMENTARY BUSINESS TYPE — e.g., "electrician," "real estate agent," "HVAC company"]

The no-direct-link-ask rule in Template 1 is the most counterintuitive but most effective principle in local link building outreach. Journalists and editors receive dozens of link requests disguised as story pitches. The moment you mention "we would love a link," the email goes in the trash. Lead with the story. Establish yourself as a local expert source. If the coverage happens, the link follows naturally. This approach has a dramatically higher response rate than any direct link request template.

Prompt 11: Local FAQ Page Content

Local FAQ pages serve two simultaneous purposes: they target "People Also Ask" and voice search queries in local Google results, and they answer the pre-purchase questions that convert local searchers into customers. A plumber's FAQ page that answers "How much does a water heater replacement cost in Austin?" and "How quickly can a plumber come to my house in Austin?" is capturing traffic from local informational searches while simultaneously addressing the exact objections that prevent someone from calling. 5 Tips for Getting the Most from Local SEO Prompts

The 12 prompts above give you the raw material. These five tips ensure that material actually moves your Google Maps rankings.

1. Always Hyperlocalize Your Variables

The single most common mistake with local SEO prompts is under-specifying the location variables. Writing "[City]: Austin" is good. Writing "[Neighborhoods]: Westlake, Tarrytown, Hyde Park, South Congress, Mueller, Cherrywood, Barton Hills" is better. The more specific your geographic inputs, the more specific the AI output — and the more genuine local relevance signals the content contains. Google's local algorithm is trained to recognize genuine geographic specificity versus surface-level city-name mentions. Neighborhood references, local landmarks, school district names, and community events in your content signal genuine local presence in ways that city-name repetition alone cannot achieve.

2. Publish GBP Posts Every Week Without Exception

GBP posts expire after seven days. A Google Business Profile with no recent posts looks abandoned to both Google and to potential customers who scroll through your listing. Set a recurring calendar reminder for the same day each week and use Prompt 2 to generate four weeks of posts in a single session. Batch production is the key: spending 30 minutes once a month to generate four weeks of posts is infinitely more sustainable than trying to write a fresh post every Monday morning when you have other priorities competing for your attention.

3. Respond to Every Review Within 48 Hours

Google rewards review activity as a freshness signal. A business that receives and responds to reviews consistently ranks higher than an equally-reviewed business that goes weeks without responding. Use Prompts 5 and 6 to build a library of review response templates — customized for your business and city — so responding to any new review takes less than two minutes. The goal is not to draft a perfect response for each review from scratch. The goal is to respond to every review, every time, with a response that feels personal but is built on a tested template.

4. Build City-Specific Pages Before Targeting New Service Areas

If your goal is to expand into a new city or neighborhood, the local landing page (Prompt 3) is the first asset to create — before you run any local ads, before you hand out business cards, and before you ask for reviews in that area. A well-optimized local landing page gives Google a specific URL to associate with your local presence in that city. Once Google indexes that page and begins associating your domain with that city's search queries, your GBP posts, reviews, and citations in that area will gain local ranking power faster than if you had no city-specific landing page at all.

5. Audit Your Schema Markup Every Quarter

Local schema markup needs maintenance. Business hours change. New services are added. Phone numbers are updated. Review counts grow. An outdated schema that lists incorrect hours or a disconnected phone number sends conflicting signals to Google — the structured data says one thing, the page says another, and the GBP says a third. Set a quarterly reminder to run your homepage and landing pages through Google's Rich Results Test (search.google.com/test/rich-results) and update the schema generated by Prompt 8 to match your current business details. Ten minutes per quarter prevents the kind of NAP inconsistency that can quietly suppress local rankings for months.
