10 AI Prompts for Retargeting Ads That Recover Lost Sales

ContentKit AI•13 min read

AI prompts for retargeting ads are structured instructions that generate high-converting follow-up ads for people who visited your site, viewed a product, abandoned a cart, or watched your videos — matched to each audience segment's exact intent level to maximize return on ad spend. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every major retargeting scenario across Facebook, Google Display, and email — each built on proven segmentation frameworks that recover lost sales instead of repeating cold-traffic messaging to warm audiences.

Retargeting is the highest-ROI activity in most paid advertising programs. The math is straightforward: the average website converts between 1% and 3% of visitors on the first visit. That means 97–99% of people who clicked your ad, read your copy, and showed enough interest to visit your site left without buying. Those people already know who you are. They have already expressed interest. The only thing standing between them and a purchase is the right message at the right moment — and a retargeting ad is exactly that moment.

But most retargeting campaigns make a fundamental mistake: they serve cold-traffic creative to warm audiences. The same generic brand ad that ran for first-time visitors gets recycled into the retargeting campaign. The result is that your warmest audience — people who almost bought — sees the same message they already ignored, which confirms their hesitation instead of overcoming it.

Effective ai prompts for retargeting ads solve this by engineering the message to the specific behavior the visitor took. A cart abandoner needs urgency and a friction-reducing offer. A product page visitor needs social proof and differentiation. A blog reader needs a low-commitment next step that moves them deeper into the funnel. A past customer needs a win-back offer anchored to what they bought before. Each segment requires different copy — and the 10 prompts below generate that copy precisely, for every major retargeting use case.

For context on the broader paid advertising landscape, see our guide to AI prompts for Google Ads and our ChatGPT prompts for Facebook Ads — the retargeting prompts below are designed to complement those cold-traffic campaigns.

Why Retargeting Copy Must Match Audience Intent

Retargeting is not one audience — it is a hierarchy of intent. At the bottom are people who bounced from the homepage after five seconds. At the top are people who filled their cart, entered their payment details, and still did not complete the purchase. Every segment in between represents a different level of readiness to buy, and each level requires a fundamentally different message.

The mistake most advertisers make is treating retargeting as a single campaign. They build one audience ("all website visitors, last 30 days") and one ad set ("our product is great, buy now"). That approach ignores the enormous difference between a homepage bouncer and a cart abandoner — and serves irrelevant copy to both.

The Intent Hierarchy of Retargeting Audiences

Understanding where each audience sits in the intent hierarchy determines what your retargeting ad should say and what action it should ask for.

  • Cart abandoners are your highest-intent segment. They chose a product, added it to their cart, and stopped. The barrier is usually price, shipping cost, distraction, or indecision. The retargeting message should reduce friction: address the specific objection, offer free shipping or a small discount, and create urgency with a time-limited offer.
  • Product page visitors are high-intent but earlier in the decision process. They know the product. They are evaluating whether it is right for them. The message should add social proof (reviews, case studies, ratings), address common objections, and differentiate from alternatives they are comparing.
  • Pricing page visitors are evaluating affordability and ROI. They want to understand if the value justifies the cost. The retargeting message should anchor value, highlight ROI, and offer a lower-risk entry point (free trial, installment payment, money-back guarantee).
  • Blog readers are research-mode visitors. They are not ready to buy, but they are building awareness of the problem your product solves. The retargeting message should offer the next step in the journey — a free resource, a product demonstration, or a webinar — rather than asking for a direct purchase.
  • Past customers already trust you. The barrier to repeat purchase is not awareness or trust — it is relevance and timing. Win-back ads should reference what they bought before, show what is new or complementary, and offer a loyalty incentive.

The prompts below are organized by audience segment, with each prompt engineered to match the specific intent level of that audience. Here are the 10 prompts.

10 AI Prompts for Retargeting Ads

Prompt 1: Facebook Retargeting Ad (Visited Product Page)

Someone visited a specific product page on your site and left without buying. They know the product, they saw the price, and something stopped them. This Facebook retargeting ad targets that exact audience within a custom audience built from product page URL visitors. The copy must do two things: reinforce why the product is the right choice and address the most common reason people hesitate after viewing a product. This prompt generates a Facebook ad with primary text, headline, and description structured for the feed placement.

Prompt RT-01: Facebook Retargeting Ad — Product Page Visitor
Act as a Facebook Ads specialist with deep experience in retargeting and audience segmentation. Write a Facebook retargeting ad for people who visited a specific product page but did not purchase.

Ad structure:
— Primary Text (125 characters recommended, 500 max): Open with social proof or a curiosity hook. Do not reference the visit directly. Reference the product naturally. Address the most common objection in lines 2–3. Close with a low-friction CTA.
— Headline (40 characters max): Benefit-focused or urgency-driven. Include the product name or key benefit.
— Description (30 characters max): Supporting detail — shipping, return policy, or secondary benefit.
— CTA Button: Recommend the best option (Shop Now, Learn More, Get Offer, See More).

Write 3 variations testing different angles:
1. Social proof angle: Lead with customer review count, star rating, or a paraphrased testimonial
2. Objection-removal angle: Address the most common hesitation head-on (price, shipping, returns)
3. Urgency angle: Limited stock, time-limited offer, or price-increase warning

Rules: Never say "we noticed you visited" or anything surveillance-like. Keep the primary text conversational — write like a friend telling someone about a product, not like a corporation. The ad must feel native to the Facebook feed.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Key Benefit: [PRIMARY VALUE PROPOSITION — e.g., "saves 3 hours a week," "lasts 10 years," "made in the USA"]
— Social Proof: [REVIEWS OR RATINGS — e.g., "4.8 stars from 1,200 customers," "featured in Forbes"]
— Most Common Objection: [REASON PEOPLE HESITATE — e.g., "price," "not sure it will fit," "shipping cost"]
— Objection Remover: [HOW YOU ADDRESS IT — e.g., "free returns," "30-day guarantee," "free shipping over $50"]
— Urgency Element: [SCARCITY OR DEADLINE — e.g., "only 12 left," "sale ends Sunday," "price increases next week"]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Shop Now," "Claim Offer," "Get Yours"]

Variation 1 — Social Proof:
Primary Text: Over 1,200 customers gave the Apex Running Pack 4.8 stars — and the most common thing they say is they wish they bought it sooner. Free shipping on every order. Free returns if it is not perfect.
Headline: The Pack 1,200 Runners Chose
Description: Free shipping & returns
CTA: Shop Now

Variation 2 — Objection Removal:
Primary Text: Unsure about the fit? Every Apex Running Pack ships free and returns free — no questions asked within 60 days. 4.8 stars from 1,200+ runners who were exactly where you are.
Headline: Try It Risk-Free for 60 Days
Description: Free returns. No questions.
CTA: Get Yours

Variation 3 — Urgency:
Primary Text: The Apex Running Pack is down to its last 12 units in most sizes. 4.8 stars, free shipping, 60-day returns. If you have been thinking about it — now is the moment.
Headline: Only 12 Left — Ships Free
Description: Low stock. Ships today.
CTA: Shop Now

The three-variation approach is essential for product page retargeting. Different people hesitated for different reasons — some balked at the price, some were not sure about sizing, some just got distracted. Running all three variations and letting Facebook's algorithm allocate budget to the best-performing angle is how you maximize recovery from this segment.

Prompt 2: Google Display Retargeting Ad

Google Display retargeting reaches past visitors across millions of websites, apps, and Gmail as they browse the web after leaving your site. Unlike Facebook's single-image feed format, Google Display ads must work across dozens of banner sizes — from a 300x250 rectangle to a 728x90 leaderboard. That means your copy must be modular: short headlines that work alone, long headlines that expand the message, and descriptions that pair with any headline. This prompt generates a complete Google Display retargeting asset set for responsive display ads.

Prompt RT-02: Google Display Retargeting Ad
Act as a Google Display advertising specialist experienced with retargeting and remarketing campaigns. Write a complete responsive display ad asset set for a retargeting campaign targeting people who visited the website but did not convert.

Asset requirements:
— Short Headlines (30 characters max): Provide 5. Must work standalone — one keyword-match, one benefit, one urgency, one offer, one trust signal.
— Long Headlines (90 characters max): Provide 5. Expanded value propositions that work as standalone statements for larger ad sizes.
— Descriptions (90 characters max): Provide 5. One addressing the objection, one with social proof, one with the offer, one with urgency, one with the CTA.

Display retargeting principles:
— The viewer already knows the brand. Do not introduce the product. Advance the conversation.
— Lead with what is new or different from what they saw on site: a new offer, stronger proof, reduced risk.
— Keep copy concise — banner ads are seen for 1–2 seconds. The headline must work without the description.
— Every asset must stand alone (Google mixes and matches them across banner sizes).

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Key Benefit: [PRIMARY VALUE PROPOSITION]
— Retargeting Offer: [INCENTIVE FOR RETURN VISITORS — e.g., "10% off," "free shipping," "extended trial"]
— Social Proof: [REVIEWS, RATINGS, OR CUSTOMER COUNT]
— Risk Reducer: [GUARANTEE OR RETURN POLICY]
— Urgency Element: [SCARCITY OR DEADLINE]

The standalone rule is more critical for Display than for any other format because Google's responsive display ad system automatically tests thousands of combinations across different banner sizes. A description that only makes sense paired with a specific headline will produce incoherent combinations in smaller banner sizes where only one or two assets appear at once. This prompt enforces standalone coherence for every asset.

Prompt 3: Cart Abandoner Retargeting Ad

Cart abandoners are your highest-converting retargeting segment. These people selected a specific product, added it to their cart, and stopped before completing checkout. The average global cart abandonment rate is 70.19% (Baymard Institute, 2025), which means for every 10 people who add a product to their cart, only 3 complete the purchase. The other 7 are recoverable — and a well-crafted retargeting ad served within 1–3 hours of abandonment is the fastest way to recover them. This prompt generates cart abandoner ad copy for Facebook and Instagram placements.

Prompt RT-03: Cart Abandoner Retargeting Ad
Act as a conversion rate optimization specialist and Facebook Ads expert. Write a retargeting ad targeting people who added a product to their cart but did not complete checkout. This is your highest-intent audience and the copy must reflect that urgency while reducing the friction that stopped them.

Ad structure:
— Primary Text: Open with a benefit reminder or social proof — not "you left something behind" (too obvious). Acknowledge the hesitation without calling it out. Offer the friction-reducer (free shipping, discount, guarantee) in line 2. Close with urgency and a direct CTA.
— Headline: Benefit + offer or urgency. Reference the product category, not the specific SKU.
— Description: Reinforce the friction-reducer or the guarantee.

Write 3 variations:
1. Discount angle: A specific percentage or dollar amount off to close the sale
2. Free shipping angle: Remove the shipping cost objection entirely
3. Scarcity angle: Limited stock or time-sensitive offer without a discount

Cart abandonment copy rules:
— Timing matters more than copy: serve this ad within 1–3 hours of abandonment for best results.
— The viewer is one decision away from buying. Remove one obstacle — do not add new information.
— Every word should reduce friction, not add complexity. Short sentences. Direct language.
— Do not guilt-trip. Do not pressure. Make coming back feel easy and rewarding.

Variables:
— Product Category: [PRODUCT TYPE — e.g., "running shoes," "project management software," "skincare kit"]
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Discount Offer: [DISCOUNT — e.g., "10% off," "$15 off your order," "free gift with purchase"]
— Shipping Offer: [SHIPPING DEAL — e.g., "free shipping, no minimum," "free 2-day shipping today only"]
— Scarcity Element: [URGENCY WITHOUT DISCOUNT — e.g., "selling fast," "limited stock," "offer expires midnight"]
— Guarantee: [RISK REDUCER — e.g., "30-day money-back guarantee," "free returns," "cancel anytime"]
— CTA: [BUTTON TEXT — e.g., "Complete Your Order," "Claim Your Discount," "Shop Now"]

Variation 1 — Discount:
Primary Text: Still thinking it over? Here is a nudge: 10% off your order, today only. 4.8 stars from 1,200+ customers who love theirs. 30-day returns, no questions asked. The code applies automatically at checkout.
Headline: 10% Off — Today Only
Description: Auto-applied at checkout.

Variation 2 — Free Shipping:
Primary Text: Your order ships free today — no minimum, no code needed. Over 1,200 five-star reviews. 30-day money-back guarantee if it is not exactly right.
Headline: Free Shipping — No Minimum
Description: Ships today. Returns free.

Variation 3 — Scarcity:
Primary Text: Popular sizes are selling out fast this week. 4.8 stars. Free shipping. Free returns. If yours is still in stock, now is the time.
Headline: Stock Running Low — Ships Free
Description: Check availability now.

The timing instruction in this prompt matters as much as the copy itself. Retargeting served within one hour of cart abandonment converts at roughly 3x the rate of ads served 24 hours later. Set up your custom audience to update in real time and your campaign to serve immediately — the prompts generate the copy, but the audience setup determines when it lands.

Prompt 4: Blog Reader Retargeting Ad

Blog readers are research-mode visitors. They arrived at your content looking for information — not necessarily to buy. Retargeting them with a direct-purchase ad ignores where they are in the funnel and almost never converts. Instead, the most effective strategy is to offer the next logical step: a piece of content that goes deeper, a free resource that is more actionable, or a low-commitment product demonstration. This prompt generates retargeting ads for blog audience segments that move readers from content consumption to product consideration.

Prompt RT-04: Blog Reader Retargeting Ad
Act as a content marketing specialist and paid advertising strategist. Write a retargeting ad for people who read a specific blog post or article on the website but did not take any commercial action. This audience is research-mode — they are building awareness of the problem your product solves but are not ready to buy yet. The ad must offer a logical next step that advances them toward purchase without demanding it.

Funnel progression strategy:
— If they read a problem-awareness post, offer a solution-focused resource (guide, checklist, template)
— If they read a solution-comparison post, offer a product demonstration or free trial
— If they read a how-to post, offer a tool that does the task for them

Ad structure:
— Primary Text: Reference the topic they read about (without mentioning the specific post). Position the offer as the natural next step. Keep it conversational — write like a recommendation, not an ad. Max 150 characters for the opening line.
— Headline: The offer, not the product. "Free [Resource Name]" or "Get [Specific Outcome]."
— Description: What they will learn or achieve by clicking.

Write 2 variations:
1. Free resource angle: Offer a downloadable guide, checklist, or template related to the blog topic
2. Product demo angle: Offer a free trial or live demonstration of the tool that solves the problem they read about

Variables:
— Blog Topic: [TOPIC OF THE POST THEY READ — e.g., "how to write better email subject lines," "B2B lead generation strategies," "reducing customer churn"]
— Brand/Product Name: [BRAND AND PRODUCT]
— Free Resource Offer: [LEAD MAGNET — e.g., "50 email subject line templates," "lead generation playbook," "churn prevention checklist"]
— Demo/Trial Offer: [PRODUCT ENTRY POINT — e.g., "14-day free trial," "live 20-minute demo," "free account, no credit card"]
— Outcome Promise: [WHAT THEY WILL ACHIEVE — e.g., "higher open rates," "more qualified leads," "reduce churn by 30%"]

Blog reader retargeting succeeds when the ad feels like a recommendation from someone who knows what you were just reading, not like an interruption from a brand that tracked you. The key is making the offer feel like a natural continuation of the article — "if you found that useful, this goes even deeper" — rather than a pivot to a sales message. This prompt enforces that conversational continuity.

Prompt 5: Free Trial Expiring Retargeting Ad

Free trial users who have not converted are a unique retargeting segment: they have already used your product, experienced its value (or failed to), and are making a final decision. The retargeting ad that reaches them at the moment their trial expires is the highest-leverage touchpoint in a SaaS or subscription funnel. Most companies send only email at this stage and miss users who are not opening their inbox. This prompt generates a retargeting ad for trial-expiring users that addresses the specific barriers to paid conversion: price uncertainty, feature clarity, and the fear of losing progress.

Prompt RT-05: Free Trial Expiring Retargeting Ad
Act as a SaaS marketing specialist focused on trial-to-paid conversion optimization. Write a retargeting ad for users whose free trial is expiring within 48–72 hours who have not yet upgraded to a paid plan. This is a warm audience with direct product experience. The copy must address the specific conversion barriers for trial users: price uncertainty, feature uncertainty, fear of losing their data or progress, and inertia.

Ad structure:
— Primary Text: Open with what they stand to lose (work, data, access) or gain (continued results). Address the most common objection to upgrading. Close with the lowest-friction next step.
— Headline: Outcome + urgency. "Keep [Result] Going" or "Your Trial Ends in [X] Days."
— Description: Price anchor or risk reducer (money-back guarantee, cancel anytime).

Write 3 variations:
1. Loss aversion angle: Focus on what they lose when the trial ends (access, data, progress, settings)
2. Value anchor angle: Frame the monthly cost against the value they have already received in the trial
3. Momentum angle: "You have already done the hard part" — they set up the account, uploaded data, learned the tool. Upgrading is the easy step.

Trial expiration copy rules:
— Do not make them feel like failures for not converting. Make upgrading feel like continuation, not a new commitment.
— Use specific numbers: days remaining, the cost per month, the ROI they have already seen.
— The CTA should go directly to the upgrade or pricing page, not the homepage.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Trial Length: [TRIAL DURATION — e.g., "14-day," "30-day"]
— Days Remaining: [HOW MANY DAYS LEFT — e.g., "3 days," "48 hours"]
— Starting Price: [ENTRY-LEVEL PLAN PRICE — e.g., "$29/month," "$9/seat/month"]
— Key Value Already Delivered: [WHAT TRIAL USERS TYPICALLY ACCOMPLISH — e.g., "connected their team," "launched first campaign," "saved 5 hours"]
— What They Lose at Expiry: [DATA/ACCESS AT RISK — e.g., "all project data," "automated workflows," "team connections"]
— Risk Reducer: [GUARANTEE — e.g., "30-day money-back guarantee," "cancel anytime, no penalties"]

Loss aversion is the most powerful psychological trigger for trial-expiring retargeting. The fear of losing something already built — the project data, the team setup, the automated workflows — consistently outperforms the promise of future gains in trial conversion messaging. This prompt generates three angles, but the loss aversion variation typically delivers the highest conversion rate when tested head-to-head.

Prompt 6: Past Customer Win-Back Ad

A customer who bought from you once is your best prospect for a second purchase — they trust your brand, they know your product quality, and they do not need to be convinced that you are legitimate. But win-back is not automatic. Customers who bought 6–18 months ago and have not returned need a reason to re-engage: a new product launch, a loyalty reward, a personalized recommendation based on their previous purchase, or a compelling limited-time offer. This prompt generates a past customer win-back ad that leverages the existing relationship instead of treating them like a cold prospect.

Prompt RT-06: Past Customer Win-Back Ad
Act as a customer retention specialist and Facebook Ads strategist. Write a win-back retargeting ad targeting past customers who have not purchased in the last 90–180 days. This audience already trusts the brand — do not re-introduce it. The ad must give them a reason to come back that is relevant to what they bought before and feels like a personalized recommendation, not a mass ad.

Ad structure:
— Primary Text: Open with something that acknowledges their customer status (without being creepy). Reference their purchase category, not the specific SKU. Introduce what is new, what is improved, or what they have been missing. Include a loyalty offer or exclusive deal. Close with a warm, direct CTA.
— Headline: The new thing or the offer. "New [Product Line] Is Here" or "Exclusive Offer for Returning Customers."
— Description: Loyalty acknowledgment or offer details.

Write 3 variations:
1. New product angle: A relevant new arrival or updated version of what they bought
2. Loyalty discount angle: A reward for being a past customer (exclusive discount they cannot get elsewhere)
3. "What you have been missing" angle: Show what the product has helped other customers achieve since their last purchase

Win-back copy rules:
— The tone must feel warm and personal, not corporate. These people chose you before. Honor that.
— Never imply they made a mistake by not returning. Create desire, not guilt.
— The offer should be exclusive to past customers — a generic sitewide sale does not have the same pull as "for past customers only."
— Connect the new offer to what they already bought (complementary products, upgrades, refills).

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Previous Purchase Category: [WHAT THEY BOUGHT — e.g., "skincare," "project management software," "running gear"]
— New or Complementary Product: [WHAT TO SHOW THEM NOW — e.g., "new serum launch," "advanced features plan," "winter running collection"]
— Loyalty Offer: [EXCLUSIVE INCENTIVE — e.g., "20% off for past customers," "early access before public launch," "free gift with next order"]
— Social Proof Since Their Last Purchase: [NEW PROOF — e.g., "now 4.9 stars from 5,000 reviews," "just named #1 by Wirecutter," "500,000 customers worldwide"]

The "exclusive for past customers" framing is the highest-converting element in win-back advertising. When someone sees an offer framed as recognition of their loyalty, it activates reciprocity — they feel the brand has done something for them, and they are more inclined to reciprocate with a purchase. A generic "20% off" promotion feels like a mass discount. The same offer framed as "reserved for customers like you" converts at a meaningfully higher rate.

Prompt 7: Video Viewer Retargeting Ad

Video viewers are a retargeting segment most advertisers overlook, but they represent a high-quality mid-funnel audience. Someone who watched 50% or more of a product video, brand story, or testimonial video has demonstrated significant intent — they chose to watch, they stayed engaged, and they processed your message in a way that static ads cannot match. The retargeting ad that reaches them after the video should advance the conversation: extend the message from the video, offer a related resource, or present a direct conversion opportunity. This prompt generates retargeting ads for video viewer audiences built from Facebook or YouTube watch-time data.

Prompt RT-07: Video Viewer Retargeting Ad
Act as a video marketing specialist and paid advertising strategist. Write a retargeting ad targeting people who watched 50%+ of a specific video (product demo, testimonial, brand story, or educational content) but did not take a commercial action afterward. This audience has high intent — they voluntarily watched significant video content about your brand or product. The retargeting ad must build on that engagement rather than repeating the same message.

Ad structure:
— Primary Text: Reference the topic of the video without naming it. Open with the key message the viewer took away. Extend that message with the next logical piece of information — the proof, the offer, or the direct CTA they did not see in the video. Keep the primary text under 150 characters in the opening line.
— Headline: The next step in the journey. "See It in Action" or "Read the Case Study" or "Start Free Today."
— Description: The specific outcome or conversion incentive.

Write 2 variations based on video type:
1. For product demo viewers: The ad should offer a free trial or personalized demo — they have seen the features, now give them the experience
2. For testimonial/social proof video viewers: The ad should offer more proof — additional case studies, review count, or a specific ROI statistic — and then the direct offer

Video retargeting copy rules:
— Do not repeat what the video said. The viewer has already processed it. Move the conversation forward.
— The tone can be slightly warmer than cold-traffic ads — this person already spent time with your brand.
— Short, direct, specific. Video viewers have demonstrated attention span; do not waste it with vague copy.

Variables:
— Video Topic: [WHAT THE VIDEO COVERS — e.g., "product features demo," "customer success story," "founder brand story"]
— Product/Brand: [PRODUCT OR BRAND NAME]
— Key Message from Video: [THE MAIN TAKEAWAY — e.g., "the tool saves 5 hours a week," "the customer went from X to Y," "the product is made differently"]
— Next Step Offer: [CONVERSION OFFER — e.g., "14-day free trial," "personalized demo," "free case study download"]
— Additional Social Proof: [PROOF NOT IN THE VIDEO — e.g., "10,000 customers," "4.9 stars from 800 reviews," "featured in TechCrunch"]

Segmenting video viewers by watch percentage dramatically improves retargeting performance. Someone who watched 25% of a video has different intent than someone who watched 95% — treat them as separate audiences with separate ads. For 25–50% viewers, use a softer next-step offer like a related article or free resource. For 75%+ viewers, go directly to the conversion offer — they are ready.

Prompt 8: Lead Magnet Downloader Retargeting Ad

A lead magnet downloader is one of the most valuable retargeting audiences in content-driven businesses. This person voluntarily gave you their email, downloaded your resource, and consumed your content — all strong signals of genuine interest in the problem your product solves. But downloading a free guide and buying a product are very different commitments. The retargeting ad that bridges that gap must connect the insight from the lead magnet to the product that delivers the outcome at scale. This prompt generates retargeting ads for lead magnet audiences that move from education to conversion.

Prompt RT-08: Lead Magnet Downloader Retargeting Ad
Act as a content marketing and lead nurturing specialist. Write a retargeting ad targeting people who downloaded a specific lead magnet (guide, checklist, template, or ebook) but have not yet purchased the product or signed up for the service. This audience is interested in solving the problem — they just have not committed to your solution yet. The ad must bridge the gap between "I am interested in this problem" and "I am ready to use this product."

Bridge framework:
Step 1 (in primary text): Acknowledge the insight from the lead magnet without naming it directly
Step 2: Introduce the gap — why doing it manually or with the guide alone is slower or harder than it needs to be
Step 3: Position the product as the tool that does the heavy lifting
Step 4: Offer a low-friction entry point (free trial, demo, or starter plan)

Ad structure:
— Primary Text (opening line under 125 characters): Start with the insight or the problem the lead magnet addressed. Build the bridge to the product in 2–3 sentences. Close with a direct CTA and the offer.
— Headline: The product outcome or the offer. Not the feature.
— Description: The social proof or the risk reducer.

Write 2 variations:
1. "Do it faster" angle: Position the product as the tool that automates or accelerates what the lead magnet taught them to do manually
2. "See it working for others" angle: Lead with customer results (social proof) and offer the trial as a way to get the same outcome

Variables:
— Lead Magnet Topic: [WHAT THE GUIDE/CHECKLIST COVERED — e.g., "how to write cold email sequences," "SEO audit checklist," "social media content calendar"]
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— What the Product Does: [HOW IT DELIVERS THE LEAD MAGNET OUTCOME — e.g., "generates and sends cold emails automatically," "runs the full SEO audit in 60 seconds," "plans and schedules your entire month of content"]
— Customer Result: [SOCIAL PROOF — e.g., "teams using this book 40% more meetings," "pages audited see 60% more organic traffic in 90 days," "users save 6 hours a week on content planning"]
— Entry Point: [FREE TRIAL OR DEMO OFFER]

The bridge framework in this prompt is the key structural element that separates effective lead-magnet retargeting from generic product ads. You are not selling the product — you are selling the same outcome the lead magnet promised, now delivered faster and more reliably by the product. That framing resonates with lead magnet downloaders because it continues the conversation they already started with your brand.

Prompt 9: Dynamic Product Retargeting Ad

Dynamic product retargeting automatically shows each person the exact product they viewed on your site — no manual creative required. Facebook's Dynamic Ads and Google's Dynamic Remarketing both pull from your product catalog to serve personalized ads at scale. But the surrounding copy — the primary text, headline, and description that appear alongside the dynamic product image — still needs to be written once and applied across every product in your catalog. That copy must be compelling enough to work for any product a visitor viewed, from a $20 accessory to a $2,000 item. This prompt generates catalog-level retargeting copy that works universally across your product range.

Prompt RT-09: Dynamic Product Retargeting Ad
Act as a Facebook Dynamic Ads specialist and e-commerce advertising strategist. Write the static copy elements for a dynamic product retargeting campaign where the product image, name, and price are pulled automatically from the catalog — but the primary text, headline template, and description must be written once and work universally across all products in the range.

Static copy elements to write:
— Primary Text (4 variations): Must work with any product in the catalog, from low-ticket to high-ticket. Cannot reference a specific product. Must acknowledge the viewer's interest, add value (social proof, guarantee, offer), and drive the click.
— Headline Template: A formula that works with the dynamic product name inserted. Example: "[Product Name] — Ships Free Today" or "Still Thinking About [Product Name]?"
— Description (3 variations): Short supporting copy that works alongside any product — guarantee, shipping offer, or review count.

Universal copy rules:
— Never reference a specific product. The dynamic tag inserts the correct product name automatically.
— The primary text must be compelling for both impulse buys and considered purchases simultaneously.
— Focus on universal trust signals: brand reputation, shipping policy, return policy, review count.
— Avoid price references in static copy — the catalog populates price dynamically.

Variables:

Prompt 10: Sequential Retargeting Campaign (Multi-Touchpoint)

Sequential retargeting serves different ad creatives to the same person across multiple exposures — changing the message at each touchpoint based on how many times they have seen your ads. Touchpoint one introduces the value proposition. Touchpoint two adds social proof and addresses objections. Touchpoint three delivers urgency and the final offer. This approach mirrors how a skilled salesperson builds a case across multiple conversations, and it consistently outperforms single-message retargeting by reducing ad fatigue while advancing the conversion conversation with each exposure. This prompt generates a complete three-touchpoint sequential retargeting script.

5 Tips for Getting More from Your Retargeting Campaigns

These 10 prompts generate the copy. These five principles ensure the campaigns built around that copy perform at their highest potential.

1. Segment Audiences Before Writing a Single Word

Retargeting copy only works when it is matched to the right audience. Before you open a prompt, define your segments: cart abandoners, product page visitors, pricing page visitors, blog readers, and past customers each need separate audiences, separate ad sets, and separate copy. Running all retargeting visitors into a single "all website visitors" audience and serving them one creative is the most common retargeting mistake, and it is why most advertisers see poor retargeting ROAS. Build the audiences first, then use the prompts above to generate copy for each segment individually.

2. Match Message Intensity to Intent Level

High-intent audiences (cart abandoners, trial expirers) can handle direct, urgent, conversion-focused copy. Low-intent audiences (blog readers, homepage visitors) need a softer approach — an offer for a next step, not a demand to buy now. Serving urgent, discount-heavy copy to a blog reader who has never seen your product will generate negative reactions and waste budget. Serving soft, educational copy to a cart abandoner who is ready to buy leaves money on the table. Calibrate the intensity of your message to the intensity of the audience's intent.

3. Set Frequency Caps and Exclusions

Without frequency caps, retargeting becomes stalking. Set a cap of 3–5 impressions per person per week on Facebook and 2–3 impressions per week on Google Display. Equally important: exclude converters from your retargeting campaigns in real time. Someone who just purchased should immediately be removed from the purchase retargeting audience and optionally added to a post-purchase upsell audience with different creative. Nothing erodes brand trust faster than serving a "buy now" ad to someone who bought yesterday.

4. Test Creative Format, Not Just Copy

The copy prompts above work across static images, carousel ads, and video ads. For Facebook retargeting specifically, test your winning copy across multiple creative formats: a static single-image ad, a 3-card carousel showing product details or benefits, and a short (15–30 second) video retargeting ad. Carousel ads typically outperform single-image in retargeting because they allow you to show multiple products, multiple angles, or a before/after narrative. Use the prompt copy across all three formats and let the data tell you which format resonates with each segment.

5. Refresh Creative Every 3–4 Weeks

Retargeting creative fatigues faster than cold-traffic creative because the audience is smaller and the frequency is higher. The same people see your ad repeatedly, and once they have seen it five or six times, they stop registering it — and your CPMs rise while CTR falls. Regenerate new creative every 3–4 weeks using the prompts above. Change the visual (new product image, lifestyle photo, or video clip), swap the copy angle, or rotate the offer. Consistent creative refresh is the single most underused lever in retargeting optimization.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Retargeting Ads

What is the best audience to start retargeting first? +
Cart abandoners are the highest-value retargeting audience because they demonstrated the strongest purchase intent — they added a product to their cart and did not complete checkout. Average cart abandonment rates across e-commerce hover around 70%, which means 7 out of 10 people who were ready to buy left without completing the purchase. A retargeting ad served to cart abandoners within 1–3 hours of abandonment, with a specific reference to the product they almost bought and a low-friction offer (free shipping, 10% discount), consistently delivers the highest return on retargeting spend. Start with cart abandoners, then layer in product page visitors, then blog readers.
How long should a retargeting window be? +
Retargeting window length depends on your product's purchase cycle. For impulse purchases and low-ticket items ($20–$100), a 7–14 day window is sufficient — if someone has not returned to buy within two weeks, they have likely moved on. For mid-ticket products ($100–$500), use a 30-day window. For high-consideration purchases like software subscriptions, B2B products, or items over $500, a 60–90 day window captures buyers who take longer to evaluate options. The prompts in this article include window-length guidance for each audience segment. The key rule: cap your retargeting window before your ads become repetitive enough to annoy rather than convert.
How many times should someone see a retargeting ad before I stop? +
Set a frequency cap of 3–7 impressions per person per week on Facebook and Google Display. Beyond that threshold, ad fatigue sets in and CPMs rise while CTR drops — you are paying more to annoy people instead of convert them. The sequential retargeting prompt in this article structures a 3-touchpoint campaign that changes the creative angle at each exposure: social proof in touchpoint one, urgency in touchpoint two, and a value-focused offer in touchpoint three. After 3 impressions with no conversion, suppress the user from that campaign and either move them to a longer-window campaign with a different offer or let them exit the retargeting funnel entirely.
Should retargeting ads mention that someone visited my site? +
No. Ads that explicitly reference browsing behavior ("We saw you looking at our shoes!") feel surveillance-like and generate negative sentiment even among people who would otherwise convert. The better approach is to create ads that are highly relevant to the product or page the visitor viewed without acknowledging the visit directly. For product page visitors, show the specific product with social proof and an offer. For cart abandoners, lead with the product name and a reason to complete the purchase. For blog readers, offer the next logical step in their journey. The connection is obvious to the viewer without feeling intrusive — and the conversion rates are significantly better.
Do AI prompts for retargeting ads work on both Facebook and Google? +
Yes. The prompts in this article are written with platform-specific guidance built in — Facebook retargeting ads include primary text, headline, and description fields with character counts appropriate for Meta's ad formats, while Google Display retargeting ads follow Google's asset specifications. However, the core psychology and segmentation strategy — matching ad message to audience intent level — works identically across both platforms. You can also adapt the Facebook prompts for Instagram, the Google prompts for YouTube retargeting, and the email retargeting prompt for SMS win-back sequences. The copy framework is universal; only the format constraints change.
