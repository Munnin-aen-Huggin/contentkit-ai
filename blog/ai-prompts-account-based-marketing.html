8 AI Prompts for Account-Based Marketing Campaigns

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for account based marketing are structured instructions that generate hyper-personalized campaign assets for specific target accounts — from research briefs and outreach emails to custom landing pages, LinkedIn ads, direct mail, and multi-channel playbooks — in a fraction of the time it takes to build them manually. This guide delivers 8 copy-paste prompts covering every core ABM asset, each built around real account intelligence so the output feels written specifically for the company you are targeting.

Account-based marketing is the highest-ROI demand generation strategy for B2B companies with average contract values above $25,000. Instead of generating thousands of leads and filtering for quality, ABM flips the funnel: you identify the 50, 100, or 200 accounts that represent your biggest revenue opportunity, and then you run coordinated, personalized campaigns specifically at those companies until the right stakeholders raise their hands.

The economics are compelling. Forrester reports that ABM programs generate 200% more revenue than standard demand generation for the same marketing investment. ITSMA research finds that 87% of B2B marketers report ABM delivers higher ROI than other marketing activities. But those results depend entirely on one thing: personalization at scale. The accounts that respond to ABM campaigns do so because the marketing feels like it was built for them — because it addresses their specific industry challenges, references their company's situation, and speaks to the priorities their leadership team cares about most.

That is where most ABM programs stall. Building genuinely personalized assets for 100 target accounts requires an enormous amount of research and writing time. Marketers either over-personalize a handful of top-tier accounts and under-serve the rest, or they produce watered-down "personalization" that amounts to swapping a company name into a generic template. Neither approach delivers the pipeline results that make ABM worth the investment.

Structured ai prompts for account based marketing solve this problem by separating the research from the writing. You gather the account intelligence — the company's industry, recent trigger events, known pain points, technology stack, and the stakeholders in the buying committee — and the prompt tells the AI exactly how to translate that intelligence into a personalized campaign asset. The result is copy that reflects real account knowledge without requiring a copywriter to build each piece from scratch.

The 8 prompts below cover every major ABM asset: a target account research brief to structure your intelligence before any outreach, a personalized email for the initial account-specific contact, a custom landing page for when accounts click through, an account-specific case study pitch to advance late-stage conversations, a LinkedIn ad configured for company-list targeting, a direct mail package for high-value accounts, a custom content hub to serve as an account's dedicated resource center, and a full multi-channel campaign playbook. Each prompt includes the specific variables, formatting guidance, and strategic framework needed to produce output that is genuinely ready to use.

Why ABM Demands a Different Prompting Approach

Most AI marketing prompts are designed for scale: generate 10 subject line variations, write 15 ad headlines, produce 5 landing page versions for A/B testing. That logic works for demand generation, where you are optimizing for the best-performing message across a large anonymous audience.

ABM inverts every assumption. You are not optimizing for the average; you are personalizing for the specific. A target account at a Series B SaaS company in the DevOps space with 200 engineers and a recent CTO hire has a completely different set of priorities, pain points, and buying triggers than a target account at a 5,000-person financial services firm dealing with compliance pressures and legacy infrastructure. The same email template fails both.

Here is what makes AI prompts for ABM structurally different from general marketing prompts.

Account Intelligence Is the Input, Not an Afterthought

Every prompt below requires you to input real account intelligence before the AI generates anything. Company name, industry, company size, known pain points, recent trigger events, technology stack, and the specific stakeholder you are addressing. When you treat account intelligence as a structured input rather than a variable to swap in later, the AI output reflects that intelligence throughout the entire asset — not just in the salutation or a single sentence of personalization.

Buying Committee Complexity Requires Role-Specific Messaging

Enterprise ABM deals typically involve 6–10 stakeholders in the buying committee. The CFO cares about ROI and total cost of ownership. The CTO cares about integration complexity and security. The VP of Operations cares about implementation timeline and team disruption. The end users care about workflow impact and ease of adoption. Generic copy that tries to speak to all of them speaks to none of them. Several prompts below include explicit role-specific variables so you can generate assets that address the specific concerns of the stakeholder you are reaching at each stage of the campaign.

Trigger Events Create Timing Advantage

ABM campaigns perform significantly better when they are timed around account-level trigger events: a new funding round that signals budget availability, a leadership change that signals new priorities, a product launch that signals a competitive moment, a regulatory change that creates a compliance need, or a job posting that reveals technology investment direction. The prompts below include trigger event variables that allow the AI to weave the timing rationale into the outreach naturally — so your message arrives at exactly the right moment, with a reason to act now.

The bottom line: ai prompts for account based marketing do not replace your account research. They multiply it — turning an hour of good account intelligence into a full suite of personalized campaign assets that would otherwise require a full day of writing. Here are the 8 prompts.

8 AI Prompts for Account-Based Marketing

Prompt 1: Target Account Research Brief

Every ABM campaign starts with intelligence, but intelligence only becomes useful when it is organized into a format that drives campaign decisions. A well-structured account research brief synthesizes firmographic data, technographic signals, stakeholder mapping, pain point hypotheses, and trigger events into a single document that every campaign asset — the email, the landing page, the ad, the direct mail — can draw from. This prompt generates a one-page account intelligence brief formatted specifically for ABM campaign planning, so your entire team works from the same foundation.

Prompt ABM-01: Target Account Research Brief
Act as a senior B2B account-based marketing strategist with experience running ABM programs at enterprise SaaS and professional services companies. Create a structured target account research brief for the company below. This brief will be used to inform all downstream ABM campaign assets: outreach emails, landing pages, LinkedIn ads, direct mail, and the multi-channel campaign playbook.

Brief structure:
— Account Overview (3–4 sentences): Company snapshot — what they do, their market position, company size, funding stage, and primary business model
— Firmographic Profile: Industry, sub-vertical, headcount range, revenue estimate, headquarters, key geographies, and growth trajectory
— Technology Stack Signals: Known or inferred tools in the relevant category (CRM, data stack, marketing tech, security, infrastructure) and what those signals suggest about buying readiness
— Buying Committee Map: List the 4–6 most likely stakeholders in the buying committee for our solution. For each: title, primary business priority, likely objection, and preferred content format
— Pain Point Hypotheses: 3 specific business pain points this account likely faces based on their industry, size, and current growth stage — ranked by urgency
— Trigger Events: List any recent events (last 6–12 months) that create buying urgency or signal timing alignment: funding, leadership changes, product launches, M&A, regulatory changes, job postings
— Competitive Context: Likely incumbent vendors or status quo this account is defending, and the switching cost/barrier we need to address
— Recommended ABM Tier: One-to-one (full customization), one-to-few (cluster personalization), or one-to-many (programmatic), with rationale
— Primary Hook: A single sentence that summarizes the most compelling reason this account should talk to us right now

Variables:
— Target Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Our Product/Service: [WHAT WE SELL — e.g., "AI-powered data governance platform," "B2B revenue operations consulting," "enterprise HR software"]
— Our ICP Definition: [IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE — e.g., "Series B-D SaaS companies, 100–500 employees, data-driven GTM teams, using Salesforce + HubSpot"]
— Known Account Intelligence: [PASTE ANY RESEARCH YOU HAVE: recent news, LinkedIn posts, job postings, tech stack tools, executive quotes]
— Primary Stakeholder to Target First: [ROLE — e.g., "VP of Revenue Operations," "Chief Data Officer," "Head of Marketing"]
— Our Key Differentiator: [UNIQUE ADVANTAGE — e.g., "no-code implementation," "SOC 2 certified," "guaranteed 90-day ROI"]

Account Overview: Meridian Analytics is a Series C data infrastructure company (raised $42M, March 2025) serving mid-market e-commerce brands that have outgrown standard BI tools. Their 280-person team is split between product and GTM, with aggressive hiring signals in data engineering and enterprise sales. They compete in the analytics-as-a-service space against Looker and Metabase but position on speed-to-insight rather than feature depth.

Primary Hook: Meridian just raised $42M and posted 6 senior data engineering roles — they are scaling a data stack under time pressure and have the budget to solve the governance problem that typically emerges at this stage. Our 90-day implementation guarantee makes us the lowest-risk decision for a team that cannot afford a 9-month data project.

The research brief pays dividends across every subsequent asset. When your email writer, your designer building the custom landing page, your sales development rep doing the LinkedIn outreach, and your direct mail vendor all work from the same brief, the campaign has coherence. The account experiences a coordinated message, not a series of disconnected touches that feel like they came from three different companies.

Prompt 2: Personalized Outreach Email (Account-Specific)

The first outreach email in an ABM sequence is the most consequential. It either earns a response — or it earns a delete. The difference is almost always specificity. When a VP of Revenue Operations opens an email that references their company's recent funding round, correctly identifies a challenge that comes with rapid headcount scaling, and connects that challenge to a business outcome the executive cares about, the email gets read. When the same executive receives a templated email with their company name inserted in the first line, it gets deleted. This prompt generates a genuinely personalized first-contact email that is built around real account intelligence, not surface-level name-swapping.

Prompt ABM-02: Personalized Outreach Email (Account-Specific)
Act as a senior ABM strategist and B2B copywriter specializing in enterprise outreach for complex sales cycles. Write a personalized first-contact email for a target account in an ABM campaign. This email must feel like it was written specifically for this company and this person — not a template with a name swapped in.

Email structure:
— Subject line: Reference the trigger event or a specific insight about the company. Do NOT use generic subject lines like "Quick question" or "Thought this might help." 8–12 words max.
— Opening line (1 sentence): Lead with something specific to this company or this person — a trigger event, a recent announcement, a quote they made, or a challenge specific to their stage of growth. Do not start with "I" or compliment the recipient.
— Problem bridge (2–3 sentences): Connect the account-specific trigger to a business pain that their peers at similar companies also face. Make them feel understood, not sold to.
— Proof point (1–2 sentences): Reference a specific customer at a similar company that solved this problem with our help. Use a concrete outcome: time saved, revenue gained, cost reduced, risk eliminated.
— Soft CTA (1 sentence): Ask for a specific, low-commitment next step. Not "schedule a demo" — something smaller: a 15-minute call, a relevant resource, or a specific question.
— P.S. line: Add a second personalized hook that references something specific to the recipient (a recent LinkedIn post, a conference they spoke at, a job posting that signals their priorities).

Rules: Under 150 words total (body, excluding subject and P.S.). No jargon. No feature lists. No pressure. The goal of this email is to earn a reply, not close a deal.

Variables:
— Target Company: [COMPANY NAME]
— Recipient Name and Title: [NAME, TITLE — e.g., "Sarah Chen, VP of Revenue Operations"]
— Trigger Event: [RECENT ACCOUNT EVENT — e.g., "raised $42M Series C in March," "announced expansion into EMEA," "hired a new CTO from Stripe"]
— Pain Point: [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE THIS ACCOUNT FACES — e.g., "data governance gaps when scaling past 100 engineers," "CRM data quality degrading with new sales team"]
— Proof Customer: [SIMILAR COMPANY + OUTCOME — e.g., "a Series C fintech in Austin cut reporting time by 60% in 90 days"]
— Our Solution: [WHAT WE DO IN ONE PHRASE — e.g., "automated data governance layer that sits above your existing stack"]
— Sender Name and Title: [YOUR NAME, TITLE]
— P.S. Hook: [SPECIFIC DETAIL ABOUT RECIPIENT — e.g., "saw your LinkedIn post on scaling RevOps teams," "noticed you're hiring 3 data engineers this quarter"]

Subject: What Meridian's $42M round usually means for data governance

Hi Sarah,

Series C rounds that fund aggressive engineering hiring almost always surface the same data governance problem six months later — too many pipelines, not enough visibility into which ones are trustworthy.

We helped a Series C fintech in Austin fix that before it became a board-level issue — they cut reporting errors by 72% and gave their data team 15 hours back per week, within 90 days of implementation.

Worth a 15-minute call to see if the timing makes sense for Meridian?

— [Name]

P.S. Noticed you're posting 6 senior data engineering roles this quarter — that's usually the moment this problem gets expensive to ignore.

The 150-word constraint is not arbitrary — it is strategic. Executive buyers read email on mobile, often between meetings, and the fastest way to lose them is a wall of text. Every sentence in an ABM outreach email must earn its place. When the AI operates within that constraint and fills it with account-specific intelligence rather than generic claims, the result consistently outperforms longer, more detailed emails.

Prompt 3: Custom Landing Page Copy (for Target Account)

When a target account executive clicks a link in your outreach email or LinkedIn message, where they land determines whether the campaign advances or dies. Sending an ABM prospect to your generic product homepage is a waste of the personalization you built in the outreach. A custom landing page that mirrors the account's industry, references the pain point you opened with, and presents a relevant case study and a low-friction CTA converts ABM traffic at 3–5x the rate of standard product pages. This prompt generates a full custom landing page copy deck for a target account, organized by section so it is ready to hand to a designer or plug into a tool like Unbounce, Mutiny, or HubSpot landing pages.

Prompt ABM-03: Custom Landing Page Copy (for Target Account)
Act as a B2B conversion copywriter specializing in account-based marketing landing pages. Write a complete landing page copy deck for a custom ABM landing page built for a specific target account. This page will be accessed only by contacts at this company (via personalized URL or IP targeting) and should feel like it was built specifically for them.

Page sections (write each in full):
— Hero Headline (8–12 words): Addresses this account's specific challenge or growth stage. Do NOT use our product name as the headline. Lead with their outcome.
— Hero Subheadline (1–2 sentences): Acknowledge the account's specific situation and promise a relevant outcome. Reference their industry or company stage.
— Hero CTA Button: A low-friction action appropriate for a first visit (e.g., "See How [Industry] Teams Do It," "Watch a 3-Minute Walkthrough," "Get the [Industry] Playbook").
— Problem Section (2–3 short paragraphs): Name the 3 specific pain points this account type faces at their growth stage. Use their language, not our product language. This section should make the reader feel understood.
— Solution Section (2 paragraphs + 3 bullet points): Introduce our solution in the context of their specific problem. Three bullets should be outcomes, not features: what gets better for them, not what our product does.
— Social Proof Section: One customer story (3–4 sentences) from a company in the same industry or at the same growth stage. Include a specific metric. Add a pull-quote from the customer.
— CTA Section: Headline, supporting text (1–2 sentences), and primary CTA button. Secondary option: a relevant content asset for those not ready to talk.

Variables:
— Target Company: [COMPANY NAME]
— Account Industry / Vertical: [INDUSTRY — e.g., "mid-market SaaS," "enterprise financial services," "B2B logistics"]
— Account Growth Stage: [STAGE — e.g., "Series C, scaling GTM," "post-acquisition integration," "pre-IPO compliance build-out"]
— Primary Pain Point: [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE — e.g., "data governance gaps," "CRM data quality degrading," "manual reporting consuming analyst hours"]
— Our Solution: [PRODUCT NAME AND ONE-LINE DESCRIPTION]
— Proof Customer (Same Industry): [COMPANY TYPE + OUTCOME + QUOTE]
— Primary CTA: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Book a 20-minute discovery call," "Watch the 3-minute demo," "Get the industry report"]
— Secondary CTA (Content Asset): [RESOURCE TITLE — e.g., "The Series C Data Governance Playbook," "2026 RevOps Benchmark Report"]

The custom landing page is the highest-leverage ABM asset most teams under-invest in. The outreach email gets the click; the landing page earns the meeting. When the page mirrors the language of the email, addresses the pain points the email raised, and presents proof from a company the prospect recognizes, the conversion rate on that traffic is dramatically higher than any generic product page. Tools like Mutiny and RollWorks make it possible to serve these pages dynamically based on the visitor's IP address, so you can create one version and serve it to every contact at the account without needing individual personalized URLs.

Prompt 4: Account-Specific Case Study Pitch

Case studies are the most persuasive content asset in the late-stage ABM toolkit, but only when they are relevant. A case study about a 5,000-person pharmaceutical company does nothing for a 150-person SaaS startup, even if the business outcome is compelling. The problem is that most companies have a small library of case studies and little guidance on how to position them for accounts where the fit is imperfect. This prompt generates a tailored case study pitch that frames an existing case study in the context of the target account's specific situation — bridging the gap between your proof and their relevance concern without requiring you to write a brand-new case study for every account.

Prompt ABM-04: Account-Specific Case Study Pitch
Act as a B2B enterprise sales strategist with expertise in account-based marketing. Write an account-specific case study pitch that introduces an existing customer success story in a way that is directly relevant to the target account's situation. The goal is not to present the case study as a generic proof point — it is to make the prospect feel that the story was selected specifically because it mirrors their world.

Pitch structure:
— Relevance Bridge (2–3 sentences): Open by naming the specific parallel between the case study company and the target account. What do they have in common that makes this story worth reading? Growth stage, industry challenge, team structure, technology environment, or a specific trigger event they both faced.
— The Before State (2 sentences): Describe what the case study company was dealing with before working with us. Use the same language the target account would use to describe their own problem — not our product language.
— The Solution (2 sentences): Briefly describe what we did for the case study company. Focus on the approach and the implementation speed, not on features. Avoid technical jargon.
— The Outcome (3 specific metrics): Three measurable results the case study company achieved. Format as bullet points with numbers: time, money, percentage improvement, or risk reduction.
— The So What (1–2 sentences): Connect the case study outcome to what it could mean for the target account specifically — their business situation, their growth goals, or their current constraint.
— CTA (1 sentence): Offer to share the full case study or set up a 15-minute call to walk through the story and discuss how the approach applies to their situation.

Variables:
— Target Company: [TARGET ACCOUNT NAME]
— Case Study Company (can be anonymized): [COMPANY NAME OR "a Series B fintech in Austin"]
— Shared Characteristic: [THE PARALLEL BETWEEN THEM — e.g., "both are scaling data teams post-Series C," "both operate in regulated industries," "both run Salesforce + Snowflake"]
— Before State Pain: [WHAT THE CASE STUDY COMPANY WAS STRUGGLING WITH]
— Solution Summary: [WHAT WE DID IN ONE PHRASE]
— 3 Outcomes: [METRIC 1, METRIC 2, METRIC 3 — e.g., "72% reduction in reporting errors," "15 hours/week returned to data team," "implemented in 67 days"]
— The So What for This Account: [HOW THE OUTCOME MAPS TO THEIR SPECIFIC SITUATION]

The relevance bridge is the element that transforms a case study pitch from a proof point into a conversation starter. When a VP at the target account reads "you and [case study company] are both at the same inflection point — Series C, scaling data infrastructure, operating under board pressure to show predictable metrics," the case study stops being marketing content and starts being a mirror. That is the moment ABM content earns a response.

Prompt 5: LinkedIn Ad for ABM (Targeting Specific Companies)

LinkedIn's Company List targeting feature is one of the most powerful ABM tools available — you can upload a list of up to 300,000 target accounts and serve ads exclusively to employees at those companies. But most ABM teams use this capability with generic product ads that perform no better than standard awareness campaigns. The opportunity is to run ads that feel like they were written for the people at those specific companies: copy that references the challenges those companies face, the outcomes their peers have achieved, and a CTA relevant to their current buying stage. This prompt generates LinkedIn ad copy structured for company-list ABM campaigns.

Prompt ABM-05: LinkedIn Ad for ABM (Targeting Specific Companies)
Act as a B2B LinkedIn advertising specialist with expertise in account-based marketing campaigns. Write LinkedIn Sponsored Content ad copy for a company-list ABM campaign. These ads will only be served to employees at a specific list of target accounts, so the copy can be more specific and assumption-heavy than standard awareness ads.

Provide 3 ad variations, each with:
— Introductory text (150 characters max): The hook that appears above the image. Lead with a pain point, a provocative question, or a trigger-event acknowledgment relevant to this account segment.
— Headline (70 characters max): The bold text on the link preview. Benefit-driven or curiosity-driven. Not our product name.
— Description (100 characters max): Supporting line that reinforces the headline with specificity or social proof.
— CTA Button: Choose the most appropriate LinkedIn CTA option from: Learn More, Download, Sign Up, Register, Get Quote, Apply Now, Contact Us, Watch Now.

Ad angle strategy:
— Variation 1: Pain-first angle — leads with the specific challenge this account segment faces
— Variation 2: Peer proof angle — leads with a result achieved by a company similar to the target accounts
— Variation 3: Trigger event angle — references the category of growth moment this account segment is navigating

Rules: This is not a cold audience. These are companies that match your ICP. Assume they are vaguely aware of the problem you solve. The ad's job is to make them feel like you understand their world specifically, then earn the click. Avoid generic B2B language: "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "robust," "seamless."

Variables:
— Our Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND ONE-LINE DESCRIPTION]
— Target Account Segment: [DESCRIPTION OF COMPANIES ON THE LIST — e.g., "Series B-D SaaS companies, 100–500 employees, using Salesforce"]
— Primary Pain Point: [SHARED CHALLENGE THIS SEGMENT FACES]
— Peer Proof: [RESULT ACHIEVED BY A SIMILAR COMPANY — e.g., "a 200-person SaaS company cut data pipeline failures by 80% in Q1"]
— Trigger Event Category: [GROWTH MOMENT — e.g., "scaling past 150 employees," "post-Series C headcount expansion," "entering a new market"]
— Content Asset or CTA Destination: [WHAT THEY CLICK TO — e.g., "industry benchmark report," "15-minute ROI assessment," "account-specific demo"]

Headline: 72% Fewer Reporting Errors in 67 Days

Variation 3 (Trigger Event):

The three-variation approach matters in ABM LinkedIn campaigns because different stakeholders within the same target account respond to different angles. The CTO responds to the trigger event frame. The VP of Data Engineering responds to the peer proof. The CFO responds to the pain-first ROI framing. Running all three simultaneously and tracking which roles engage with which variation gives you valuable intelligence about where the account is in its buying journey — before a single outreach call has been made.

Prompt 6: Direct Mail / Gift Package Copy

Physical direct mail has experienced a renaissance in enterprise ABM because it cuts through the inbox noise in a way that no digital channel can. When a VP of Operations receives a physical package addressed specifically to them with content that references their company's situation, the attention it earns is qualitatively different from an email. For high-value target accounts, a well-executed direct mail campaign — a custom book, a relevant gift with a handwritten note, or a branded box with a personalized report inside — can accelerate pipeline movement at deal sizes where the $200 cost per package is trivial relative to the potential contract value. This prompt generates all the copy components of a direct mail ABM package.

Prompt ABM-06: Direct Mail / Gift Package Copy
Act as an enterprise ABM strategist with experience running direct mail campaigns for high-value B2B accounts. Write all copy components for a direct mail gift package to be sent to a specific target account contact. The package is designed to break through inbox noise, create a memorable brand experience, and generate a response (a reply, a booked call, or a LinkedIn connection).

Copy components to write:
— Outer Package Label / Address Block: The label copy on the outside of the package, including any teaser text that earns the open. This is your first impression before the package is opened.
— Personalized Letter (250–300 words): A physical letter inside the package. Structured as: account-specific opening that references a trigger event or shared observation, why we selected them specifically for this program, the connection between the gift and the business problem we help solve, a single specific ask (a 20-minute call, a reply to schedule time), and a P.S. that adds a second personal touch.
— Card Insert Copy (50–75 words): A shorter gift card or notecard version of the message for packages where a full letter is too formal. Warm, direct, and specific.
— Follow-Up Email Subject Line (for the email sent 3 days after package delivery): References the package. Not "Did you receive my package?" — something more clever that continues the conversation.
— Follow-Up Email Body (100 words max): A brief check-in that references the gift, continues the narrative from the letter, and reiterates the ask.

Variables:
— Target Company: [COMPANY NAME]
— Recipient Name and Title: [NAME, TITLE]
— Gift or Package Contents: [WHAT IS IN THE PACKAGE — e.g., "a signed copy of Predictable Revenue," "a custom data governance report for their industry," "a Moleskine notebook with their company name embossed," "a specialty coffee sampler"]
— Connection Between Gift and Business Problem: [HOW THE GIFT RELATES TO THE VALUE PROP — e.g., "the book is about scaling revenue teams, which is what we help with," "the coffee is for the long data pipeline reviews that our tool eliminates"]
— Trigger Event: [RECENT ACCOUNT EVENT REFERENCED IN THE LETTER]
— The Specific Ask: [DESIRED NEXT STEP — e.g., "20-minute discovery call," "reply with a question about their current stack," "LinkedIn connection request"]
— Our Solution Benefit: [WHAT WE HELP THEM ACHIEVE — one sentence]

The follow-up email strategy is as important as the package itself. The email sent 3 days after confirmed package delivery is your highest open-rate email of the entire ABM sequence — because the recipient has a physical object in their office that creates a mental connection when they see your name in their inbox. This prompt generates that email as part of the same asset set, ensuring the follow-up continues the narrative rather than starting over with a cold opening.

Prompt 7: ABM Content Hub (Custom Resource Center)

An ABM content hub is a dedicated, password-protected or IP-gated web page that functions as a custom resource center built for a specific target account. It collects the most relevant content assets for that account — industry research, relevant case studies, the personalized landing page copy, a recorded walkthrough, and a direct scheduling link — into a single destination that the entire buying committee can access. Rather than sending each stakeholder individual emails with different links, you send one URL that adapts to their role and presents everything they need to evaluate your solution in the context of their company. This prompt generates the content strategy and copy framework for an ABM content hub.

Prompt ABM-07: ABM Content Hub (Custom Resource Center)
Act as an ABM content strategist and B2B copywriter. Create a complete content hub plan and copy framework for a custom account-specific resource center built for a target account. This hub is a dedicated web page (or Notion page, or password-protected microsite) that serves as the single destination for all stakeholders at this account who are evaluating our solution.

Deliver:
— Hub Headline and Subheadline: The headline should reference the account by name and address their specific growth situation. Subheadline explains what this hub contains and why it was built for them.
— Welcome Paragraph (3–4 sentences): A brief personalized introduction that acknowledges the account's situation, explains why we created this custom resource, and tells them how to navigate the hub.
— Content Section 1 — "The Challenge" (title + 2-sentence description): Name the specific business problem this account faces. Link to or describe the content piece that best explains this problem (industry report, thought leadership article, video).
— Content Section 2 — "The Proof" (title + 2-sentence description): Present the most relevant case study or customer success story for this account. Explain the parallel between the case study company and the target account.
— Content Section 3 — "The Solution" (title + 2-sentence description): Present the product overview or demo resource. Frame it in the context of their specific use case, not as a generic product tour.
— Content Section 4 — "The Numbers" (title + 2-sentence description): Present ROI data, pricing calculator, or benchmark report relevant to their industry or company size.
— Content Section 5 — "Next Steps" (title + 2-sentence description + CTA): Tell them exactly what to do next. Make it specific and low-friction.
— Stakeholder Navigation Note: A short sentence or tab label system that helps different buying committee members find the content most relevant to their role (e.g., "For the CTO," "For Finance," "For the Implementation Team").

Variables:
— Target Company: [COMPANY NAME]
— Account Industry and Stage: [INDUSTRY, GROWTH STAGE]
— Primary Pain Point: [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE]
— Buying Committee Roles: [LIST OF STAKEHOLDER ROLES — e.g., "CTO, VP of Data Engineering, CFO, Head of Compliance"]
— Best-Fit Case Study: [CASE STUDY COMPANY AND OUTCOME]
— Key ROI Metric: [SPECIFIC NUMBER — e.g., "3.2x ROI in year one," "67-day average implementation," "$180K average annual savings"]
— Primary CTA: [DESIRED NEXT ACTION — e.g., "schedule a 30-minute ROI assessment," "request a custom data audit," "book a stakeholder call"]

The content hub solves one of the most persistent ABM challenges: champion enablement. When a mid-level champion inside the target account wants to advance the conversation internally, they need materials they can share with their CFO, their CTO, and their implementation team — without you being in the room. A content hub with role-specific sections gives your champion everything they need to sell internally, organized in a way that makes them look well-prepared and thoughtful. That is the kind of sales tool that actually moves enterprise deals forward.

Prompt 8: ABM Campaign Playbook (Multi-Channel)

A campaign playbook is the document that turns an ABM strategy into an executable program. It maps every touchpoint across every channel — email, LinkedIn, paid ads, direct mail, phone, executive events — across a 90-day timeline, specifying who sends what, when, through which channel, with which content asset, and what the ideal response looks like at each stage. Without a playbook, ABM campaigns become a series of disconnected touches that feel uncoordinated to the account and create confusion internally between marketing and sales. Prompt ABM-08: ABM Campaign Playbook (Multi-Channel)
Act as a senior ABM program manager with experience running multi-channel enterprise ABM campaigns. Create a complete 90-day multi-channel ABM campaign playbook for a target account or account cluster. This playbook will be used by both the marketing team and the sales development team to coordinate outreach and ensure every touchpoint is intentional, timed, and connected to the previous one.

Playbook structure:
— Campaign Overview (3–4 sentences): Account segment, campaign objective, success metrics, and the primary narrative thread that ties all touchpoints together
— Buying Committee Map: List each stakeholder role, their primary buying concern, their preferred channel, and the content angle most likely to resonate with them
— Channel Mix and Rationale: Which channels this campaign uses and why each is appropriate for this account segment (email, LinkedIn InMail, LinkedIn Sponsored Content, direct mail, phone, executive event, content hub)
— 90-Day Touchpoint Timeline: Organized by week, list each touchpoint with: Week number, Channel, Who sends it (marketing or SDR), Content asset or message type, Personalization element for this account, Expected outcome or reply goal
— Provide at minimum 12 touchpoints across the 90 days, spread across at least 4 different channels
— Escalation Path: What happens at each positive response (reply to email, LinkedIn connection, content hub visit, direct mail acknowledgment) — the next step the SDR should take within 24 hours
— Pause / Exit Criteria: When to pause the sequence (out-of-office, "not the right time," "connect me with someone else") and when to exit the account from the active campaign and move to a nurture track
— Content Asset List: Every asset the campaign requires (personalized email copy, LinkedIn ad variations, landing page, case study pitch, direct mail letter, content hub, follow-up sequence)
— Measurement Framework: 4–5 KPIs to track at the account level and the campaign level, with the reporting cadence

Variables:
— Target Account / Segment: [COMPANY NAME OR SEGMENT DESCRIPTION]
— Campaign Objective: [GOAL — e.g., "book 5 discovery calls with VP-level stakeholders," "move 3 accounts from awareness to evaluation stage," "re-engage 10 churned accounts"]
— Primary Buying Committee Roles: [LIST OF STAKEHOLDER ROLES]
— Available Channels: [CHANNELS AVAILABLE TO THIS TEAM — e.g., "email, LinkedIn InMail, LinkedIn Ads, direct mail, SDR phone outreach"]
— Campaign Theme / Narrative: [CENTRAL MESSAGE THREAD — e.g., "data governance at scale," "RevOps consolidation," "compliance readiness for 2027 regulations"]
— Primary Content Asset: [ANCHOR ASSET — e.g., "industry benchmark report," "custom ROI assessment," "executive roundtable invitation"]
— SDR-to-Marketing Ratio: [HOW MANY SDRs SUPPORT THIS CAMPAIGN — affects how many personalized touches are feasible]

Week 1–2 (Awareness): Marketing launches LinkedIn Sponsored Content (Variations 1–3) to company list. SDR sends personalized first email to primary stakeholder (VP level). Content hub is live and linked in email signature.

Week 3 (Engagement): Direct mail package ships. SDR sends LinkedIn connection request to secondary stakeholder (technical role) with a note referencing the industry benchmark report.

Week 4 (Follow-Up): SDR sends direct mail follow-up email (3 days after delivery confirmation). Marketing retargets content hub visitors with peer proof LinkedIn ad.

Week 6–8 (Deepening): SDR sends case study pitch email to primary stakeholder. Secondary stakeholder receives role-specific LinkedIn InMail. Marketing runs ROI-focused ad variation to CFO-level job titles at the account.

Week 10–12 (Decision): SDR makes warm call attempt. Executive alignment email to primary stakeholder requesting a 30-minute call with our VP. Offer to send customized ROI analysis.

The playbook is where ABM campaigns succeed or fail operationally. Without it, the email team sends the first touch, the SDR sends a generic LinkedIn request three weeks later, and the direct mail package arrives with no coordinated follow-up. The account experiences three disconnected messages from three people at your company and forms no coherent impression. The 90-day playbook ensures that every touch advances a single narrative and that the account always knows what comes next — because your team always knows what comes next.

5 Tips for Running High-Converting ABM Campaigns with AI

The 8 prompts above give you the assets. These five tips ensure your ABM program delivers the pipeline results that make the investment worthwhile.

1. Invest in Account Research Before Running Any Prompt

The quality of every AI-generated ABM asset depends entirely on the quality of the account intelligence you provide as input. Spend 30–45 minutes per account before generating any copy: read the company's most recent press releases, scan their job postings for technology investment signals, review the LinkedIn activity of your target stakeholders in the past 90 days, check Crunchbase for funding history, and run their domain through a tool like BuiltWith or Sifdata to map their technology stack. The research you input is the personalization the AI outputs. Generic inputs produce generic assets — no matter how good the prompt structure is.

2. Map Assets to Buying Stage, Not Just Account Tier

Most ABM teams organize their asset library by account tier: Tier 1 accounts get custom landing pages and direct mail, Tier 2 accounts get semi-personalized email sequences. But the more important variable is buying stage. An account in the awareness stage needs thought leadership and pain-point content. An account in the evaluation stage needs case studies and competitive differentiation. An account in the decision stage needs ROI calculators and implementation plans. Use the ABM Campaign Playbook prompt to map the right asset to the right stage, regardless of tier, and you will see significantly faster pipeline progression.

3. Coordinate Sales and Marketing on a Single Playbook

The most common ABM failure mode is the disconnect between marketing's nurture track and the SDR's outreach sequence. Marketing sends a case study at week 4; the SDR has no idea it was sent and cold-calls three days later without referencing it. The account receives a fragmented experience that undermines the personalization you built. The ABM Campaign Playbook prompt generates a unified document that both teams use, with explicit handoff points that tell the SDR exactly what marketing has done, what the account has engaged with, and what the next appropriate touchpoint is.

4. Use Trigger Event Monitoring to Time Your Campaigns

An account that just closed a funding round is in buying mode for the next 60–90 days. An account that just hired a new CXO is evaluating every vendor relationship for the next 6 months. An account that just posted 10 job listings in your solution area has revealed a strategic initiative. The prompts in this article include trigger event variables for exactly this reason: timing your campaign to a real account-level event increases response rates by 3–5x compared to outreach with no timing rationale. Set up Google Alerts, LinkedIn alerts, and Crunchbase notifications for your top 50 target accounts so you always know when a campaign window opens.

5. Measure Account Engagement, Not Just Lead Volume

ABM campaigns should not be measured with demand generation metrics like MQL volume, email open rate, or cost per lead. The right metrics are account-level: percentage of target accounts reached (account coverage), percentage of target accounts showing engagement signals (account engagement rate), number of accounts advancing from awareness to evaluation to decision stage (pipeline velocity), and revenue generated from ABM accounts versus non-ABM accounts. When you measure at the account level, you can see which campaign elements are actually moving accounts through the funnel — and which are generating activity metrics that do not translate to pipeline.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Account Based Marketing

What is account-based marketing and why does it need different AI prompts? +
Account-based marketing (ABM) flips the traditional demand-generation funnel. Instead of casting a wide net and filtering for qualified leads, ABM identifies specific high-value target accounts first and then runs personalized marketing campaigns directed at the buying committees within those accounts. This requires fundamentally different prompts because every asset — the email, the landing page, the ad, the case study pitch — must be customized to the specific company, industry, pain points, and stakeholders. Generic copy templates fail in ABM because the entire strategy depends on the prospect feeling that your message was written specifically for them. The prompts in this article are structured to produce account-specific outputs by requiring you to input real account intelligence before the AI generates anything.
How do I identify the right target accounts for an ABM campaign? +
Strong ABM starts with an Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) built from your best existing customers. Analyze your current top 10–20 accounts by revenue, retention, and expansion potential. Identify the firmographic patterns: industry, company size, tech stack, growth stage, geographic location, and funding history. Then use that profile to identify companies that match — through LinkedIn Sales Navigator, Apollo, ZoomInfo, Bombora intent data, or your CRM's lookalike modeling. The Target Account Research Brief prompt in this article helps you structure that research into a one-page brief that drives every downstream ABM asset.
Do these ABM prompts work for one-to-one, one-to-few, and one-to-many ABM programs? +
Yes, with different levels of customization for each tier. One-to-one ABM (strategic accounts) requires fully customized outputs — you fill in all the account-specific variables with real research for each individual company. One-to-few ABM (cluster programs) can use the same prompt with shared variables across 5–15 accounts that share similar firmographic and pain-point profiles. One-to-many ABM (programmatic) uses the prompts to create personalization tokens and dynamic content rules rather than bespoke copy for each account. The prompt structures in this article explicitly call out which variables drive the most personalization impact so you can prioritize where to invest research time based on your ABM tier.
How many touchpoints does a typical ABM campaign need? +
Enterprise ABM campaigns typically require 8–15 touchpoints before a target account engages meaningfully. The mix matters as much as the volume: research shows that accounts engaging across 3 or more channels (email, LinkedIn, direct mail, display retargeting) convert at significantly higher rates than single-channel ABM outreach. The ABM Campaign Playbook prompt in this article generates a full multi-channel sequence mapped across 90 days, specifying the channel, timing, content asset, and personalization element for each touchpoint. This gives your sales and marketing teams a shared playbook rather than improvised outreach.
Can AI really personalize content at the account level, or does it produce generic output? +
AI produces generic output when you give it generic inputs. The prompts in this article are structured to prevent that by requiring specific account intelligence before generating any copy: the target company name, industry, known pain points, recent trigger events (funding round, leadership change, product launch), technology stack, and the specific stakeholder role being targeted. When you feed the AI real account intelligence — pulled from LinkedIn, the company's recent press releases, their job postings, or their annual report — the output is genuinely personalized. The prompt structure is the scaffolding; the account research is the fuel.
