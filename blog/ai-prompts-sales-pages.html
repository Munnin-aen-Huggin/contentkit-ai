7 AI Prompts for Sales Pages That Convert Cold Traffic

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for sales pages are structured instructions that generate each section of a direct-response long-form page — headline, pain section, benefit stack, social proof, objection handling, offer stack, and urgency close — using proven copywriting frameworks that move cold visitors from skeptical to sold. This guide delivers 7 copy-paste prompts that cover every major section of a high-converting sales page, each built on the same frameworks professional copywriters charge $5,000–$25,000 to write manually.

Cold traffic is the hardest traffic to convert. When someone lands on your sales page from a paid ad, a social post, or an organic search result, they arrive with zero brand familiarity, maximum skepticism, and a hair-trigger for the back button. They do not know you, do not trust your claims, and have not yet decided they need what you sell. Converting cold traffic into buyers requires a page that walks them through a specific psychological sequence — not just a list of features and a buy button.

That sequence is what professional direct-response copywriters are paid to architect. They spend years learning how to open with a problem that makes the right reader lean forward, agitate that problem until the reader feels the cost of inaction, stack benefits in the order that builds desire, layer in social proof at the exact moment doubt appears, demolish objections before the reader voices them internally, present an offer that feels like a steal, and close with urgency that removes the "I'll think about it" exit. Each of those moves has a name, a framework, and a proven structure.

The 7 ai prompts for sales pages below encode those frameworks into instructions you can paste into ChatGPT, Claude, or any AI. Each prompt covers one section of the page, with variables for your product, audience, proof points, and offer. Run all 7 in sequence and you have a complete long-form sales page — structured the way a $15,000 copywriter would write it, in under an hour.

Why Cold Traffic Requires a Different Page Structure

Most sales pages are built for warm traffic — people who already follow the brand, have seen the offer before, or were referred by someone they trust. Those pages can skip straight to the offer because familiarity and trust are already established. Cold traffic pages cannot make the same assumption.

A cold visitor lands on your page knowing nothing about you. If the first thing they read is your product name and a list of features, they bounce. Features mean nothing without context. The price feels arbitrary without perceived value. The testimonials seem made-up without established credibility. Cold traffic pages must earn trust before they ask for the sale — and that earning happens section by section, in a deliberate sequence.

The Problem-First Structure

Cold traffic converts when the page opens with the reader's problem rather than your product. When someone reads a headline that names their exact pain — the thing keeping them up at night, the frustration they have tried to solve before — they feel understood. Feeling understood is the first form of trust. The page earns the right to present a solution only after it has demonstrated that it understands the problem deeply enough to have solved it.

Proof Must Precede the Offer

On a warm traffic page, you can introduce your offer in the first third of the page. Cold traffic pages need social proof before the offer appears. When a cold visitor sees your price before they have seen evidence that your solution works for people like them, the price looks like a risk rather than an investment. The 7 prompts below are sequenced to build social proof into the page before the offer stack, which is the structural difference between a page that converts cold traffic and one that does not.

Every Objection Is a Reason Not to Buy

Cold visitors have more objections than warm ones. They question whether the solution works at all, whether it works for their specific situation, whether it is worth the price, whether they can trust the seller, and whether now is the right time. A cold traffic sales page must address all of these objections explicitly — not hope the reader overlooks them. Prompt 5 generates a dedicated objection-handling section that surfaces and neutralizes the five most common conversion barriers before the offer is presented.

7 AI Prompts for Sales Pages

Prompt 1: Sales Page Headline + Subheadline (PAS Framework)

The headline is the most important element on your sales page. Eight out of ten visitors read the headline and decide whether to continue in the first five seconds. A weak headline — one that leads with your product name, your company name, or a vague benefit — loses most of those visitors before they read a single word of your copy. A strong cold traffic headline names a specific problem the reader recognizes as their own, creates tension by implying a cost to that problem, and promises a credible resolution. This prompt generates 10 headline and subheadline combinations using the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework, testing different emotional angles and levels of specificity so you can identify which resonates most with your audience.

Use the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework: the headline names the problem, the subheadline agitates the cost of that problem, and together they imply a solution the reader wants to discover.

Requirements:
— Write 10 complete headline + subheadline pairs
— Vary the emotional angle across pairs: frustration, fear of loss, desire for transformation, curiosity, social proof, specificity
— Headlines: 10–14 words max. Direct, specific, problem-focused. No corporate language, no puns, no clever wordplay.
— Subheadlines: 20–30 words max. Agitate the cost of the problem (time lost, money wasted, opportunity missed, stress endured) and hint at a better outcome.
— At least 3 headlines must include a specific number (timeframe, dollar amount, percentage, or quantity)
— At least 2 headlines must speak directly to a failed previous attempt ("If you've tried X and it didn't work...")
— Do not mention the product name in any headline — the problem earns the right to introduce the solution

Prompt 2: Pain / Problem Section (Agitation)

The pain section is where the page does the emotional heavy lifting. After the headline earns the visitor's attention, the pain section earns their emotional investment by describing their problem in more vivid detail than they have ever seen it described. A great pain section makes the reader feel two things simultaneously: "This person understands exactly what I am going through" and "I cannot continue living with this problem." That combination creates the readiness to consider a solution. Without it, the benefit section and the offer land in a vacuum — the reader sees features but feels nothing compelling enough to act. This prompt generates a complete pain section using the agitation structure that professional copywriters use to create emotional momentum before the solution is introduced.

Prompt SP-02: Pain / Problem Section (Agitation)
Act as a direct-response copywriter specializing in long-form cold traffic sales pages. Write the Pain/Problem section of a sales page. This section appears immediately after the headline and its job is to make the reader feel deeply understood before any solution is presented. Do NOT mention the product or solution anywhere in this section.

Structure:
— Opening paragraph (3–4 sentences): Name the problem with specificity. Start with "If you're..." or "You already know..." to create immediate self-identification. Use second person (you/your) throughout.
— Agitation paragraph 1 (4–5 sentences): Describe the daily experience of living with this problem. Include sensory details: what it feels like, what it looks like in their day, the moment they notice it.
— Agitation paragraph 2 (4–5 sentences): Name the cost — what the problem is stealing from them. Make costs concrete: hours per week, dollars per month, opportunities missed, stress accumulating.
— Failed attempts paragraph (3–4 sentences): Acknowledge what they have already tried that did not work. This validates that the problem is genuinely hard, not a result of their failure. It also pre-handles the objection "I've tried things before."
— Pivot sentence (1 sentence): One sentence that begins to create hope without naming the solution yet. Example: "But there is a specific reason those approaches failed — and it's not the one you think."

Rules: No solution language. No product mentions. No CTAs. Pure problem immersion. Write at a 7th-8th grade reading level. Short sentences. Active voice. Avoid corporate language.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Audience: [WHO THIS IS WRITTEN FOR]
— Core Problem: [PRIMARY PAIN POINT]
— Daily Experience of the Problem: [WHAT IT FEELS LIKE TO LIVE WITH THIS — e.g., "opening Google Analytics every morning hoping the numbers changed, seeing they haven't, closing the tab"]
— Concrete Costs: [SPECIFIC COSTS — e.g., "12 hours/week on manual work," "$8,400/year in wasted tools," "3 deals lost last quarter to a faster competitor"]
— Previous Failed Attempts: [WHAT THEY'VE TRIED — e.g., "hired a freelancer, bought a course, tried a different tool, read every blog post"]

The failed attempts paragraph is the most underused element in sales page pain sections. When you name what your reader has already tried and validate that those attempts were reasonable but incomplete, you accomplish two things at once: you eliminate the skepticism that comes from "well I've tried things before," and you create curiosity about why the familiar approaches did not work. That curiosity pulls the reader into the solution section with genuine interest rather than defensive skepticism.

Prompt 3: Benefit Stack Section (Features to Benefits to Outcomes)

Most sales pages list features. Features describe what a product does. Benefits describe what the feature does for the buyer. Outcomes describe what the buyer's life looks like after the benefit is delivered. Cold traffic pages convert on outcomes, not features. Someone buying a project management tool is not buying task boards and Gantt charts — they are buying the feeling of shipping on time, the confidence to take on a larger client, and the relief of not getting a panicked message at 11pm because something fell through the cracks. This prompt generates a complete benefit stack that takes each feature through the full Features-Benefits-Outcomes (FBO) chain, producing copy that sells the transformation rather than the tool.

Prompt SP-03: Benefit Stack Section (Features to Benefits to Outcomes)
Act as a direct-response copywriter who specializes in benefit-driven sales copy for cold traffic audiences. Write the Benefit Stack section of a sales page using the Features-Benefits-Outcomes (FBO) framework.

— Benefit Layer: What that feature does for the buyer (practical improvement in their day)

Format for each benefit:

Write 6–8 benefit blocks. Order them strategically: lead with the most emotionally resonant outcome, end with the most practically useful benefit, place the most surprising or unexpected benefit in the middle where attention dips.

Rules: Never lead a benefit block with a feature name. Always lead with the outcome or transformation. Avoid adjective inflation ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "ultimate"). Every claim must feel like it could be proven — even if no citation is given.

Prompt 4: Social Proof / Testimonial Section

Testimonials on most sales pages are a wasted opportunity. Generic praise ("This changed my life!"), vague results ("My business grew so much!"), and unverifiable claims ("Best purchase ever") generate zero trust with cold traffic because they provide no specific evidence the reader can evaluate. High-converting social proof sections are built on specific, verifiable, story-structured testimonials that describe the transformation from a specific before state to a specific after state, ideally with a named result, a timeframe, and a detail that proves the reviewer is a real person in a recognizable situation. This prompt generates a testimonial section framework and rewrites your existing testimonials into the story structure that cold traffic actually trusts.

If you have one real testimonial from a former skeptic, lead your social proof section with it.

Prompt 5: Objection-Handling Section

Every cold visitor who reads through your pain section, benefit stack, and testimonials arrives at the offer section carrying at least three or four unresolved objections. These are not unusual doubts — they are the predictable, universal objections that every buyer in your market has before spending money. "Is this too expensive?" "Will this work for my specific situation?" "What if I don't have time to implement this?" "Is this company going to be around if I have a problem?" Unaddressed, these objections cause the reader to close the tab and tell themselves they will "think about it." Addressed directly and specifically, they remove the psychological exit ramps that prevent conversion. This prompt generates a complete objection-handling section that surfaces the five most common objections in your market and neutralizes each with a specific, credible counter-argument.

Prompt 6: Pricing / Offer Stack Section

The offer stack is where most sales pages underperform. They list a price, maybe mention a few features, and call it done. A high-converting offer stack on a cold traffic page does something fundamentally different: it builds perceived value so comprehensively that the price feels like an obvious deal before the buyer is asked to pay it. This is accomplished by stacking every component of the offer with individual values, totaling that stack, and then revealing the actual price as a fraction of the stated value. Combined with a risk-reversal guarantee and a clear bonus structure, a well-written offer stack can lift conversion rates by 30–60% without changing anything else on the page. This prompt generates a complete offer stack with tiered options, value stacking, and a guarantee presentation optimized for cold traffic.

Prompt SP-06: Pricing / Offer Stack Section
Act as a direct-response copywriter who specializes in offer construction and pricing page copy for cold traffic sales pages. Write the Offer Stack section of a sales page. This section must make the price feel like an obvious deal before the reader sees it, using value stacking, tiered options, and guarantee framing.

Section structure:
— Value Stack Table (for each pricing tier):
  • List every component of the offer with an individual value assigned
  • Total the stack ("Total Value: $X")
  • Reveal the actual price ("Today: $X")
— Tier Comparison (2–3 tiers):
  • Starter / Core tier: Core offer at the entry price
  • Full / Premium tier: Core offer + bonuses + access upgrades at the higher price
  • Name the tiers clearly. Highlight the recommended tier.
— Guarantee Block:
  • Name the guarantee boldly (e.g., "The 30-Day No-Questions Guarantee")
  • Describe exactly what happens if the buyer is not satisfied: what they do, what they get back, how fast

Rules: Every value assigned to a component must be defensible — what would this component cost if purchased separately? Never inflate values beyond what a reasonable buyer would accept. Guarantee language must be specific: "full refund within 30 days" beats "satisfaction guaranteed." One tier should be clearly better value to guide the buyer's choice.

Variables:

The value stack works because it reframes the price comparison. Without it, the buyer compares your price to their internal sense of what things "should" cost — an arbitrary and usually low number. With it, the buyer compares your price to the stacked value number you constructed, which makes the actual price look like a significant discount. This is not manipulation; it is showing the buyer what they are actually getting in terms they can evaluate. The rule is simple: every value you assign must be defensible if the buyer asks "could I buy just this component elsewhere?" If the answer is yes, you can use the market price as the value. If the answer is no, you assign a reasonable consulting or production equivalent.

Prompt 7: Final CTA Section (Urgency Close)

The final CTA section is the last chance to convert a reader who has been with you through the entire page. These are your most qualified visitors — they read through the problem section, the benefit stack, the social proof, the objection handling, and the offer. They are interested. But a significant portion of them will still hesitate at the final decision point without a deliberate urgency close. The urgency close is not about fake countdown timers or artificial scarcity. It is about returning to the cost of inaction one final time, making the decision concrete and immediate, and lowering the final psychological barrier between interest and action. This prompt generates a final CTA section that summarizes the decision, creates urgency through consequence rather than manipulation, and presents the buy button with maximum clarity.

Tips for Getting the Best Output from Sales Page Prompts

The 7 prompts above give you the structure. These five tips determine how compelling the output is once the AI fills in that structure.

1. Feed Specific Variables, Not Vague Descriptions

The single biggest driver of generic AI output is vague variable inputs. "Increases revenue" produces weak copy. "$11,200 in additional monthly revenue within 90 days for 47 e-commerce founders" produces copy with the specificity that cold traffic trusts. Before running any prompt, spend 10 minutes filling in every variable with the most specific, concrete data you have. Customer quotes, exact dollar amounts, named timeframes, specific job titles, real competitor names in the objection section — every specific detail you give the AI reduces the generic language in the output.

2. Run the Prompts in Sequence as a Single Session

Each of the 7 prompts builds on context established by the previous one. Run them in sequence in the same AI chat session, and the AI will maintain consistency in voice, audience language, and narrative thread across all seven sections. Starting a new session for each prompt produces sections that feel disconnected and require more editing to merge into a coherent page. The sequential approach produces a page that reads as a unified argument rather than seven independently written sections.

3. Extract Real Language from Customer Reviews

The most persuasive sales page language comes directly from your customers, not from a copywriter's imagination or an AI's training data. Before running the pain section and testimonial prompts, mine your reviews on G2, Trustpilot, Amazon, or Reddit for the exact phrases your customers use to describe their problems, their hesitations, and their results. Paste those phrases into the variable fields. When your pain section uses the same words your best customers used to describe their before state, cold visitors recognize themselves in the copy instantly — because those words came from someone exactly like them.

4. Use the Objection Section as a Research Tool

Before filling in the "Previous Failed Attempts" and "Common Objection" variables, run a quick search on Reddit, Quora, or in relevant Facebook groups for the exact phrases your market uses when describing what has not worked for them. What do people say when they complain about courses that did not deliver? What objections do they raise when recommending against a competitor? That language goes directly into your objection-handling section variables and produces counter-arguments that address real objections in the language real buyers use, not hypothetical objections in generic marketing language.

5. Test Headline Variants Before Investing in the Full Page

Before spending time writing and publishing the full page, test the top 3 headlines as ad creative — image ads on Meta or headlines in a Google search campaign — and measure which drives the highest CTR with your cold audience. The headline that earns the most clicks from cold traffic is the headline your full sales page should lead with. This two-step process (headline test first, then full page) prevents the expensive mistake of writing a complete long-form page around a headline that cold traffic does not respond to.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Sales Pages

Can AI write a sales page that converts cold traffic? +
Yes, when the prompts are built on direct-response frameworks rather than generic writing instructions. Cold traffic converts at lower rates than warm traffic because visitors do not know your brand, do not trust your claims, and have not decided they need your solution yet. AI prompts that sequence the page correctly — problem agitation first, then benefit proof, then social validation, then objection handling, then a risk-reduced offer — mirror the psychological journey a cold visitor needs to take before buying. The seven prompts in this article follow that sequence, so the AI output builds persuasion in the right order rather than front-loading features a cold visitor does not yet care about.
What is the PAS framework and why does it work for sales page headlines? +
PAS stands for Problem-Agitation-Solution. The headline names the problem the reader recognizes, the subheadline agitates why that problem is costing them something real (time, money, opportunity, peace of mind), and the body introduces the solution. It works for sales page headlines because the reader self-selects: if the problem described is their problem, they keep reading. If it is not, they leave — which is fine, because non-buyers leaving early improves your conversion rate on qualified traffic. PAS is the most tested framework in direct-response copywriting, used across decades of print ads, VSLs, and long-form sales pages.
How long should a sales page be for cold traffic? +
Cold traffic sales pages should be longer than warm traffic pages, not shorter. Cold visitors arrive with more skepticism, more unanswered objections, and less context about why your solution is the right one. Long-form pages that work through problem agitation, benefit stacks, social proof, objection handling, and multiple CTAs consistently outperform short pages for cold audiences in most product categories. The seven prompts in this article build out each section independently, so you can sequence them into a complete long-form page of 2,000 to 4,000 words — the range that performs best for cold traffic offers priced above $97.
Do these prompts work for physical products, digital products, and services? +
Yes. Each prompt includes product-type variables that let you specify whether you are selling a physical product, digital download, online course, SaaS subscription, coaching program, or professional service. The underlying frameworks — PAS headlines, feature-benefit-outcome stacks, objection-handling sections, social proof blocks, and urgency closes — apply across all product types. The AI fills in the specifics based on your variables. A physical product benefit stack focuses on tangible outcomes and sensory details. A digital product stack focuses on transformation and speed of delivery. A service stack focuses on expertise, process, and risk reduction.
How do I make the AI-generated sales page copy sound less generic? +
The single biggest driver of generic AI copy is vague variable inputs. When you fill in the prompt variables with specific, concrete details — exact customer quotes, precise numbers, named competitors, real transformation stories — the AI output becomes specific and persuasive instead of generic and forgettable. Instead of writing "increases revenue," write "helped 47 e-commerce founders add $11,200/month in recurring revenue within 90 days." Instead of "easy to use," write "set up in 22 minutes without a developer." Specificity is the antidote to generic AI copy, and it starts with what you put into the variable fields.
