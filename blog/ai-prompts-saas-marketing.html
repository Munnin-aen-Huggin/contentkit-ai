12 AI Prompts for SaaS Marketing and Growth

ContentKit AI•14 min read

AI prompts for SaaS marketing are structured instructions that generate high-converting landing pages, onboarding emails, feature announcements, case studies, and growth content — built on the specific frameworks that drive trial signups, activation, and expansion revenue. This guide delivers 12 copy-paste prompts covering every core SaaS marketing asset, from hero sections and comparison pages to quarterly content calendars and Product Hunt launches.

SaaS marketing operates under constraints that most marketing advice ignores. Your buyer is evaluating three to five alternatives simultaneously. Your free trial window is 14 days, maybe 30. Your onboarding sequence must drive the user to their first meaningful value moment before they lose interest and stop logging in. Your pricing page must justify recurring cost against a one-time-purchase mindset. And your content must serve dual masters: search engines that reward depth and busy practitioners who need the answer in the first paragraph.

The prompts below are built for these constraints. Each one includes the specific variables, frameworks, and structural instructions that produce SaaS copy ready to publish, not copy that needs another hour of rewriting before it sounds like a human wrote it. The difference between generic AI output and output that actually converts is the quality of the prompt — and every prompt here has been built around the formats, channels, and buyer psychology specific to SaaS.

Why SaaS Marketing Needs Specialized AI Prompts

Generic marketing prompts produce generic marketing copy. "Write a landing page for my project management software" produces bland, feature-listing copy that could describe any tool on the market. The SaaS buyer reads that copy, recognizes it as generic, and moves on to the next tab.

SaaS marketing copy that converts does four specific things that generic prompts cannot produce without explicit instruction.

It Speaks to the ICP Job Title, Not a Generic "User"

The VP of Engineering evaluating your DevOps tool has completely different objections, priorities, and success metrics than the Marketing Director evaluating your campaign management platform. SaaS copy that converts uses language that makes the reader think "this was written for me" — referencing the specific pain points, workflows, and success metrics of their exact role. The prompts below include an ICP (ideal customer profile) variable that forces this specificity into every output.

It Sells the Outcome, Not the Feature

SaaS buyers do not buy features. They buy the state of the world after those features do their job. "Automated report generation" is a feature. "Your Monday morning starts with the report already in your inbox" is an outcome. The difference is visceral. Every prompt below instructs the AI to lead with outcomes and use features only as proof that the outcome is achievable.

It Addresses the Switching Cost Objection

Every SaaS purchase involves a switching cost calculation. The buyer is not just evaluating your product — they are evaluating whether your product is worth migrating from whatever they are using now. That means migration tools, onboarding support, time-to-value, and risk reduction need to appear in your copy. The prompts here include switching cost variables that surface these objections and address them before the buyer raises them.

The bottom line: ai prompts for saas marketing work when they include the ICP, the outcome, the differentiator, the objection, and the proof. Here are the 12 prompts that do all of that.

12 AI Prompts for SaaS Marketing

Prompt 1: SaaS Landing Page Hero Section

The hero section of a SaaS landing page carries more conversion weight than any other element on the page. It must do four things in under five seconds: communicate exactly what the product does, establish who it is for, demonstrate the primary benefit, and give the visitor a reason to scroll or take action. Most SaaS hero sections fail at step one — they use product names and category jargon that mean nothing to a first-time visitor. This prompt generates a complete hero section: headline, subheadline, primary CTA, secondary CTA, and a social proof hook that earns the scroll.

Prompt SM-01: SaaS Landing Page Hero Section
Act as a SaaS conversion copywriter with 10+ years of experience writing landing pages for B2B software companies. Write the hero section for the SaaS landing page below.

Deliver:
— Primary Headline (10 words max): What the product does + the primary outcome. No product names. No jargon. A first-time visitor must immediately understand what this tool does and why they should care.
— Subheadline (25 words max): Expands on the headline. Names the ICP explicitly. Adds one specific proof point or differentiator. Uses plain language.
— Primary CTA button text (5 words max): Action-oriented, outcome-focused. Avoid "Get Started" and "Learn More."
— Secondary CTA button text (5 words max): Lower commitment option (e.g., watch demo, see pricing, read case study).
— Social proof hook (15 words max): One line directly below the CTAs. Uses a specific number. Reduces risk or establishes trust.
— 3 headline variations: Same structure, different angle (benefit, urgency, curiosity).

Copywriting rules: Lead with the outcome, not the feature. Name the ICP in the subheadline. Every word must earn its place. Remove adjectives that do not add information ("powerful," "easy-to-use," "seamless"). End CTAs with verbs, not nouns.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— What It Does (one sentence): [PLAIN-LANGUAGE DESCRIPTION — e.g., "connects your sales CRM to your support desk so reps see the full customer history before every call"]
— ICP Job Title: [WHO THIS IS FOR — e.g., "VP of Sales," "marketing ops managers," "solo founders"]
— Primary Outcome: [THE #1 RESULT CUSTOMERS GET — e.g., "close deals 30% faster," "cut churn by half," "ship features 2x faster"]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT — e.g., "no-code setup in 10 minutes," "the only tool with real-time intent data," "works inside Slack"]
— Social Proof: [SPECIFIC NUMBER — e.g., "4,200+ teams," "rated #1 on G2," "$2.4M in pipeline recovered"]
— Risk Reducer: [FRICTION REMOVER — e.g., "no credit card required," "free 14-day trial," "cancel anytime"]

Primary Headline: Stop Losing Deals to Slow Follow-Up
Subheadline: Flowpipe gives sales ops managers a real-time pipeline view across every rep — so nothing falls through the cracks again.
Primary CTA: See Your Pipeline Now
Secondary CTA: Watch a 3-Minute Demo
Social Proof Hook: Trusted by 4,200+ revenue teams. No credit card required.

Variation B (Benefit angle): Your Sales Team Closes 30% More Without Working Harder
Variation C (Urgency angle): Every Day of Slow Follow-Up Costs You Pipeline
Variation D (Curiosity angle): What If Your CRM Told You Who Was About to Churn?

The three headline variations are not afterthoughts — they are your A/B test plan. Run the primary headline for two weeks, then swap in Variation B. The winning headline will often surprise you: benefit and curiosity angles frequently outperform direct-outcome headlines on cold traffic, while urgency headlines convert best in retargeting campaigns.

Prompt 2: Feature Announcement Email

Feature announcement emails are one of the most underutilized channels in SaaS. Most product teams send a changelog-style update ("We shipped X") that reads like internal documentation, not marketing. A well-structured feature announcement does three things: reminds the reader why they signed up in the first place, connects the new feature to a job they are trying to get done, and gives them a single clear action that delivers immediate value. This prompt generates a feature announcement email that feels like news, not a changelog.

Prompt SM-02: Feature Announcement Email
Act as a SaaS product marketing manager writing a feature announcement email to existing customers. Write an email that makes readers feel the new feature was built specifically for their biggest frustration — not like a product update memo.

Structure:
— Subject line (50 characters max): The outcome the feature delivers, not the feature name. Use a hook that creates curiosity without being clickbait.
— Preview text (90 characters max): Complements the subject line. Adds specificity or urgency.
— Opening paragraph (3 sentences max): Name the pain point the feature solves. Make the reader feel seen. Do not mention the feature yet.
— Feature introduction (2 sentences): Introduce the feature by name. Connect it directly to the pain point from the opening.
— 3 bullet points: Each one is a "now you can" statement — a specific action the user can take with this feature that they could not take before.
— Social proof insert (1 sentence): A quote or stat from a beta user, if available, or the number of users who requested this feature.
— CTA button: Action-oriented. Links to the feature inside the product or a short video walkthrough.
— Closing (1 sentence): Personal, warm. Reinforces the team's commitment to the user's success.

Variables:
— Feature Name: [FEATURE NAME]
— Pain Point It Solves: [THE SPECIFIC FRUSTRATION — e.g., "manually exporting data every week to build reports"]
— 3 "Now You Can" Actions: [THREE SPECIFIC THINGS USERS CAN NOW DO — e.g., "schedule reports to auto-deliver every Monday at 8am," "share a live dashboard link with your board," "filter by any custom field in one click"]
— Beta User Proof: [QUOTE OR STAT — e.g., "250 beta users saved an average of 3 hours per week" or skip if unavailable]
— CTA Destination: [WHERE THE BUTTON LINKS — e.g., "Try it now in your dashboard," "Watch the 90-second walkthrough"]
— Sender Name/Role: [NAME AND TITLE — e.g., "Sarah, Head of Product"]

The "now you can" bullet structure is one of the most effective patterns in SaaS email copy. It forces you to think in user actions, not product attributes. "Now you can schedule reports to auto-deliver every Monday morning" is infinitely more motivating than "Automated report scheduling is now available." The user can immediately visualize Monday morning without the manual export headache, which is the emotional trigger that drives the click.

Prompt 3: Free Trial Welcome Email Sequence

The free trial welcome email is the most important email in your entire SaaS stack. A user who just signed up has maximum intent and maximum anxiety simultaneously — they want the product to work, but they have not experienced any value yet and they are already thinking about whether this was worth their time. The first email must accomplish three things: confirm the signup (obvious), set expectations for the trial experience (underrated), and give the user one specific action that creates their first value moment within 10 minutes. This prompt generates a three-email welcome sequence designed to drive activation, not just open rates.

Prompt SM-03: Free Trial Welcome Email Sequence (3 Emails)
Act as a SaaS lifecycle marketer specializing in trial activation. Write a three-email welcome sequence for new free trial users. The sequence goal is to drive product activation — getting users to their first meaningful value moment — not just to be friendly. Every email must have a single, clear next action that moves the user toward activation.

Email 1 (send: immediately after signup):
— Subject: Confirms the signup. Sets expectation. Uses their first name.
— Body: Welcome (1 sentence). What they can achieve in the trial (1 sentence). The single most important first action (1 sentence with a button). What to expect from the sequence (1 sentence).
— CTA: One action only. The first step to their "aha moment."

Email 2 (send: Day 3):
— Subject: Progress-check angle. Assumes they have not completed the key action yet.
— Body: Acknowledge that setup can feel like friction. Remove the biggest barrier (link to docs, video, or offer a quick call). Remind them what is waiting on the other side (the outcome).
— CTA: Pick up where they left off. Deep-link into the relevant product area.

Email 3 (send: Day 7, trial midpoint):
— Subject: Urgency without pressure. Reminds them of time remaining with a reason to care.
— Body: What they will miss when the trial ends (the outcome, not the feature). One customer story or stat. A reason to upgrade now vs. waiting.
— CTA: Upgrade or extend trial. Both options presented.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Trial Length: [14 DAYS / 30 DAYS / ETC.]
— First "Aha Moment" Action: [THE SINGLE ACTION THAT DELIVERS FIRST VALUE — e.g., "connect your CRM," "create your first board," "invite a teammate"]
— Primary Outcome: [WHAT ACTIVATED USERS EXPERIENCE — e.g., "never miss a follow-up again," "ship features without status meetings"]
— Biggest Setup Barrier: [THE #1 REASON USERS GET STUCK — e.g., "connecting data sources," "importing existing projects," "setting up permissions"]
— Customer Proof: [ONE STAT OR QUOTE FROM AN ACTIVATED CUSTOMER]
— Upgrade Incentive: [REASON TO UPGRADE DURING TRIAL — e.g., "lock in current pricing," "get 2 months free on annual," "keep your data after trial ends"]

Three emails over seven days is the minimum viable welcome sequence for a 14-day trial. Studies of SaaS activation data consistently show that users who do not reach their aha moment within the first three days have a dramatically lower conversion rate than those who do. Email 1 directs them there. Email 2 removes the friction blocking them. Email 3 creates urgency. The sequence is not about relationship building — it is about getting users activated before they forget why they signed up.

Prompt 4: SaaS Onboarding Email Sequence

Onboarding emails are the difference between a trial that converts and a trial that churns silently. Where the welcome sequence (Prompt 3) focuses on the first seven days and activation, the full onboarding sequence covers the arc from signup through conversion-ready — driving the user from their first value moment through to seeing the full depth of the product and understanding why upgrading makes sense. This prompt generates a six-email onboarding sequence built on the Jobs-to-be-Done framework, where each email corresponds to a specific job the user is trying to accomplish with your product.

Prompt SM-04: SaaS Onboarding Email Sequence (6 Emails)
Act as a SaaS onboarding specialist using the Jobs-to-be-Done framework to design email sequences that increase trial-to-paid conversion. Write a six-email onboarding sequence that takes the user from first login through upgrade-ready. Each email maps to a specific "job" the user is trying to accomplish with the product.

Sequence structure:
— Email 1 (Day 1): The First Job — the most common reason users sign up. Drive them to complete this single core action.
— Email 2 (Day 4): The Collaboration Job — invite a teammate or stakeholder. Products with multiple users churn at 3x lower rates.
— Email 3 (Day 7): The Integration Job — connect the tool to their existing workflow (CRM, Slack, calendar). Integration = stickiness.
— Email 4 (Day 10): The Power Feature Job — introduce the feature that heavy users love but new users miss. Frame it as "you are ready for this now."
— Email 5 (Day 12): The Social Proof Email — a customer story from someone in the same role/industry as the reader. Let the customer sell the upgrade.
— Email 6 (Day 13): The Conversion Email — direct ask to upgrade. Benefits summary, pricing anchor, risk reduction, deadline.

For each email provide: Subject line, Preview text, Opening hook (1 sentence), Body (3–5 sentences), CTA button text, CTA destination.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Most Common First Job: [WHAT USERS DO FIRST — e.g., "create a project," "connect their inbox," "build a report"]
— Collaboration Trigger: [HOW USERS ADD TEAMMATES — e.g., "invite by email," "share a workspace link," "assign a task"]
— Key Integration: [MOST POPULAR INTEGRATION — e.g., "Slack," "Salesforce," "Google Calendar," "Zapier"]
— Power Feature: [THE FEATURE THAT DRIVES THE HIGHEST RETENTION — e.g., "automated workflows," "custom dashboards," "AI summaries"]
— Customer Story ICP: [JOB TITLE AND COMPANY TYPE OF CUSTOMER STORY SUBJECT]
— Upgrade Benefit #1: [PRIMARY PAID PLAN BENEFIT]
— Upgrade Benefit #2: [SECONDARY PAID PLAN BENEFIT]
— Pricing Anchor: [HOW TO FRAME THE PRICE — e.g., "less than one hour of your time per month," "$9/user — less than a team lunch"]
— Urgency Element: [WHY UPGRADE NOW — e.g., "trial ends tomorrow," "current pricing ends March 31," "data is deleted after trial"]

The collaboration email (Day 4) is the single highest-ROI email in this sequence. Product analytics from dozens of SaaS companies show that users who invite a teammate within the first week convert at two to three times the rate of solo users and churn at a fraction of the rate. The onboarding sequence makes inviting a teammate feel like the natural next step in getting value, not like a task the product is asking you to do for its own benefit.

Prompt 5: Case Study (Customer Success Story)

Case studies are the most credible content in the SaaS marketing stack and the most universally underdeveloped. Most SaaS case studies read like a press release — generic praise from a generic customer about a generic outcome. The case studies that actually move sales conversations forward follow a specific narrative structure: a real problem with measurable stakes, a specific decision-making moment, an implementation story that builds confidence, and results expressed in numbers that prospects can map to their own business. This prompt generates a complete case study from an interview transcript or notes, following the Before-After-Bridge narrative structure with a problem-specific intro that doubles as an SEO-optimized headline.

Prompt SM-05: Case Study (Customer Success Story)
Act as a B2B content strategist and case study writer. Write a complete customer case study following the Before-After-Bridge narrative structure. The case study must be specific enough that a prospect in the same role at a similar company reads it and thinks "that is exactly our situation."

Structure:
— Headline (10 words max): "[Company] [Achieved Specific Result] in [Timeframe] Using [Product]" or "[Job Title] at [Company Type] [Achieved Result] Without [Pain]"
— Results snapshot (3 bullets, numbers only): Lead with the most impressive metrics. Prospect reads these first.
— The Before (2 paragraphs): Describe the situation before your product. Name the specific pain, its business impact (time, money, risk), and what they tried that did not work. Use the customer's own language where possible.
— The Decision (1 paragraph): What made them choose your product over the alternatives? Was it a specific feature, a recommendation, a trigger event? This is where prospects recognize their own buying situation.
— The Implementation (1 paragraph): How long did setup take? Who was involved? What surprised them about the process (positively)? This section reduces implementation anxiety for prospects.
— The After (2 paragraphs): Specific results with numbers. How their day-to-day changed. An emotion — relief, confidence, excitement — that a prospect can project onto themselves.
— Pull quote (25 words max): The most quotable, specific, emotional line from the customer.
— CTA: What the reader should do next (start trial, book demo, see more stories).

Variables:
— Customer Name and Title: [NAME, TITLE, COMPANY]
— Company Description: [COMPANY SIZE, INDUSTRY, STAGE]
— Problem Before: [THE SPECIFIC PAIN POINT AND ITS BUSINESS IMPACT]
— What They Tried Before: [PREVIOUS SOLUTIONS THAT FAILED]
— Why They Chose You: [THE DECIDING FACTOR]
— Implementation Details: [TIME TO SETUP, WHO WAS INVOLVED, WHAT SURPRISED THEM]
— Results (with numbers): [SPECIFIC METRICS — e.g., "reduced reporting time from 6 hours to 45 minutes," "increased MRR by 34% in 90 days," "cut churn by 22%"]
— Pull Quote Candidate: [DIRECT QUOTE FROM CUSTOMER]

The results snapshot at the top is not a nice-to-have — it is the entire strategy. Busy buyers scan case studies rather than reading them linearly. If the metrics are buried in paragraph six, most prospects never see them. By leading with three specific numbers, you give scanners the proof they need to decide whether the full story is worth their time. Make the numbers as specific as possible: "reduced reporting time from 6 hours to 45 minutes" is 10 times more credible than "saved significant time on reporting."

Prompt 6: Comparison Page (vs. Competitors)

Comparison pages are among the highest-converting pages in any SaaS content strategy. When a buyer searches "[Your Product] vs [Competitor]" or "[Competitor] alternative," they are in the final stage of evaluation — they are already sold on the category, they just need to pick the winner. A comparison page that shows up for this search and makes an honest, structured case for your product closes deals without a sales call. The key word is honest: buyers are sophisticated enough to spot a rigged comparison table, and a dishonest comparison destroys the trust that closes deals. This prompt generates a comparison page that is assertive without being misleading.

Prompt SM-06: Comparison Page (vs. Competitors)
Act as a SaaS product marketer writing a competitor comparison page. The page must be honest, specific, and persuasive without being negative or misleading. Buyers at this stage are sophisticated — a rigged comparison table destroys credibility. Write a comparison that a skeptical buyer would trust.

Page structure:
— SEO headline (H1): "[Your Product] vs [Competitor]: Which Is Right for Your Team in [Year]?"
— TL;DR summary (3 sentences): Honest summary of the comparison. When is each product the better fit? This section pre-qualifies the reader.
— 3 structural advantages of your product (H2 sections each): Each advantage must be specific, defensible, and illustrated with an example or customer proof. Not marketing claims — verifiable differences.
— Feature comparison table (8 rows): Choose features where you objectively win or are comparable. Do not include features where you lose unless you acknowledge them honestly. Honest tables convert better.
— When to choose [Competitor] (1 paragraph): Name the specific use cases where the competitor is the better fit. This builds credibility and screens out wrong-fit buyers before they become churned customers.
— When to choose [Your Product] (1 paragraph): Mirror the above. Specific use cases, company stages, or job titles where your product wins.
— Switching section (1 paragraph): What does migration look like? Time, effort, data portability. Reduce implementation anxiety.
— Social proof from switchers (1–2 quotes): Real quotes from customers who switched from the competitor.
— CTA: Trial or demo with a switching-specific incentive.

Variables:
— Your Product Name: [YOUR PRODUCT]
— Competitor Name: [COMPETITOR]
— Your 3 Structural Advantages: [THREE SPECIFIC, VERIFIABLE DIFFERENCES — e.g., "no per-seat pricing," "built-in AI summaries," "direct Salesforce sync vs. Zapier dependency"]
— Feature Comparison Rows: [8 FEATURES TO COMPARE WITH HONEST WIN/LOSS/TIE ASSESSMENT]
— Competitor's Strengths (honest): [WHERE THE COMPETITOR LEGITIMATELY WINS — e.g., "stronger enterprise SSO," "larger integration library," "more established brand"]
— Ideal Switcher Profile: [WHO SHOULD SWITCH TO YOU — e.g., "growing teams of 5–50 who need workflow automation without a developer"]
— Migration Time/Effort: [HOW EASY IS THE SWITCH — e.g., "import from Competitor in one click, full setup in under an hour"]
— Switcher Quotes: [1–2 QUOTES FROM CUSTOMERS WHO SWITCHED FROM THIS COMPETITOR]
— Switching Incentive: [OFFER FOR SWITCHERS — e.g., "free migration support," "3 months free on annual plan," "match your current pricing"]

The "when to choose the competitor" section is counterintuitive but essential. Acknowledging where the competitor wins signals intellectual honesty to the reader — and instantly makes everything else on the page more credible. It also pre-qualifies your pipeline: a buyer who needs the one thing the competitor does better will have a bad experience with your product and churn. The comparison page that screens them out before they sign up saves your customer success team time and improves your aggregate retention metrics.

Prompt 7: SaaS Blog Post (Thought Leadership)

SaaS blog content serves two masters simultaneously: search engines that reward comprehensive, keyword-optimized coverage of a topic, and practitioners who will close the tab within 30 seconds if the first paragraph does not answer their question or create genuine curiosity. This prompt generates a complete thought leadership blog post structure built on the Answer First framework — the reader gets the core insight in the opening paragraph, not buried after 800 words of background context. The post includes SEO structure (H2/H3 hierarchy, keyword placement, internal linking anchors) alongside practitioner-grade content that earns shares and backlinks.

Prompt SM-07: SaaS Blog Post (Thought Leadership)
Act as a senior SaaS content strategist writing a long-form blog post that ranks for a target keyword and earns genuine shares from practitioners. Use the Answer First framework: the reader gets the core insight or answer in the first 100 words, not after a long introduction.

Deliver:
— SEO Title (60 characters max): Keyword-first. Specific. Includes a number or compelling modifier ("how to," "the complete guide," "why most [X] fail at [Y]").
— Meta description (155 characters max): Expands on the title. Includes the keyword naturally. Ends with a reason to click.
— H1 (can differ from SEO title): More engaging version for readers who land on the page.
— Answer-first opening paragraph (150 words max): Lead with the core argument or answer. State the thesis immediately. No preamble.
— Complete H2/H3 outline with 1-sentence descriptions of each section's key point.
— 3 data points or studies to source and include (cite relevant industry research).
— 2 practitioner-grade insights: Observations or frameworks not found in a standard Google search. This is what earns shares and backlinks.
— Internal linking opportunities: 3 anchor text suggestions for linking to related product pages or articles.
— CTA integration: Where in the post to place a contextual CTA (not just at the end).

Variables:
— Target Keyword: [PRIMARY KEYWORD — e.g., "saas churn reduction," "product-led growth strategy," "b2b email marketing"]
— ICP Reader: [WHO IS READING THIS — e.g., "Head of Growth at a Series A SaaS," "founder of a bootstrapped B2B tool"]
— Core Argument / Thesis: [THE CENTRAL CLAIM OF THE POST — e.g., "most SaaS companies measure churn wrong and the mistake costs them 20% of recoverable revenue"]
— 3 Supporting Points: [THE THREE STRONGEST ARGUMENTS THAT PROVE THE THESIS]
— Proprietary Data or Unique POV: [ANY DATA, FRAMEWORKS, OR PERSPECTIVES YOUR COMPANY UNIQUELY OWNS — e.g., "analysis of 500 SaaS companies in our benchmark report"]
— Related Product Feature: [PRODUCT FEATURE THAT CONTEXTUALLY RELATES TO THE POST — for natural CTA integration]

The practitioner-grade insights section is what separates a blog post that ranks from a blog post that earns links and shares. A post that cites the same studies everyone else cites and makes the same points everyone else makes will rank eventually if it is long enough and optimized correctly. But a post that contains one observation or framework that practitioners have never seen articulated before gets shared in Slack channels, bookmarked, and linked to from other posts. That earned distribution compounds over time in ways that SEO alone cannot replicate.

Prompt 8: Product Hunt Launch Copy

Product Hunt is a unique launch channel with its own conventions, culture, and reader psychology. The Product Hunt audience skews toward early adopters, makers, and fellow founders — they are skeptical of hype, allergic to corporate language, and looking for tools they can adopt and advocate for. The copy that wins on Product Hunt is direct, specific, and written in a voice that feels like a founder talking to a peer, not a marketing team talking to a customer. This prompt generates your Product Hunt tagline, description, first comment, and a maker post that drives upvotes from the community.

Prompt SM-08: Product Hunt Launch Copy
Act as a Product Hunt launch strategist who has successfully launched multiple products in the top 10 of the day. Write the complete copy set for a Product Hunt launch. The PH audience is technical, maker-oriented, skeptical of hype, and has seen thousands of product launches. Write like a founder talking to a peer, not a marketer talking to a customer.

Deliver:
— Tagline (60 characters max): What the product does in one punchy line. No buzzwords. Specific. Could be a statement, a question, or a provocative claim.
— Description (260 characters max): Expands on the tagline. What it does, who it is for, why it is different. Plain language. No jargon. A maker reading this in 10 seconds should immediately understand the value.
— First comment (the maker post, 300–500 words): This is the most important piece of copy on your launch. Written by the founder. Tell the real story: why you built this, what problem you personally experienced, what you have learned from early users, and what you are working on next. Be honest about what the product is not yet. End with a specific ask (feedback on a feature, use cases you want to explore, beta testers for a new integration).
— 3 follow-up comment templates: For responding to: (1) a positive comment, (2) a question about a missing feature, (3) a comparison to a competitor.
— Gallery image caption suggestions (5): Brief captions for your screenshot carousel that highlight specific use cases, not generic feature screenshots.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— What It Does: [ONE-SENTENCE PLAIN DESCRIPTION]
— Founder Origin Story: [WHY YOU BUILT THIS — the specific problem you experienced personally]
— Early User Insights: [WHAT YOU LEARNED FROM FIRST 100 USERS — e.g., "the feature we thought was core turned out to be secondary; the one we almost cut became the thing everyone loves"]
— What the Product Is Not (Yet): [HONEST LIMITATION — e.g., "we do not have a mobile app yet," "enterprise SSO is on the roadmap for Q2"]
— Specific Ask from Community: [WHAT FEEDBACK YOU WANT — e.g., "we are exploring integrations — which tools do you wish this connected to?"]
— Key Competitor Differentiator: [HOW YOU ARE DIFFERENT FROM THE OBVIOUS COMPARISON]

The first comment is the most underrated piece of Product Hunt launch strategy. While most makers write a generic "Hi PH! We are excited to launch" opener, the products that hit top 5 of the day use the first comment to tell a real story. Authenticity — including honest admissions about what the product does not do yet — signals confidence and earns the trust of an audience that has been burned by over-promised tools before. Write the first comment as if you are posting in a community of your peers, not pitching to customers.

Prompt 9: SaaS Google Ad (Search)

SaaS Google Ads occupy some of the most competitive and expensive keyword categories on the platform. "Project management software," "CRM for startups," and "marketing automation tool" command CPCs that make every word of ad copy worth optimizing. The mistake most SaaS advertisers make is writing ads that describe the category rather than the differentiation. When five ads on the same page all say "powerful project management software," the buyer has no reason to prefer one over the others. This prompt generates search ad copy that leads with your specific differentiator, not your category membership.

Prompt SM-09: SaaS Google Ad (Search)
Act as a Google Ads specialist with deep experience in SaaS search campaigns, where CPC is high and differentiation is the primary lever for performance. Write a complete responsive search ad (RSA) for the SaaS product below. Provide 15 headlines and 4 descriptions.

Headline strategy (30 characters max each):
— Headlines 1–3: Category keyword + differentiator (not just category match)
— Headlines 4–6: Outcome-focused — the result the buyer achieves, not the feature
— Headlines 7–9: ICP-specific — names the buyer's role or company stage
— Headlines 10–12: Trust and proof (G2 rating, customer count, years, award)
— Headlines 13–15: Trial/offer focused (free trial, no credit card, free migration)

Description strategy (90 characters max each):
— Description 1: Outcome + differentiator in one sentence
— Description 2: ICP pain point addressed + proof point
— Description 3: Trial offer + risk reduction + secondary benefit
— Description 4: Social proof + CTA with urgency

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts in SaaS Marketing

The 12 prompts above generate the first draft. These five principles determine whether that first draft becomes content that converts.

1. Always Fill Every Variable Before Generating

The quality of AI-generated SaaS copy is directly proportional to the specificity of the variables you fill in. A prompt with placeholders like "[KEY BENEFIT]" left as-is produces generic output. A prompt where "[KEY BENEFIT]" is filled in as "closes the reporting loop between sales and finance without a BI tool or a data analyst" produces copy that sounds like it was written by someone who deeply understands the product. Before you run any prompt, spend five minutes filling every variable with the most specific, concrete language you can. The output time you save will exceed the input time you invest by a factor of ten.

2. Use Your ICP's Language, Not Your Internal Language

SaaS teams have a vocabulary problem: they use internal product language in external marketing copy. "Workflow automation" means something specific inside your company and something vague to your buyer. "Stop copying deal notes from Salesforce into Slack every morning" means something visceral to every SDR who has ever done exactly that. Before running any prompt, translate your internal product language into the language your ICP uses to describe their own problem. Pull this language from support tickets, sales call transcripts, G2 reviews, and customer interview recordings. Paste that language directly into the prompt variables.

3. Generate Multiple Variations and Test

Run each prompt two or three times with different inputs in the variable fields — or ask the AI to generate three variations of the same output in a single pass. SaaS copy benefit especially from variation testing because your buyer's context and intent vary by channel, by funnel stage, and by the specific problem they are experiencing in the moment they encounter your content. A landing page hero that converts cold paid traffic may not convert organic search traffic from a different keyword. A subject line that works for trial users will not work for cold outreach. Generate variation, test it, and let the data tell you what works.

4. Edit for Specificity, Not for Length

The most common editing mistake with AI-generated SaaS copy is cutting words to make it shorter without adding specificity. A 50-word hero subheadline that is specific and differentiated will outperform a 20-word subheadline that is vague. When you edit AI output, ask "is this specific enough?" before asking "is this too long?" If a sentence contains the words "powerful," "easy," "seamless," or "robust," replace them with a specific outcome or number. If a sentence could describe any SaaS product in your category, rewrite it until it can only describe yours.

5. Use the Output to Brief Your Team, Not Just to Publish

AI-generated prompts produce strong first drafts, but the highest-value use is sometimes using that output as a brief. When the AI generates a landing page hero section, it has also surfaced the core positioning decisions: which outcome to lead with, which ICP to name, which differentiator to call out. If your team does not agree on the AI's choices, that disagreement reveals a positioning problem worth solving before you publish anything. Use the prompt output as a structured starting point for alignment conversations, not just as copy to publish and move on from.
