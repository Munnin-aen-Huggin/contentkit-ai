5 AI Prompts for Mission and Vision Statements

ContentKit AI•10 min read

AI prompts for mission and vision statements are structured instructions that generate distinctive company missions, vision statements, core values, purpose statements, and about page copy — grounded in your specific business, customer, and competitive differentiation rather than the generic language that plagues most brand foundations. This guide delivers 5 copy-paste prompts covering every foundational brand document, each built on proven strategic frameworks from brand positioning, organizational design, and narrative identity work.

Mission and vision statements are the most under-invested documents in most companies. Teams spend months writing go-to-market strategies and product roadmaps, then spend an afternoon copy-pasting something that vaguely sounds like what Amazon or Apple would say. The result is a mission statement that could apply to a thousand other companies, values that nobody remembers, and an about page that reads like a press release from a company that does not want to be found.

The problem is not that companies lack clarity about who they are. It is that translating that clarity into crisp, memorable language is genuinely hard. Most founders can explain what they do and why in a three-minute conversation. But condensing that into one sentence that a new hire can repeat on day three, a VC can quote in a portfolio blog, and a customer can find in a thirty-second website skim — that is a craft skill that normally requires a brand strategist, a positioning consultant, and several weeks of workshops.

This is where structured ai prompts for mission and vision statements change the equation. When you give the AI the right inputs — your customer, the problem you solve, your mechanism for solving it, and what makes your approach different — it can generate ten variations of your mission statement in the time it takes to pour a second cup of coffee. You are not outsourcing the thinking. You are compressing the drafting so you can focus on the judgment: which version is true, which is distinctive, which one actually sounds like your company.

The 5 prompts below cover every core brand foundation document: the mission statement that defines your purpose today, the vision statement that describes your future, the core values that guide decision-making, the purpose statement that connects your work to something larger, and the about page copy that integrates all four into a narrative customers and candidates actually want to read.

Why Most Mission Statements Fail (and How AI Prompts Fix It)

Bad mission statements share a common set of failure modes. Understanding them is the prerequisite for using any prompt effectively — because if you feed a prompt the wrong inputs, you will get better-written versions of the same generic output.

Generic Language Is the Enemy

The majority of mission statements fail because they use interchangeable language. "We empower businesses to achieve more." "We connect people with the solutions they need." "We deliver innovative solutions that drive results." These statements are grammatically correct and completely meaningless — because any company in any industry could say exactly the same thing. Generic mission statements do not fail because the company lacks purpose. They fail because the prompt (whether given to an AI or a human writer) did not specify what makes this company different from the ten competitors who also serve the same customers.

No Customer, No Specificity

The fastest way to make a mission statement specific is to name the customer and their problem with precision. "We help small business owners" is vague. "We help first-generation immigrant entrepreneurs navigate US business formation and compliance without a $500-per-hour attorney" is not. The more precisely you describe who you serve and what their specific problem is, the more specific — and therefore more credible and differentiated — your mission becomes. The prompts in this article require customer specificity as a variable. You cannot skip it.

Vision Without Ambition, Mission Without Grounding

Vision statements fail when they are simply inflated mission statements — describing the same present-tense work in grander language. A vision statement must describe a future that does not yet exist: a world changed by your company's success. And mission statements fail when they are so lofty they give employees no guidance for Monday morning decisions. The prompts below address both problems by structuring the mission around present-tense clarity and the vision around future-state ambition. The two documents are intentionally distinct.

The bottom line: ai prompts for mission and vision statements are only as good as the specificity you bring to them. These prompts will ask you hard questions — about your customer, your mechanism, your differentiator, and the future you are building toward. Answer them honestly, and the output will be worth using. Answer them lazily, and you will get polished versions of the same generic statements you already have. Here are the 5 prompts.

5 AI Prompts for Mission and Vision Statements

Prompt 1: Company Mission Statement

A mission statement answers one question: why does this company exist right now, and for whom? The best mission statements are short enough to memorize (one to three sentences), specific enough to be meaningful (they could not apply to a different company), and clear enough to guide decision-making (employees can use them to resolve genuine ambiguity). This prompt generates multiple variations across different lengths and tonal registers — from concise and punchy to narrative and grounded — so you can choose the version that fits your brand voice and the context in which it will live.

Prompt MV-01: Company Mission Statement
Act as a brand strategist with 15+ years of experience developing mission statements for companies ranging from early-stage startups to Fortune 500s. Write a company mission statement for the business below.

Provide 5 variations across different lengths and approaches:
— Variation 1: One sentence (15–25 words). Crisp, declarative, specific. No corporate language.
— Variation 2: One sentence with a subordinate clause (25–40 words). Names the customer, the problem, and the approach.
— Variation 3: Two sentences (40–60 words). First sentence = what we do and for whom. Second sentence = how we do it differently.
— Variation 4: Three sentences (60–80 words). Full foundation: customer, problem, mechanism, and differentiator.
— Variation 5: One-sentence "employee-facing" version. Written so any team member can use it to make a difficult decision.

Rules:
— No generic verbs: no "empower," "leverage," "disrupt," "innovate," "transform" unless they are the most precise word available
— Name the customer specifically — not "businesses" or "people" but the exact type
— Include the specific problem being solved, not a category of problems
— Include the mechanism — how the company solves it, not just that it solves it
— The mission must be false for a direct competitor (if it is true for every company in the space, it is too generic)

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— What You Do: [SPECIFIC PRODUCT OR SERVICE — e.g., "AI-powered accounts receivable software," "organic meal kit delivery," "bilingual mental health therapy"]
— Exact Customer: [SPECIFIC CUSTOMER TYPE — e.g., "independent dental practices with 1–5 locations," "parents of children with ADHD," "first-generation college students"]
— The Core Problem: [THE SPECIFIC PROBLEM YOU SOLVE — describe the pain, not the category]
— Your Mechanism: [HOW YOU SOLVE IT — your unique approach, method, or technology]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT COMPETITORS DO NOT OR CANNOT DO]
— Tone: [TONAL DIRECTION — e.g., "direct and pragmatic," "warm and human," "bold and ambitious," "quiet confidence"]

Example output for a B2B fintech company:

Variation 1: Brightflow helps small manufacturers get paid in full, on time, without hiring a collections team.

Variation 2: Brightflow exists to solve the cash flow crisis that kills profitable small manufacturers — not by offering loans, but by automating the receivables process that most owners have been managing manually in spreadsheets for years.

Variation 3: Brightflow builds accounts receivable software for manufacturers with 10 to 200 employees who are profitable on paper but perennially cash-strapped. We automate invoice follow-up, dispute resolution, and payment reconciliation so owners stop losing sleep over money that is already theirs.

Variation 4: Brightflow exists because small manufacturers should not have to choose between growing their business and getting paid for the work they have already done. We serve manufacturing businesses with 10 to 200 employees — companies that are profitable by every measure except cash in the bank. By automating the full receivables cycle, from invoice delivery to payment posting, we give owners back the time and capital they need to build the business they intended to build. We do this without debt, without factoring fees, and without a six-month implementation.

Variation 5 (employee-facing): If a decision makes it easier for a small manufacturer to get paid for work they have already done, it is probably the right decision.

Notice how each variation increases in specificity and context. The one-sentence version is memorable enough to put on a slide. The employee-facing version is operational enough to actually use. Having all five gives your leadership team the right tool for every context — pitch deck, career page, all-hands meeting, and policy discussions.

Prompt 2: Company Vision Statement

A vision statement describes a future that does not yet exist. It should be ambitious enough to be inspiring, specific enough to be directional, and realistic enough to be credible. The common failure mode is a vision statement that is simply an inflated mission — describing the same present-tense work in bigger words. A real vision statement points to a changed world: the industry restructured, the problem eliminated at scale, the behavior normalized, the injustice corrected. This prompt generates vision statements calibrated to a 5-to-10-year timeframe, specific enough to guide strategic decisions.

Prompt MV-02: Company Vision Statement
Act as a brand strategist and organizational narrative expert. Write a company vision statement that describes the future this company is working to create — not what the company does today, but what the world looks like when the company succeeds at scale.

Provide 4 variations across different framings:
— Variation 1: World-change framing (what the world looks like when you win). "A world where..." or "A future where..."
— Variation 2: Industry-change framing (how the industry is different because of you). Describes the structural change, not just a bigger version of your current business.
— Variation 3: Customer-change framing (how the lives of your customers are fundamentally different). Specific behavioral or circumstantial change, not just satisfaction.
— Variation 4: North Star framing (a single aspirational sentence that functions as a guiding star for all decisions). This is the one executives can say from memory at all-hands meetings.

Rules:
— Vision must describe a future state, not a present-tense activity
— It must be ambitious enough to feel like a 5–10-year horizon, not something achievable in 18 months
— It must be specific enough to be false for a competitor — generic vision statements ("a better world for everyone") provide no direction
— Avoid the word "best" — it is a superlative claim, not a vision
— Do not confuse vision with mission — this is where you are going, not what you do today

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— What You Do Today: [CURRENT PRODUCT OR SERVICE]
— The Problem at Scale: [THE PROBLEM IF NEVER SOLVED — what is the cost to the world of the problem you are solving at full scale?]
— Success at Scale: [WHAT DOES WINNING LOOK LIKE? — e.g., "50% of small manufacturers in the US are cash-flow positive," "mental health care is as accessible as primary care," "every child reads at grade level by age 8"]
— Timeframe: [HOW FAR OUT IS THIS VISION — e.g., "5 years," "10 years," "a generation"]
— Tone: [TONAL DIRECTION — e.g., "bold and declarative," "measured and credible," "inspiring and human"]

The "false for a competitor" test is the most useful quality check for vision statements. If your vision is "a world where every business has access to the tools it needs to succeed," your direct competitor could put that on their wall tomorrow without changing a word. A vision statement that passes the false-for-a-competitor test describes a specific future that is only achievable by a company solving your specific problem with your specific approach. That is what makes it directional rather than decorative.

Prompt 3: Core Values Definition

Core values are the most abused documents in business. Most companies have five to ten values that were generated in an afternoon workshop, voted on by a committee, and installed on a wall where they have been politely ignored ever since. Real core values are behavioral: they describe how a company behaves when that behavior is costly, inconvenient, or in conflict with short-term results. This prompt generates core values with behavioral definitions and real-world examples — so they function as actual decision-making tools rather than aspirational wallpaper.

Prompt MV-03: Core Values Definition
Act as an organizational culture strategist who has designed core values for companies that actually use them. Write a set of core values for the company below.

For each value, provide:
— The Value Name: 1–3 words. Specific enough to be memorable, not so vague it could mean anything.
— One-line definition (15–25 words): What this value means in practice — not an aspiration but a behavioral commitment.
— Behavioral example (1–2 sentences): A specific scenario where this value guides a difficult decision. Name the tension it resolves (e.g., "when speed conflicts with quality, we choose quality and communicate the delay").
— What it rules out (1 sentence): The specific behavior this value prohibits. Values only work if they eliminate options. If your value does not rule anything out, it is not a value, it is a preference.

Generate 5 values. They should collectively cover: how we treat customers, how we treat each other, how we make decisions under pressure, how we handle failure, and what we prioritize when we cannot have everything.

Rules:
— No interchangeable values: "Integrity," "Excellence," "Innovation," "Teamwork" as standalone words are not values — they are aspirations. Every company claims them. Make each value specific to how THIS company operates.
— Each value should pass the "hire/fire test": Would you hire someone who violates this value? Would you fire someone who repeatedly violates it? If the answer is no, the value is not operational.
— Values must be true today, not aspirational for a future state. They describe who you are, not who you want to become.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Industry: [INDUSTRY]
— Company Stage: [STAGE — e.g., "seed-stage startup," "Series B," "established SMB," "enterprise"]
— Culture Priorities: [3–5 BEHAVIORAL TRAITS THAT ARE GENUINELY TRUE OF YOUR BEST EMPLOYEES — describe behaviors, not adjectives]
— Culture Anti-Patterns: [2–3 BEHAVIORS THAT HAVE CAUSED REAL PROBLEMS AT YOUR COMPANY — the things you hire around and exit for]
— Decision-Making Style: [HOW DECISIONS GET MADE — e.g., "data-first," "customer-first," "consensus," "owner-led"]
— Failure Philosophy: [HOW THE COMPANY HANDLES MISTAKES — e.g., "blameless post-mortems," "high accountability individual ownership," "learn fast and move on"]

Example value output:

Say It in the Room
Definition: We raise concerns, disagreements, and difficult feedback in the meeting where the decision is made — not in Slack threads afterward.
Behavioral example: When a team member disagrees with a product decision, they say so in the design review, not in a private message to the CEO. The cost of silence is higher than the discomfort of conflict.
What it rules out: Passive agreement in the room followed by private criticism. If you did not say it when the decision was being made, it is not the team's problem — it is yours to raise before execution begins.

The "what it rules out" element is what separates functional values from decorative ones. A value that does not eliminate any option is not a value — it is a preference. When you force each value to name a specific behavior it prohibits, you create a values system that employees can actually use to navigate ambiguity. That is what makes values operational rather than ornamental.

Prompt 4: Purpose Statement / "Why We Exist"

A purpose statement goes deeper than a mission. Where the mission answers "What do we do and for whom?", the purpose answers "Why does this company exist beyond making money?" Purpose is the belief that drives the mission — it connects the company's day-to-day work to a larger human, social, or systemic need. Not every company needs a separate purpose statement, but companies competing for top talent, operating in commodity markets, or building long-term brand equity almost always benefit from one. This prompt generates purpose statements using the Simon Sinek Golden Circle framework and the Jim Collins BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal) structure.

Prompt MV-04: Purpose Statement / "Why We Exist"
Act as a brand strategist trained in narrative identity and organizational purpose. Write a purpose statement (sometimes called a "why statement" or "reason for being") for the company below. This is distinct from the mission statement: it articulates the deeper belief or conviction that drives everything the company does — the reason this company exists beyond commercial success.

Provide 3 variations using different frameworks:
— Variation 1 (Golden Circle "Why"): Written in the format Simon Sinek describes: "We believe [conviction]. We exist to [action that flows from that conviction]. We do this by [mechanism]." Each part should be a single sentence. The belief must be a genuine conviction, not a market observation.
— Variation 2 (Injustice framing): Starts with the problem in the world that this company was founded to correct. Names the injustice, gap, or dysfunction that should not exist but does. Then pivots to the company's role in correcting it.
— Variation 3 (Legacy framing): Describes what this company wants to be true of the world in 20–30 years because it existed. Written for a founder reflection or anniversary moment — the long-arc story of what the company was for.

Rules:
— Purpose is not a marketing statement — it is a belief. It should feel slightly uncomfortable to say out loud because it makes a claim about what matters.
— Avoid "changing the world" without specificity — name which part of the world, for whom, and by what measure.
— The purpose must be achievable in direction but not in completion — it is a guiding star, not a finish line.
— Do not list features or product benefits in a purpose statement. The product is the mechanism; the purpose is the reason the mechanism matters.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Founding Belief: [THE CONVICTION THAT DRIVES THE COMPANY — what does the founder believe to be deeply true about the world that most people ignore or accept? This is the hardest variable and the most important.]
— The Injustice or Gap: [WHAT SHOULD NOT BE TRUE BUT IS — the specific dysfunction, inequity, or inefficiency the company was built to correct]
— Who Bears the Cost: [WHO SUFFERS WHEN THE PROBLEM IS UNSOLVED — specific people, not categories]
— The Change You Are Making: [THE SPECIFIC SHIFT YOU ARE DRIVING — behavioral, structural, economic, or cultural]
— 20-Year Legacy: [IF THE COMPANY SUCCEEDS COMPLETELY, WHAT IS DIFFERENT ABOUT THE WORLD IN 20 YEARS?]

Example output using the Golden Circle framework for an edtech company:

We believe that where you grow up should have no bearing on how far you can go. We exist to close the opportunity gap between students in under-resourced communities and their peers in well-funded districts. We do this by putting the academic support systems that private school families take for granted — personalized tutoring, college counseling, and study skills coaching — into the hands of every student with a phone and an internet connection.

Injustice framing:
A child born in a low-income zip code is statistically less likely to graduate college than a child born twenty miles away in a high-income zip code — not because of intelligence or effort, but because of access. Pathfinder exists to make that statistic obsolete. Not by fixing schools. By giving every student the individual support that closes the gap between their potential and their opportunity.

The founding belief is the hardest variable in this prompt — and the most important. Most founders can name the problem they solve. Fewer can articulate the underlying conviction: what they believe to be deeply true about the world that justifies the existence of this company. When you force that belief into a sentence, the rest of the purpose statement writes itself. The AI handles the craft; you provide the conviction.

Prompt 5: About Page Copy (Integrating Mission + Vision + Values)

The about page is where mission, vision, and values have to perform together — in public, for a reader who is deciding whether to become a customer, a candidate, or a partner. Most about pages fail because they either recite corporate documentation (three paragraphs of mission-speak that nobody reads) or tell a founder story that has no strategic point (a sentimental narrative that ends without connecting to what the company does or why the reader should care). This prompt generates about page copy that integrates all four brand foundation documents into a narrative that converts — whether the goal is to close a hire, earn a customer, or attract a partner.

Prompt MV-05: About Page Copy (Mission + Vision + Values Integration)
Act as a brand copywriter who specializes in about page copy for companies that need to attract customers, top-tier candidates, and strategic partners simultaneously. Write a full about page for the company below. The copy must integrate the company's mission, vision, and values into a coherent narrative — not list them as separate documents, but weave them into a story that makes the reader want to engage.

Structure the copy in these sections:
— Opening Hook (1 sentence): The most arresting, specific thing you can say about the problem this company solves or the belief that drives it. Not a mission statement. Not a tagline. A sentence that makes the reader stop scrolling.
— The Problem (2–3 sentences): Paint the problem in vivid, specific terms. Name who it affects, what it costs them, and why it has been tolerated for so long. This is where the reader thinks "yes, that is exactly the problem."
— Our Answer (2–3 sentences): Introduce the company and its approach. Not a feature list — a narrative of how you approach the problem differently. Connect back to the belief from the opening hook.
— Mission Statement (1–2 sentences): Pull the mission into the narrative. It should feel like a natural escalation, not a shift into corporate-speak.
— The Future We Are Building (2–3 sentences): The vision, translated into human terms. What does the world look like when this company succeeds? Make it concrete and specific.
— How We Work (3–5 values woven into prose, not listed as bullets): Describe the company's operating principles in narrative form. Each value should appear as a behavioral commitment, not an adjective.
— Closing CTA (2–3 sentences): Direct readers to the next step based on who they are — customer, candidate, or partner. End with specificity about what happens when they take that step.

Rules:
— Total length: 350–500 words. About pages are read, not skimmed. Keep it human and clear.
— Write in second person for the problem section (the reader should see themselves) and first-person plural for the company sections (this is who we are)
— No jargon. No corporate language. If a 12-year-old would not understand a sentence, rewrite it.
— The values section must feel like a story about how the team actually works, not a list of admirable traits

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Mission Statement: [PASTE YOUR MISSION STATEMENT — from Prompt MV-01]
— Vision Statement: [PASTE YOUR VISION STATEMENT — from Prompt MV-02]
— Core Values (with behavioral definitions): [PASTE YOUR VALUES — from Prompt MV-03]
— Founding Story (optional): [1–3 sentences about why the company was started — only include if the story adds meaning, not just color]
— Primary Audience for This About Page: [WHO IS THE MOST IMPORTANT READER — e.g., "prospective enterprise customers," "engineering candidates," "impact investors"]
— Call to Action Goal: [WHAT DO YOU WANT THE READER TO DO AFTER READING — e.g., "book a demo," "apply for a role," "read the case studies"]

The structure of this prompt is deliberate: the problem section is written for the reader before the company is introduced. Too many about pages start with "we" — "We are a company that..." — before the reader has any reason to care. When you open with the problem and make the reader feel seen in it, the introduction of your company is an answer to a question they are already asking. That is the difference between an about page that bounces and one that converts.

5 Tips for Using AI to Write Mission and Vision Statements

The 5 prompts above give you the drafts. These tips ensure the output is worth publishing.

1. Front-Load Your Specifics, Not Your Aspirations

The quality of AI-generated mission and vision copy is directly proportional to the specificity of your inputs. When you fill in the customer variable with "businesses," you get a mission statement that sounds like every other company in your space. When you fill it in with "independent physical therapy clinics with 2–8 locations that are transitioning from paper-based patient records," you get a mission statement that could only be yours. The aspiration comes from the prompt structure. The specificity comes from you. Do not trade them.

2. Run the "False for a Competitor" Test on Every Output

After generating any mission, vision, or values output, apply one test: could your three closest competitors put this on their website without changing a word? If yes, the output is not specific enough. Go back to the variables and add more precision: name the customer more exactly, describe the mechanism more specifically, articulate the differentiator more sharply. Regenerate until the output is false for at least two of your direct competitors. That is the minimum bar for a brand foundation document that is worth publishing.

3. Use the Employee-Facing Version for Internal Alignment

The mission statement on your website and the mission statement your team uses to resolve daily ambiguity do not have to be the same document. The website version can be aspirational and polished. The internal version should be operational and behavioral. Prompt MV-01 generates both: the Variation 5 employee-facing version is designed specifically for internal use. Publish it on your company wiki, include it in offer letters, and use it in performance reviews. If people are making decisions that contradict the mission, the operational version is the one to hold them accountable to.

4. Validate Values Against Real Hiring and Firing Decisions

Before finalizing your core values from Prompt MV-03, validate each one against the last five people you hired and the last person who did not work out. Ask: did we hire that person because they demonstrated this value? Did the person who left violate it? If your values do not track with your actual hiring and exit decisions, they are aspirational rather than operational. Either update the values to reflect who you actually are, or update your hiring criteria to enforce who you want to become. Both are valid. Misalignment between stated values and actual behavior is not.

5. Sequence the Prompts in Order

The five prompts in this article are designed to build on each other. Run Prompt MV-01 (mission) first, then MV-02 (vision), then MV-03 (values), then MV-04 (purpose). By the time you reach MV-05 (about page), you have four documents to paste directly into the variables. The about page prompt is explicitly designed to integrate the outputs of the first four. Running them out of order means the about page prompt will ask for inputs you have not yet developed — and you will be tempted to fill in those variables with approximate answers rather than the considered output from the earlier prompts.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Mission and Vision Statements

What is the difference between a mission statement and a vision statement? +
A mission statement defines what your company does today — its purpose, the customers it serves, and the value it delivers right now. A vision statement describes where the company is going — the future state it is working to create, often 5 to 10 years out. Mission answers "Why do we exist?" Vision answers "Where are we headed?" Both are necessary. A mission without a vision is operational with no destination. A vision without a mission is aspirational with no foundation. The two work together: the mission grounds daily decisions, and the vision aligns the team around a shared future.
How long should a mission statement be? +
The most effective mission statements are one to three sentences — typically 25 to 50 words. They should be short enough to memorize, specific enough to be meaningful, and clear enough that any employee can repeat them without looking at the wall. If your mission statement requires a paragraph to explain, it is not a mission statement — it is a business description. The AI prompt in this article generates multiple length variations so you can choose the right fit: a one-sentence version for your website header, a two-sentence version for your about page, and a three-sentence version for investor materials.
Can AI actually write a good mission statement? +
Yes, when given the right inputs. The failure mode for AI-generated mission statements is generic output — vague language about "empowering people" and "driving innovation" that could apply to any company. The fix is specificity in the prompt: your exact customer, the specific problem you solve, your differentiated approach, and your measurable impact. When you feed those details into a structured prompt, the AI produces mission statements that are grounded and distinctive. The prompt in this article requires you to define your customer, your mechanism, and your differentiator before the AI writes a single word — which is why the output is usable rather than generic.
How many core values should a company have? +
Three to six core values is the research-backed range. Fewer than three feels thin and uncommitted. More than seven becomes impossible to remember, which means employees cannot actually use them to make decisions — and core values that nobody remembers are decorative, not functional. Each value should be specific enough to guide behavior in an ambiguous situation: "Be honest" is decorative. "Say the hard thing in the room rather than after the meeting" is a behavioral value. The core values prompt in this article generates values with behavioral definitions and real-world examples — so your values work as decision-making tools, not just wall art.
What is a purpose statement and how does it differ from a mission statement? +
A purpose statement (sometimes called a "why statement" after Simon Sinek's Golden Circle framework) goes deeper than a mission statement. Where the mission answers "What do we do and for whom?", the purpose answers "Why does this company exist beyond making money?" Purpose statements connect the work to a larger human or societal need. Patagonia's purpose is environmental activism. Warby Parker's is vision access for underserved populations. Not every company needs a purpose statement distinct from their mission — but companies that operate in commoditized markets, recruit talent in competitive fields, or want to build a values-aligned culture benefit enormously from one.
