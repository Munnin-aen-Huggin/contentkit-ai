10 AI Prompts for Lead Magnets That Build Your Email List

ContentKit AI•13 min read

AI prompts for lead magnets are structured instructions that generate high-converting checklists, PDF guides, templates, quizzes, and swipe files — complete with opt-in page copy and promotion strategies — so you build your email list faster without hiring a copywriter or spending weeks creating assets from scratch. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every major lead magnet format, each designed to produce a subscriber-ready resource in under 30 minutes.

Your email list is the only marketing channel you actually own. Social media accounts get banned, algorithm changes tank organic reach overnight, and paid ad costs rise every year. But an email list of engaged subscribers compounds in value over time: every new product launch, every promotion, every piece of content you produce goes out to an audience that has already raised their hand and said they want to hear from you.

The problem most creators and marketers face is not knowing they need a lead magnet — it is the blank-page paralysis that comes when it is time to actually create one. What topic? What format? How long? What do I put on the opt-in page? How do I promote it? These questions stall list-building for weeks or months, which means missed subscribers, missed revenue, and missed opportunities to build an audience that belongs to you.

This is where structured ai prompts for lead magnets change everything. Instead of staring at a blank document trying to figure out what to include in your checklist, you paste a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude and get a complete, structured checklist — title, subtitle, all line items organized by category, and a delivery sequence — in under five minutes. The AI handles the structure and content. You handle the branding and final polish.

The 10 prompts below cover every major lead magnet format: checklists, PDF guides, templates and swipe files, cheat sheets, quizzes and assessments, free tool concepts, mini email courses, resource libraries, opt-in landing pages, and full promotion strategies. Each prompt includes fill-in-the-blank variables so you can adapt it to any niche, audience, or offer in minutes.

Why Lead Magnets Are the Fastest Path to a Qualified Email List

A generic newsletter signup converts at 1–2% of website visitors. A targeted lead magnet — one that solves a specific, urgent problem for your exact audience — converts at 15–40%. That difference is not subtle. At 1,000 monthly visitors, a generic signup form adds 10–20 subscribers per month. A well-crafted lead magnet adds 150–400. Over a year, that is the difference between a list of 240 subscribers and a list of 4,800.

But the quality of the subscribers matters as much as the quantity. A lead magnet that is tightly aligned with your paid offer attracts subscribers who are pre-qualified buyers, not just email collectors. When someone downloads your "7-Step Facebook Ads Audit Checklist," they are telling you they run Facebook ads, care about results, and are open to tools and strategies that improve their performance. That is a warm lead from the moment they hit your list.

The Anatomy of a Lead Magnet That Actually Converts

Every high-converting lead magnet shares four characteristics. It is specific — it solves one defined problem, not a broad topic area. It is fast — subscribers can consume and implement it in under 30 minutes. It is immediately valuable — the subscriber gets something useful right now, not someday. And it is closely related to your paid offer — solving a problem adjacent to what you sell so the subscribers you attract are likely buyers.

The AI prompts below are built on these four principles. They generate lead magnets that are scoped tightly, structured for fast consumption, and positioned to attract your ideal customer. Here are the 10 prompts.

10 AI Prompts for Lead Magnets

Prompt 1: Checklist Lead Magnet

Checklists are the highest-converting lead magnet format for one reason: they communicate instant, tangible value with zero ambiguity. "27-Point Launch Checklist" tells the subscriber exactly what they get, how many items are included, and what outcome they will achieve. There is no wondering whether the content is relevant — if you are launching a product, you need a launch checklist. This prompt generates a complete, publication-ready checklist lead magnet with a compelling title, organized categories, all line items, and a delivery welcome sequence hook.

Prompt LM-01: Checklist Lead Magnet
Act as a lead magnet strategist and conversion copywriter who has built email lists for 200+ online businesses. Create a complete checklist lead magnet for the audience and topic below.

Deliverables:
— Title (include a number, power word, and specific outcome — e.g., "The 27-Point SaaS Launch Checklist")
— Subtitle (one sentence: who this is for + what they will achieve by completing it)
— 3–5 category headers (organize the checklist into logical phases or topic areas)
— 5–8 specific, actionable checklist items per category (each item starts with a verb and is concrete enough to check off)
— A "Quick Win" box: the single most important item a subscriber should do first and why
— Welcome email hook: the first line of the delivery email that creates anticipation and explains the value (2–3 sentences)

Rules: Every checklist item must be specific enough to take action on today. No vague items like "optimize your website" — use "Add a benefit-driven H1 tag that includes your primary keyword." Numbers, specifics, and action verbs throughout.

Variables:
— Topic / Niche: [TOPIC — e.g., "SaaS product launch," "freelance client onboarding," "email list building"]
— Target Audience: [AUDIENCE — e.g., "first-time SaaS founders," "freelance designers," "coaches building their list"]
— Desired Outcome: [RESULT — e.g., "launch without missing critical steps," "onboard clients professionally," "grow to 1,000 subscribers"]
— Audience's Biggest Fear: [FEAR — e.g., "missing a critical step and losing launch momentum," "seeming unprofessional," "spending months building the wrong thing"]
— Related Paid Offer: [YOUR PRODUCT/SERVICE — so the checklist attracts your ideal buyer]

Example output (SaaS launch audience):

Title: The 29-Point SaaS Launch Checklist: Ship Without Missing Anything
Subtitle: For first-time SaaS founders who want to launch confidently and capture their first 100 paying customers without scrambling at the last minute.

Category 1: Pre-Launch Technical (items 1–7)
1. Configure Stripe or payment processor and run a test transaction end-to-end
2. Set up error tracking (Sentry or equivalent) and verify alerts reach your inbox
3. Test the signup → onboarding → first value moment flow in an incognito window
...

Quick Win: Before anything else, manually complete your own signup flow as a new user. Most founders discover 2–3 friction points they missed entirely.

Welcome email hook: Your checklist is attached — but before you open it, do this: set a 90-minute timer, pick the category that scares you most, and complete every item in it today. The founders who launch successfully are not the ones who read more checklists — they are the ones who use them.

The checklist format works because subscribers can see progress. They check off items, feel momentum, and experience the value of your lead magnet in real time. That positive experience with your free resource directly increases trust in your paid offer — which is why checklist subscribers convert to buyers at higher rates than subscribers from generic newsletter signups.

Prompt 2: PDF Guide / eBook Outline

A PDF guide or short ebook works best when the audience needs more depth than a checklist provides — when the problem requires explanation, context, or a multi-step framework, not just a list of tasks. The key is keeping the guide focused and short: 5–15 pages of genuinely actionable content beats a 40-page document that covers a broad topic shallowly. This prompt generates a complete guide outline with a title, chapter structure, key insights per chapter, and the exact promise the guide makes on its cover.

Prompt LM-02: PDF Guide / eBook Outline
Act as a content strategist and lead magnet specialist. Create a complete PDF guide outline that solves a specific problem for the audience below. The guide should be 5–12 pages maximum — focused, fast to read, and immediately actionable.

Deliverables:
— Guide title (specific, outcome-focused, includes a number or timeframe)
— Cover page promise (1–2 sentences: what the reader will be able to do after reading this guide)
— Introduction hook (3–4 sentences: the problem, why it is harder than it should be, and the promise of this guide)
— 4–6 chapter titles with a 2–3 sentence summary of what each chapter covers and the key insight or takeaway
— 2–3 action items per chapter (specific things the reader does, not just things they learn)
— Conclusion and next step (what the reader should do immediately after finishing the guide — ideally a natural bridge to your paid offer)

Rules: Every chapter must deliver a standalone insight. A reader who only reads one chapter should still get value. No filler content, no padded introductions. Every action item must be completable in under 30 minutes.

Variables:
— Guide Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "writing cold emails that get replies," "setting up Google Analytics 4," "pricing your freelance services"]
— Target Audience: [AUDIENCE]
— Specific Problem Solved: [THE ONE PROBLEM THIS GUIDE SOLVES COMPLETELY]
— Reader's Current Situation: [WHERE THEY ARE NOW — e.g., "sending cold emails with 3% reply rates," "no analytics setup at all"]
— Reader's Desired Situation: [WHERE THEY WANT TO BE — e.g., "20%+ reply rates," "full visibility into traffic and conversions"]
— Paid Offer Bridge: [YOUR PRODUCT/SERVICE THE GUIDE LEADS TOWARD]

The structure of this prompt forces a guide that is genuinely useful rather than a long sales pitch disguised as a resource. Each chapter must deliver a standalone insight, which means subscribers gain real value even if they never buy from you. That generosity is what builds the trust that converts subscribers into buyers over time.

Prompt 3: Template / Swipe File

Templates and swipe files are the highest-value lead magnets for audiences who need to produce something — emails, proposals, social posts, contracts, pitches — and want a head start. Instead of learning how to write a proposal from scratch, the subscriber gets a done-for-you template they can fill in and send today. The value is immediately obvious: it saves hours of work and removes the blank-page problem. This prompt generates a complete template or swipe file with all sections, fill-in-the-blank fields, and usage instructions.

Prompt LM-03: Template / Swipe File
Act as a copywriter and template designer who creates done-for-you resources for professional audiences. Create a complete template or swipe file that saves the user significant time and removes the need to create this from scratch.

Deliverables:
— Template title (make it clear what the template is and who it saves time for)
— When to use this template (1 paragraph: the exact situation where this template applies)
— Full template with all sections, including:
    — [FILL-IN-THE-BLANK FIELDS] in brackets throughout
    — Section headers that explain what each part accomplishes
    — Example text in italics alongside the blank fields so the user understands what to write
— Customization notes (3–5 bullets on how to adapt the template for different situations or audiences)
— Common mistakes section (3 things most people get wrong with this type of document and how this template prevents them)

Rules: Every blank field must include an example. Do not leave the user guessing. The template should be usable in under 30 minutes of customization. If the template has optional sections, label them clearly.

Variables:
— Template Type: [TYPE — e.g., "client proposal," "cold email sequence," "Instagram caption swipe file," "freelance contract," "sales call script"]
— Industry/Niche: [NICHE]
— Target User: [WHO USES THIS TEMPLATE]
— Outcome of Using the Template: [RESULT — e.g., "win more clients," "book more sales calls," "grow Instagram following"]
— Most Time-Consuming Part This Replaces: [WHAT THEY NO LONGER HAVE TO DO FROM SCRATCH]

The reason templates convert so well as lead magnets is the explicitness of the value exchange. "Give me your email and I will give you a 6-figure client proposal template you can use today" is a much clearer trade than "subscribe to my newsletter for marketing tips." Explicit, tangible, immediately useful — that is the template formula.

Prompt 4: Cheat Sheet / Quick Reference

A cheat sheet is a condensed reference tool — the kind of resource someone keeps open in a browser tab or printed on their desk while doing the work. Unlike a guide or ebook, a cheat sheet does not teach. It references. It answers the question "what do I do next?" or "what are the exact steps?" in a format that fits on one or two pages. The conversion power comes from frequency of use: subscribers come back to your cheat sheet repeatedly, which keeps your brand top of mind and builds the familiarity that leads to purchases. This prompt generates a complete cheat sheet with a title, all reference content, and a format-ready layout.

Prompt LM-04: Cheat Sheet / Quick Reference
Act as an expert in the field below and a visual information designer. Create a one-to-two-page cheat sheet that serves as the definitive quick reference for someone actively doing this work. This is not a teaching document — it is a reference tool they keep open while working.

Deliverables:
— Title (format: "The [Topic] Cheat Sheet" or "[Audience]'s Quick Reference Guide to [Topic]")
— 4–6 sections, each covering a distinct subtopic or phase of the work
— Each section contains: a bold header, 5–8 reference items (formulas, steps, rules, shortcuts, or key metrics)
— At least one "Formula" or "Rule of Thumb" callout box per section (the single most important shortcut in that area)
— A "Common Mistakes" sidebar (5 quick-scan mistakes people make in this area, each in one line)
— A "Key Numbers to Know" section (5–8 benchmarks, thresholds, or standard values experts know by memory)

Rules: Every item must be reference-worthy. Ask: would an expert refer back to this? If not, cut it. No explanations — just the facts, formulas, steps, and benchmarks. Dense, scannable, immediately useful.

Variables:
— Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "Facebook Ads," "email deliverability," "SEO on-page optimization," "financial modeling"]
— Audience Skill Level: [LEVEL — e.g., "intermediate practitioners," "beginners who just started," "advanced users who want quick reference"]
— Primary Use Case: [WHEN THEY USE THIS — e.g., "while setting up ad campaigns," "during website audits," "while building financial models"]
— 3 Most Common Questions Practitioners Have: [QUESTIONS THIS CHEAT SHEET SHOULD ANSWER]

Example — Email Deliverability Cheat Sheet excerpt:

Key Numbers to Know:
— Spam complaint rate: keep below 0.08% (Google/Yahoo threshold)
— Open rate benchmark by industry: SaaS 22%, e-commerce 18%, media 28%
— Hard bounce threshold: above 2% triggers inbox provider scrutiny
— List hygiene interval: clean unengaged subscribers every 90 days
— Warm-up volume: start at 50 emails/day, double every 3 days
— DNS propagation window: allow 24–48 hours after DMARC/DKIM changes

Rule of Thumb — Send Frequency: Email your list at least once per week or your open rates will drop 15–30% per month of inactivity. Consistency beats volume.

The density of a well-crafted cheat sheet is its superpower. When a subscriber sees that every line of your cheat sheet is genuinely useful, their trust in your expertise multiplies. That trust is the psychological foundation for every future purchase.

Prompt 5: Quiz / Assessment Lead Magnet

Quizzes and assessments are the most engaging lead magnet format because they are inherently interactive and personalized. Instead of receiving a static document, subscribers answer questions and receive a custom result — their score, their type, their readiness level, their diagnosis. That personalization creates an experience subscribers share on social media and talk about, which turns your lead magnet into a distribution channel. This prompt generates a complete quiz framework: all questions, scoring logic, result segments, and the personalized copy for each result that naturally bridges to your paid offer.

Prompt LM-05: Quiz / Assessment Lead Magnet
Act as a quiz funnel strategist who builds lead-generating assessments for online businesses. Create a complete quiz lead magnet framework that subscribers take to receive a personalized result, with each result segment bridging naturally to the paid offer.

Deliverables:
— Quiz title (format: "What [Type/Score] Are You?" or "[Topic] Assessment: Find Out Your [Score/Level/Type]")
— Quiz hook (3–4 sentences for the landing page: why this quiz matters and what insight the result reveals)
— 8–10 questions with 3–4 answer options each. Questions should be easy to answer (no research required) and reveal the respondent's situation, behavior, or mindset.
— Scoring system (how answers map to 3 result segments — e.g., Beginner/Intermediate/Advanced or Type A/Type B/Type C)
— 3 result segments, each with:
    — Segment name and headline (e.g., "The Stuck Starter" or "The Scaling Pro")
    — 2–3 sentences describing this person's situation accurately (make them feel seen)
    — Their biggest obstacle right now
    — The specific next step recommended for this segment
    — Natural CTA to your paid offer tailored to this segment's problem

Rules: Questions must feel effortless to answer. Never ask about revenue, team size, or sensitive data up front. Frame every answer option positively — no "bad" answers. Each result segment should feel like an accurate, flattering description that makes the respondent nod and think "this is exactly me."

Variables:
— Quiz Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "email marketing readiness," "business growth stage," "content strategy maturity"]
— Target Audience: [AUDIENCE]
— What the Quiz Reveals: [INSIGHT — e.g., "your current growth bottleneck," "your marketing personality type," "your list-building score"]
— Paid Offer: [PRODUCT/SERVICE THE QUIZ LEADS TO]
— Tone: [TONE — e.g., "professional and data-driven," "playful and conversational," "warm and coaching-style"]

The most powerful aspect of a quiz lead magnet is the segmentation it creates automatically. Every subscriber who completes your quiz self-identifies their situation, which means your follow-up email sequences can be personalized from the first message. A "Beginner" subscriber gets a different nurture sequence than an "Advanced" subscriber — and that personalization dramatically increases email open rates, click rates, and conversions to your paid offer.

Prompt 6: Free Tool / Calculator Concept

Interactive tools and calculators are the most viral lead magnets because they produce a unique, personalized output that subscribers want to share. A ROI calculator that shows a business owner exactly how much they could save, or a pricing calculator that tells a freelancer what they should charge, solves a real problem in a way no static PDF can. The challenge is defining the tool clearly enough that a developer or no-code builder can build it. This prompt generates a complete tool concept: inputs, calculation logic, output format, and the lead capture mechanism.

Prompt LM-06: Free Tool / Calculator Concept
Act as a product strategist and lead magnet specialist who designs interactive tools for audience building. Create a complete concept specification for a free calculator or tool that solves a real problem for the audience below and captures emails in exchange for results.

Deliverables:
— Tool name and tagline (clear, benefit-driven, specific)
— The one question this tool answers (the core problem it solves in one sentence)
— Required inputs (all user-entered fields, with field labels, input types, and example values)
— Calculation or logic explanation (step-by-step how inputs produce the output — detailed enough for a developer)
— Output format (exactly what the user sees after entering their information — number, score, recommendation, or breakdown)
— Email gate strategy (where in the flow the email is captured — before results, after a preview, or after full results with an upgrade option)
— Results page copy (headline announcing the result, 2–3 sentences interpreting what the result means, and a CTA to the next step)
— Shareable output recommendation (how to make the result shareable on social media to drive viral growth)

Variables:
— Tool Type: [TYPE — e.g., "ROI calculator," "pricing calculator," "readiness score," "budget allocator," "audit tool"]
— Niche: [NICHE — e.g., "email marketing," "freelance consulting," "SaaS," "e-commerce"]
— The Problem Solved: [PROBLEM — e.g., "don't know what to charge," "can't justify marketing spend," "not sure if ready to hire"]
— Audience: [TARGET USER]
— Paid Offer: [PRODUCT/SERVICE THE TOOL LEADS TO]

Free tools have the highest lifetime value of any lead magnet format because they get bookmarked, shared, and re-used. A subscriber who returns to your tool monthly stays connected to your brand in a way a downloaded PDF cannot achieve. The upfront investment to build the tool (even with no-code builders like Outgrow or Typeform) pays back through years of ongoing lead generation from a single asset.

Prompt 7: Mini Email Course (5-Day)

A mini email course is the lead magnet format that best demonstrates expertise while building the deepest relationship with new subscribers. Over five days, subscribers receive one focused lesson per email — each delivering a specific insight, a concrete action to take, and a reason to open tomorrow's email. By day five, they have had five interactions with your thinking, which creates a familiarity and trust level that no single-download lead magnet can match. This prompt generates a complete 5-day email course: all five subject lines, all five lesson outlines, all five CTAs, and the series arc that leads naturally to your paid offer.

Prompt LM-07: Mini Email Course (5-Day)
Act as an email marketing strategist and course designer. Create a complete 5-day mini email course that teaches one specific skill or framework to the audience below, with each email delivering a standalone lesson and building toward a natural introduction of the paid offer on day 5.

Deliverables for each of the 5 emails:
— Subject line (curiosity-driven, specific, under 50 characters; also provide 1 A/B test variant)
— Preview text (complements the subject line, adds curiosity, under 90 characters)
— Lesson title and core concept (what today's lesson teaches in one sentence)
— 3–4 key points the email covers (bullet outline, not full copy)
— The one action item for today (specific, completable in under 30 minutes)
— Cliffhanger / teaser for tomorrow's email (1 sentence that creates anticipation)

Series structure:
— Day 1: The biggest misconception about this topic (the "unlearn" email)
— Day 2: The core framework or system (the "how it works" email)
— Day 3: The most common mistake and how to avoid it (the "warning" email)
— Day 4: A real example or case study (the "proof" email)
— Day 5: The next level — what becomes possible + introduction of paid offer (the "invitation" email)

Rules: Every email must deliver one complete, actionable lesson. No cliffhangers that withhold the core insight — deliver value first, then tease tomorrow. Day 5 CTA must feel like a natural continuation, not a sudden sales pitch.

Variables:
— Course Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "building your first email list," "writing landing page copy," "setting up your first paid ad campaign"]
— Target Audience: [AUDIENCE]
— Course Promise: [WHAT SUBSCRIBERS CAN DO AFTER ALL 5 EMAILS — e.g., "launch a lead magnet that adds 100 subscribers/week"]
— Paid Offer on Day 5: [YOUR PRODUCT/SERVICE]
— Tone: [TONE — e.g., "conversational and direct," "warm and mentoring," "data-driven and tactical"]

The email course format builds more trust than any other lead magnet precisely because it requires the subscriber to show up five days in a row. Every email they open is a small commitment that deepens their investment in the topic and in you as their guide. By day five, when you introduce your paid offer, you are not pitching a stranger — you are offering the next step to someone who has already spent a week learning from you.

Prompt 8: Resource Library / Toolkit

A resource library or toolkit bundles multiple assets under a single opt-in — and the bundled value feels dramatically higher than any single resource. "Get access to our full marketing toolkit: 12 templates, 3 checklists, 2 swipe files, and a 40-minute workshop" converts at 2–3x the rate of a single PDF because the subscriber sees a vault of value behind the gate. This prompt generates a complete resource library concept: all assets to include, how to name and position the bundle, and the landing page structure that maximizes conversions.

Prompt LM-08: Resource Library / Toolkit
Act as a lead magnet strategist specializing in high-value free offers and content vaults. Design a complete resource library or toolkit lead magnet that bundles multiple assets under a single opt-in, maximizing perceived value while staying focused on one audience and one core problem.

Deliverables:
— Library / Toolkit name (brand it as a "Kit," "Vault," "Toolkit," or "Library" — not a "free guide")
— Positioning statement (1 sentence: who this is for, what it includes, and what it helps them do)

    — Asset name and type (template, checklist, cheat sheet, video, swipe file, etc.)
    — One-sentence description of what this asset does
    — Estimated time to use (e.g., "5-minute checklist," "fill-in template takes 20 minutes")
— Bundle headline for the opt-in page (lead with the number of assets and the combined value)
— 3 bullet points for the opt-in page (each describing one asset and its specific benefit)
— Suggested "anchor" asset (the one asset that would make someone sign up even if nothing else was included)

Rules: Every asset in the bundle must be directly useful to the same target audience. No filler. If an asset would not stand alone as a lead magnet, cut it from the bundle or replace it with something better.

Variables:

The resource library is particularly powerful for established businesses that already have multiple pieces of content. If you have written three blog posts, recorded two videos, and created one checklist, a resource library prompt can help you bundle them into a high-value free offer in an afternoon — without creating anything new. The positioning of the bundle, not just its contents, is what drives conversions.

Prompt 9: Lead Magnet Landing Page Copy

The most common lead magnet failure is not the asset itself — it is the landing page. Marketers spend hours creating a brilliant checklist, then put it behind a landing page that says "Subscribe to get our free checklist." That generic positioning converts at under 2%. A landing page built on a proven direct-response framework — with a headline that names the outcome, bullet points that create desire, and a form that reduces friction — converts at 20–40%. This prompt generates complete opt-in landing page copy using the AIDA framework, optimized for the specific lead magnet you created.

Prompt LM-09: Lead Magnet Landing Page Copy
Act as a direct-response copywriter who specializes in high-converting opt-in landing pages. Write complete landing page copy for the lead magnet below using the AIDA framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action). This page has one goal: get the visitor to enter their email address.

Deliverables:
— Hero headline (outcome-driven, specific, creates immediate desire — not a clever tagline)
— Hero subheadline (who this is for + what they will be able to do + timeframe)
— Lead paragraph (2–3 sentences: agitate the problem this lead magnet solves)
— "What you get" section: 5–7 bullet points, each starting with a power verb, each describing a specific benefit (not a feature)
— Social proof element (even if small: testimonial placeholder, number of downloads, or credibility statement)
— Objection crusher (1–2 sentences addressing the #1 reason someone would not sign up)
— Form headline (the text directly above the email field — this is the last push before they decide)
— CTA button copy (not "Submit" or "Subscribe" — benefit-focused, first person, specific)
— Privacy reassurance (1 short line below the form that removes the spam fear)
— A/B test variants: provide 2 alternative hero headlines to test against the primary

Rules: Never use "subscribe" or "sign up" as the CTA. Use first-person benefit language: "Send Me the Checklist," "Yes, I Want the Free Guide," "Get My [Result] Now." The headline must name the outcome, not the format.

Variables:
— Lead Magnet Name: [LEAD MAGNET TITLE]
— Lead Magnet Type: [FORMAT — checklist, guide, template, quiz, etc.]
— Target Audience: [AUDIENCE]
— Core Promise: [THE ONE OUTCOME THE LEAD MAGNET DELIVERS]
— Biggest Pain Point: [THE PROBLEM IT SOLVES]
— Social Proof Available: [TESTIMONIALS, DOWNLOAD COUNT, OR CREDIBILITY SIGNALS]
— Top Objection: [WHY SOMEONE MIGHT NOT SIGN UP — e.g., "I already get too many emails," "I don't have time to read it," "Is this actually useful?"]

Example output — key elements:

Hero Headline: Get 100 New Email Subscribers This Month (Without Running a Single Ad)
Hero Subheadline: For coaches and creators who want to build their list with organic traffic — using a free checklist that takes 30 minutes to implement.

What you get bullets:
— Discover the 3-sentence opt-in headline formula that converts at 28% (page 1)
— Copy the exact 5-step content upgrade strategy that drives subscribers from every blog post
— Use our 7-point landing page audit checklist to fix the leaks in your current opt-in page today

CTA Button: Send Me the Free List-Building Checklist
Privacy line: No spam. Unsubscribe anytime. Your email is safe.

The landing page prompt is the one most marketers skip — and the one with the highest leverage. A 10% improvement in landing page conversion rate doubles your subscriber growth at zero additional traffic cost. The difference between a 2% conversion page and a 20% conversion page is almost entirely the copy, and the copy structure in this prompt is built on patterns that have been tested across thousands of opt-in pages.

Prompt 10: Lead Magnet Promotion Strategy

Creating the lead magnet is only half the work. A brilliant checklist sitting behind a landing page that gets 50 visitors a month adds 5–10 subscribers per month. The same checklist with a systematic promotion strategy running across paid, organic, and partnership channels adds 500–1,000 subscribers per month. This prompt generates a complete, multi-channel promotion strategy for your specific lead magnet: paid social ads, organic content distribution, content upgrade placements, partnership outreach, and a 30-day launch plan.

Act as a growth marketing strategist who specializes in lead magnet distribution and email list building. Create a complete 30-day promotion strategy for the lead magnet below, covering paid, organic, and partnership channels.

Deliverables:

1. Paid social strategy (Facebook/Instagram):

4. Partnership and referral strategy:
— 3 types of partnership accounts or creators to approach for cross-promotion

5. 30-day launch calendar:
— Week 1: Launch activities (owned channels, email blast to existing list)
— Week 2: Paid amplification begins
— Week 3: Partnership outreach and content upgrades go live

Variables:
— Lead Magnet: [LEAD MAGNET TITLE AND FORMAT]

5 Tips for Creating Lead Magnets That Convert

The 10 prompts above give you the raw material for every major lead magnet format. These five principles ensure the assets you create actually convert visitors into subscribers.

1. Name the Outcome, Not the Format

The fastest way to kill your lead magnet conversion rate is to name it by its format: "Free Checklist," "PDF Download," "Free Guide." These titles communicate nothing about why someone should give you their email address. Instead, name the outcome: "The 23-Step Product Launch Checklist That Ships Without Last-Minute Chaos," or "The Freelance Pricing Guide: How to Charge What You Are Worth Without Losing Clients." The format is implicit in the title once you name the outcome clearly. When subscribers can see the result in the title, they convert at 3–5x the rate of a format-named lead magnet.

2. Solve One Problem Completely

The most common lead magnet mistake is trying to cover too much. A "Complete Guide to Marketing Your Business" is too broad to be immediately useful and too ambitious to feel achievable. A "7-Step Framework for Writing Instagram Captions That Drive Profile Clicks" solves one specific problem completely. The narrower and more specific your lead magnet, the higher your conversion rate — because the subscriber can immediately see themselves using it and knows exactly what problem it will solve. Use the AI prompts above to generate tightly scoped lead magnets, then resist the urge to expand the scope.

3. Make Implementation Faster Than Learning

A lead magnet that teaches is less valuable than a lead magnet that does. When someone downloads a template, they do not have to learn anything — they just fill it in. When someone uses a checklist, they do not have to figure out the steps — they just check them off. When someone completes a quiz, they do not have to diagnose their situation — they just answer questions and receive the diagnosis. The best lead magnets reduce the subscriber's cognitive effort to near zero. Design for implementation, not education.

4. Align Your Lead Magnet with Your Paid Offer

A lead magnet that attracts the wrong audience is worse than no lead magnet at all. An email list full of subscribers who downloaded a free recipe book but have no interest in your paid coaching program is a list with zero monetization potential. Every lead magnet you create should attract subscribers who are likely buyers of your paid offer — people with the same problem your product solves, at an earlier stage of awareness. A "Facebook Ads Audit Checklist" attracts business owners running Facebook ads — the exact audience for a Facebook ads course or agency. Design your lead magnet to pre-qualify your list, not just grow it.

5. Test Two Formats Before Scaling

Before investing heavily in promotion, test two lead magnet formats with the same audience and topic. Create both a checklist and a quiz, drive 200 visitors to each opt-in page from the same source, and measure conversion rates. The winning format gets the promotion budget. Most marketers assume they know which format their audience prefers — but the data consistently surprises. A quiz in a market full of PDF guides often doubles conversion rates simply by being different. Use the AI prompts above to build both assets quickly, test them side by side, and let your audience tell you what they want.

What makes a lead magnet high-converting? +

Which lead magnet format gets the most email signups? +

PDF guides can run 5–15 pages if each page delivers specific, actionable content.
