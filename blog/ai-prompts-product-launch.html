10 AI Prompts for Product Launch Campaigns That Sell Out

ContentKit AI•13 min read

AI prompts for product launch campaigns generate every piece of copy you need to sell out — from the first teaser email four weeks out to the post-launch follow-up sequence — across email, social media, paid ads, landing pages, and press, all built on direct-response frameworks that create anticipation, urgency, and conversion. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts that cover the full launch arc, each engineered for the specific job it needs to do at that stage of the buyer journey.

Most product launches underperform not because the product is weak, but because the copy is written too late, spread too thin, and lacks the structural consistency that turns a launch moment into a launch movement. Why Product Launches Need a Full-Funnel Copy System

A product launch is not a single email. It is a sequence of coordinated messages across multiple channels, each performing a specific function in a buyer's journey from unaware to excited to converted. The failure mode for most launches is treating each piece of copy as an isolated asset — writing the landing page with no connection to the email sequence, creating social posts that do not align with the ad creative, sending a launch email that does not match the landing page headline.

When copy is inconsistent across channels, the cumulative effect of the launch dissipates. A buyer sees your Instagram post, clicks your ad, lands on your page, and gets a different message each time. The consistency is broken. Trust degrades. The sale does not happen.

The Three Stages of a High-Converting Launch

Every successful product launch operates in three stages, and each stage requires different copy with a different emotional objective.

Pre-launch (weeks 1–3): The objective is anticipation. Copy at this stage builds curiosity, establishes the problem your product solves, and makes the audience feel that something significant is coming. Teaser emails, countdown posts, and waitlist landing pages all belong here. The reader should finish pre-launch content feeling like they need to know what you are about to release.

Launch day (week 4, day 1–3): The objective is conversion. Every piece of copy now drives a single action: buy. The landing page, launch email, launch-day social posts, and ads all converge on the purchase decision. Urgency is real (limited inventory, early bird price, founding member access). The copy must be specific enough to close and simple enough to convert without friction.

Post-launch (days 4–14): The objective is recovery. Not everyone who wanted to buy converted on launch day. They got busy, they hesitated, they needed to think about it. Post-launch emails address objections, share early buyer testimonials, and give fence-sitters a final reason to act. This stage often captures 20–40% of total launch revenue from buyers who did not convert in the first 48 hours.

The 10 prompts below map directly to this three-stage framework, giving you purpose-built copy for every position in the launch arc.

10 AI Prompts for Product Launch Campaigns

Prompt 1: Pre-Launch Teaser Email Sequence

The pre-launch teaser sequence is the most underestimated asset in any product launch. Brands that build anticipation before their launch day consistently outperform brands that announce and sell in a single email. A three-email teaser sequence, sent across the two weeks before launch, warms your existing audience from passive subscribers to actively interested buyers who are waiting for the buy button to go live. This prompt generates a complete three-email teaser sequence that builds curiosity progressively without revealing the full product until the launch announcement.

Prompt PL-01: Pre-Launch Teaser Email Sequence
Act as a direct-response email copywriter specializing in product launch campaigns. Write a three-email pre-launch teaser sequence that builds curiosity and anticipation for a product launch without revealing the full product until the launch announcement.

Email 1 — "The Problem" (send 14 days before launch):
— Subject line: Opens with a question or provocative statement about the core problem
— Body: 200–250 words. Describe the problem your audience knows intimately. Make it vivid. Do not mention the product. Close with a hint that something is coming.
— P.S. line: Adds intrigue and a reason to watch their inbox

Email 2 — "The Teaser" (send 7 days before launch):
— Subject line: Creates curiosity with a partial reveal
— Body: 150–200 words. Reference the problem from email 1. Hint at the solution without naming it. Share one specific detail about the product that creates desire (a feature, a stat, a benefit). Close with a countdown or "mark your calendar" CTA.
— P.S. line: Drives email opens for the launch announcement

Email 3 — "Tomorrow" (send 24 hours before launch):
— Subject line: Specific and urgent. References the launch date.
— Body: 100–150 words. Build final anticipation. If there is an early bird offer, mention it (but not the price). Give the exact launch time. Create a sense that something they have been waiting for is finally here.

Write all subject line variants (A/B test pair for each email).

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Type: [PHYSICAL PRODUCT / DIGITAL PRODUCT / SAAS / COURSE / etc.]
— Core Problem Solved: [THE PROBLEM YOUR PRODUCT SOLVES — be specific]
— Target Audience: [WHO THESE EMAILS ARE GOING TO]
— One Teaser Detail: [ONE SPECIFIC THING THAT CREATES DESIRE WITHOUT FULL REVEAL]
— Launch Date & Time: [DATE AND TIME, INCLUDING TIMEZONE]
— Early Bird Offer: [YES/NO — IF YES, MENTION EXISTENCE BUT NOT DETAILS YET]
— Brand Voice: [E.G., "professional and confident," "casual and conversational," "urgent and direct"]

Email 1 Subject Lines (A/B pair):
A: "Why does this keep happening?" | B: "The real reason [PROBLEM] never gets fixed"

Email 2 Subject Lines (A/B pair):
A: "We built the thing you asked for" | B: "One week from today, this changes"

Email 3 Subject Lines (A/B pair):
A: "It goes live tomorrow at 9am ET" | B: "Tomorrow. Your inbox. 9am ET."

Email 3 Body excerpt:
Tomorrow at 9am Eastern, [Product Name] goes live.
If you have been following along for the past two weeks, you already know what this is about. If you are just seeing this now, check your inbox for the last two emails.
We have been working on this for [X months]. The waitlist gets first access and a founding member price that we will never offer again. Set your alarm. This one matters.

The three-email arc works because it mirrors the emotional journey of a launch buyer: awareness of the problem, excitement about a solution, and urgency about the opportunity. By the time the launch email arrives, the reader is primed — not surprised. That priming is what separates a launch that converts at 3% from one that converts at 12%.

Prompt 2: Launch Day Announcement (Email + Social)

The launch day announcement is the single highest-stakes piece of copy in the entire campaign. Every pre-launch teaser pointed toward this moment. The announcement email must deliver on the anticipation the teaser sequence created, communicate the product's value clearly and quickly, and drive the reader to the landing page with enough momentum that they click immediately. This prompt generates the launch announcement email and a complementary social media post for simultaneous distribution on launch day.

Prompt PL-02: Launch Day Announcement (Email + Social)
Act as a product launch copywriter specializing in launch day campaigns. Write the launch day announcement email and a corresponding social media announcement post that will be published simultaneously.

Launch Announcement Email:
— Subject line: Direct, specific, and exciting. The reader knows this is the launch they have been waiting for.
— Preview text: Reinforces the subject with the single biggest benefit or hook.
— Opening line: The most compelling sentence you can write. No warm-up, no preamble. Start with the most exciting thing about this product.
— Body (300–400 words): (1) Lead with the transformation — what the buyer's life looks like after using this product. (2) Cover the 3 most important features or benefits with specificity. (3) Address the price and justify the value. (4) Include any launch-specific urgency: early bird pricing, limited inventory, founding member access. (5) Single clear CTA button copy.
— P.S. line: Reinforces the most important urgency element.

Social Media Announcement Post (works for Instagram caption and Facebook post):
— Hook (first line): Must stop the scroll. Works without context for cold audiences.
— Body (150–200 words): Announces the launch, communicates the core benefit, includes social proof if available, and drives to the link in bio or landing page.
— Hashtags: 5–8 relevant hashtags
— CTA: Clear instruction for next step

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Core Transformation: [WHAT THE BUYER CAN DO / BECOME / ACHIEVE AFTER PURCHASE]
— Top 3 Features/Benefits: [LIST THEM]
— Price: [LAUNCH PRICE AND REGULAR PRICE IF DIFFERENT]
— Urgency Element: [EARLY BIRD DEADLINE / INVENTORY LIMIT / FOUNDING MEMBER WINDOW]
— Social Proof: [BETA USER COUNT, WAITLIST SIZE, TESTIMONIAL, REVIEW STAT — IF AVAILABLE]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]
— Brand Voice: [TONE]

The launch email opening line is the most important sentence in the campaign. If the opening line does not earn the next sentence, the reader closes the email and the launch fails for that subscriber. The prompt instructs the AI to lead with the transformation — not a description of the product, not a "we are excited to announce," but the specific outcome the buyer gets. That is what earns the read.

Prompt 3: Product Launch Landing Page Copy

Your landing page is the destination for every traffic source in the launch: emails, ads, social posts, and press coverage all funnel here. If the page does not convert, the entire campaign underperforms regardless of how well every other piece of copy performed. A launch landing page must do five things: communicate the core transformation in the headline, establish credibility above the fold, convert the reader with a compelling offer, handle objections in the FAQ and body sections, and create urgency at the bottom. This prompt generates a complete landing page using the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework with all five sections.

Prompt PL-03: Product Launch Landing Page Copy
Act as a conversion copywriter specializing in product launch landing pages. Write a complete landing page for a product launch using the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework. This page will receive traffic from email, paid ads, and social media, so the copy must work for both warm audiences (email subscribers) and cold audiences (ad traffic).

Structure:
— Hero Section: Headline (outcome-focused, not product-focused, under 12 words), subheadline (1–2 sentences expanding on the headline promise), and above-the-fold CTA button copy
— Problem Section (150–200 words): Describe the painful situation your target customer is in before they have your product. Make it specific enough that the right reader says "that is exactly me."
— Agitation Section (100–150 words): Amplify the consequences of the problem remaining unsolved. What does life look like if they do not fix this? What does it cost them — in time, money, opportunity, stress?
— Solution Section (200–250 words): Introduce your product as the solution. Cover 3–5 specific features with their corresponding benefits. Use bullet points. Each bullet = "[Feature] so you can [benefit]."
— Proof Section: Placeholder copy for testimonials or beta user stats (tell me what social proof elements to request from beta users or early customers)
— Offer Section (100–150 words): State the price, what is included, any bonuses, and the launch-specific incentive (early bird, founding member, limited quantity)
— FAQ Section: Write 4 objection-handling FAQ answers addressing the most common reasons people do not buy (price, timing, trust, and "is this right for me?")
— Final CTA Section: Urgency-driven closing statement (50–75 words) and CTA button copy

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Type: [PHYSICAL / DIGITAL / SAAS / COURSE]
— Target Audience: [DESCRIBE YOUR IDEAL BUYER IN DETAIL]
— Core Problem: [THE PAINFUL PROBLEM THIS PRODUCT SOLVES]
— Core Transformation: [WHAT THE BUYER ACHIEVES AFTER USING IT]
— Top 5 Features/Benefits: [LIST THEM WITH BOTH THE FEATURE AND ITS BENEFIT]
— Price: [PRICE AND WHAT IS INCLUDED]
— Bonuses: [ANY LAUNCH BONUSES OR EXTRAS]
— Urgency: [EARLY BIRD DEADLINE / INVENTORY COUNT / FOUNDING MEMBER WINDOW]
— Guarantee: [MONEY-BACK GUARANTEE OR RISK REVERSAL OFFER]

The hero headline is the single highest-leverage element on the page. A headline that states the product (“Introducing [Product Name]”) will always underperform a headline that states the outcome (“Launch Your Product in Half the Time”). The prompt enforces outcome-focused headlines by explicitly instructing the AI to write about what the buyer gets, not about the product itself. That single constraint typically increases above-the-fold conversion by 20–40%.

Prompt 4: Launch Day Social Media Posts (Multi-Platform)

A product launch that only activates your email list leaves organic reach on the table. Social media posts published on launch day extend your reach to followers who may not be on your list, generate shares that bring in new audiences, and create social proof by making the launch feel like an event. The challenge is that each platform has different norms: what works as an Instagram caption reads wrong on LinkedIn, and what works on LinkedIn feels stiff on X (Twitter). This prompt generates platform-native launch day posts for Instagram, X, LinkedIn, and Facebook — each optimized for the expectations of that platform's audience.

Prompt PL-04: Launch Day Social Media Posts (Multi-Platform)
Act as a social media copywriter specializing in product launch campaigns. Write launch day posts for four platforms, each optimized for that platform's format, tone, and audience behavior.

Instagram (caption + hashtags):
— Hook (first line): Stop-the-scroll opener that works without context for non-followers
— Body (150–200 words): Announce the launch, communicate the core benefit, include one specific result or stat, end with CTA to link in bio
— Hashtags: 8–10 relevant hashtags

X / Twitter (thread, 5 tweets):
— Tweet 1: The announcement hook (max 280 characters — punchy, shareable)
— Tweet 2: The problem this solves (with specificity)
— Tweet 3: The core solution and top benefit
— Tweet 4: Social proof or backstory (how long this took to build, beta users, etc.)
— Tweet 5: CTA with link and urgency

LinkedIn (long-form post):
— Hook (first 2 lines, visible before "see more" cutoff): Professional but compelling
— Body (250–350 words): Personal story of why this product was built, what problem it solves for the professional audience, specific outcomes, and a clear CTA
— No hashtags or minimal (2–3 max)

Facebook (post):
— Hook (first 2 lines): Conversational, community-oriented opener
— Body (100–150 words): Friendly announcement with the key benefit, a reason to share, and a direct link

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Core Benefit: [PRIMARY OUTCOME THE BUYER GETS]
— Launch Backstory: [HOW LONG YOU BUILT THIS, WHY YOU BUILT IT, OR THE PROBLEM THAT INSPIRED IT]
— Social Proof: [BETA USERS, WAITLIST SIZE, EARLY RESULTS, OR TESTIMONIAL SNIPPET]
— Price / Offer: [LAUNCH PRICE OR EARLY BIRD OFFER]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]
— Target Audience: [WHO THIS IS FOR]
— Urgency: [DEADLINE OR SCARCITY ELEMENT]

The X thread format is particularly powerful for product launches because it allows you to build a narrative across five posts that earns shares at each step. A well-constructed launch thread — problem, solution, proof, backstory, CTA — can reach audiences far beyond your follower count when any single tweet gets engagement. This prompt builds that narrative arc into the thread structure automatically.

Prompt 5: Product Launch Facebook/Instagram Ad

Paid social ads during a product launch serve a specific function: they bring in cold-audience buyers who have never heard of you, extending the reach of the launch beyond your existing community. Unlike email, which converts warm audiences, Facebook and Instagram ads must do the heavier lift of introducing your product and driving a purchase decision from someone who sees your ad for the first time. This prompt generates launch ads using the hook-problem-solution-proof-CTA framework, with variations for both image-based and video ads.

Prompt PL-05: Product Launch Facebook/Instagram Ad
Act as a paid social media copywriter specializing in product launch campaigns. Write Facebook and Instagram ad copy for a product launch targeting cold audiences who have never heard of this brand. The ad must introduce the product, establish trust, and drive a purchase decision from someone who sees this ad for the first time.

Write two ad variants:

Variant A — Short-Form (image or carousel ad):
— Primary text (150 words max): Hook + problem + solution + proof + CTA. Written for someone scrolling fast who needs to understand the entire value proposition in under 20 seconds.
— Headline (40 characters max): Outcome-focused
— Description (30 characters max): Supporting detail or urgency
— CTA button: Recommend the best option (Shop Now, Learn More, Sign Up, Get Offer)

Variant B — Long-Form (video ad script OR long-copy image ad):
— Primary text (400–500 words): Longer narrative that starts with a relatable problem, builds the case for the solution, establishes credibility with specifics, handles the top objection, presents the offer with urgency, and closes with a risk-reversing guarantee.
— Headline and CTA (same structure as Variant A)

Rules: Both variants must work without the image or video — the text alone must be compelling. Do not open with "Introducing" or the product name. Open with the reader's problem or a pattern-interrupt statement. Include at least one specific number in both variants.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Target Audience: [DEMOGRAPHICS + PSYCHOGRAPHICS + INTERESTS]
— Core Problem: [THE PAINFUL SITUATION THE AD OPENS WITH]
— Core Solution: [WHAT THE PRODUCT DOES]
— Proof Point: [STAT, TESTIMONIAL SNIPPET, OR BETA RESULT]
— Price & Offer: [LAUNCH PRICE, EARLY BIRD DISCOUNT, OR FOUNDING MEMBER PRICE]
— Guarantee: [RISK REVERSAL — MONEY-BACK PERIOD]
— Top Objection: [THE #1 REASON YOUR TARGET CUSTOMER WOULD NOT BUY]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]

The rule about not opening with "Introducing" or the product name is the most important constraint in this prompt. Facebook and Instagram users are scrolling, not searching. Your ad is an interruption. If the first thing they read is a product name they do not recognize, they scroll past. If the first thing they read is a description of their own frustration, they stop. Open with the reader's world, not your product's name.

Prompt 6: Launch Press Release

A press release serves two functions in a product launch: it gives journalists and bloggers a formatted, factual document they can use to write about your launch, and it creates a permanent indexed piece of content that search engines crawl for the product name and category keywords. Most startup press releases fail because they bury the news in corporate language and take three paragraphs to get to the point. Journalists receive hundreds of press releases daily. If your lead paragraph does not contain the who, what, when, and why in two sentences, the release goes unread. This prompt generates a press release following the AP Style inverted pyramid format used by every major publication.

Prompt PL-06: Launch Press Release
Act as a PR copywriter with experience writing press releases for product launches covered by TechCrunch, Forbes, and industry publications. Write a product launch press release following AP Style and the inverted pyramid format (most important information first).

Structure:
— Headline: Newsy, specific, and factual. Includes the company name, product name, and the most significant benefit or milestone. Under 100 characters.
— Subheadline: One sentence that expands the headline with a specific detail or stat.
— Dateline: [CITY, STATE] — [DATE] —
— Lead Paragraph (50–75 words): The five Ws. Who launched what, when, for whom, and why it matters. A journalist should be able to run this paragraph alone as a news item.
— Second Paragraph (75–100 words): Product details — what it does, how it works, what problem it solves, and who it is for.
— Quote (founder or CEO, 40–60 words): Personal, specific, and quotable. Not corporate boilerplate. Should sound like a real person said it.
— Third Paragraph (50–75 words): Market context — why this matters now, the size of the problem, or the trend this product addresses.
— Second Quote (customer, beta user, or advisor, 30–50 words): Social proof from a real person who has used the product.
— Availability & Pricing Paragraph: Where to buy, launch price, and any special launch offer.
— Boilerplate (50–75 words): Standard "About [Company]" paragraph for the bottom of every press release.
— Contact Information: PR contact name, email, and phone number.
— End marker: ###

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Launch Date: [DATE]
— City & State: [HEADQUARTERS LOCATION]
— Founder/CEO Name & Title: [NAME, TITLE]
— Core Problem & Solution: [WHAT THE PRODUCT DOES AND FOR WHOM]
— Market Context: [RELEVANT INDUSTRY STAT OR TREND]
— Beta User / Customer Quote Source: [NAME, TITLE, COMPANY]
— Pricing: [LAUNCH PRICE AND WHERE TO BUY]
— PR Contact: [NAME, EMAIL, PHONE]

The founder quote is the most commonly wasted element in a press release. Quotes like “We are thrilled to announce” and “This product represents our commitment to innovation” are filler that journalists delete immediately. A good press release quote sounds like something a real person said on a podcast — specific, opinionated, and quotable. The prompt instructs the AI to write it that way, and the result is a quote that actually gets used when journalists excerpt the release.

Prompt 7: Early Bird / Limited Offer Copy

Early bird and founding member offers are the single most effective conversion mechanism in a product launch. A time-limited price or access window creates a real, legitimate reason for fence-sitters to act now instead of later — and the buyers who act now are often your most engaged, most valuable long-term customers. But most early bird copy undersells the urgency or creates artificial scarcity that feels manipulative. This prompt generates early bird offer copy built around genuine constraints — real deadlines, real inventory limits, and real price increases — for use in emails, landing page sections, and social posts.

Prompt PL-07: Early Bird / Limited Offer Copy
Act as a launch offer copywriter specializing in early bird and founding member campaigns. Write copy for a limited launch offer that creates genuine urgency without feeling manipulative. The copy must communicate that the scarcity is real and the deadline is firm.

Write four pieces of copy:

1. Early Bird Email (send at launch, 200–250 words):
— Subject line: Communicates the offer and the deadline in the subject itself
— Body: States the founding member price, what is included, what the price goes to after the window closes, and when the window closes (exact date and time). Includes one urgency element beyond price: exclusive access, bonus, or founding member status.
— CTA: Direct and specific

2. Early Bird Deadline Reminder Email (send 24 hours before deadline, 150–200 words):
— Subject line: "[X] hours left" or "[date] is the last day"
— Body: Recap the offer, state exactly how many hours remain, remind them what they get at this price vs. the future price, close with a single CTA.

3. Landing Page Early Bird Section (75–100 words):
— A callout box or banner section that communicates the founding member offer, the deadline, and the price change. Should stand alone as a module on the page.

4. Social Media Countdown Post (50–75 words):
— A short post usable on Instagram Stories, X, or Facebook that announces the deadline with urgency and a direct link.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Founding Member / Early Bird Price: [PRICE]
— Regular Price After Launch Window: [PRICE]
— Deadline: [EXACT DATE AND TIME WITH TIMEZONE]
— What Is Included at This Price: [LIST EVERYTHING IN THE OFFER]
— Exclusive Early Buyer Bonus: [BONUS SPECIFIC TO EARLY BUYERS — E.G., PRIVATE GROUP ACCESS, EXTRA MODULE, PERSONAL ONBOARDING CALL]
— Number of Founding Member Spots: [NUMBER OR "UNLIMITED" UNTIL DEADLINE]

Early Bird Email Subject (A/B pair):
A: "Founding member pricing closes Friday at midnight" | B: "Your founding member spot: $197 until Friday, then $297"

Deadline Reminder Subject (A/B pair):
A: "Last 24 hours at $197" | B: "Tonight at midnight, the price goes up"

Landing Page Early Bird Section:
Founding Member Offer — Closes [DATE] at [TIME]
Join today for $[PRICE] — the only time this will ever be offered at this price. After [DATE], [Product Name] will be $[REGULAR PRICE]. No exceptions. Founding members also receive [EXCLUSIVE BONUS]. [CTA BUTTON: Claim Your Founding Member Spot]

The most important principle in early bird copy is specificity about the deadline. "Price goes up soon" is vague and unconvincing. "Price goes up Friday March 7 at 11:59pm Eastern" is specific, credible, and motivating. The prompt enforces this by requiring the exact date, time, and timezone in every piece of early bird copy. Vague urgency is ineffective urgency.

Prompt 8: Product Launch Video Script

A launch video — whether a 60-second social video, a two-minute product demo, or a five-minute founder story — dramatically increases conversion rates on landing pages and social posts. Pages with video convert 34% higher on average, and launch videos shared on social reach audiences far beyond what static posts achieve. But most founders either skip the video (too time-consuming) or create a bland feature demo that fails to create emotional connection. This prompt generates a complete launch video script using the PAS framework, structured for both professional production and self-recorded founder-style video.

Prompt PL-08: Product Launch Video Script
Act as a video scriptwriter specializing in product launch videos. Write a 90-second to 2-minute product launch video script that works for both professional production (filmed by a videographer) and self-recorded founder-style video (recorded on a phone or webcam). The script must work without graphics — every claim must be communicated through spoken words and visuals described in stage directions.

Structure (each section timed):
— Hook (0:00–0:10): 10-second opening that identifies the audience and their frustration by name. No product mention yet. Just a description of their world that makes them say "that is me."
— Problem (0:10–0:30): Expand the problem with two specific examples or consequences. Acknowledge what they have already tried that has not worked.
— Solution Reveal (0:30–0:50): Introduce the product by name. State what it does in one sentence. Transition: "That is why we built [Product Name]."
— Proof (0:50–1:10): Two specific results — either from beta users or the founder's own experience. Use numbers where possible.
— Offer (1:10–1:30): State the price and the founding member offer. Make the value stack clear: "For $[X], you get [A], [B], and [C]."
— CTA (1:30–2:00): Direct instruction for next step. State the URL or action. Reinforce the urgency. End on energy — not a fade-out.

For each section, provide:
— Spoken script (word-for-word)
— Stage direction (what the camera shows or what the speaker does)

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Speaker: [FOUNDER NAME OR "A PRODUCT SPOKESPERSON"]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS WATCHING]
— Core Problem: [THE SPECIFIC FRUSTRATION TO OPEN WITH]
— Things They Have Already Tried: [2–3 ALTERNATIVES OR WORKAROUNDS THEY HAVE USED]
— What The Product Does: [ONE SENTENCE DESCRIPTION]
— Proof Points: [BETA RESULTS, USER COUNT, FOUNDER STORY STAT]
— Price & Offer: [FOUNDING MEMBER PRICE AND WHAT IS INCLUDED]
— CTA URL: [LANDING PAGE URL]

The 10-second hook is the most important moment in a launch video, and it has nothing to do with your product. If you spend the first ten seconds introducing yourself or explaining what you are about to show, you lose 40% of viewers before the product mention. The prompt starts with the audience's frustration — a description of their world that creates instant recognition — which earns the next 90 seconds of attention.

Prompt 9: Post-Launch Follow-Up Email

The buyers who did not purchase on launch day are not lost. They saw the launch, considered it, and hesitated for a specific reason — price, timing, uncertainty about fit, or they simply got distracted and forgot to come back. A post-launch follow-up sequence, sent 3–7 days after the launch announcement, captures this segment with copy that addresses common objections, shares social proof from early buyers, and presents a final opportunity to purchase before the founding member price expires. This prompt generates a three-email post-launch follow-up sequence that typically recovers 20–40% of additional launch revenue.

Prompt PL-09: Post-Launch Follow-Up Email Sequence
Act as an email copywriter specializing in post-launch follow-up sequences. Write a three-email follow-up sequence for subscribers who opened the launch announcement but did not purchase. Each email addresses a different objection and presents a different reason to buy now.

Email 1 — "Social Proof" (send 3 days after launch):
— Subject line: References early customer results or reactions
— Body (200–250 words): Share what early buyers are saying. Use 2–3 specific testimonial quotes or a stat about early results. Remind them what is included. Reiterate the offer deadline if still active.

Email 2 — "Objection Handler" (send 5 days after launch):
— Subject line: Opens with the most common objection as a question
— Body (200–300 words): Address the three most common reasons people did not buy: (a) "Is this right for me?" — get specific about who this IS for and who it is NOT for; (b) "Is it worth the price?" — reframe the cost as an investment with a specific ROI or time-saving calculation; (c) "I need to think about it" — share the deadline and what happens to the price after.

Email 3 — "Last Chance" (send 7 days after launch or 24 hours before offer expires):
— Subject line: States the deadline explicitly. Short and direct.
— Body (150–200 words): Final urgency email. Summarize the offer in three bullet points. State the exact deadline one more time. Give a single CTA. No lengthy explanation — they have seen the previous emails. This is the reminder.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts to Execute a Sell-Out Launch

The prompts above give you every piece of launch copy. These five execution principles ensure that copy performs at its highest possible level when deployed in a real campaign.

1. Start Generating Copy Four Weeks Before Launch, Not Four Days

The most common launch failure is a compressed content timeline. Founders spend months building the product and four days writing the launch copy. With AI prompts, generating all 10 assets takes a single working day. The reason to start four weeks early is not writing time — it is sequencing time. Your pre-launch emails need to be scheduled, your ad audiences need time to build, and your press release needs to go out five to seven days before launch to give journalists time to publish. Generate everything in week one. Deploy it on schedule across the following three weeks.

2. Use the Same Core Messaging Across Every Channel

Consistency of messaging across email, social, ads, and the landing page is what turns a launch campaign into a coherent story. Use the same headline from your landing page hero in your launch email subject. Use the same three core benefits across every platform. Use the same urgency language in every deadline-related touchpoint. When a buyer sees your Instagram post, then your email, then your ad, and all three communicate the same thing differently, the message compounds. When each channel says something different, the message dilutes. The prompts in this article all include overlapping variable slots precisely to enable this consistency.

3. Collect Social Proof Before You Need It

The post-launch emails, product page, and follow-up sequence all depend on testimonials and early results. The time to collect that social proof is during beta testing or the week before launch — not after the launch when you need it immediately. Ask beta users three specific questions: What was your situation before using this? What happened after you used it? Who would you recommend it to? Those three answers form the foundation of every testimonial in the launch campaign. Use the AI prompts to draft the outreach message to beta users asking for feedback in this exact format.

4. Build Your Paid Ad Audiences Before the Launch Date

Facebook and Instagram ads perform significantly better when the targeting algorithms have had time to learn. If you activate cold-audience ads on launch day with no prior data, you spend the first 48 hours in the learning phase while your launch window is burning. The solution is to run low-budget awareness ads in week two and three, building a pixel audience, a video view audience, and an email list custom audience before the launch. Then when launch day arrives, your retargeting audiences are warm, your lookalike audiences have data to optimize against, and your cold-audience ads launch against an already-formed custom audience. This approach typically reduces your cost per purchase by 30–50% in the launch window.

5. Send the Post-Launch Emails Even If You Think the Window Is Closed

Most founders skip the post-launch follow-up sequence because they feel like the launch is "over" and they do not want to bother their list. This is the most expensive mistake in a product launch. The buyers who did not purchase in the first 48 hours are the second-most-qualified segment in your entire audience — they saw the launch, considered it, and made a decision to wait. That decision was almost always based on a solvable objection: price, timing, or uncertainty about fit. The three-email post-launch sequence in Prompt 9 is specifically engineered to solve those three objections. Send all three emails. The revenue recovery from this sequence alone often equals 25–40% of total launch revenue.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Product Launch

How far in advance should I start AI-generating product launch content? +
Four weeks before launch is the minimum runway for a coordinated campaign. Week one covers pre-launch teaser content and waitlist-building emails. Week two covers landing page copy and social media assets. Week three covers ad creative and the press release. Launch week covers launch-day emails, social posts, and the early bird offer sequence. Using AI prompts, you can collapse a four-week content calendar into two to three days of actual writing time — but the scheduling and sequencing still need the full four-week window to build audience anticipation and algorithmic momentum before the launch date.
Can AI prompts replace a copywriter for a product launch? +
AI prompts replace the drafting and structural work that consumes most of a copywriter's time — generating email sequences, ad copy, landing page sections, and social posts in minutes instead of days. What they do not replace is strategic positioning, brand voice refinement, and the judgment calls that require deep knowledge of your specific customer. The most effective approach is to use AI prompts to generate first drafts across every launch asset, then apply human editing to sharpen the brand voice, adjust the tone, and ensure the messaging hierarchy is correct. This approach delivers agency-quality launch copy at a fraction of the time and cost.
What is the most important asset to get right for a product launch? +
The launch landing page is the single most important asset because it is the destination for every other channel — email, ads, social, and press. If the landing page copy does not convert, it does not matter how good your email sequence is or how much you spend on ads. The landing page must communicate the product's core transformation in the headline, establish credibility above the fold, handle objections in the body, and create urgency at the bottom. Prompt 3 in this article generates all four sections using the PAS framework, which is the highest-converting structure for launch landing pages.
How do I create urgency in a product launch without sounding manipulative? +
Genuine scarcity and real deadlines create urgency without manipulation. If you are selling a physical product with limited inventory, stating the exact number available is honest and compelling. If you are offering a launch discount that expires at midnight on launch day, that deadline is real. If you have a founding member price that will increase after the first week, that is a legitimate reason to act now. The early bird copy prompt in this article generates urgency language around real constraints — inventory limits, price increase dates, and founding member access windows — not artificial countdown timers or fake sold-out notices.
Do these product launch prompts work for digital products, physical products, and SaaS? +
Yes. Every prompt in this article includes product type as a variable, so the AI adapts the copy framework to your specific category. For digital products (courses, ebooks, templates), the prompts emphasize transformation and instant access. For physical products, they emphasize the unboxing experience, materials, and delivery timeline. For SaaS, they focus on workflow improvement, integration, and the cost of the problem being solved. Fill in the variables with your product specifics and the AI generates category-appropriate copy that matches your audience's expectations for that product type.
