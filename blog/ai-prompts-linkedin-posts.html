12 AI Prompts for LinkedIn Posts That Generate Leads

ContentKit AI•14 min read

AI prompts for LinkedIn posts are structured instructions that generate thought leadership content, storytelling posts, carousels, and B2B lead magnets designed to grow your audience, earn comments, and turn connections into inbound pipeline. This guide delivers 12 copy-paste prompts covering every high-performing LinkedIn post format, each built on engagement frameworks used by the platform's top B2B creators.

LinkedIn is the only social network where your professional content is expected — and rewarded. On Instagram or Twitter, posting about your work feels self-promotional. On LinkedIn, sharing what you learned, what you built, and what you believe positions you as the expert your ideal clients want to hire. The average B2B decision-maker spends 6.3 hours per week on LinkedIn. They are there to learn, not to be sold to. The founders and consultants who generate the most inbound leads are the ones who consistently show up with content worth reading.

But consistency is the hard part. Writing a compelling LinkedIn post from scratch three to five times per week — while running a business, managing clients, or building a product — is not sustainable for most professionals. The result: sporadic posting, generic updates, and zero compounding effect from the algorithm.

This is where structured ai prompts for linkedin posts change the equation. Instead of staring at a blank text box trying to summon a hook, you paste a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude with your topic, your audience, and your key insight, and you get a complete, ready-to-post draft in under two minutes. The AI handles the structure, the pacing, the hook mechanics, and the engagement question at the end. You handle the story and the strategy.

The 12 prompts below cover every LinkedIn post format that consistently earns reach and generates leads: hook-first thought leadership posts, personal vulnerability stories, data-driven insight posts, contrarian opinion posts, step-by-step frameworks, client wins, carousel outlines, polls, document posts, video scripts, lead magnet promotions, and a complete 30-day content calendar. Each prompt includes the audience specification, format requirements, tone guidance, and B2B engagement framework for that post type.

Why AI Prompts Produce Better LinkedIn Content

LinkedIn's algorithm is built around dwell time and early engagement. When someone pauses on your post, reads to the end, and leaves a comment, the algorithm interprets that as a signal the content is worth distributing further. The posts that earn this treatment are not the most polished ones. They are the ones with the strongest hooks, the clearest structure, and the most specific insights. Those are exactly the elements that structured AI prompts enforce.

Hooks Are Engineered, Not Inspired

The first line of your LinkedIn post is the only line most people see before the "see more" truncation. LinkedIn cuts off at roughly the third line — around 200 characters. If those first three lines do not stop the scroll, the post is invisible. The best LinkedIn hooks follow predictable patterns: a surprising statistic, a contrarian statement, a confession, a specific number, or a bold claim. When you tell an AI prompt to "write a hook using the pattern: [Surprising claim]. Here is what I learned:" you consistently get hooks that work — because the pattern itself is what makes it work, and the AI can generate ten variations of that pattern in seconds.

Structure Creates Reading Momentum

LinkedIn posts that earn high engagement share a structural trait: they are easy to scan. Short paragraphs. Line breaks between each thought. A clear progression from hook to insight to takeaway to question. AI prompts that specify "use single-sentence paragraphs, add a line break between each thought, end with a question that invites a specific type of comment" produce posts with this structure by default. Without that instruction, AI models default to dense paragraphs that feel like blog posts — and get scrolled past.

Formats Drive Different Outcomes

Not every LinkedIn post has the same goal. A thought leadership post builds authority. A personal story builds likability. A step-by-step framework builds saves and shares. A lead magnet post generates DMs and email subscribers. A poll drives comments and algorithmic reach. When you use format-specific prompts — one for each of these goals — you create a content mix that builds audience and generates pipeline simultaneously. The 12 prompts below give you one prompt per format, so you can rotate through formats based on your weekly goal.

The bottom line: ai prompts for linkedin posts do not replace your expertise or your voice. They give your expertise a structure that LinkedIn's algorithm rewards and your audience can actually consume. Here are the 12 prompts.

Prompt 1: Hook-First Text Post (Thought Leadership)

The hook-first text post is the workhorse of B2B LinkedIn content. It opens with a single bold statement designed to stop the scroll, delivers a structured insight in short punchy paragraphs, and ends with a question that invites specific comments. This format works for any professional insight, industry observation, or lesson learned from your work. The key is the hook-to-value ratio: the hook earns the click, and the body must deliver on that promise immediately. This prompt generates a complete hook-first thought leadership post calibrated for maximum reach and comment engagement.

Prompt LI-01: Hook-First Text Post (Thought Leadership)
Act as a B2B LinkedIn content strategist with experience growing professional audiences from 0 to 50,000+ followers. Write a hook-first text post for LinkedIn that establishes the author as a thought leader in their industry.

Structure:
— Hook (line 1): A single sentence that creates a knowledge gap, makes a surprising claim, or states a strong opinion. Must be under 200 characters. No emoji. No corporate language.
— Hook support (lines 2–3): One or two short sentences that add just enough context to make the reader want to keep reading but withhold the full insight. End before "see more."
— Body (5–8 short paragraphs, 1–3 sentences each): Deliver the insight with specificity. Use a numbered list or short paragraphs with line breaks. Each paragraph should add a new piece of value.
— Closing insight (1 sentence): A sharp, memorable takeaway the reader can apply or share.
— Engagement question (1 sentence): A question that invites a specific, answerable comment — not "what do you think?" Ask for an experience, a number, a tool, or a prediction.

Format rules: Single-sentence paragraphs where possible. Line break between every paragraph. No hashtags in the body. 3–5 hashtags at the very end, each on its own line. Target length: 900–1,200 characters.

Variables:
— Author Name / Role: [YOUR NAME AND TITLE — e.g., "Sarah Chen, SaaS Founder," "Marcus Rivera, B2B Sales Coach"]
— Industry / Niche: [YOUR INDUSTRY — e.g., "B2B SaaS," "executive coaching," "supply chain consulting"]
— Core Insight / Topic: [THE MAIN POINT — e.g., "most sales reps lose deals in the discovery call, not the demo," "B2B buyers read 10+ pieces of content before talking to sales"]
— Audience: [WHO YOU ARE WRITING FOR — e.g., "CMOs at Series A-C SaaS companies," "independent consultants," "supply chain managers at manufacturers"]

Prompt 2: Personal Story / Vulnerability Post

Personal story posts are the highest-engagement format on LinkedIn — consistently outperforming pure thought leadership posts in reach and comment volume. When a professional shares a genuine failure, a pivotal moment, or an uncomfortable truth, the relatability creates emotional connection that drives shares and saves. The vulnerability post format is powerful precisely because it is rare: most professionals are afraid to share anything that makes them look imperfect. This prompt generates a personal story post using the Situation-Complication-Resolution-Lesson framework that the platform's most-shared posts follow.

Prompt LI-02: Personal Story / Vulnerability Post
Act as a B2B LinkedIn ghostwriter specializing in personal brand content for executives and founders. Write a personal story post that feels authentic, builds trust with the audience, and ends with a professional insight the reader can apply.

Use the SCRL framework:
— Situation (2–3 sentences): Set the scene. Give the reader enough context to picture the moment. Be specific about time, place, and circumstance.
— Complication (3–5 sentences): Describe the problem, failure, or challenge. Do not soften it. The discomfort is what creates relatability. Include a specific detail that makes it real (a number, a conversation, a feeling).
— Resolution (3–5 sentences): What happened. What you did. What changed. Be honest about what worked and what did not.
— Lesson (2–3 sentences): The transferable insight. What can the reader apply from your experience? This is the professional value that justifies the personal disclosure.
— Engagement close (1 sentence): A question inviting readers to share a parallel experience of their own.

Tone rules: First-person throughout. Short sentences. No corporate phrases. No "I am humbled to share" or "excited to announce" language. Write the way you talk, not the way you write in emails. Vulnerability is specific; vague language kills the effect.

Target length: 1,000–1,400 characters. Single-sentence paragraphs. Line breaks between each paragraph.

Variables:
— The Situation: [DESCRIBE THE STARTING CONTEXT — e.g., "launching my first consulting firm," "pitching to a room of 200 executives," "losing my biggest client in year two"]
— The Complication: [WHAT WENT WRONG OR WHAT YOU STRUGGLED WITH — e.g., "I had no pipeline," "the pitch failed completely," "revenue dropped 60% in a quarter"]
— The Resolution: [HOW IT RESOLVED — e.g., "a cold email that changed everything," "the one decision that turned it around," "what I learned after the fall"]
— The Lesson: [THE PROFESSIONAL INSIGHT TO SHARE — e.g., "referrals only work after you've done extraordinary work," "no investor cares about your market size slide"]
— Audience: [WHO THIS STORY IS FOR]

The most common mistake with vulnerability posts is being vague about the painful part. "It was a really hard time for me" does not create connection. "I had $12,000 left in my business account and three employees to pay" creates connection. The prompt enforces specificity by instructing the AI to include a number, a conversation, or a feeling in the complication section. That specificity is what earns the save and the share.

Prompt 3: Data-Driven Insight Post

Data posts perform disproportionately well on LinkedIn because they provide immediate, shareable value. When you turn a statistic, a research finding, or a trend into a structured insight post, you become the person in your network who surfaces valuable information. Decision-makers save data posts and share them with their teams. The key is not just citing the data — it is explaining what the data means for your audience and what they should do about it. This prompt generates a data-driven insight post that moves from stat to implication to action in a format optimized for saves and shares.

Prompt LI-03: Data-Driven Insight Post
Act as a B2B content strategist who turns research and data into actionable LinkedIn posts for senior professionals. Write a data-driven insight post that leads with a surprising or counterintuitive statistic and ends with a specific recommendation the reader can act on.

Structure:
— Data hook (1 sentence): Open with the most surprising number or finding. Format: "[Stat]. [Brief context]."
— Context (2–3 sentences): Where does the data come from? Why is it significant? What does it reveal about a broader trend?
— Implication breakdown (3–5 numbered points): What does this data mean for the reader? Use short, punchy statements. Each point should be a standalone insight.
— Contrarian angle (2–3 sentences): What are most people getting wrong because they do not know this data? What is the conventional wisdom that this stat disproves?
— Action step (1–2 sentences): One specific thing the reader can do differently this week based on this data.
— Source attribution: Cite the source clearly at the end (e.g., "Source: Gartner 2025 B2B Buyer Survey").
— Engagement question (1 sentence): Ask the reader whether this matches what they are seeing in their own work.

Variables:
— The Data Point / Statistic: [THE KEY STAT — e.g., "75% of B2B buyers prefer no-contact purchasing for deals under $50K (Gartner)," "only 3% of your LinkedIn followers see any given post (LinkedIn internal data)"]
— Source: [DATA SOURCE — e.g., "Gartner," "HubSpot State of Marketing 2025," "McKinsey Global Survey"]
— Industry / Audience: [WHO THIS INSIGHT IS FOR]
— What Most People Are Getting Wrong: [THE CONVENTIONAL WISDOM THAT IS WRONG]
— Recommended Action: [THE SPECIFIC THING TO DO DIFFERENTLY]

Data posts earn saves because readers want to come back to the information later. They earn shares because professionals want to look informed when they share industry research with their networks. This combination — saves and shares — is exactly what LinkedIn's algorithm uses to determine whether to push a post to second- and third-degree connections. A single well-crafted data post can reach 10x your normal audience if the data is genuinely surprising.

Prompt 4: Contrarian Opinion Post

Contrarian opinion posts generate the highest comment-to-impression ratios of any LinkedIn format because they invite disagreement. When you state a position that runs counter to conventional wisdom in your industry, people who agree feel validated and comment to say so. People who disagree feel compelled to respond. Both types of engagement signal quality to the algorithm, which distributes the post further. The key is backing the contrarian claim with specific reasoning — otherwise it reads as provocation rather than insight. This prompt generates a well-reasoned contrarian opinion post that invites debate without becoming a flame war.

Prompt LI-04: Contrarian Opinion Post
Act as a B2B thought leader known for challenging conventional wisdom in your industry. Write a contrarian opinion post that states an unpopular or counterintuitive position and backs it with specific reasoning and evidence.

Structure:
— Contrarian hook (1 sentence): State the unpopular opinion directly. No hedging. No "controversial opinion:" prefix. Just the claim.
— Acknowledge the conventional view (2–3 sentences): Show you understand why most people believe the opposite. This establishes credibility and fairness.
— Your reasoning (4–6 short paragraphs): Explain why you disagree. Each paragraph should offer a distinct reason, example, or data point. Use "Because" at the start of supporting paragraphs to signal logical backing.
— Who benefits from the conventional wisdom (1–2 sentences): Name who has an incentive to maintain the status quo belief. This adds analytical depth without personal attacks.
— Your conclusion (1–2 sentences): Restate your position with confidence. Do not backtrack or over-qualify.
— Debate invitation (1 sentence): Explicitly invite disagreement. Example: "Tell me where I am wrong in the comments."

Tone: Direct and confident, not arrogant. Back every claim. Avoid absolutes like "always" and "never." Use "in most cases" or "for most businesses" to be accurate without losing force.

Variables:
— The Contrarian Claim: [THE UNPOPULAR POSITION — e.g., "most B2B companies should not be on LinkedIn at all," "cold outreach converts better than content marketing for most founders," "NPS is a misleading metric for early-stage SaaS"]
— Industry / Audience: [YOUR INDUSTRY AND AUDIENCE]
— Conventional Wisdom Being Challenged: [WHAT MOST PEOPLE BELIEVE]
— Your Evidence or Reasoning: [DATA, EXPERIENCE, OR LOGIC SUPPORTING YOUR POSITION]
— Who Profits from the Conventional Wisdom: [THE INDUSTRY OR VENDORS THAT BENEFIT FROM THE STATUS QUO BELIEF]

Most content marketing advice is written by people selling content marketing services.

I know, because I used to be one of them.

The conventional wisdom: publish consistently, build your audience, and leads will follow. It sounds logical. It does not work for most B2B founders in years one and two.

Because your first 200 followers are colleagues who will not buy from you. Because LinkedIn reach is still 3% of your followers per post. Because content compounds over 18 months, and most businesses need pipeline in 18 weeks.

The agencies and consultants who sell content strategies have a direct financial interest in you believing that posting is the primary driver of B2B revenue. In most cases, it is not. Direct outreach with specific, relevant value converts faster for early-stage businesses.

Content works. It just works at a different stage than most people think.

Tell me where I am wrong.

#B2BMarketing #ContentMarketing #FounderAdvice #MarketingStrategy

The example shows the structure in action: the hook makes a bold claim, the body supports it with specific reasoning rather than pure opinion, and the closing invitation genuinely welcomes debate. That invitation is what earns the comments that trigger algorithmic distribution — and what separates a contrarian post from a rant.

Prompt 5: Step-by-Step Framework Post

Framework posts are the most-saved format on LinkedIn. When you turn your professional process, decision framework, or problem-solving approach into a numbered list with clear steps, readers save it to implement later. The save signal is powerful for the algorithm: it indicates the content has lasting value, not just momentary interest. Framework posts also position you as the person who has solved the problem your audience faces — which is exactly the positioning that generates inbound inquiries. This prompt generates a complete step-by-step framework post in a format optimized for saves.

Prompt LI-05: Step-by-Step Framework Post
Act as a B2B consultant or operator who has developed a proprietary framework or process for solving a problem your audience faces. Write a step-by-step framework post that teaches the process in enough detail that readers can implement it, positioning the author as the expert who has solved this problem.

Structure:
— Framework name hook (1 sentence): Open with the name of the framework and the outcome it achieves. Example: "The 3-Step Pipeline Audit That Doubled Our Close Rate."
— Problem statement (2–3 sentences): Describe the problem this framework solves. Be specific about who it affects and what it costs them.
— The framework (5–7 numbered steps): Each step should include: the action, why it matters, and one specific detail (a tool, a metric, a threshold, or a decision rule). Steps should be specific enough to implement, not vague enough to be useless.
— Results / proof (2–3 sentences): What does this framework produce when applied correctly? Use specific numbers where possible.
— Save prompt (1 sentence): Explicitly tell readers to save the post if they want to reference the framework later. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards posts that earn saves.
— Engagement question (1 sentence): Ask which step is most relevant to their situation right now.

Variables:
— The Framework Name: [NAME YOUR PROCESS — e.g., "The 5-Step ICP Audit," "The Weekly Revenue Review," "The 4-Part Discovery Call Framework"]
— The Problem It Solves: [SPECIFIC PROBLEM — e.g., "most founders spend time on deals that never close," "marketing teams produce content their sales team never uses"]
— The Steps: [LIST YOUR PROCESS STEPS IN ORDER — e.g., "1. Identify top 20% of revenue sources, 2. Map the common attributes, 3. Score every new lead against those attributes..."]
— The Results: [OUTCOMES ACHIEVED WITH THIS FRAMEWORK — e.g., "reduced sales cycle by 40%," "increased qualified pipeline 3x in 90 days"]
— Audience: [WHO THE FRAMEWORK IS FOR]

The explicit "save this post" instruction at the end of the framework may feel obvious, but it measurably increases save rate. LinkedIn readers do not instinctively click the bookmark icon unless reminded. When a post earns saves at a higher rate than average, the algorithm classifies it as evergreen content and continues distributing it for days rather than hours. Framework posts with save prompts routinely earn 3–5x the reach of equivalent posts without them.

Prompt 6: Client Win / Case Study Post

Client win posts are the most direct lead-generation format on LinkedIn. When you share a specific, measurable result you achieved for a client — naming the problem, the approach, and the outcome — you give potential clients a preview of what working with you looks like. The key is specificity: "helped a client grow revenue" is invisible; "helped a 12-person SaaS team increase qualified pipeline 3x in 90 days by rebuilding their ICP scoring model" is a post that attracts ten similar businesses. This prompt generates a case study post that positions your work without sounding like a sales pitch.

Prompt LI-06: Client Win / Case Study Post
Act as a B2B consultant, agency founder, or service provider who wants to share a client success story on LinkedIn without sounding promotional. Write a case study post that tells the story of a client transformation using the PAR framework (Problem-Action-Result), positioning the author as a skilled practitioner while making the client the hero of the story.

Structure:
— Result hook (1 sentence): Open with the outcome. Do not bury the result at the end. Example: "A client came to us with a $2M pipeline. In 90 days, it was $7.4M."
— Client context (2–3 sentences): Describe the client anonymously or with permission. Industry, size, and the specific problem they faced when they came to you.
— The problem in detail (3–4 sentences): What was costing them time or money? Be specific about the symptom (low conversion rate, long sales cycles, high churn) and the underlying cause they had not yet identified.
— The approach (4–6 sentences or a numbered list): What did you do? Name the specific methods, tools, or frameworks. This is where your expertise shows — be specific enough that readers understand the sophistication of the work without revealing every detail of your process.
— The result (3–5 sentences): Specific, measurable outcomes. Numbers wherever possible. Timeline from start to result.
— The transferable lesson (2–3 sentences): What can other readers apply from this story, even if they are not your client?
— Soft CTA (1 sentence): A low-pressure invitation for readers who recognize their situation in the story. Example: "If this sounds familiar, I have one advisory slot opening in April."

Variables:
— Client Industry / Type: [CLIENT DESCRIPTION — e.g., "a Series A SaaS company," "a 20-person executive search firm," "a regional manufacturing business"]
— The Problem: [SPECIFIC PROBLEM — e.g., "close rate stuck at 14% despite strong inbound volume," "marketing and sales were misaligned on ICP definition," "customer acquisition cost doubled in 18 months"]
— The Approach: [WHAT YOU DID — e.g., "rebuilt their qualification criteria," "ran a 3-session sales process audit," "rebuilt the demand gen strategy from ICP definition outward"]
— The Result: [MEASURABLE OUTCOME WITH TIMELINE — e.g., "close rate went from 14% to 31% in 60 days," "pipeline qualified rate improved from 22% to 58% in one quarter"]
— Availability / CTA: [WHAT YOU ARE OFFERING — e.g., "1 advisory slot in April," "free 30-minute pipeline audit," "new cohort starting in May"]

The result-first hook is counterintuitive for most professionals who are taught to set context before delivering the punchline. On LinkedIn, the result is the hook. When someone sees "A client came to us with a $2M pipeline. In 90 days, it was $7.4M," they immediately ask two questions: who is this person and how did they do that? Those two questions drive the engagement that turns a single post into multiple inbound inquiries.

Prompt 7: Carousel Post (Slide-by-Slide Outline)

LinkedIn carousels — document uploads displayed as swipeable slides — earn 3x the average reach of standard text posts because each swipe is counted as engagement. The more slides someone swipes through, the stronger the dwell-time signal sent to the algorithm. Carousels that teach a framework, tell a story through visuals, or deliver a numbered list in slide format consistently outperform equivalent text posts. This prompt generates a complete slide-by-slide outline for a carousel document, including the hook slide, content slides, and CTA slide — ready to hand to a designer or build in Canva.

Prompt LI-07: Carousel Post (Slide-by-Slide Outline)
Act as a LinkedIn carousel creator who builds high-reach educational content for B2B professionals. Write a complete slide-by-slide outline for a LinkedIn carousel document that teaches a specific framework, process, or insight in a format optimized for swipe-through engagement.

Carousel structure (8–12 slides total):
— Slide 1 (Cover): Bold headline that makes a promise. Include a subheadline. This is the thumbnail visible before the reader opens the carousel — it must earn the click.
— Slide 2 (Problem): Name the problem the carousel solves. One sentence headline, 2–3 supporting bullet points.
— Slides 3–9 (Content): Each slide = one step, one insight, or one framework element. Format: Slide number + Bold headline + 3–5 bullet points or a 2–3 sentence explanation. Keep text minimal — carousel slides are not blog posts.
— Slide 10 (Summary): A visual summary of all slides in the carousel. One sentence recap per slide.
— Slide 11 (CTA): A single clear call to action. Follow the author, download a resource, or DM for more. One action only.
— Slide 12 (Optional: About the Author): Short bio and credibility line for readers who want to know more.

For each slide, provide:
— Slide number and title
— Headline text (8 words max)
— Body text / bullet points (25 words max per slide)
— Design note: background color suggestion or visual element recommendation

The post caption (for the text area above the carousel):
— Hook (1 sentence): Promise the value of the carousel
— Teaser (2–3 sentences): Give just enough of the insight to make the reader open the carousel
— Swipe CTA: Tell them to swipe to get the full framework

Variables:
— Topic / Framework Title: [CAROUSEL TOPIC — e.g., "The 5-Step LinkedIn Profile Audit," "How to Build a B2B Content Strategy in 90 Minutes," "The Inbound Lead Scoring Framework"]
— Target Audience: [WHO THE CAROUSEL IS FOR]
— Key Insight or Outcome Promised: [WHAT THE READER WILL KNOW OR BE ABLE TO DO AFTER SWIPING THROUGH]
— Number of Steps / Slides: [HOW MANY CONTENT SLIDES — 5, 7, OR 10]
— Brand Colors: [YOUR BRAND COLORS OR "USE PROFESSIONAL BLUE/WHITE SCHEME"]
— CTA at End: [FOLLOW / DM / DOWNLOAD / BOOK A CALL]

The slide count matters more than most carousel creators realize. LinkedIn's algorithm measures average slides viewed per open — the ratio of total slides swiped to total opens. An 8-slide carousel where most people swipe through all 8 will outperform a 20-slide carousel where most people stop at slide 4. Keep carousels tight: 8 to 12 slides, each delivering one complete idea, and end the carousel on slide 8 or 9 before the reader gets impatient.

Prompt 8: Poll + Commentary Post

LinkedIn polls generate more reach per impression than almost any other post format because every vote is counted as an engagement event that triggers additional distribution. The mistake most poll-posters make is stopping at the poll itself — four options and a vague question. The posts that earn both poll votes and genuine comments pair the poll with a commentary section that shares the author's perspective before the results are in. This approach works because the author's opinion gives voters a position to agree or disagree with, which drives comments alongside votes. This prompt generates a complete poll-with-commentary post.

Prompt LI-08: Poll + Commentary Post
Act as a LinkedIn content strategist who uses polls to drive reach and genuine conversation simultaneously. Write a poll post that includes the poll itself and an author commentary section that makes readers want to vote AND comment.

Structure:
— Hook (1 sentence): A question or claim that establishes the topic and creates curiosity about the poll.
— Author commentary (3–5 short paragraphs): Share your perspective on the topic before revealing the poll. Disagree with the expected majority answer. This creates the contrast that drives comments from people who agree AND disagree with you.
— Poll setup sentence (1 sentence): Introduce the poll with a reason to vote. Example: "I want to test this assumption. Vote below."
— Poll question: A clear, specific question with 4 answer options that represent genuinely different positions (not just variations of the same answer).
— Poll duration: 1 week (standard for maximum response volume).
— Post-poll engagement question (1 sentence): Ask readers to explain their vote in the comments.

Poll question rules: Questions with a "correct" answer perform worse than questions with a genuinely debatable answer. The best poll questions are ones where informed professionals in your field would genuinely disagree. Avoid "what is your favorite X" polls — they generate votes but not conversation.

Variables:
— Topic / Industry Debate: [THE DEBATABLE TOPIC — e.g., "whether SDRs should be quota-bearing in year one," "whether content marketing beats cold outreach for B2B leads," "whether weekly 1:1s are necessary for remote teams"]
— Your Perspective: [YOUR OPINION ON THE TOPIC — e.g., "I think SDRs should not carry quota in year one," "in my experience cold outreach consistently outperforms content for early-stage companies"]
— The 4 Poll Options: [YOUR PROPOSED ANSWER OPTIONS — or write "generate 4 options based on the debate"]
— Audience: [WHO THE POLL IS AIMED AT]

The counter-intuitive poll strategy — sharing your perspective before the votes come in rather than waiting to react to the results — is what separates high-comment polls from high-vote-only polls. Votes alone do not generate conversation. Votes paired with a stated opinion that some readers disagree with generate both the algorithmic signal from votes and the conversation signal from comments. Both signals together push the post to a dramatically wider audience.

Prompt 9: Document / PDF Post

LinkedIn document posts (uploaded PDFs and slide decks) consistently rank among the highest-organic-reach formats on the platform because the document viewer keeps readers on the LinkedIn app — exactly the behavior the algorithm rewards. Unlike carousels (which are built slide-by-slide), document posts can include dense content: templates, checklists, research reports, and toolkits that provide genuine reference value. When someone downloads and saves your document, LinkedIn counts that as a strong positive signal. This prompt generates a complete document post: the document outline and the post caption that drives downloads.

Prompt LI-09: Document / PDF Post
Act as a B2B content creator who builds high-value reference documents for LinkedIn audiences. Write both the document outline and the LinkedIn post caption for a document post that drives maximum downloads and saves.

Document outline requirements:
— Title page: Document title + author name + one-sentence description of what the document delivers
— Introduction (1 page): The problem this document solves and who it is for
— Core content (4–8 pages): The framework, checklist, template, or research findings. Each page should contain one complete idea with headers and bullet points. Avoid paragraphs — use scannable formatting throughout.
— Quick-reference summary (1 page): A one-page summary of the document's key points — the page most readers screenshot and save
— About the author (1 page): Brief bio and social CTA

Post caption requirements:
— Hook (1 sentence): Name what the document is and the outcome it delivers
— Credibility sentence (1 sentence): Why should readers trust this document? Years of experience, clients helped, data collected?
— What is inside (5–7 bullet points): Quick list of the most valuable sections of the document
— Download CTA (1 sentence): Tell readers exactly how to get the document (download button below the post)
— Comment CTA (1 sentence): Ask readers to drop a specific word in the comments (e.g., "Comment 'AUDIT' and I will send you the companion worksheet"). This drives comment volume that amplifies reach.

Variables:

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts to Build a LinkedIn Lead Engine

The 12 prompts above give you the content. These five tips ensure you use that content to build a system that generates consistent inbound leads rather than just occasional impressions.

1. Lead with Specificity, Not Industry Knowledge

The most common failure in AI-generated LinkedIn content is vagueness. When you fill in the prompt variables with generic inputs — "I am in B2B marketing" — you get generic outputs. When you fill in "I help Series A SaaS founders in the HR tech vertical build inbound pipeline from LinkedIn content," the AI generates content that speaks to a specific audience with specific pain points. The narrower your audience specification in the variables, the more your content resonates with the exact people you want as clients. Specificity is the competitive advantage that AI cannot generate without your input.

2. Batch Your Content Creation Weekly

Do not write LinkedIn posts reactively — one at a time, the morning they need to go live. Instead, block two hours every Monday morning, run five to seven prompts back to back with your week's topics, and draft all five posts in a single session. Then schedule them with LinkedIn's native scheduler or a tool like Buffer. This batching approach is what makes the three-to-five-posts-per-week cadence sustainable for professionals running a business. The AI handles the structure; your two-hour Monday session handles the strategy and the personalization.

3. Edit for Voice Before You Post

AI-generated LinkedIn posts require a final editing pass for voice. Read the draft out loud. If any sentence sounds like something you would never say in a real conversation, change it. Replace formal phrases with your natural language. Add a detail from your actual experience that the AI could not have known. The goal is a post that sounds exactly like you — just more structured than you would naturally write on your own. This editing pass takes five minutes and is what prevents your content from sounding like every other AI-generated post your audience sees in their feed.

4. Respond to Every Comment Within the First Hour

LinkedIn's algorithm gives significant weight to post velocity — how quickly and consistently a post earns engagement in the first 60 minutes after publishing. A post that earns 10 comments in the first hour gets distributed more broadly than a post that earns the same 10 comments across 24 hours. When you reply to every comment within the first hour of posting, you double your comment count (every reply is a comment), extend the engagement window, and signal to the algorithm that the post is generating active conversation. Schedule your posts to go live at a time when you can spend 20 minutes immediately after publication responding to comments.

5. Track Leading Indicators, Not Just Impressions

Most LinkedIn creators optimize for impressions because that is the most visible metric. But impressions do not generate revenue — profile visits, connection requests, DMs, and comment quality do. Track these leading indicators weekly alongside your post performance: How many profile visits did your posts generate this week? How many new connection requests from ideal clients? How many DMs referencing your content? A post with 500 impressions and five profile visits from ideal clients is worth more than a post with 5,000 impressions and zero profile activity. Over time, this tracking helps you identify which post types generate the most qualified inbound interest — and lets you weight your content calendar toward them.
