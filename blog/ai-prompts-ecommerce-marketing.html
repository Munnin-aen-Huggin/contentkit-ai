15 AI Prompts for E-commerce Marketing That Drive Sales

ContentKit AI•2026-03-04•15 min read

AI prompts for ecommerce marketing are structured instructions that generate high-converting product copy, email flows, ad creative, social content, and SEO articles — covering every channel an online store needs to drive traffic and turn visitors into buyers. This guide delivers 15 copy-paste prompts built on proven direct-response frameworks, each tailored to the specific conversion goals of e-commerce: higher add-to-cart rates, stronger email open rates, lower abandoned cart rates, and more repeat purchases.

E-commerce marketing is a volume game. A single Shopify store needs product descriptions for hundreds of SKUs, a welcome email sequence, flash sale announcements, post-purchase flows, seasonal campaigns, buying guides for SEO, Google Shopping feed titles, and a social media calendar — every month. Most store owners either hire expensive agencies to handle this content or publish generic, unoptimized copy that fails to convert. Both outcomes are unnecessary when structured AI prompts can produce professional-grade output in minutes.

The difference between AI-generated copy that converts and copy that does not comes down to prompt structure. A vague prompt like "write a product description for my coffee maker" produces bland, feature-list output. A structured prompt that specifies the target customer's frustrations, the key outcome they want, the copywriting framework to use, the social proof to include, and the exact format of the output produces copy that reads like it was written by a conversion copywriter charging $5,000 per project.

That is what the 15 prompts below deliver. Each one is built for a specific e-commerce marketing job: product pages, collection pages, homepage hero copy, welcome sequences, flash sale announcements, product launch sequences, review request emails, cross-sell campaigns, seasonal copy, influencer briefs, UGC campaigns, loyalty program launches, buying guide blog posts, Google Shopping feed titles, and monthly content calendars. Fill in the variables, run the prompt, and publish.

Why Structured Prompts Outperform Generic AI Requests for E-commerce

E-commerce copy has a specific job: move a visitor from browsing to buying as efficiently as possible. That requires addressing three conversion barriers in sequence — desire (do they want this?), trust (do they believe it?), and urgency (why now?). Generic AI requests produce copy that describes products. Structured prompts produce copy that sells them.

Frameworks Force the Right Persuasion Sequence

The prompts in this guide specify copywriting frameworks like PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution), AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action), and feature-benefit-proof. These frameworks exist because decades of direct-response testing have shown that readers respond to copy in a predictable sequence: they need to recognize their problem before they can appreciate the solution. When you tell the AI to "write this product description using the PAS framework," it leads with the customer's pain point before introducing the product — which is always more persuasive than leading with the product itself.

Variables Lock In the Specifics That Drive Conversions

The single biggest predictor of whether AI copy converts is specificity. "Trusted by thousands of customers" loses to "4.8 stars from 2,341 verified reviews." "Ships fast" loses to "ships same day if ordered before 2 PM EST." Each prompt below includes bracketed variables for exactly these specifics: review counts, star ratings, shipping timeframes, guarantees, pricing, and key differentiators. Fill them in accurately and the AI output will contain the specificity that separates converting copy from copy that gets scrolled past.

Format Instructions Match Platform Requirements

E-commerce copy lives in constrained formats. Google Shopping titles have a 150-character limit. Email subject lines have a 50-character sweet spot. Instagram captions need hooks in the first line before the "more" cutoff. Product page above-the-fold copy needs to communicate the full value proposition before any scroll. Each prompt specifies the exact format required for that channel, so the AI output is ready to paste without reformatting.

The bottom line: ai prompts for ecommerce marketing are not a shortcut around good copywriting — they are a way to apply good copywriting at the volume and speed that modern e-commerce demands. Here are the 15 prompts.

15 AI Prompts for E-commerce Marketing

Prompt 1: Product Page Copy (Above the Fold)

The above-the-fold section of a product page is the highest-leverage copy on your entire store. It is what visitors see before they scroll, and it determines whether they keep reading or bounce. Most product pages waste this space on feature lists and technical specifications. The most converting product pages lead with the customer outcome, follow with the key differentiator, add social proof, and close with a low-friction CTA. This prompt generates complete above-the-fold copy for any product, following a proven four-element structure used by top-performing Shopify stores.

Prompt EC-01: Product Page Copy (Above the Fold)
Act as a conversion copywriter specializing in e-commerce product pages with a track record of improving add-to-cart rates. Write the above-the-fold copy for the product page below. This is the section visitors see before scrolling — it must make the case for buying without requiring any scroll.

Structure (in order):
— Product Title (H1): Product name + primary benefit or key attribute in 10 words or fewer
— Subheadline: One sentence expanding on the primary benefit and who it is for
— Benefit Bullets (3–4 bullets): Each bullet = one outcome the customer gets, written as "[Result] so you can [life improvement]"
— Social Proof Line: One line with star rating and review count formatted as "X stars from Y verified buyers"
— Guarantee/Trust Line: One sentence on the guarantee, free shipping threshold, or return policy
— CTA Button Text: 3–5 words, action-oriented, benefit-framed (not just "Add to Cart")

Copywriting rules:
— Lead with the outcome the customer achieves, not the product's features
— Write to the customer's specific frustration with current alternatives
— Every sentence must earn its place — cut anything that does not move the visitor closer to the cart
— Use the customer's language, not marketing language

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY — e.g., "skincare serum," "standing desk mat," "ceramic coffee mug set"]
— Target Customer: [WHO BUYS THIS — e.g., "remote workers with back pain," "coffee enthusiasts who care about flavor," "parents of toddlers"]
— Primary Outcome: [THE #1 RESULT THE CUSTOMER WANTS — e.g., "no more afternoon energy crashes," "deeper sleep without medication," "clearer skin in 30 days"]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES THIS BETTER THAN ALTERNATIVES]
— Social Proof: [STAR RATING + NUMBER OF REVIEWS — e.g., "4.9 stars from 1,847 verified buyers"]
— Guarantee/Shipping: [TRUST ELEMENT — e.g., "30-day money-back guarantee," "free shipping over $50," "free returns within 60 days"]
— Price: [PRICE POINT]

Product Title: Luminate Vitamin C Serum — Visible Glow in 14 Days

Subheadline: Finally, a brightening serum that works for sensitive skin without the redness, peeling, or $200 price tag.

Benefit Bullets:
• Fades dark spots and hyperpigmentation so you can skip the heavy foundation
• Boosts collagen production so your skin looks firmer with every week of use
• Lightweight, non-greasy formula so it layers under SPF without pilling
• Fragrance-free and dermatologist-tested so sensitive skin types can use it daily

Social Proof: 4.9 stars from 2,341 verified buyers

Trust Line: Try it risk-free — 30-day money-back guarantee, free shipping on orders over $45.

CTA Button: Get Glowing Skin →

Notice the CTA button text: "Get Glowing Skin" instead of "Add to Cart." The button text is part of the conversion copy, not just a UI label. When the CTA names the outcome the customer is buying, it reinforces the decision at the exact moment they are committing to it. This single change consistently lifts add-to-cart rates by 5–15% across product categories.

Prompt 2: Collection/Category Page Description

Collection pages are one of the most neglected pieces of real estate in e-commerce. Most stores leave the description blank, or write a single sentence that says something like "Shop our collection of [product type]." But collection pages are often the first page a visitor lands on from Google or a category-level ad, and they rank for high-volume category keywords. A well-written collection page description helps with both conversion (by framing the selection before the visitor browses) and SEO (by giving Google more context about the page's relevance). This prompt generates a collection page description that does both jobs.

Prompt EC-02: Collection/Category Page Description
Act as an e-commerce SEO copywriter who specializes in writing collection and category page descriptions that rank in Google and convert visitors into buyers. Write a collection page description that appears above the product grid.

Structure:
— Opening paragraph (2–3 sentences): Hook that addresses the customer's goal or shopping intent, naturally including the primary category keyword
— What makes this collection different (2–3 sentences): The curation criteria, quality standards, or selection philosophy that sets this collection apart from generic alternatives
— Who it is for (1–2 sentences): Specific customer profile with their specific use case
— Trust element (1 sentence): Review stats, number of products, brands, or guarantee
— Browse CTA (1 sentence): Soft invitation to explore the products below

SEO requirements:
— Include the primary keyword naturally in the first sentence
— Include 2–3 secondary/related keywords naturally throughout
— Total length: 120–180 words (enough for SEO, short enough not to overwhelm the grid)

Variables:
— Collection Name: [COLLECTION/CATEGORY NAME — e.g., "Men's Running Shoes," "Organic Skincare," "Home Office Furniture"]
— Primary Keyword: [TARGET SEO KEYWORD]
— Secondary Keywords: [2–3 RELATED TERMS]
— Target Customer: [WHO SHOPS THIS COLLECTION]
— Curation Standard: [WHAT MAKES PRODUCTS IN THIS COLLECTION SPECIAL — e.g., "all products are OEKO-TEX certified," "curated by our in-house nutritionist," "every style is available in sizes XS–4X"]
— Number of Products: [HOW MANY PRODUCTS IN THE COLLECTION]
— Trust Signal: [REVIEW STATS OR GUARANTEE]

The 120–180 word target is deliberate. Google needs enough text to understand the page's topical relevance, but visitors do not read walls of text before browsing products. This length hits the SEO minimum without creating a barrier between the visitor and the product grid below.

Prompt 3: Homepage Hero Section Copy

Your homepage hero is your store's first impression. It has roughly three seconds to answer two questions every new visitor asks: "What is this?" and "Is it for me?" Most e-commerce homepages fail on both counts — they lead with brand name and a vague tagline instead of a clear value proposition. This prompt generates hero copy that immediately communicates who you serve, what transformation you provide, and why you are the right choice — with a CTA that drives the right next action for your funnel.

Prompt EC-03: Homepage Hero Section Copy
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in e-commerce homepage optimization. Write the hero section copy for an online store. The hero must answer "What is this?" and "Is it for me?" within 3 seconds of landing on the page.

Provide 3 complete hero variations, each with:
— Headline (8 words max): The single most compelling reason to shop here. Lead with the customer's outcome, not the store's name.
— Subheadline (15–20 words): Expand the headline with specificity — who it is for, what they get, and what makes it different
— Primary CTA button (3–5 words): Outcome-oriented, not generic
— Secondary CTA link (3–5 words): Lower-commitment option for browsers who are not ready to shop yet
— Hero badge/trust element (5–8 words): Small text near the CTA with a trust signal

Variation angles:
— Variation 1: Outcome-focused (what their life looks like after buying)
— Variation 2: Problem-focused (the pain you solve)
— Variation 3: Identity-focused (who your customer is and what they stand for)

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Product Category: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Customer: [CUSTOMER PROFILE]
— Primary Outcome: [THE #1 RESULT CUSTOMERS GET]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT]
— Trust Signal: [SOCIAL PROOF OR GUARANTEE]
— Best-Selling Category or Product: [WHERE TO SEND PRIMARY CTA TRAFFIC]

Three variations matter because different traffic sources convert differently. Paid social traffic that has seen your ad already needs an outcome-focused headline. Organic search traffic landing on the homepage for the first time often responds better to problem-focused copy. Testing all three variations against your actual traffic mix will reveal which angle resonates most with your specific audience.

Prompt 4: E-commerce Email Welcome Sequence

The welcome sequence is the most important email automation in any e-commerce store. New subscribers are at peak interest in your brand — open rates for welcome emails average 50–60%, versus 20–25% for regular campaigns. Most stores send a single welcome email with a discount code and then go silent. A properly structured welcome sequence builds brand affinity, educates the subscriber on your best products, shares social proof, and converts them into a first-time buyer before the initial excitement fades. This prompt generates a complete 4-email welcome sequence with subject lines, preview text, and body copy for each email.

Prompt EC-04: E-commerce Email Welcome Sequence
Act as an email marketing strategist who specializes in e-commerce welcome flows that convert subscribers into first-time buyers. Write a 4-email welcome sequence for the store below. The sequence should be sent over 7 days and move the subscriber from brand-aware to purchase-ready.

Email structure (for each of the 4 emails):
— Subject Line: Under 50 characters, curiosity or benefit-driven, A/B test variant included
— Preview Text: Under 90 characters, complements the subject line without repeating it
— Opening Hook: First sentence must create a reason to keep reading
— Body: 100–150 words. Conversational, brand-voice appropriate, with one clear purpose per email
— CTA: One per email, specific and action-oriented

Email sequence strategy:
— Email 1 (Immediate): Welcome + deliver the lead magnet or discount. Set expectations for what is coming.
— Email 2 (Day 2): Brand story or mission. Why you exist. Who you are for. Build emotional connection.
— Email 3 (Day 4): Best-sellers or hero product. Social proof heavy. Remove purchase objections.
— Email 4 (Day 7): Urgency. Reminder of the offer. Address the "not yet" objection.

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Product Category: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Brand Voice: [TONE — e.g., "warm and conversational," "bold and direct," "expert and educational"]
— Lead Magnet/Offer: [WHAT THEY SIGNED UP FOR — e.g., "10% off first order," "free size guide," "free recipe ebook"]
— Brand Mission: [WHY YOUR BRAND EXISTS — e.g., "we started because sustainable activewear was ugly and overpriced"]
— Best-Seller: [YOUR TOP PRODUCT OR COLLECTION]
— Social Proof: [REVIEW STATS — e.g., "4.8 stars from 3,200 buyers"]
— Discount Code/Offer: [OFFER CODE AND VALUE]
— Offer Expiry: [WHEN THE OFFER EXPIRES — e.g., "expires in 7 days," "valid for 48 hours"]

The four-email structure outperforms a single welcome email by giving each message one job. Email 1 delivers the promise and sets expectations. Email 2 makes the subscriber care about the brand, not just the discount. Email 3 makes the product decision easy with social proof. Email 4 closes with urgency before the subscriber's initial enthusiasm fades. This sequence reliably generates 3–5x more first-purchase revenue than a single welcome email.

Prompt 5: Flash Sale Announcement (Email + Social)

Flash sales are one of the highest-ROI tactics in e-commerce when executed with urgency-driven copy. The problem is that most flash sale emails read like a discount announcement: "20% OFF everything this weekend!" This generates a spike in sales but leaves conversion on the table. High-converting flash sale copy uses scarcity (limited time, limited stock), loss aversion (what they miss if they do not act), and social momentum (others are shopping now) to drive immediate action. This prompt generates coordinated flash sale copy for email, Instagram caption, and SMS — all with matching urgency messaging across channels.

Prompt EC-05: Flash Sale Announcement (Email + Social)
Act as an e-commerce campaign copywriter who specializes in high-urgency promotional copy. Write a coordinated flash sale announcement across three channels: email, Instagram caption, and SMS. All three must use the same urgency framing and sale details but be optimized for each channel's format.

Email (full campaign email):
— Subject Line: Under 50 characters, includes the discount or product name + urgency signal
— Preview Text: Under 90 characters, adds urgency or specificity not in subject line
— Body: 80–120 words. Lead with urgency, then sale details, then top products on sale, then CTA
— CTA: Specific product or collection, urgent button text

Instagram Caption:
— Hook (first line): Must stop the scroll before the "more" cutoff. Under 125 characters.
— Body: 3–5 short lines with sale details, what is on sale, urgency, and emoji sparingly
— CTA: "Link in bio" + what they will find there
— Hashtags: 5–8 relevant hashtags in first comment format

SMS (160 characters max):
— Store name + discount + what is on sale + CTA link. Every character counts.

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Discount/Offer: [OFFER — e.g., "25% off sitewide," "BOGO on all supplements," "$20 off orders over $100"]
— Sale Duration: [TIMEFRAME — e.g., "48 hours only," "ends Sunday at midnight," "today only"]
— Products on Sale: [WHAT IS INCLUDED — e.g., "all skincare," "select running shoes," "everything in the summer collection"]
— Discount Code (if applicable): [CODE]
— Landing Page URL: [SALE PAGE URL]
— Urgency Element: [SCARCITY — e.g., "stock limited," "only 200 units at this price," "ends when the timer hits zero"]

Email Subject: 48-hour flash: 25% off everything (no exceptions)
Preview Text: Your cart won't wait — neither will this price.

Browse the sale →

Instagram Hook: 25% off everything. 48 hours. No exclusions. No excuses. ↓

SMS: [StoreName] FLASH: 25% off everything. Code FLASH25. Ends Sun midnight. Shop: [URL] Reply STOP to opt out

The coordinated approach across channels creates a surround-sound effect during the sale window. Subscribers who see the email, then the Instagram post, then receive the SMS are significantly more likely to convert than those who only see one touchpoint. The copy in each channel reinforces the same urgency framing while respecting each platform's format and tone.

Prompt 6: Product Launch Sequence

A product launch is not a single announcement — it is a campaign arc that builds anticipation, drives day-one sales momentum, and converts fence-sitters in the days following launch. Most e-commerce brands either over-communicate (daily emails that train subscribers to ignore them) or under-communicate (a single launch email that gets lost in crowded inboxes). This prompt generates a 5-email product launch sequence that builds heat before launch and converts aggressively on and after launch day.

Prompt EC-06: Product Launch Sequence
Act as an e-commerce launch strategist who specializes in product release campaigns that generate immediate sales momentum. Write a 5-email product launch sequence that builds anticipation before launch and drives purchases in the 5 days following launch.

Sequence structure:
— Email 1 (7 days before): Tease. Hint at what is coming without revealing it fully. Build curiosity and a waitlist.
— Email 2 (3 days before): Reveal. Name the product and the main benefit. Why it is different. Open early access or waitlist sign-up.
— Email 3 (Launch day): It is live. Full product pitch. Lead with the customer problem, then the solution, then social proof if available. Urgency: launch pricing or limited stock.
— Email 4 (Day 2 post-launch): Address objections. FAQ format or "you asked, we answered." Include early buyer reactions if available.
— Email 5 (Day 5 post-launch): Final push. Launch offer ending. Social proof from early buyers. Scarcity.

Each email needs: Subject line, preview text, body (80–150 words), and CTA.

Variables:
— Store/Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY]
— Main Customer Problem Solved: [PROBLEM]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES IT DIFFERENT FROM ALTERNATIVES]
— Launch Offer: [LAUNCH PRICING OR BUNDLE — e.g., "launch price of $79 (regular $99)," "free gift with first 100 orders"]
— Launch Offer Deadline: [WHEN THE LAUNCH OFFER ENDS]
— Common Objections: [TOP 2–3 REASONS SOMEONE WOULD NOT BUY]

The teaser-reveal-launch arc works because it converts a product announcement into a mini-event that subscribers look forward to. By the time the launch email arrives, subscribers who opened Emails 1 and 2 are already primed to buy. Early access and launch pricing create genuine urgency without manufactured scarcity. And the post-launch objection email catches the fence-sitters who wanted to buy but talked themselves out of it.

Prompt 7: Customer Review Request Email

Reviews are the highest-leverage asset in e-commerce. A product with 4.8 stars from 200 reviews converts at 2–4x the rate of the same product with no reviews. Yet most stores either do not ask for reviews or send a generic "How did we do?" email that generates a 5% response rate. This prompt generates a review request email designed to maximize both response rate and review quality — because a detailed, specific review with a photo is worth 10 times more than a star rating without context.

Prompt EC-07: Customer Review Request Email
Act as an e-commerce customer experience specialist who builds review generation systems. Write a post-purchase review request email that feels personal, makes leaving a review feel easy, and encourages specific, photo-rich reviews rather than generic star ratings.

Structure:
— Subject Line: Personal, not corporate. Sound like it is from a human, not a system. Under 50 characters.
— Preview Text: Curiosity or appreciation angle. Under 90 characters.
— Opening: Acknowledge what they bought and how long they have had it. Be specific.
— Ask (the key paragraph): Tell them exactly what to write about — what to mention, what photo to share. The more specific the ask, the better the review.
— Incentive (if applicable): Small incentive framed as a thank-you, not a bribe
— Simplicity reassurance: "It takes 2 minutes" + direct link to the review form
— Fallback: If they had an issue, give them a direct contact path before they go to public reviews

Timing: Send 7–14 days after delivery (enough time to experience the product).

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Product Purchased: [SPECIFIC PRODUCT NAME]
— Days Since Delivery: [TIMING — e.g., "7 days," "10 days," "2 weeks"]
— What to Review (specific prompt): [GUIDE WHAT THEY WRITE — e.g., "how your skin looks after two weeks of use," "how the shoes felt on your first run," "whether the assembly was as easy as we promised"]
— Review Incentive (optional): [INCENTIVE — e.g., "10% off your next order," "entry into our monthly $100 gift card draw," or leave blank if none]
— Review Platform: [WHERE TO LEAVE REVIEWS — e.g., "our website," "Google," "Trustpilot"]
— Support Email: [SUPPORT EMAIL FOR UNHAPPY CUSTOMERS]

The specific ask is the highest-leverage element of this prompt. When you tell a customer to write about "how your skin looks after two weeks of use and share a before/after photo," you get exactly that. When you ask "how was your experience?", you get one-sentence reviews that do not help undecided shoppers. Guiding the review content while keeping the tone authentic is the difference between a review program that drives conversion and one that generates data you cannot use.

Prompt 8: Cross-Sell/Upsell Email

Post-purchase emails are the most underused revenue channel in e-commerce. A customer who just bought from you has demonstrated both trust and purchasing intent — they are the warmest possible audience for a cross-sell or upsell. Yet most brands either send a generic "you might also like" product recommendation email or nothing at all. This prompt generates a personalized cross-sell or upsell email that ties the recommendation directly to what the customer already bought, framing the additional product as the natural next step in achieving their goal.

Prompt EC-08: Cross-Sell/Upsell Email
Act as an e-commerce email strategist specializing in post-purchase revenue optimization. Write a cross-sell or upsell email for the purchase scenario below. The email must feel like a helpful recommendation, not a sales pitch — the customer should feel like you know what they bought and what they need next.

Choose the appropriate approach based on the variables:
— Cross-sell: Recommend a complementary product from a different category that enhances the original purchase
— Upsell: Recommend a higher-tier version, subscription, or larger quantity of the same product

Structure:
— Subject Line: Reference the original product. Under 50 characters.
— Preview Text: Hint at the recommendation. Under 90 characters.
— Opening: Acknowledge their purchase and connect it to the recommendation. Make the logic of the recommendation explicit.
— Recommendation: Name the product, one sentence on what it does, and two benefit bullets for this customer specifically
— Social proof: Reviews or stats on the recommended product
— Offer (optional): Bundle discount, loyalty points, or exclusive offer for existing customers
— CTA: Specific to the recommended product
— Total length: 100–140 words

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Original Purchase: [WHAT THE CUSTOMER BOUGHT]
— Recommended Product: [CROSS-SELL OR UPSELL PRODUCT]
— Why It Pairs Well: [LOGICAL CONNECTION — e.g., "most customers who buy the yoga mat also get the foam roller within 30 days," "this serum works 40% better when paired with the SPF moisturizer"]
— Key Benefit of Recommended Product: [MAIN OUTCOME]
— Social Proof: [REVIEWS ON RECOMMENDED PRODUCT]
— Cross-Sell Offer: [INCENTIVE IF ANY — e.g., "15% off as a current customer," "free shipping when added to your order," or leave blank]

The key phrase in this prompt is "make the logic of the recommendation explicit." A cross-sell email that says "you bought X, so you might like Y" without explaining why feels random. An email that says "most customers who buy our yoga mat add the foam roller within 30 days because muscles need recovery as much as the workout" makes the recommendation feel like advice, not a sales push. That framing is the difference between a 3% click rate and a 12% click rate on post-purchase emails.

Prompt 9: Seasonal Campaign Copy

Seasonal campaigns — Black Friday, holiday gift guides, Valentine's Day, summer sales — represent the highest-revenue periods of the year for most e-commerce stores. But seasonal copy is among the hardest to write well because the same season means the same clichés: "perfect gift for everyone," "holiday magic," "celebrate in style." This prompt generates seasonal campaign copy that cuts through the noise with customer-specific language, concrete benefits, and urgency framing that goes beyond countdown timers.

Prompt EC-09: Seasonal Campaign Copy
Act as an e-commerce seasonal campaign strategist who specializes in cutting through holiday marketing noise with differentiated copy. Write a complete seasonal campaign copy package for the season and store below. Avoid all seasonal clichés — no "perfect gift," no "holiday magic," no "tis the season."

Deliverables:
— Campaign headline (hero banner): 6–10 words that lead with the customer or outcome, not the season
— Campaign subheadline: 15–20 words. Specific, benefit-driven.
— Email subject line (3 variations): Under 50 characters each. One curiosity, one benefit, one urgency angle.
— Email body (100–130 words): Opens with a seasonal insight specific to your customer, then delivers the offer, then creates urgency with a real deadline
— Instagram caption: 120–180 characters for the hook + 4–6 lines of body + CTA
— Paid ad headline (3 variations): Under 30 characters each for Google, or under 40 for Meta

Rules: The season is context, not the message. The customer's desire is the message. Write to what they want to achieve or give during this season, not the season itself.

Variables:
— Store Name: [STORE NAME]
— Season/Holiday: [e.g., "Black Friday," "Christmas gift guide," "Valentine's Day," "Back to School," "Summer Sale"]
— Product Category: [WHAT IS ON SALE OR FEATURED]
— Campaign Offer: [DISCOUNT OR SPECIAL OFFER]
— Campaign Deadline: [END DATE OR URGENCY]
— Target Customer Seasonal Need: [WHAT THIS CUSTOMER WANTS DURING THIS SEASON — e.g., "to give a gift that does not feel generic," "to actually stick to a fitness goal this January," "to look good at holiday parties without overspending"]

The rule "the season is context, not the message" is the most important principle for seasonal copy. Black Friday campaigns that lead with the customer's desire — "finally get the skincare routine that actually works" — convert better than campaigns that lead with "Black Friday is here!" The season creates the opportunity and deadline; the customer's desire creates the motivation to act. Good seasonal copy does both jobs.

Prompt 10: Influencer Collaboration Brief

Influencer marketing fails most e-commerce brands not because of reach, but because of brief quality. A vague brief produces generic sponsored content that the influencer's audience recognizes and ignores. A specific, well-structured brief gives creators the information they need to make content that feels authentic to their voice while communicating the key messages that drive your conversions. This prompt generates a comprehensive influencer brief that covers all the details creators need to produce content that converts — without scripting them in a way that kills authenticity.

Prompt EC-10: Influencer Collaboration Brief
Act as an influencer marketing manager who writes briefs that result in high-converting, authentic creator content. Write a comprehensive influencer collaboration brief for the campaign below. The brief must give the creator enough direction to hit the key messages while leaving enough creative freedom to keep their content authentic.

Brief structure:
— Campaign Overview (2–3 sentences): What the campaign is about, the objective, and who the product is for
— Product Details: Name, key benefits (3 max), differentiator, and how to use it
— Must-Mention Points (3 max): The non-negotiable messages that must appear in the content. Be specific. "Mention the 30-day guarantee" is better than "talk about our policies."
— What NOT to Say: 3–5 specific things to avoid (competitor comparisons, unverified claims, discount language, etc.)
— Creative Direction: Suggested content angles, hooks, and formats — presented as options, not requirements
— Deliverables: Exactly what you need (number of posts, format, story frames, link in bio, etc.)
— Discount Code: Creator-specific code and what it offers followers
— Timeline: Key dates for draft approval, posting, and performance review

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product: [PRODUCT NAME AND BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
— Campaign Goal: [PRIMARY OBJECTIVE — e.g., "drive first-time purchases," "increase brand awareness in [niche]," "generate UGC for paid ads"]
— Target Audience (creator's followers): [WHO FOLLOWS THIS CREATOR — e.g., "fitness enthusiasts 25–40," "home cooks who meal prep," "sustainable fashion shoppers"]
— Key Product Benefits: [TOP 3 BENEFITS]
— Unique Selling Point: [ONE CLEAR DIFFERENTIATOR]
— Creator's Discount Code: [CODE AND OFFER — e.g., "CREATOR15 for 15% off"]

5 Tips for Getting the Best Results from E-commerce AI Prompts

The 15 prompts above give you the frameworks. These five principles ensure you apply them effectively.

1. Fill In Every Variable Before Running the Prompt

The most common mistake with e-commerce AI prompts is running them with placeholder variables or vague inputs. If your prompt says "Target Customer: [WHO BUYS THIS]" and you fill it in as "people who like fitness," the output will be generic. If you fill it in as "remote workers aged 28–42 who spend 8+ hours at a desk and have chronic lower back pain from sitting," the AI has enough specificity to write copy that resonates. Spend 5 minutes filling in every variable accurately before running any prompt. The output quality increases proportionally to the specificity of your inputs.

2. Use Real Review Language in Your Variables

Your customer reviews contain the exact language your best prospects use to describe their problems and desires. Before filling in the "Target Customer" and "Primary Outcome" variables in any prompt, read your 20 most recent 5-star reviews and pull out the specific phrases customers use. If customers consistently say "I finally stopped spending 45 minutes curling my hair every morning," that phrase belongs in your prompt variables — not a paraphrase of it. AI-generated copy that uses your customers' actual language converts at significantly higher rates than copy written in marketing speak.

3. Generate Multiple Variations and A/B Test

Run each prompt 2–3 times with slightly different variable inputs to generate multiple copy variations. For product pages, test different above-the-fold headlines. For email sequences, test different subject line angles. For ad copy, run multiple variations simultaneously in your ad platform. E-commerce brands that continuously A/B test AI-generated copy variations find winning angles faster than brands that rely on a single "best" version. The marginal cost of generating an additional variation with AI is essentially zero — the A/B test insights compound over time.

4. Combine Prompts for Full-Funnel Campaigns

The 15 prompts in this guide are designed to work together. A product launch campaign uses Prompt 3 (homepage hero) for the launch announcement, Prompt 6 (launch sequence) for the email flow, Prompt 5 (flash sale) adapted for the launch offer, Prompt 10 (influencer brief) for creator amplification, and Prompt 11 (UGC campaign) to build social proof for the product pages. Running prompts in combination produces coordinated campaigns with consistent messaging across every touchpoint — which amplifies conversion rates compared to executing each channel in isolation.

5. Update Variables Quarterly and After Major Milestones

Your social proof variables (review counts, star ratings) grow over time. Your differentiators may change as competitors evolve. New seasonal campaigns require updated timing variables. Refresh the variables in your highest-performing prompts quarterly — especially review count, current offer, and competitive positioning. E-commerce brands that run the same prompts with updated variables every quarter consistently outperform those who generate copy once and let it run without refreshing. AI copy ages in proportion to how current your inputs are.
