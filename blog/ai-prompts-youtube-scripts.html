10 AI Prompts for YouTube Scripts That Retain Viewers

ContentKit AI•14 min read

AI prompts for YouTube scripts are structured instructions that generate complete video scripts — hooks, body, CTAs, and closings — built around proven retention frameworks that keep viewers watching past the critical 30-second drop-off point. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every major YouTube format, each engineered with audience retention psychology, open-loop structures, and timed re-engagement hooks that match how top-performing channels script their content.

YouTube is the second-largest search engine on Earth, with over 2.7 billion monthly active users and more than 500 hours of video uploaded every minute. In that volume, the difference between a video that earns 50,000 views and one that earns 500,000 is rarely production quality — it is script structure. The algorithm rewards watch time. Watch time rewards retention. Retention is determined, overwhelmingly, by the first 30 seconds and the re-engagement framework woven through the rest of the video.

Most creators write YouTube scripts the way they write essays: introduction, body, conclusion. That structure kills retention. Viewers do not owe you their attention. If they do not feel the pull of a curiosity gap or the promise of a clear payoff in the first ten seconds, they click away — and YouTube logs that drop-off as a signal to show your video to fewer people. A script that earns 65% average viewer retention gets pushed by the algorithm. A script that earns 35% gets buried.

This is where structured ai prompts for youtube scripts change the equation. Instead of writing from a blank page, you paste a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude, fill in your topic and audience, and receive a complete script built on the same frameworks used by channels with hundreds of thousands of subscribers: the 4-part hook, the open loop, the pattern interrupt, and the payoff bridge. The AI handles the structural heavy lifting. You handle the creative direction and on-camera delivery.

The 10 prompts below cover every major YouTube format: hook and intro scripts for the critical first 30 seconds, tutorial and how-to scripts with timed checkpoints, listicle scripts with ranked reveal structures, storytelling scripts with narrative arcs, product review scripts with balanced credibility frameworks, YouTube Shorts scripts for 60-second content, video descriptions with timestamps, title and thumbnail copywriting, channel trailer scripts, and YouTube pre-roll ad scripts. Each prompt includes retention-specific instructions that match the format's unique viewer psychology.

Why AI Prompts Produce Better YouTube Scripts

YouTube's algorithm has one primary optimization target: watch time. Every metric the platform tracks — click-through rate, average view duration, audience retention percentage, re-watch rate — is a proxy for how long people spend watching videos on YouTube. Channels that consistently earn high watch time get recommended. Channels that do not, get buried.

Script structure is the most controllable variable in the watch time equation. Here is why AI prompts that specify retention frameworks produce better scripts than scripts written without them.

Open Loops Create Compulsion to Keep Watching

An open loop is a question, promise, or reveal that your brain cannot close until you get the answer. "Before I show you the three prompts, I need to tell you about the mistake almost everyone makes first" is an open loop. The viewer cannot leave without finding out what the mistake is. Top YouTube scripts layer multiple open loops — one in the hook, one at each chapter break, one going into the middle section — so there is always a reason to stay one more minute. AI prompts that explicitly instruct the model to "open a loop at the 2-minute mark that resolves at the 6-minute mark" produce scripts with this structure baked in. Writing it from scratch requires conscious effort most creators skip.

Pattern Interrupts Reset Attention Every 90 Seconds

Human attention resets approximately every 60–90 seconds. After that window, the brain starts wandering unless something new pulls it back. Pattern interrupts are the technique top creators use to reset attention: a shift in pace, a bold statement, a surprising fact, a direct call to the viewer by name ("If you are still watching at this point, here is what most people miss"). AI prompts that specify "insert a pattern interrupt at the 90-second mark" generate these resets systematically throughout the script, maintaining retention across longer videos without requiring the creator to remember to do it manually.

Frameworks Beat Inspiration

The best YouTube scripts are not written from inspiration — they are built on frameworks. The AIDA framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action) structures hooks. The PAS framework (Problem, Agitation, Solution) structures tutorial videos. The story arc (Setup, Conflict, Resolution) structures storytelling content. When you tell an AI to "write a tutorial script using the PAS framework with a pattern interrupt every 90 seconds and an open loop going into the mid-roll position," the output follows a structure that is statistically associated with higher retention. The 10 prompts below encode these frameworks as explicit instructions — so you get framework-driven output without needing to know every framework by name.

The bottom line: ai prompts for youtube scripts do not replace your expertise, your on-camera presence, or your creative vision. They give you a structurally sound script that the algorithm rewards — so you spend less time rewriting and more time creating. Here are the 10 prompts.

10 AI Prompts for YouTube Scripts

Prompt 1: YouTube Hook/Intro Script (First 30 Seconds)

The first 30 seconds of a YouTube video determine everything. YouTube's own data shows that the sharpest retention drop-off happens in the first 30 seconds — not the middle, not the end, but the very beginning. Viewers who make it past 30 seconds are significantly more likely to reach the 50% mark. That means your hook is not just important — it is the highest-leverage 200 words in your entire video. This prompt generates a 4-part hook built on the Question Hook, Credibility Bridge, Stakes Statement, and Viewing Promise framework used by channels with 1M+ subscribers.

Prompt YT-01: YouTube Hook/Intro Script (First 30 Seconds)
Act as a YouTube scriptwriter who specializes in audience retention for the first 30 seconds of video content. Write a hook and intro script for the video described below. The hook must stop the viewer from clicking away before the content begins.

Use this 4-part framework:
— Part 1 — Question Hook (5–10 seconds): Open with a provocative question or bold statement that creates an immediate curiosity gap. Do NOT start with "Hey guys, welcome back to my channel." Start mid-thought, as if continuing a conversation already in progress.
— Part 2 — Credibility Bridge (5 seconds): One sentence that establishes why you are the right person to answer this question. Be specific, not vague ("I have tested 47 prompts over 90 days" not "I am an expert").
— Part 3 — Stakes Statement (10 seconds): Tell the viewer what they stand to gain by watching (specific outcome) and what they risk by not watching (cost of inaction). Make it concrete.
— Part 4 — Viewing Promise (5 seconds): Deliver a roadmap of what the video covers — 3 specific things they will learn. This commits the viewer to a contract: "Stay with me and I will deliver X, Y, and Z."

Total word count: 150–200 words. Write for spoken delivery, not reading — short sentences, natural contractions, occasional emphasis markers like [PAUSE] or [SLOW DOWN].

Variables:
— Video Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "how to grow a YouTube channel from 0 to 10K subscribers," "why most people fail at intermittent fasting," "the best AI tools for freelancers in 2026"]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS WATCHING — e.g., "beginner YouTubers," "people trying to lose weight," "freelancers and solopreneurs"]
— Creator Credibility: [YOUR PROOF POINT — e.g., "grew my channel from 0 to 50K in 8 months," "lost 40 lbs using this method," "built a 6-figure freelance business with AI"]
— Key Payoff: [WHAT THE VIEWER WILL LEARN OR BE ABLE TO DO AFTER WATCHING]
— Channel Tone: [TONE — e.g., "conversational and direct," "energetic and fast-paced," "calm and educational"]

Example Output (YouTube Channel Growth Topic):

Most people who start a YouTube channel quit within 90 days. Not because they lack talent. Not because they lack equipment. Because nobody told them the one thing that actually makes the algorithm push your videos. [PAUSE]

I grew from zero to 50,000 subscribers in 8 months. Not with better editing. Not with expensive gear. With a script structure I am going to show you today.

Here is what is at stake: if you keep posting without this structure, you are going to keep getting the same 200-view videos. Frustrating. Demoralizing. And completely avoidable.

In the next 12 minutes I am going to show you the exact hook framework that doubled my retention rate, the re-engagement technique I use every 90 seconds, and the CTA sequence that turned casual viewers into 4,000 new subscribers last month. Stay with me.

Notice what the hook does not include: a channel intro, a "smash that subscribe button," or a slow setup explaining the premise. It opens mid-thought, delivers the stakes immediately, and commits to a specific payoff. That is the structure the algorithm rewards.

Prompt 2: Tutorial/How-To Video Script

Tutorial videos are the highest-intent content category on YouTube. When someone searches "how to edit videos in Premiere Pro" or "how to make sourdough bread," they are actively trying to accomplish something — and they will watch the entire video if it helps them do it. The challenge is structure: most tutorial creators front-load information without considering the viewer's learning curve, include unnecessary tangents, and bury the most important steps halfway through. This prompt generates a full tutorial script with timed checkpoints, a PAS framework opening, and re-engagement hooks at every major transition.

Prompt YT-02: Tutorial/How-To Video Script
Act as a YouTube scriptwriter specializing in tutorial and educational content. Write a complete tutorial video script for the topic below. The script must maintain audience retention through a structured how-to framework with built-in re-engagement hooks at every step transition.

Script structure:
— Hook (0–30 seconds): Open with the problem the viewer is trying to solve. Do not start with the solution — start with the pain. Use PAS: Problem, Agitation, Solution preview.
— Quick win (30–60 seconds): Give one immediately actionable tip before the main content begins. This rewards viewers for staying past the hook and builds trust.
— Overview (60–90 seconds): Number the steps. "I am going to walk you through 5 steps. Step 1 takes about 3 minutes. Step 5 is where most people make the mistake that wastes all their effort."
— Steps 1 through [N] (main body): Each step gets a sub-hook (what this step accomplishes), the instruction, a common mistake warning, and a transition bridge that opens a loop to the next step. Include a [PATTERN INTERRUPT] marker every 90 seconds of estimated speaking time.
— Recap (last 60 seconds): Summarize the steps as a numbered checklist. Reinforce the payoff: what can they now do that they could not before?
— CTA (last 20 seconds): One specific call to action. Link to related video for next-step viewers.

Word count target: approximately 130 words per minute of intended video length.

Variables:
— Tutorial Topic: [WHAT YOU ARE TEACHING — e.g., "how to set up a Shopify store," "how to do keyword research for YouTube," "how to make cold brew coffee at home"]
— Target Audience Skill Level: [BEGINNER / INTERMEDIATE / ADVANCED]
— Number of Steps: [HOW MANY STEPS THE TUTORIAL HAS]
— Intended Video Length: [MINUTES — e.g., "8 minutes," "12 minutes," "15 minutes"]
— Common Mistake to Address: [THE ERROR MOST BEGINNERS MAKE — this becomes a key retention hook mid-video]
— Final Outcome: [WHAT THE VIEWER CAN DO AFTER COMPLETING ALL STEPS]
— Related Video CTA: [THE NEXT VIDEO YOU WANT THEM TO WATCH]

The "quick win" in the first 60 seconds is the most underused retention tool in tutorial content. Viewers who receive value before the main content begins feel invested in the video. They have already gotten something — now they want the rest. This prompt makes the quick win a required script element, not an optional add-on.

Prompt 3: Listicle Video Script ("Top 10..." Format)

Listicle videos — "Top 10," "7 Best," "5 Mistakes" — are among the highest-clicked formats on YouTube because the number in the title creates an implicit viewing contract. The viewer knows exactly what they are getting and how long the commitment is. But most listicle creators simply read their list in order, which produces a flat retention curve. This prompt generates a listicle script with a ranked reveal structure: items are delivered in ascending order of impact (save the best for last), with a teaser at the top for the number-one item that keeps viewers watching through the entire list.

Prompt YT-03: Listicle Video Script ("Top 10..." Format)
Act as a YouTube scriptwriter specializing in high-retention listicle content. Write a complete "Top [N]" video script using a ranked reveal structure designed to maximize viewer retention through the entire video.

Retention framework for listicle videos:
— Hook (first 30 seconds): Open by teasing the #1 item without naming it ("The last one on this list is the reason most [audience] never [achieve goal] — and almost nobody talks about it"). This creates an open loop that lasts the entire video.
— Number each item in ascending order (worst/obvious first, best/surprising last). This is counterintuitive to most creators but creates a "rewarded patience" effect: viewers who stay get progressively better information.
— Each item structure: Item name and number → What it is (one sentence) → Why it matters (the specific result or consequence) → How to apply it (one concrete action) → Transition bridge that teases the next item.
— Mid-video re-engagement: At the halfway point, reference the #1 item again ("We are halfway through. I promised you the one thing most people completely ignore — we are almost there.").
— #1 item reveal: Spend 50% more time on the top item than on any other. This is the payoff viewers waited for — deliver it fully.
— Outro and CTA: Recap the list in reverse (best to worst recap), give one action item, and direct to a related video.

Variables:
— List Topic: [WHAT THE LIST IS ABOUT — e.g., "AI tools for content creators," "mistakes beginner investors make," "ways to grow on social media faster"]
— Number of Items: [HOW MANY ITEMS ON THE LIST — e.g., 5, 7, 10]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS WATCHING]
— The #1 Item (Most Surprising or Valuable): [YOUR BEST POINT — this is what the hook teases]
— Channel Tone: [CONVERSATIONAL / ENERGETIC / EDUCATIONAL / DRY HUMOR]
— Intended Video Length: [MINUTES]

Example Hook (AI Tools for Content Creators):

I am going to show you 7 AI tools that content creators are using right now to produce more in less time. But I am saving the last one for the end — because it is the one tool that almost nobody is talking about, and it is the reason some channels are growing 10x faster than yours. [PAUSE] We are starting with the obvious ones and building to the one that actually changes everything. Let's go.

The ascending order structure feels counterintuitive to most creators, who want to lead with their best material. But the data supports it: videos that save their most valuable content for later see significantly higher average view duration than videos that front-load their best points. The tease at the beginning does the work of keeping viewers invested through the middle.

Prompt 4: Storytelling Video Script

Story-driven YouTube videos outperform information-only videos on nearly every retention metric because the human brain is wired to follow narrative arcs. We stay until the resolution. We want to know what happens next. The challenge for content creators is structuring a story that serves both the narrative and the informational goal of the video — without turning the story into a rambling anecdote that loses viewers who came for advice. This prompt generates a storytelling script built on the three-act narrative structure with informational payoffs woven into each act.

Prompt YT-04: Storytelling Video Script
Act as a YouTube scriptwriter who specializes in narrative-driven content that blends personal story with practical takeaways. Write a storytelling video script using a three-act narrative structure. The script should feel like a compelling story while delivering concrete, actionable value to the viewer.

Three-act structure:
— Act 1 — Setup (first 20% of video): Introduce the protagonist (you, a client, or a character), establish their goal, and reveal the conflict or obstacle. End Act 1 with a "point of no return" moment that raises the stakes and makes the viewer need to know what happens.
— Act 2 — Conflict (middle 60% of video): Show the attempts, failures, and discoveries. This is where the practical lessons live — each obstacle the protagonist overcomes teaches the viewer something concrete. Use pattern interrupts at 90-second intervals. Include one major "dark night of the soul" moment at the 60% mark: the lowest point before the breakthrough.
— Act 3 — Resolution (last 20% of video): Deliver the breakthrough and the result. State the lesson explicitly. Bridge from the story to the viewer's situation: "If you are in the same place I was in Act 1, here is the exact thing you need to do right now."
— CTA: Invite viewers to share their own story in the comments (high-engagement prompt) and link to the resource or next video.

Tone instructions: First-person. Vulnerable but not self-indulgent. The story serves the viewer — every narrative detail should either build emotional investment or teach a lesson. Cut any detail that does neither.

Variables:
— Story Subject: [THE STORY TO TELL — e.g., "how I failed my first business and what I learned," "the day I almost quit YouTube," "how I went from $0 to $10K freelancing in 6 months"]
— Main Lesson: [THE CORE TAKEAWAY FOR VIEWERS]
— Target Audience: [WHO THIS STORY IS FOR]
— Protagonist Starting Point: [WHERE THE STORY BEGINS — the before state]
— Resolution and Result: [THE OUTCOME — be specific with numbers or measurable results]
— Intended Video Length: [MINUTES]

The "dark night of the soul" at the 60% mark is the most important structural element in a storytelling video. This is the moment when all hope seems lost — the moment before the breakthrough. Viewers who reach the dark night are emotionally invested enough to stay for the resolution. Skipping it produces a flat story arc that loses viewers before the payoff.

Prompt 5: Product Review Script

Product review videos are the highest-converting content type on YouTube for affiliate and direct revenue — but they are also the format most likely to feel promotional and untrustworthy. Viewers watch product reviews specifically to get an honest assessment before making a purchase decision. A review script that reads like an ad will earn low retention and low affiliate conversions. This prompt generates a review script built on the Honest Review Framework: structured with genuine pros, genuine cons, and a clear recommendation based on specific buyer profiles.

Prompt YT-05: Product Review Script
Act as a YouTube scriptwriter specializing in honest product review content that builds trust and drives affiliate conversions. Write a product review script that feels genuinely balanced — not promotional — while guiding the viewer toward a confident purchase decision.

Review script structure:
— Hook (0–30 seconds): Open with the buying question the viewer has, not the product's name. "I spent [X days / weeks] testing [product] so you do not have to make the same mistake I almost made." Tease the most surprising finding.
— Context section (30–90 seconds): Who this product is for. Who it is not for. Your testing conditions and timeframe. This establishes credibility without claiming you are an expert.
— Feature-by-feature breakdown (main body): Cover 5–7 features or use cases. For each: what it promises, what it actually does, and your honest rating (not just positive). Use the format: [Feature] → [Claim] → [Reality] → [Verdict: Worth it / Overhyped / Depends].
— Honest cons section: Dedicate at least 2 minutes to what does not work, what is disappointing, or what limitations exist. Viewers trust reviews that include real criticisms.
— Who should buy it (buyer profile section): Name 2–3 specific buyer types who will get value. Name 1–2 who should skip it or consider an alternative.
— Final verdict and CTA: Clear recommendation. Affiliate link mention that is transparent ("I may earn a commission but my opinion is my own"). Related comparison video CTA.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT OR SERVICE BEING REVIEWED]
— Product Category: [CATEGORY — e.g., "AI writing tool," "fitness tracker," "espresso machine," "project management software"]
— Testing Period: [HOW LONG YOU USED IT — e.g., "30 days," "6 weeks," "3 months"]
— Most Surprising Finding: [THE UNEXPECTED PRO OR CON THAT WILL HOOK THE VIEWER]
— Main Competitors: [2–3 COMPETING PRODUCTS FOR CONTEXT]
— Your Recommendation: [BUY / SKIP / ONLY IF... — be specific]

The dedicated "cons section" is the single biggest trust-builder in a product review. Most viewers watch reviews specifically because they suspect promotional content will hide the downsides. When your script explicitly dedicates time to what does not work, viewers interpret the rest of your review as credible — and their purchase confidence in your recommendation increases. Counterintuitively, highlighting real flaws increases conversion rates for affiliate reviews.

Prompt 6: YouTube Shorts Script (60 Seconds)

YouTube Shorts is YouTube's fastest-growing format, delivering channel growth 3–5x faster than long-form videos for many creators. But Shorts require a completely different script architecture. There is no time for context-setting, no room for a gradual buildup, and no tolerance for a slow hook — viewers can swipe away in under a second. A Shorts script must earn attention in the first 2–3 words, sustain it with zero filler, and land a payoff in 60 seconds or less. This prompt generates a 4-section Shorts script timed to the second.

Prompt YT-06: YouTube Shorts Script (60 Seconds)
Act as a YouTube Shorts scriptwriter who specializes in vertical video content with maximum swipe-stop power. Write a 60-second Shorts script that hooks in the first 3 seconds and sustains attention through a punchy, fast-paced delivery with zero filler.

60-second script structure (timed to the second):
— Hook (0–3 seconds): The first words must be the most compelling thing in the video. Do NOT say "Hey everyone" or introduce yourself. Start with the most surprising statement, question, or fact. Examples: "Most people do this completely wrong." / "I tested this for 30 days. Here is what actually happened." / "[Controversial claim] and I have the receipts."
— Setup (3–15 seconds): Establish the context or problem in 3–4 sentences. Speak fast. No wasted words. Every sentence must earn its place.
— Payoff (15–50 seconds): Deliver the value. 3–5 punchy points or one clear step-by-step process. Use on-screen text cues with [TEXT: ...] markers. Include one [PATTERN INTERRUPT] midway — a sudden change in pace, a bold statement, or a direct address ("Stay with me — this is the part everyone skips").
— CTA (50–60 seconds): One action. Follow for more. Comment your answer. Watch the full video. Pick one and state it clearly. End on a punchy final line, not a slow fade.

Total word count: 120–180 words. Mark on-screen text suggestions with [TEXT: ...]. Mark delivery cues with [PAUSE], [FASTER], or [EMPHASIS].

Variables:
— Short Topic: [WHAT THE SHORT IS ABOUT — single, specific point. Not a broad topic.]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS SWIPING THROUGH THEIR FEED]
— Core Message: [THE ONE THING THE VIEWER SHOULD REMEMBER]
— Hook Type: [CHOOSE ONE: Surprising Fact / Common Mistake / Controversial Opinion / Quick Tutorial / Before-After]
— CTA Goal: [FOLLOW / COMMENT / WATCH FULL VIDEO / SAVE]

Example Shorts Script (AI Prompt Mistake):

[0–3s] Most people are using AI prompts completely wrong. [EMPHASIS]

[3–15s] They write vague prompts and get vague output. Then they blame the AI. The problem is not the AI — it is the prompt structure. [TEXT: The problem is your prompt]

[15–50s] Here is what changes everything. Add three things to every prompt: First — a role. "Act as a [expert]." [TEXT: #1 Give it a role] Second — a constraint. "Under 100 words" or "in bullet points." [TEXT: #2 Add a constraint] Third — an example. Show it what good looks like. [TEXT: #3 Give an example] [PATTERN INTERRUPT] I know, sounds obvious. But 90% of people skip at least one of these. Every single time.

[50–60s] Try it right now and comment what changed. Follow for one AI tip a day. [FASTER]

The [TEXT: ...] markers are essential for Shorts scripts because on-screen text is how Shorts retain viewers whose audio is off. YouTube reports that a significant portion of Shorts are watched without sound. A Shorts script that relies entirely on spoken words loses that audience completely. Writing the on-screen text into the script ensures your video communicates with or without audio.

Prompt 7: Video Description + Timestamps

Video descriptions are the most underutilized SEO asset on YouTube. A 5,000-character description that includes the target keyword, semantic keyword variants, timestamps, and clear links is the difference between a video that ranks in YouTube search and a video that exists only in your subscribers' feeds. Most creators write two-sentence descriptions and leave thousands of impressions on the table. This prompt generates a complete, SEO-optimized video description with timestamps, keyword variants, and links — ready to paste directly into YouTube Studio.

Prompt YT-07: Video Description + Timestamps
Act as a YouTube SEO specialist who writes video descriptions optimized for both YouTube search ranking and viewer conversion. Write a complete video description for the video below, including timestamps, keywords, and calls to action.

Description structure:

— Paragraph 2 (expanded summary): 3–5 sentences describing what the video covers, using natural language that includes 3–5 semantic keyword variants of the target keyword. Do not keyword-stuff — write for a reader, not a bot.

Variables:

— Channel Niche: [YOUR CHANNEL TOPIC]
— Semantic Keywords: [3–5 RELATED TERMS TO INCLUDE — e.g., "youtube script template, how to write a youtube script, youtube retention tips"]

The above-the-fold text — the first 2–3 lines visible before "Show more" — is the highest-priority section of the description because it appears in YouTube search results. Viewers decide whether to click based on this text alongside the thumbnail and title. Most creators waste this space on "Hi! In today's video..." — a phrase that communicates no value and includes no keywords. This prompt treats the above-the-fold text as the most valuable real estate in the description.

Prompt 8: YouTube Title + Thumbnail Ideas

Click-through rate (CTR) from impressions is determined almost entirely by your title and thumbnail. YouTube's algorithm delivers impressions based on watch time history — but whether viewers click when served an impression is up to your title and thumbnail. A 1% improvement in CTR on a video with 100,000 impressions means 1,000 additional viewers with zero additional algorithm favor. This prompt generates 10 title variations using five proven title frameworks plus five thumbnail concepts with specific text overlay and visual direction for each.

Prompt YT-08: YouTube Title + Thumbnail Ideas
Act as a YouTube growth strategist specializing in click-through rate optimization. Generate 10 title variations and 5 thumbnail concepts for the video described below. Titles and thumbnails must work together to create a compelling reason to click without being clickbait — the video must deliver on every promise made in the title.

Generate 2 titles using each of these 5 frameworks:
— Framework 1 — The Curiosity Gap: Promise something surprising without revealing it fully. ("The [Topic] Strategy Nobody Is Talking About" / "Why [Common Belief] Is Wrong")
— Framework 2 — The Specific Outcome: Lead with the result, include a number. ("How I [Result] in [Timeframe]" / "[X] Ways to [Achieve Goal] in [Timeframe]")
— Framework 3 — The Warning: Address a fear or mistake. ("Stop Doing [Common Thing] Until You Watch This" / "[X] [Topic] Mistakes That Are Killing Your [Goal]")
— Framework 4 — The Comparison or Controversy: Take a position. ("[Option A] vs [Option B]: The Truth After [X] Months" / "I Tried [Popular Method] for [Timeframe]. Here Is What Happened.")
— Framework 5 — The Direct Benefit: State the value plainly with specifics. ("[X] [Topic] Tips That [Specific Result]" / "The Only [Topic] Guide You Will Ever Need")

For each of the 5 thumbnail concepts, provide:
— Visual composition: What appears in the image (person, product, graphic, before/after)
— Text overlay (3–5 words max): The bold, high-contrast text on the thumbnail
— Emotion or expression: If a person appears, what emotion they convey
— Color contrast direction: Primary and secondary colors for visibility in small size

Variables:
— Video Topic: [WHAT THE VIDEO COVERS]
— Target Audience: [WHO YOU WANT TO CLICK]
— Key Result or Payoff: [THE MAIN VALUE THE VIEWER RECEIVES]
— Most Surprising or Counterintuitive Point: [YOUR BEST HOOK MATERIAL]
— Channel Visual Style: [DESCRIBE YOUR THUMBNAIL STYLE — e.g., "bold text, bright colors, close-up face shots" or "minimalist, dark background, white text"]

The thumbnail and title must form a single coherent promise — not two competing messages. The most common CTR mistake is a title that says one thing and a thumbnail that shows something unrelated. A title saying "I Lost $50K in One Day" paired with a thumbnail showing a shocked expression and text overlay "LOST IT ALL" creates a unified emotional hook. A title saying the same thing paired with a thumbnail of a laptop on a desk creates dissonance that suppresses clicks even when the content is strong.

Prompt 9: Channel Trailer Script

Your channel trailer is shown to non-subscribers as the first video on your channel homepage. It is the highest-stakes 60–90 seconds of content on your channel because it converts random visitors into subscribers — or sends them away forever. Most channel trailers fail because they try to explain everything about the channel instead of doing one thing: making a specific promise to a specific audience and giving them a compelling reason to subscribe right now. This prompt generates a channel trailer script built on the subscriber conversion framework used by channels that convert at 3%+ from channel page visits.

Prompt YT-09: Channel Trailer Script
Act as a YouTube channel strategist who writes channel trailers that convert first-time visitors into subscribers. Write a 60–90 second channel trailer script that makes a specific promise to a specific audience and delivers a compelling reason to subscribe.

Channel trailer framework:
— Opening (0–10 seconds): Address the target viewer directly and immediately. "If you are a [specific audience] who wants to [specific goal], you are in the right place." No preamble. No generic "welcome to my channel."
— Channel promise (10–30 seconds): State exactly what you publish, how often, and what the viewer will be able to do after watching your videos. Tips for Using AI to Write Better YouTube Scripts

The 10 prompts above give you structurally sound, retention-optimized scripts. These five tips ensure you use the output effectively for your specific channel and audience.

1. Always Rewrite the Hook in Your Own Voice

The hook is the highest-impact section of any YouTube script, and it is also the section most likely to feel generic if you paste AI output verbatim. After generating the hook with the prompt, rewrite the first two sentences in your own words. Add a specific personal detail, a niche reference your audience will recognize, or a verbal tic you use on camera. The rest of the script can stay closer to the AI output, but the hook needs to sound unmistakably like you — because your regular viewers will hear the difference immediately.

2. Use [PATTERN INTERRUPT] Markers as Editing Checkpoints

The prompts in this article instruct the AI to insert [PATTERN INTERRUPT] markers approximately every 90 seconds. When you receive the script, treat these markers as editing checkpoints. At each marker, ask: is the viewer still engaged here? What can I do at this moment to reset their attention? You can use a change of pace, a direct question to the audience, a surprising statistic, or a visual change on camera. The marker tells you where to intervene — but the type of interrupt should match your channel style and the specific context of that moment in the video.

3. Time Your Script Before Recording

AI-generated scripts frequently run 10–20% longer or shorter than the intended video length because AI models estimate speaking pace differently. Before you record, read your script aloud at your natural delivery speed and time it. If a 10-minute script times out at 8 minutes, add depth to two or three sections. If it runs 13 minutes, identify the sections with the lowest information density and cut them. The timed read-through also reveals awkward phrasing, sentences that are too long to deliver naturally, and moments where you need a breath cue.

4. Generate Titles and Descriptions Separately from the Script

Do not ask the AI to write your title, description, and script in a single prompt. Each requires a different optimization framework: the script optimizes for retention, the title optimizes for CTR, and the description optimizes for search ranking. Running separate prompts for each — using Prompts 7 and 8 in this article alongside your script prompts — produces output that is purpose-built for each element rather than a generic response trying to do everything at once.

5. Iterate on Prompts Based on Your Retention Analytics

After publishing videos scripted with these prompts, review your YouTube Analytics retention curve after 14 days. Identify the specific timestamps where viewer drop-off spikes. Those timestamps reveal which structural elements need adjustment in your next script. If viewers consistently drop off at the 3-minute mark, your pattern interrupt is too late or too weak. If retention spikes back up at the 7-minute mark, your mid-video re-engagement hook is working — but viewers are drifting before they reach it. Feed this data back into your prompt variables: "My retention curves show a 15% drop at the 3-minute mark. Add a stronger pattern interrupt at the 3-minute point in this script."
