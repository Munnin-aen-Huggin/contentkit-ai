12 AI Prompts for Sales Funnel Copy (Top to Bottom)

ContentKit AI•March 4, 2026•14 min read

AI prompts for sales funnel copy are stage-specific instructions that generate persuasive copy for every touchpoint in your funnel — from the first cold-traffic ad a stranger sees to the post-purchase email a new customer receives — each calibrated to the buyer's awareness level, intent, and objections at that exact moment. This guide delivers 12 copy-paste prompts covering every funnel stage, built on proven direct-response frameworks that move prospects from unaware to converted.

Most marketing copy fails not because the words are bad, but because the words are wrong for the stage. A bottom-of-funnel sales page argument dropped into a cold-traffic ad sounds pushy and gets ignored. A top-of-funnel awareness angle written into a sales page loses the sale because it never asks for the order. The mismatch between funnel stage and copy approach is the single most common reason funnels underperform — and it is almost always invisible to the business running the funnel.

Here is what a properly stage-matched funnel looks like. At the top, cold strangers see an ad or read a blog post that matches their existing awareness — they have a problem or desire, and your content speaks to it without asking for anything. In the middle, those interested prospects exchange an email address for a lead magnet, then receive a nurture sequence that builds trust and moves them toward the offer. At the bottom, warm leads who are ready to buy see a sales page, urgency emails, and a friction-free checkout. After purchase, a thank-you page upsell and onboarding email sequence protect your customer lifetime value.

Writing all of this copy manually — with the right tone, framework, and persuasion level for each stage — can take weeks. These ai prompts for sales funnel copy compress that work into hours. Each prompt below is stage-specific: it tells the AI what the buyer knows, what they are feeling, what objections they hold, and what persuasion framework fits the moment. You fill in the variables. The AI does the writing.

Why Funnel Stage Determines Everything About Copy

Eugene Schwartz's concept of customer awareness levels is the foundation of direct-response copywriting. Schwartz identified five stages: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, and most-aware. Each stage requires a different copy approach because the buyer has different information, different objections, and different needs.

A cold-traffic Facebook ad talks to an unaware or problem-aware audience. The copy cannot reference your product by name because the reader does not know it exists. Instead, it leads with the problem or an unexpected insight that makes the reader think "that is exactly my situation." Compare that to a sales page, which talks to a product-aware or most-aware audience. These readers know what you sell and are weighing whether to buy. The copy can use your product name, highlight features, stack proof, and directly address price objections — because the reader is ready for that conversation.

This is why generic AI prompts for "write me some marketing copy" produce mediocre output. The AI has no idea where in the funnel this copy will live, who will read it, or what they already know. Stage-specific ai prompts for sales funnel copy solve this by giving the AI the buyer's awareness level, the persuasion objective, and the appropriate framework before it writes a single word.

12 AI Prompts for Sales Funnel Copy

Prompt 1: Top-of-Funnel — Awareness Ad (Cold Traffic)

Cold-traffic ads target people who have never heard of your brand. They are scrolling through social media, not looking for you, and will skip your ad in 1.5 seconds if it does not grab attention with something that feels immediately relevant to their life. The framework that works here is Problem-Agitate-Solve with a curiosity hook — lead with the problem the reader already has, amplify the pain briefly, then introduce a solution without over-explaining the product. The goal is not a sale. The goal is a click, a scroll stop, or a brand impression that starts the relationship.

Prompt SF-01: Top-of-Funnel Awareness Ad (Cold Traffic)
Act as a direct-response copywriter specializing in cold-traffic paid social ads (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok). Write a cold-traffic awareness ad for the audience below. The reader does not know this brand exists and is not actively looking for a solution. The ad must earn attention by leading with a problem, pain point, or insight the target audience already lives with.

Framework: Problem → Agitate → Soft Introduce Solution (PAS). Do NOT hard-sell. Do NOT ask for a purchase. The call-to-action leads to a free resource or blog post, not a product page.

Provide:
— 3 Hook variations (the first 1–2 lines that appear above the "See More" cut): Each under 125 characters. Each takes a different angle: problem-first, curiosity-first, and statistic/fact-first.
— 1 Full ad body (150–250 words): Problem-Agitate-Solution structure. Conversational tone. No corporate language. End with a soft CTA to a free resource.
— 3 Headline variations (below the image/video, 40 characters max each): Reinforce the promise of the free resource.
— 3 CTA button copy options: Choose from "Learn More," "Download," "Get Free Guide," "Read More," or write a custom option.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL — e.g., "email marketing software for e-commerce brands"]
— Target Audience: [WHO SEES THIS AD — e.g., "e-commerce founders doing $10K–$500K/month"]
— Core Problem: [THE PAIN POINT THIS AUDIENCE LIVES WITH — e.g., "spending money on ads but their email list makes almost nothing"]
— Free Resource: [WHAT THEY CLICK TO GET — e.g., "a free guide: 5 email sequences that recover abandoned carts"]
— Tone: [BRAND VOICE — e.g., "direct, warm, no fluff, like a smart friend giving advice"]

Hook Option 1 (Problem-first): Most e-commerce stores make 80% of revenue from 20% of customers — and barely email them.

Hook Option 2 (Curiosity-first): We looked at 500 e-commerce email accounts. Here is what the top 10% do differently.

Hook Option 3 (Stat-first): Email generates $42 for every $1 spent. Most stores are collecting $3.

Ad Body: If you are running ads and getting sales, but your email list is basically a graveyard — you are leaving real money on the table every single week. The problem is not your product. It is not your audience. It is the fact that most e-commerce brands treat email like a newsletter instead of a revenue channel. The top-performing stores send differently. They have sequences running automatically: abandoned cart, post-purchase upsell, win-back, browse abandonment. Each one converts while the founder sleeps. We put together a free breakdown of the 5 email sequences driving the most revenue for stores like yours. No pitch. Just the frameworks. → Grab the free guide below.

Notice how the example body never mentions a product or brand name. It only speaks to the reader's situation and offers something free. That is the exact tone cold-traffic copy requires — feel like help, not advertising.

Prompt 2: Top-of-Funnel — Blog Post / Content

Blog content is organic cold-traffic. Someone searches a problem on Google, finds your article, reads it, and enters your funnel by subscribing, downloading a lead magnet, or clicking a CTA. The copy challenge for blog content is different from ads: you have more space, but the reader came for information, not a pitch. The structure that works is Answer-First (give the reader what they came for immediately), then layer in depth, then introduce the lead magnet or product naturally at the end. This prompt generates a complete SEO-optimized blog outline plus an introduction and conclusion section ready to paste into your CMS.

Prompt SF-02: Top-of-Funnel Blog Post / Content
Act as an SEO content strategist and direct-response copywriter. Create a complete content brief and key copy sections for a top-of-funnel blog article. The reader arrives from organic search and has a specific question or problem. They are not aware of or interested in your product yet — they want the answer to their question.

Provide:
— SEO Title (under 60 characters): Includes the target keyword, promises a specific deliverable.
— Meta Description (under 155 characters): Includes keyword, specific benefit, and a reason to click.
— H1 Headline: More compelling than the SEO title. Punchy and benefit-focused.
— Answer-First Opening Paragraph (100–150 words): Immediately answer the reader's core question in the first paragraph. Do not make them scroll to find the answer.
— Full H2/H3 Outline: 6–8 sections covering the topic comprehensively. Each H2 is a keyword-rich subheading. Each H3 is a specific point within that section.
— Lead Magnet CTA Paragraph (2–3 sentences): Naturally introduces the lead magnet at the point in the article where the reader has learned enough to want more. Does not feel like an ad.
— Conclusion (100–150 words): Summarizes the key takeaways and leads naturally to the primary CTA.

Variables:
— Target Keyword: [PRIMARY KEYWORD — e.g., "how to build an email list"]
— Target Audience: [READER PROFILE — e.g., "small business owners just starting with email marketing"]
— Core Question the Article Answers: [SEARCH INTENT — e.g., "how do I build an email list from zero?"]
— Lead Magnet to Promote: [WHAT YOU WANT THEM TO DOWNLOAD OR SUBSCRIBE TO]
— Bottom-of-Article CTA: [PRODUCT OR RESOURCE — e.g., "ContentKit AI prompt library"]

The answer-first principle is worth emphasizing. Google's search quality guidelines explicitly reward content that satisfies user intent immediately. Readers who get their answer in paragraph one stay on the page longer and trust the source more. That trust is what makes them click the lead magnet CTA three scrolls later.

Prompt 3: Mid-Funnel — Lead Magnet Landing Page

A lead magnet landing page converts anonymous visitors into email subscribers. The reader is problem-aware — they arrived from your ad, blog post, or organic search — and they are willing to exchange an email for something valuable. Landing page copy lives or dies on three elements: a headline that communicates a specific outcome (not a vague benefit), a short benefits list that makes the value undeniable, and a form with zero friction. This prompt generates a complete lead magnet landing page with every copy element.

— Pre-headline (eyebrow text, under 40 characters): Qualifies the reader. Who is this for? E.g., "For E-Commerce Founders" or "Free for SaaS Marketers."
— 3 Headline variations: Each communicates a specific, measurable outcome of the lead magnet. One leads with the result, one leads with the timeframe, one leads with the problem it solves.
— Subheadline (1–2 sentences): Expands on the headline with specificity. What exactly will they learn or get?
— Benefits List (5 bullet points): Each bullet is an outcome, not a feature. Starts with an action verb. Specific, not vague.
— Social Proof Line (1 sentence): A stat, testimonial snippet, or number that validates the resource's value. E.g., "Downloaded by 12,000+ marketers."
— Form Label + Button Copy: The label above the email field and the button text. Both should reinforce the outcome, not just say "Submit."

Button Copy: Send Me the Free Templates →

Every element of a lead magnet landing page exists to reduce the perceived cost (giving up an email) and increase the perceived value (what they get). Specificity is the key mechanism: "7 plug-and-play templates" is worth more in the reader's mind than "email marketing resources," even if the actual content is identical.

Prompt 4: Mid-Funnel — Lead Magnet Delivery Email

The lead magnet delivery email is one of the most underestimated assets in the entire funnel. Open rates are sky-high — typically 60–80% because the subscriber is expecting the email — and yet most brands waste it with a single line: "Here is your download." This is the highest-attention moment in the entire email relationship. The delivery email should deliver the resource, set expectations for what comes next, give an immediate win from the content, and prime the reader for the relationship you are about to build.

Prompt SF-04: Mid-Funnel Lead Magnet Delivery Email
Act as an email marketing strategist. Write a lead magnet delivery email that delivers the promised resource AND sets up the email relationship for maximum engagement going forward. This email will have the highest open rate of any email this subscriber ever receives from us — treat it accordingly.

Structure:
— Subject Line (5 options): Each is short and direct. The reader is expecting this email — do not be clever, be clear. Variations: confirm delivery, tease the immediate win, use their name if available.
— Preview Text (5 options, paired with each subject line, under 90 characters each): Adds context or urgency.
— Email Body (300–400 words):
  1. Deliver the resource immediately in the first paragraph (link + brief description of where to start).
  2. Give one immediate actionable win from the resource (the single best tip or template to use first).
  3. Set expectations for the next email (tell them when it arrives and what it covers — creates anticipation).
  4. Invite a reply (ask one simple question to boost deliverability and start a conversation).
— P.S. Line: Soft-introduces the paid product without a hard pitch. One sentence. Curiosity-focused.

Variables:
— Lead Magnet Name and Link: [NAME AND DOWNLOAD LINK]
— Immediate Win to Highlight: [THE ONE THING THEY SHOULD DO FIRST — e.g., "install Template 3 before anything else — it alone recovers 15% of abandoned carts"]
— Next Email Topic: [WHAT EMAIL 2 COVERS — to create anticipation]
— Reply Question: [A SIMPLE QUESTION THAT STARTS A REAL CONVERSATION — e.g., "What is the biggest challenge in your email marketing right now?"]
— Product to Soft-Introduce in P.S.: [PAID PRODUCT OR SERVICE NAME]
— Sender Name: [FROM NAME — person or brand]

The reply invitation in step 4 is not optional decoration. It is a deliverability and relationship mechanism. When subscribers reply to your emails, inbox providers like Gmail and Outlook learn that your emails are wanted, which improves placement for every future email in the sequence. One reply from a new subscriber is worth more than 10 opens.

Prompt 5: Mid-Funnel — Nurture Email Sequence (3 Emails)

After the delivery email, a nurture sequence moves the subscriber from "interested in free stuff" to "ready to consider buying." The classic structure is a three-email sequence: Email 1 teaches something valuable and builds credibility, Email 2 tells a story that creates emotional resonance and introduces the pain the product solves, Email 3 makes the offer with a clear CTA. Each email needs its own subject line, preview text, and body — and each needs to build on the previous without repeating it. This prompt generates all three emails as a complete sequence.

Prompt SF-05: Mid-Funnel Nurture Email Sequence (3 Emails)
Act as an email marketing copywriter specializing in nurture sequences for digital products and services. Write a 3-email nurture sequence that moves a new subscriber from "curious" to "ready to buy." Each email must stand alone but build momentum toward the final pitch email.

Email 1 — Teach (sent 2 days after delivery email):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (250–350 words): Delivers a specific, actionable teaching point related to the problem the product solves. No pitch. Pure value. Ends with a question or teaser for Email 2.

Email 2 — Story + Amplify Pain (sent 4 days after delivery email):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (300–400 words): Opens with a relatable story (your story, a customer's story, or a universal scenario) about struggling with the exact problem. Agitates the cost of staying stuck. Introduces the product as the turning point — but does not pitch it yet. Ends with a promise of what is in Email 3.

Email 3 — Offer (sent 6 days after delivery email):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (350–450 words): Makes the offer clearly. What is it, what is included, what does it cost, what happens when they buy. Include 2–3 objection-handlers woven naturally into the body. Clear CTA button copy and a link. End with a P.S. that adds urgency or a bonus reminder.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT OR SERVICE NAME]
— Product Price: [PRICE]
— Core Problem the Product Solves: [PRIMARY PAIN POINT]
— Teaching Point for Email 1: [WHAT YOU WILL TEACH — must be valuable standalone advice]
— Story for Email 2: [WHOSE STORY — e.g., "a customer who struggled with X," "your own journey," "a composite of 3 clients"]
— Top 3 Objections: [WHAT PREVENTS THEM FROM BUYING — e.g., "price, time, not sure it will work for their situation"]
— CTA for Email 3: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Get Instant Access," "Start Your Free Trial," "Book a Demo"]

The teach-story-offer sequence works because it mirrors how trust is built in real conversations. You would not ask someone to buy something the first time you met them. You would share something useful, then tell them a story that establishes common ground, then make the ask — because by that point, they like you, trust you, and understand what you offer. The sequence does this in three emails over six days.

Prompt 6: Mid-Funnel — Retargeting Ad

Retargeting ads reach people who visited your site, engaged with your content, or abandoned your lead magnet form without subscribing. This audience is warmer than cold traffic but colder than your email subscribers. They have shown interest but not enough to take action. The retargeting ad's job is to overcome whatever stopped them: distraction, hesitation, lack of trust, or simply bad timing. This prompt generates retargeting ad copy that feels timely and relevant, not surveillance-like — the critical line that separates effective retargeting from creepy retargeting.

Prompt SF-06: Mid-Funnel Retargeting Ad
Act as a paid social specialist experienced with retargeting campaigns. Write retargeting ad copy for the audience segment below. These viewers have previously visited the website or engaged with content but did not convert. The ad must feel helpful and timely, not surveillance-based. Never reference their visit directly ("we saw you on our site").

Retargeting psychology: This audience has already self-identified as interested. Do not re-explain what you sell. Focus on: (1) the cost of not acting, (2) a new piece of social proof or information they did not see, (3) a reduced-friction offer (free resource, free trial, free consultation), or (4) a time-limited incentive.

Provide:
— 3 Hook options (under 125 characters each): Each uses a different angle — social proof, new information, and urgency/scarcity.
— 1 Full retargeting ad body (150–200 words): Acknowledges their problem naturally (not their visit), adds new information or proof they may not have seen, and reduces the friction of the next step.
— 3 Headline options (under 40 characters each): Reinforce the offer or next step.
— 2 CTA button options: Specific action, not generic "Learn More."

Variables:
— Audience Segment: [WHO THESE PEOPLE ARE — e.g., "visited pricing page," "watched 50%+ of the webinar," "downloaded the lead magnet but never opened emails"]
— What Stopped Them: [LIKELY OBJECTION OR DISTRACTION — e.g., "price concern," "not sure it applies to their niche," "got busy"]
— New Proof to Introduce: [SOCIAL PROOF OR INFORMATION THEY MAY NOT HAVE SEEN — e.g., "a new case study," "a specific customer result," "a comparison chart"]
— Reduced-Friction Offer: [THE EASIEST NEXT STEP — e.g., "free 7-day trial," "free strategy call," "free sample chapter"]
— Urgency Element: [OPTIONAL DEADLINE OR SCARCITY — e.g., "offer ends Sunday," "only 10 spots left"]

The most effective retargeting ads do not feel like retargeting ads. They feel like a friend who heard you were thinking about something and sent you one more piece of relevant information. The prompt's explicit instruction to "never reference their visit directly" is what keeps the ad from crossing the line into surveillance-adjacent territory that damages brand trust.

Prompt 7: Bottom-of-Funnel — Sales Page Copy

The sales page is where you ask for the money. Your reader is product-aware — they know what you sell, they are seriously considering buying, and they are looking for a reason to say yes or no. Sales page copy must do everything a great salesperson does in a face-to-face conversation: establish the pain clearly, present the solution compellingly, stack the proof, handle every objection, explain the offer in complete detail, and make asking for the order feel natural rather than pushy. This prompt generates a full-length sales page outline and key copy sections, including the critical components most first drafts miss.

Prompt SF-07: Bottom-of-Funnel Sales Page Copy
Act as a direct-response copywriter who has written sales pages generating $1M+ in revenue. Write a complete sales page for the product below. The reader is product-aware and seriously considering buying. They have objections. Your job is to give them every reason to say yes and proactively remove every reason to say no.

Write all of the following sections:
— Above-the-fold (Hero Section): Pre-headline (eyebrow), H1 headline (outcome-focused, under 12 words), supporting subheadline (1–2 sentences expanding the promise), Hero CTA button copy.
— Problem Section (200–300 words): Paint the picture of their current painful situation. Use specific, relatable scenarios. Make them feel deeply understood.
— Agitation Section (100–150 words): What is the cost of staying in that situation? Time, money, stress, missed opportunity. Make the status quo feel unacceptable.
— Solution Introduction (150–200 words): Introduce the product as the turning point. What it is and what it does in plain language. Not features — transformation.
— What Is Included (full bulleted breakdown): Every component, module, template, or bonus. Each bullet = feature + benefit + outcome where possible.
— Social Proof Section (3 testimonial frameworks): Template for customer testimonials that address the 3 main objections. Show them what to ask customers for.
— Objection Handler Section (3 key objections): Named and answered directly. Use "You might be wondering..." framing.
— Guarantee Statement (2–3 sentences): Removes purchase risk. Specific, not vague.
— Pricing Section: Present the price, justify it (anchor to the alternative cost), and restate the offer.
— Final CTA (closing section): Restate the outcome. Paint the before/after. One clear CTA button. P.S. line.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Price: [PRICE AND FORMAT — e.g., "$297 one-time," "$49/month," "$997 with payment plan"]
— Core Outcome: [THE TRANSFORMATION THE BUYER GETS — what their life/business looks like after]
— Target Buyer: [EXACTLY WHO THIS IS FOR]
— What Is Included: [LIST ALL COMPONENTS, MODULES, TEMPLATES, BONUSES]
— Top 3 Objections: [MOST COMMON REASONS PEOPLE DO NOT BUY]
— Guarantee Terms: [YOUR REFUND OR GUARANTEE POLICY]

The objection-handler section is the part most first-time copywriters skip, and it is where the most sales are lost. By the time someone reaches the pricing section of a sales page, they have an objection in their head. If you do not name and answer it before they click away, you lose the sale silently. Naming the objection first — "You might be wondering whether this works if you have a small list" — signals that you understand your buyer and builds the trust needed to close.

Prompt 8: Bottom-of-Funnel — Urgency / Scarcity Email

Urgency and scarcity emails are the most powerful tools in the bottom-of-funnel email arsenal — and the most abused. Fake countdown timers and invented "limited spots" destroy trust and train subscribers to ignore urgency signals. This prompt generates urgency emails built on real scarcity (an actual deadline, a real price increase, or genuine limited capacity) combined with objection handling that converts fence-sitters without manipulating them.

Prompt SF-08: Bottom-of-Funnel Urgency / Scarcity Email
Act as a direct-response email copywriter specializing in launch and offer emails. Write a 3-email urgency sequence for the final 72 hours of a promotion or launch. These emails go to subscribers who have seen the offer but have not yet purchased. Each email addresses a different reason someone might be hesitating.

Email 1 — 72 Hours Left (Reminder + Objection Handler):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each). Subject must include the deadline or urgency element.
— Body (200–300 words): Remind them of the offer. Lead with the most common objection and answer it directly. Restate what they get. Clear CTA.

Email 2 — 24 Hours Left (Social Proof + FOMO):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each). Urgency escalates.
— Body (200–300 words): Lead with a customer result or testimonial (social proof). Amplify the cost of not acting. "Tomorrow this [closes / goes up in price / loses the bonus]." One CTA.

Email 3 — Final Hours / Last Call:
— Subject + preview text (3 options each). Direct, urgent, brief.
— Body (150–200 words): Short. The urgency is the message. State the deadline clearly. Restate the core transformation in one sentence. One CTA button. No fluff.

Rules: Every urgency claim must be real. If the deadline is fake or artificial, remove it — false scarcity destroys trust permanently. Write urgency that is honest and emotionally resonant, not manufactured.

Variables:
— Offer Name: [PRODUCT OR PROMOTION NAME]
— Urgency Reason: [WHY THE DEADLINE IS REAL — e.g., "cart closes Friday at midnight," "price increases by $100 after launch week," "only 50 spots in the cohort"]
— Top Objection: [THE MAIN HESITATION AMONG PEOPLE WHO SAW THE OFFER BUT DID NOT BUY]
— Customer Result or Testimonial: [SOCIAL PROOF TO FEATURE IN EMAIL 2]
— Core Transformation: [THE ONE-SENTENCE OUTCOME OF BUYING — e.g., "a complete AI prompt library for every marketing channel, ready in 24 hours"]

Email 3 Subject Options:
1. This closes in 4 hours.
2. Last call: [Offer Name] closes tonight.
3. Doors close at midnight — final reminder.

Email 3 Body Example:
This is the last email I am sending about [Offer Name].

The cart closes tonight at midnight Pacific.

If you have been thinking about it — this is the moment. After tonight, [price goes up / doors close / the bonus disappears].

What you get: [one sentence restating the transformation].

If it is not for you, no hard feelings — I will be back in your inbox with more free [content type] next week.

But if you are ready: [CTA Button: Get Instant Access]

[Name]

Email 3 is deliberately the shortest email in the sequence. By the final-hours email, the subscriber has already read your previous emails, seen the offer, and knows the details. What they need is not more information — it is a clear, direct, low-pressure reminder that the window is closing. Short is more powerful than long at this stage.

Prompt 9: Bottom-of-Funnel — Checkout Page Copy

The checkout page is the last conversion point before the sale, and it kills more revenue than any other page in the funnel. The average e-commerce cart abandonment rate is 70%. For digital products and SaaS, it is similarly high. Checkout copy must do three things simultaneously: reinforce the decision the buyer just made (reduce buyer's remorse before it starts), remove any remaining trust friction (security badges, guarantee reminder, testimonials), and make the form itself feel fast and safe. This prompt generates complete checkout page copy including the order summary, trust elements, and the micro-copy that lives inside and around the form fields.

Prompt SF-09: Bottom-of-Funnel Checkout Page Copy
Act as a conversion rate optimization specialist focused on checkout pages. Write complete copy for a checkout page for the product below. The buyer has made the decision to purchase and clicked to the checkout page. Your copy must reinforce that decision, remove friction, and make completing the form feel simple and safe.

Provide:
— Page Headline (2 options): Celebrates their decision without overselling. E.g., "You Are One Step Away from [Outcome]" or "Complete Your Order Below."
— Order Summary Box Copy: Product name, what is included (3–5 bullet points), and price. Each bullet restates a specific included item — reinforces value at the moment of payment.
— Trust Badge Labels (5): Short trust signals placed near the payment form. E.g., "256-Bit SSL Encryption," "30-Day Money-Back Guarantee," "Instant Digital Access." Write 5 specific to this product.
— Guarantee Reminder (1–2 sentences): Near the payment button. Restates the guarantee in plain language.
— Form Field Labels and Placeholder Text: Write the label text and placeholder for: Email, Name, Card Number, Expiry, CVV. Placeholder text should be friendly, not robotic ("your@email.com" not "Enter email address").
— Payment Button Copy (3 options): Specific, outcome-focused. Not just "Pay Now." E.g., "Get Instant Access — $[Price]" or "Complete My Order and Start Today."
— Below-Button Micro-copy (1–2 sentences): Removes the final hesitation. Restates the guarantee and delivery speed.
— Testimonial Pull-Quote (1): Short, outcome-focused quote placed beside the form. 1–2 sentences max.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Price: [PRICE]
— What Is Included: [MAIN COMPONENTS — 3–5 items]
— Guarantee: [YOUR REFUND POLICY — e.g., "30-day money-back, no questions asked"]
— Delivery Method and Speed: [HOW AND WHEN THEY GET ACCESS — e.g., "instant digital download," "access within 60 seconds," "emailed within 5 minutes"]
— Testimonial: [CUSTOMER QUOTE WITH NAME AND CONTEXT]

Checkout page micro-copy is the most overlooked category in conversion copywriting. The text on the payment button, the label above the email field, the two-sentence trust statement below the submit button — each of these elements has been proven in split tests to move conversion rates by 5–15% individually. Running this prompt for a checkout page you are already running is one of the highest-ROI uses of AI copy in the entire funnel.

Prompt 10: Post-Purchase — Thank You Page Upsell

The thank-you page after a purchase is the highest-leverage upsell moment in the funnel. The buyer just demonstrated willingness to pay, their card is already in hand (metaphorically), and their trust in you is at its peak. A well-crafted thank-you page upsell converts at 15–30% for a complementary offer priced at 30–50% of the original purchase price. Most brands waste this moment with a generic "thank you, check your email" message. This prompt generates a complete one-time-offer (OTO) upsell page that appears immediately after purchase.

Prompt SF-10: Post-Purchase Thank You Page Upsell
Act as a direct-response copywriter specializing in post-purchase upsell sequences. Write a one-time-offer (OTO) upsell page that appears immediately after a customer completes their purchase. The buyer is happy, their trust is highest, and they are open to a complementary offer. This page must feel like a natural next step, not an immediate money-grab.

Structure:
— Thank You Confirmation (2–3 sentences): Acknowledge and celebrate the purchase immediately. Confirm delivery. Then transition to the upsell.
— Upsell Headline (2 options): Frames the upsell as the natural next step to get more value from the original purchase. Not a separate pitch — a logical extension.
— Upsell Subheadline (1–2 sentences): Adds specificity to the headline. What exactly is this and why does it pair with what they just bought?
— Bridge Paragraph (100–150 words): Connects the original purchase to the upsell. "You just got X. The next bottleneck for most people who have X is Y. That is exactly what this solves."
— What Is Included (5–7 bullets): Each bullet = component + outcome.
— One-Time-Offer Framing (2–3 sentences): This offer is only available right now, at this price, on this page. Explain why without being pushy — e.g., production cost, exclusivity, or limited release reason.
— CTA + No-Thanks Link: Button copy for "yes, add to my order" and a polite dismissal link text for "no thanks."

Variables:
— Original Purchase: [WHAT THEY JUST BOUGHT]
— Upsell Offer Name: [UPSELL PRODUCT OR SERVICE]
— Upsell Price: [PRICE — typically 30–50% of original purchase]
— Why These Products Go Together: [LOGICAL CONNECTION — e.g., "they bought the prompt library, the upsell is the Notion template system to organize and deploy the prompts"]
— What Is Included in Upsell: [COMPONENTS]
— One-Time-Offer Reason: [WHY THIS PRICE OR AVAILABILITY IS EXCLUSIVE TO THIS PAGE — e.g., "normally $X, offered here at $Y because you just purchased"]

The bridge paragraph is what separates a good thank-you page upsell from a bad one. Bad upsells feel like a second sales pitch immediately after the first one. Good upsells feel inevitable — the natural next problem to solve given what the buyer just purchased. The prompt explicitly frames the upsell as a logical extension, which is why this structure converts at significantly higher rates than generic "here is another product" upsell pages.

Prompt 11: Post-Purchase — Onboarding Email

The onboarding email sequence is the most underinvested part of the funnel for most businesses. You spent all of that money to acquire a customer — and then sent them a single "here is your download" email and assumed they would figure it out. Customer success data consistently shows that buyers who get a value win within the first 48 hours of purchase have dramatically higher retention, lower refund rates, and higher lifetime value than those who do not. This prompt generates an onboarding email sequence that gets customers to their first win fast.

Prompt SF-11: Post-Purchase Onboarding Email
Act as a customer success copywriter and email strategist. Write a 3-email onboarding sequence for a new customer. The goals of this sequence are: (1) deliver access and confirm the purchase, (2) get the customer to their first meaningful win within 48 hours, (3) set expectations for how to get the most value from their purchase. High onboarding engagement reduces refunds, increases retention, and increases referrals.

Email 1 — Welcome + Access (sent immediately after purchase):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (200–250 words): Welcome them to the community/product. Confirm delivery and access. Give them one specific first action — the single most important thing to do in the first 30 minutes. Celebrate their decision briefly. Introduce what is coming in the next 2 emails.

Email 2 — First Win (sent 24 hours after purchase):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (200–300 words): Guide them to complete a specific, achievable action that delivers a real result. Walk them through it step by step. Celebrate the outcome if they complete it. Invite them to share results with you.

Email 3 — Success Path + Community (sent 72 hours after purchase):
— Subject + preview text (3 options each)
— Body (250–350 words): Paint the full picture of what is possible with this product over the next 30 days. Give them a roadmap. Introduce any community, support, or resource they may not have discovered yet. Ask for feedback and a review/testimonial if they have gotten a win.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Access Link: [HOW THEY ACCESS THE PRODUCT]
— First Action (30 minutes): [THE SINGLE BEST FIRST STEP — e.g., "install Template 1 in your ESP," "complete the Day 1 module," "fill in the brand voice worksheet"]
— First Win (24-hour action): [A SPECIFIC ACTION THAT PRODUCES A VISIBLE RESULT QUICKLY — e.g., "run Prompt #3 and generate your first landing page draft"]
— 30-Day Success Path: [WHAT THEY CAN REALISTICALLY ACHIEVE IN 30 DAYS IF THEY FOLLOW THE SYSTEM]
— Community or Support Resource: [FACEBOOK GROUP, SLACK, HELP DESK, OFFICE HOURS, ETC.]

The first-win concept from Email 2 is borrowed from game design — specifically the concept of the "tutorial win" that keeps players engaged by giving them an early success. When a new customer achieves something concrete with your product within 24 hours, their confidence that the product will work for them increases dramatically. That confidence is what prevents refund requests and creates the advocates who leave glowing reviews and refer friends.

Prompt 12: Full Funnel Map + Copy Outline

Prompt SF-12: Full Funnel Map + Copy Outline

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts Across Your Entire Sales Funnel

The 12 prompts above give you the raw copy. These five principles ensure the copy performs across the full funnel system.

1. Match Copy Sophistication to Buyer Awareness Level

The biggest mistake in funnel copy is using the same level of sophistication at every stage. Cold-traffic ads must be simple — clear problem, clear promise, low commitment ask. Sales pages can be detailed and complex because the reader has self-selected as seriously interested. When you run each prompt, ensure your variables reflect the reader's actual awareness level at that stage. The more specific you are about what the buyer knows and feels when they encounter each piece of copy, the better calibrated the AI output will be.

2. Run All 12 Prompts in a Single Session

The best way to ensure cross-funnel copy consistency is to run all 12 prompts in a single AI conversation without clearing the context window. Start with Prompt 12 (the funnel map) and work backward through the funnel, ending with Prompt 1. As the conversation builds, the model carries the brand voice, buyer profile, and key messages established in the funnel map into every subsequent prompt. The copy you get from Prompt 1 will naturally echo and reinforce the language used in Prompts 7 and 11 because the model has that context.

3. A/B Test Every Subject Line and Headline Before Scaling

Each prompt generates multiple variations of subject lines and headlines. Do not choose one arbitrarily and commit. Test them. For emails, send each subject line variation to 20% of your list and let the winner go to the remaining 60% after 4 hours. For paid ads, run each headline variation as a separate ad set with a $10–15/day budget for 72 hours, then pause the losers and scale the winner. The AI's output is the raw material for testing — real audience behavior determines the winner.

4. Inject Real Social Proof at Every Stage

The prompts include social proof as a variable throughout. Do not skip this or use placeholder text. Real proof — actual customer names, specific results, genuine testimonials — outperforms invented or generic examples every time. Before running the prompts, compile your best social proof: testimonials with specific results, download or subscriber numbers, revenue generated, time saved. Paste the most compelling proof into each prompt's social proof variable. The AI will weave it into the copy naturally, and that proof is what turns good copy into converting copy.

5. Audit Your Funnel Before Rewriting Everything

You do not need to rewrite the entire funnel at once. Identify the biggest drop-off point first: where are you losing the most people? If 40% of your email list never opens the nurture sequence, start with Prompt 5. If your checkout abandonment rate is 75%, run Prompt 9 first. If your sales page gets traffic but barely converts, Prompt 7 is your priority. The 12 prompts are modular — use them to fix the weakest link in your current funnel before building new stages. One rewrite of the highest-leverage page can double revenue from your existing traffic before you add a single new visitor.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Sales Funnel

Can AI write copy for every stage of a sales funnel? +
Yes. AI prompts can generate high-quality copy for every funnel stage: top-of-funnel awareness ads and blog content, mid-funnel lead magnets, nurture email sequences and retargeting ads, bottom-of-funnel sales pages and urgency emails, checkout copy, and post-purchase onboarding. The key is using stage-specific prompts rather than generic ones. A cold-traffic ad requires a different persuasion framework than a sales page or a checkout abandonment email. The 12 prompts in this guide are each calibrated to the awareness, intent, and objections of buyers at that specific funnel stage.
What is the most important funnel stage to get right? +
The bottom of the funnel — specifically the sales page, checkout copy, and urgency emails — drives the most direct revenue impact. A weak sales page can cost you 40–60% of potential conversions even when your top-of-funnel is working perfectly. That said, every stage depends on the one before it: weak awareness ads produce low-quality leads, weak lead magnets produce disengaged email subscribers, and disengaged subscribers do not buy. The highest-leverage move is auditing your funnel from top to bottom, finding the biggest drop-off point, and using the relevant prompt from this guide to rewrite that stage first.
How do I maintain a consistent voice across all funnel stages? +
Add a brand voice section to each prompt before running it. Describe your tone in 3–5 adjectives (e.g., "direct, warm, slightly irreverent, no corporate jargon") and include 1–2 sentences from existing copy that exemplifies your voice. Paste that block at the top of each prompt as a constant. The AI will calibrate its output to that voice across all 12 prompts. For maximum consistency, run all 12 prompts in a single conversation session without clearing context — the model carries your brand voice forward automatically as the conversation builds.
Do these prompts work with ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini? +
Yes. Every prompt in this article is model-agnostic — it works with ChatGPT (GPT-4o), Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or any large language model that accepts text input. Paste the prompt into whichever AI tool you already use, fill in the bracketed variables with your business details, and generate a complete funnel stage in under five minutes.
How often should I update my funnel copy with AI? +
Refresh your top-of-funnel ads every 4–6 weeks as creative fatigue sets in for cold audiences. Nurture email sequences can run for 3–6 months before needing a full refresh, but individual subject lines should be A/B tested continuously. Sales pages and checkout copy should be refreshed every quarter or whenever you add new social proof (reviews, case studies, revenue milestones). Post-purchase onboarding emails rarely need refreshing unless your product changes significantly. The prompts in this guide make any of these refreshes a 10-minute task rather than a multi-day copywriting project.
