20 Best Free AI Writing Prompts for Marketing in 2026

ContentKit AI•16 min read

AI writing prompts for marketing are engineered instructions that turn any AI tool into a professional copywriter, producing ads, emails, social posts, blog content, and landing pages that convert. This mega-guide delivers the 20 best free prompts across every marketing channel, updated for 2026, with frameworks, variables, and example outputs you can copy and use today.

Most marketers use AI the same way: open ChatGPT, type a vague request, get a vague response, and walk away disappointed. The output reads like a college essay — technically correct, utterly generic, and completely unusable for any real campaign. The problem is not the AI model. The problem is the instruction you gave it.

A comprehensive prompt library across all marketing channels changes the equation entirely. Instead of starting from scratch every time you need a Facebook ad, a welcome email, an Instagram caption, a blog outline, or a landing page headline, you open your prompt library, pick the right template, fill in 3–5 variables, and get a first draft in under two minutes. The difference between a marketer with a prompt library and one without is the difference between a chef with a recipe book and someone guessing at ingredient ratios. Both can cook. Only one does it consistently well.

This article is the complete prompt library. Twenty prompts across five marketing channels — ads, email, social media, blog/SEO, and landing pages — each with a proven framework, variable placeholders, and example outputs. Every prompt works with ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any AI writing tool. Every prompt is free to copy, modify, and use for commercial purposes.

Here is what makes these ai writing prompts for marketing different from the generic lists you have seen before. Each prompt includes four components that generic prompts skip:

  1. Role assignment — telling the AI exactly who it is (a direct-response copywriter, an email strategist, a social media manager) so it adopts the right voice and expertise
  2. Framework specification — naming the proven copywriting framework (PAS, AIDA, hook-story-offer) so the output follows a tested structure
  3. Variable placeholders — clearly marked inputs (product name, target audience, key benefit) that you customize for each use
  4. Output constraints — word count limits, tone parameters, and formatting instructions that prevent the AI from rambling or defaulting to corporate speak

The result is prompts that produce usable first drafts — not perfect final copy, but structured, on-strategy drafts that need 15–20 minutes of human editing instead of 3 hours of writing from scratch.

Let us get into the 20 prompts, starting with ad copy.

Ad Copy Prompts (Prompts 1–4)

Paid advertising is the fastest path to revenue, but it is also the fastest way to burn money. A single Facebook ad with the wrong angle, a Google search ad with a weak headline, or a retargeting ad that sounds desperate can drain your budget in hours. These four ad copy prompts cover the formats that drive 90% of paid marketing results: Facebook feed ads, Google responsive search ads, Instagram Story ads, and retargeting ads for warm audiences.

Each prompt is built on a specific direct-response framework. The frameworks matter because they have been tested across millions of dollars in ad spend — they work not because they are clever, but because they match the psychological state of the person seeing the ad.

Prompt 1: Facebook Ad — PAS Framework

The Problem-Agitate-Solution framework is the most reliable ad structure for cold audiences. It works because it mirrors how humans make decisions: identify a pain, feel the weight of that pain, then discover a solution. This prompt generates a complete Facebook ad with headline, primary text, and description.

Prompt WP-01: Facebook Ad — PAS
Act as a direct-response Facebook ad copywriter specializing in the PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution) framework. Write a complete Facebook ad for the product below.

Structure:
— Primary Text: Open with the problem (1 sentence that names the pain). Agitate with a specific, relatable consequence (2–3 sentences). Present the solution as the product (2–3 sentences). End with a clear CTA.
— Headline: 5–8 words, benefit-driven, no clickbait.
— Description: 1 sentence reinforcing the main benefit or adding social proof.

Output: Primary text 80–125 words. Tone: conversational, empathetic, zero hype.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME + WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [WHO THEY ARE + THEIR SITUATION]
— Core Problem: [THE #1 PAIN POINT]
— Key Benefit: [THE #1 OUTCOME AFTER USING THE PRODUCT]
— Social Proof: [TESTIMONIAL, STAT, OR CREDIBILITY MARKER]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Shop Now," "Start Free Trial"]

Here is an example output from this prompt, filled in for a project management SaaS targeting freelancers:

Primary Text:
You did not become a freelancer to spend half your day tracking deadlines in spreadsheets.

But that is where most solo consultants end up. Juggling 6 clients across email, Slack, and sticky notes. Missing a deliverable because it was buried in a thread from two weeks ago. Lying awake wondering what you forgot to send.

TaskFlow organizes every project, deadline, and client message in one dashboard built for freelancers — not enterprise teams. Set it up in 10 minutes. See everything due this week in one view. Never miss a deadline again.

Join 4,200+ freelancers who stopped drowning in admin. Start your free trial today.

Headline: Every Deadline, One Dashboard

Description: Rated 4.9/5 by 4,200+ freelancers. Free 14-day trial, no credit card required.

Prompt 2: Google Search Ad — Responsive Search Ad

Google Responsive Search Ads require 15 headlines (30 characters each) and 4 descriptions (90 characters each). Most marketers struggle to fill all 15 headline slots with meaningful variations. This prompt generates the full set, organized by angle, so Google's algorithm can test every combination.

Prompt WP-02: Google RSA
Act as a Google Ads specialist who writes high-CTR responsive search ads. Generate a complete RSA for the keyword and product below.

Output:
— 15 headlines (max 30 characters each), organized into 5 groups of 3:
  Group 1: Benefit-focused
  Group 2: Feature-focused
  Group 3: Social proof / credibility
  Group 4: Urgency / offer
  Group 5: Question / curiosity
— 4 descriptions (max 90 characters each): benefit, feature, proof, CTA

Rules: No exclamation points. No ALL CAPS. Each headline must be unique and make sense independently. Include the primary keyword in at least 3 headlines.

Variables:
— Primary Keyword: [PRIMARY KEYWORD]
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME + WHAT IT DOES]
— Key Benefit: [#1 OUTCOME FOR THE CUSTOMER]
— Price/Offer: [PRICE POINT OR CURRENT OFFER]
— Social Proof: [TESTIMONIAL, USER COUNT, OR RATING]

Prompt 3: Instagram Story Ad — Hook-First Vertical Format

Instagram Story ads live or die in the first 1.5 seconds. If the hook does not stop the thumb, the ad is gone. This prompt is built specifically for vertical, mobile-first ad formats where the visual hook and text overlay must work together. It generates the text layers for a 3-frame Story sequence.

Prompt WP-03: Instagram Story Ad
Act as a social media ad creative strategist who specializes in vertical, mobile-first Instagram Story ads. Write the text overlay copy for a 3-frame Story ad sequence.

Structure:
— Frame 1 (Hook): Bold statement or question that stops the scroll. Max 8 words. Must create an open loop or pattern interrupt.
— Frame 2 (Value): Explain what the product does and the key benefit. Max 15 words. Use the problem-solution bridge.
— Frame 3 (CTA): Clear action step with urgency or incentive. Max 10 words. Include swipe-up or link instruction.

Also provide:
— 3 alternative hooks for A/B testing
— Suggested visual direction for each frame (1 sentence each)

Tone: punchy, confident, native to the platform.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME + WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [WHO THEY ARE + AGE RANGE]
— Key Benefit: [THE #1 OUTCOME]
— Offer: [DISCOUNT, FREE TRIAL, OR LEAD MAGNET]

Prompt 4: Retargeting Ad — Warm Audience

Retargeting ads talk to people who already know you — they visited your site, viewed a product, maybe even added to cart, but did not buy. The messaging here is fundamentally different from cold ads. You do not need to introduce yourself. You need to address the reason they left. This prompt generates retargeting copy that acknowledges the visit, addresses the likely objection, and gives a reason to come back now.

Prompt WP-04: Retargeting Ad
Act as a performance marketing copywriter specializing in retargeting campaigns for warm audiences. Write a retargeting ad for users who visited the product page but did not convert.

Structure:
— Open with an acknowledgment that they visited ("Still thinking about [product]?" or similar — do not be creepy)
— Address the most likely objection (price, timing, uncertainty)
— Add a new piece of information they did not see on the first visit (testimonial, guarantee, limited offer)
— CTA with a reason to act now

Output: 60–90 words primary text. Headline: 5–8 words. Tone: helpful, not desperate. Never sound like a stalker.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME + WHAT IT DOES]
— Likely Objection: [WHY THEY DID NOT BUY — e.g., "price," "not sure it works," "need to think about it"]
— New Information: [TESTIMONIAL, GUARANTEE, OR LIMITED OFFER]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION]

Email Marketing Prompts (Prompts 5–8)

Email remains the highest-ROI marketing channel — $36 returned for every $1 spent, according to the DMA. But that average includes the brands doing it well. Most AI-generated emails sound like they were written by a committee: no personality, no structure, no reason for the reader to keep reading past the first line. These four prompts cover the email types that generate the most revenue: the welcome email that sets the relationship tone, the promotional email that drives immediate sales, the nurture email that builds long-term trust, and the subject line generator that determines whether any of those emails get opened.

Prompt 5: Welcome Email — First in Sequence

The welcome email has the highest open rate of any email you will ever send — typically 50–60%. It is the moment your subscriber is most engaged, most curious, and most likely to form a lasting impression of your brand. Waste it with a generic "thanks for subscribing" and you have trained them to ignore everything that follows. This prompt generates a welcome email that delivers the promised value, sets expectations for what comes next, and establishes the personal tone that keeps people reading.

Prompt WP-05: Welcome Email
Act as an email marketing strategist who writes high-converting welcome sequences. Write the first email in a welcome sequence for a new subscriber who just opted in for the lead magnet below.

Structure:
— Subject line: personal, curiosity-driven, under 50 characters
— Open with a warm, personal greeting that references what they signed up for (1–2 sentences)
— Deliver the promised lead magnet with a clear download link placeholder
— Set expectations: what emails they will receive, how often, and what value to expect (2–3 sentences)
— Share one quick, actionable tip related to the lead magnet topic (2–3 sentences)
— Close with a personal sign-off and a question to encourage reply

Output: 200–280 words. Tone: warm, personal, first-person. No corporate speak.

Variables:
— Lead Magnet: [LEAD MAGNET NAME + WHAT IT DELIVERS]
— Brand/Sender Name: [BRAND OR PERSONAL NAME]
— Email Frequency: [HOW OFTEN YOU WILL EMAIL — e.g., "twice a week"]
— Content Focus: [WHAT YOUR EMAILS COVER — e.g., "actionable marketing tips"]
— Quick Tip: [ONE ACTIONABLE TIP RELATED TO THE LEAD MAGNET]

Here is an example output for a content marketing consultant delivering a free SEO checklist:

Subject: Your SEO checklist is inside (plus a quick win)

Hey —

Welcome, and thanks for grabbing the 2026 SEO Checklist. I put this together after auditing 40+ websites last year and seeing the same 12 mistakes on almost every one.

Here is your download: [DOWNLOAD LINK]

A few things to expect from me. I send two emails per week — Tuesdays and Fridays. Each one covers one actionable content marketing tactic you can implement the same day. No theory dumps. No 2,000-word essays. Just the tactic, why it works, and how to do it.

Here is a quick win to start with today: Open the checklist and go straight to item #4 — internal linking. Most sites I audit have fewer than 3 internal links per page. Add 2–3 relevant internal links to your top 5 pages this week. It takes 20 minutes and typically moves rankings within 2–3 weeks.

One question for you: what is the biggest content challenge you are dealing with right now? Just hit reply — I read every response and it helps me write emails that actually help you.

Talk soon,
Sarah

Prompt 6: Promotional Email — Limited-Time Offer

Promotional emails drive immediate revenue, but they walk a fine line between persuasive and pushy. This prompt uses urgency without manufacturing it — the deadline is real, the offer is specific, and the copy gives the reader everything they need to make a decision without hype language or exclamation points.

Prompt WP-06: Promotional Email
Act as a direct-response email copywriter who writes sales emails that convert without being pushy. Write a promotional email for a limited-time offer.

Structure:
— Subject line: urgency + specificity, under 50 characters
— Open by naming what is available and the deadline (1–2 sentences, no buildup)
— State the offer clearly: what they get, what it costs, what the regular price is (3–4 sentences)
— 3 bullet points listing the most compelling benefits (outcomes, not features)
— 1 sentence of social proof (testimonial quote, user count, or rating)
— Restate the deadline
— Single CTA with link placeholder

Output: 180–250 words. Tone: direct, confident, respectful. No exclamation points. No hype words (amazing, incredible, game-changing).

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Offer Details: [DISCOUNT AMOUNT OR BONUS + DEADLINE]
— Regular Price: [REGULAR PRICE]
— Sale Price: [SALE PRICE]
— Top 3 Benefits: [BENEFIT 1, BENEFIT 2, BENEFIT 3]
— Social Proof: [TESTIMONIAL OR STAT]
— CTA Link: [CTA LINK]

Prompt 7: Nurture Email — Value-First Content

Nurture emails are the long game. They build trust, demonstrate expertise, and keep your brand top of mind between promotional sends. The best nurture emails teach one thing and teach it well — no selling, no pitching, just genuine value that makes the reader think "this person really knows their stuff." This prompt generates a teach-one-concept nurture email that positions you as a trusted authority.

Prompt WP-07: Nurture Email
Act as an expert email strategist who writes nurture emails designed to build trust and authority without selling. Write a value-first nurture email that teaches one actionable concept.

Structure:
— Subject line: curiosity or insight-driven, under 50 characters
— Open with a relatable observation or counterintuitive insight (1–2 sentences)
— Explain the concept clearly with one specific example (3–4 sentences)
— Give one actionable step the reader can implement today (2–3 sentences)
— Close with a takeaway that ties back to the opening (1 sentence)
— No CTA, no product mention, no link — pure value

Output: 200–300 words. Tone: insightful, conversational, first-person.

Variables:
— Topic/Concept: [THE ONE THING YOU ARE TEACHING]
— Specific Example: [A REAL OR REALISTIC EXAMPLE THAT ILLUSTRATES THE CONCEPT]
— Actionable Step: [WHAT THE READER SHOULD DO TODAY]
— Sender Name: [YOUR NAME]

Social Media Prompts (Prompts 9–12)

Social media is where most marketers feel the content treadmill most acutely. You need fresh posts across 3–4 platforms every day, each with its own format requirements, character limits, and audience expectations. The four prompts in this section cover the formats that drive the most engagement on the platforms that matter most: Instagram captions, LinkedIn thought leadership, Twitter/X threads, and TikTok/Reels scripts.

Prompt 9: Instagram Caption with Hook — 5 Variations

Instagram captions are read below the fold — the user has to tap "more" to see anything beyond the first line. That first line is your hook, and it determines whether anyone reads the rest. This prompt generates a complete Instagram caption with five different hook variations so you can test which opening resonates most with your audience.

Prompt WP-09: Instagram Caption
Act as a social media copywriter who specializes in high-engagement Instagram captions. Write a complete Instagram caption for the post described below, plus 4 alternative hooks.

Structure:
— Hook (first line): Bold statement, surprising stat, or provocative question that stops the scroll. Must work as a standalone line before "more" is tapped.
— Body (3–5 sentences): Expand on the hook with a story, insight, or lesson. Use short paragraphs and line breaks for mobile readability.
— CTA: Ask a question, invite a save, or prompt a share. One clear action.
— Hashtags: 5 relevant hashtags (mix of broad and niche).

Then provide 4 alternative hooks using different angles: question, statistic, controversial take, and personal story opener.

Output: Caption 80–150 words. Tone: [BRAND TONE].

Variables:
— Post Topic: [WHAT THE POST IS ABOUT]
— Target Audience: [WHO FOLLOWS YOU]
— Key Message: [THE ONE TAKEAWAY]
— Brand Tone: [TONE DESCRIPTORS — e.g., "bold, educational, slightly irreverent"]

Here is an example output for a fitness coach targeting busy professionals:

Hook: You do not need more motivation. You need a shorter workout.

I used to think 60-minute gym sessions were non-negotiable. Then I started training clients who had 25 minutes between meetings.

Here is what I learned: the person who does 20 focused minutes 5x a week will always outperform the person who does 90 minutes once and feels too sore to move for 3 days.

The best program is the one you will actually do. Stop optimizing the perfect routine and start doing the possible one.

Save this for your next "I don't have time" day.

#FitnessForBusyPeople #20MinuteWorkout #FitnessTips #WorkoutMotivation #GymLife

Alternative Hooks:
1. (Question) What if 20 minutes was enough?
2. (Statistic) 73% of gym memberships go unused within 90 days.
3. (Controversial) Long workouts are overrated. There, I said it.
4. (Personal) I cancelled my hour-long sessions. My clients got better results.

Prompt 10: LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post — Insight-Evidence-Takeaway

LinkedIn rewards posts that make people think, not posts that make people scroll. The most effective LinkedIn format is the insight-evidence-takeaway structure: open with a non-obvious observation, back it with evidence or a story, and close with a clear lesson the reader can apply. This prompt generates posts that position you as a credible voice in your industry.

Prompt WP-10: LinkedIn Thought Leadership
Act as a LinkedIn content strategist who writes thought leadership posts that generate meaningful engagement (comments and shares, not just likes). Write a LinkedIn post using the Insight-Evidence-Takeaway framework.

Structure:
— Hook (first 2 lines): A counterintuitive insight or bold claim that makes the reader stop scrolling. These 2 lines appear above the "see more" fold — they must be compelling enough to earn the click.
— Evidence (3–5 lines): Support your insight with a specific story, data point, or personal experience. Be concrete, not abstract.
— Takeaway (2–3 lines): The actionable lesson. What should the reader do differently after reading this?
— Engagement close: A question that invites discussion (not a generic "agree?").

Format: Short paragraphs. Line breaks between each thought. No hashtags in the body. 3 hashtags at the bottom. No emojis.

Output: 150–250 words.

Variables:
— Insight/Claim: [YOUR NON-OBVIOUS OBSERVATION ABOUT YOUR INDUSTRY]
— Evidence: [SPECIFIC STORY, DATA POINT, OR EXPERIENCE THAT PROVES IT]
— Takeaway: [THE ACTIONABLE LESSON]
— Your Role/Title: [YOUR PROFESSIONAL CONTEXT]

Prompt 11: Twitter/X Thread — 7-Tweet Thread

Twitter/X threads are the platform's highest-engagement format. A well-structured thread takes one idea and breaks it into a narrative arc across 7 tweets, each one delivering enough value to earn the "read more." This prompt generates a complete thread with a hook tweet, 5 body tweets, and a closing tweet with CTA.

Prompt WP-11: Twitter/X Thread
Act as a Twitter/X content strategist who writes viral threads. Write a 7-tweet thread that breaks down one idea into a compelling narrative.

Structure:
— Tweet 1 (Hook): Bold claim or promise that makes people click "Show this thread." Must work as a standalone tweet. End with a thread indicator.
— Tweets 2–6 (Body): Each tweet covers one sub-point. Each must be valuable on its own AND build on the previous tweet. Use specific examples, numbers, or mini-stories. No filler tweets.
— Tweet 7 (Close): Summarize the key takeaway in one line. Add a CTA (follow, retweet, or link).

Rules: Each tweet must be under 280 characters. No hashtags (they reduce engagement on threads). No emojis. Use line breaks within tweets for readability.

Variables:
— Thread Topic: [THE ONE IDEA YOU ARE BREAKING DOWN]
— Target Audience: [WHO SHOULD READ THIS]
— Your Credibility: [WHY YOU ARE QUALIFIED TO WRITE ABOUT THIS]
— CTA: [WHAT YOU WANT READERS TO DO AFTER THE THREAD]

Prompt 12: TikTok/Reels Script — Hook, Problem, Solution, CTA

Short-form video is the fastest-growing content format, but writing a script that works in 30–60 seconds requires a completely different skill set than long-form writing. Every second counts. This prompt generates a complete TikTok/Reels script with timestamp markers, on-screen text suggestions, and the hook-problem-solution-CTA structure that drives the highest completion rates.

Prompt WP-12: TikTok/Reels Script
Act as a short-form video scriptwriter who creates TikTok and Instagram Reels content with high completion rates. Write a 30–60 second video script.

Structure:
— [0–3s] Hook: Pattern interrupt or bold statement that stops the scroll. This must be the first words spoken. Suggest on-screen text overlay.
— [3–10s] Problem: Name the specific pain point the viewer has. Make it relatable.
— [10–25s] Solution: Present the solution with 2–3 specific steps or tips. Keep language simple and spoken-word natural.
— [25–35s] Proof/Result: Show the outcome. Use a specific number, before/after, or testimonial.
— [35–45s] CTA: Tell the viewer exactly what to do next. One action.

Also provide:
— On-screen text suggestions for each section
— 3 alternative hooks for testing

Output: Script should feel conversational when read aloud, not scripted.

Variables:
— Video Topic: [WHAT THE VIDEO IS ABOUT]
— Target Viewer: [WHO IS WATCHING]
— Solution/Product: [WHAT YOU ARE PRESENTING AS THE SOLUTION]
— Proof Point: [SPECIFIC RESULT, STAT, OR TESTIMONIAL]
— CTA: [FOLLOW, LINK IN BIO, COMMENT, SAVE, etc.]

Blog & SEO Prompts (Prompts 13–16)

Content marketing drives long-term organic traffic, but it is also the most time-consuming marketing activity. A single blog post takes 4–6 hours to research, outline, write, and optimize. These four prompts cover the content creation workflow from planning to production: a search-intent-matched blog outline, an SEO-optimized meta title and description generator, a structured content brief for freelance writers, and a listicle framework for the format that consistently earns the most clicks.

Prompt 13: Blog Post Outline — Search-Intent Matched

The most common mistake in content marketing is writing an article that does not match what the searcher actually wants. Someone searching "how to start a podcast" wants a step-by-step process, not an opinion piece about podcasting trends. This prompt analyzes the target keyword, determines the search intent, and generates an outline that matches what Google wants to rank — with H2s, H3s, word count targets, and internal linking suggestions.

Prompt WP-13: Blog Post Outline
Act as an SEO content strategist who creates blog outlines optimized for search intent and topical depth. Generate a complete blog post outline for the target keyword below.

Process:
1. Determine the primary search intent (informational, commercial, transactional, or navigational)
2. Identify the content format that best matches that intent (how-to, listicle, comparison, guide, review)
3. Generate the outline

Outline structure:
— Suggested title (with keyword naturally included)
— Meta description (under 155 characters)
— Introduction approach (2–3 sentences describing what to cover)
— 5–8 H2 sections, each with:
  — H2 heading
  — 2–3 H3 sub-sections where appropriate
  — Key points to cover (3–5 bullets per section)
  — Suggested word count for that section
— FAQ section with 3–5 questions (for People Also Ask)
— Internal linking suggestions (3 related topics to link to)
— Total suggested word count

Variables:
— Target Keyword: [PRIMARY KEYWORD]
— Secondary Keywords: [2–3 RELATED KEYWORDS TO INCLUDE]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS SEARCHING FOR THIS]
— Business Context: [WHAT YOUR BUSINESS DOES — for internal linking relevance]

Variables:

Prompt 16: Listicle Article Framework — Numbered Format

Listicles consistently outperform other content formats in click-through rate and time on page. The numbered format sets clear expectations, makes the article scannable, and gives readers a sense of progress as they read. This prompt generates a complete listicle framework with an engaging intro, numbered items with depth, and a conclusion that drives action.

Prompt WP-16: Listicle Framework
Act as a content marketing writer who creates high-performing listicle articles. Generate a complete listicle framework for the topic below.

Structure:
— Title: Number + keyword + benefit (e.g., "12 [KEYWORD] That [BENEFIT]")
— Introduction (100–150 words): State what the list covers, who it is for, and why these specific items were chosen. Include a brief methodology note.
— List items (each 150–250 words): For each numbered item, provide:
  — H2 or H3 with the item number and name
  — What it is (1–2 sentences)
  — Why it matters (1–2 sentences)
  — How to use it or implement it (2–3 sentences)
  — Pro tip or common mistake (1 sentence)
— Conclusion (100–150 words): Summarize the top 3 picks, explain how to choose, and include a CTA.

Variables:
— Listicle Topic: [TOPIC — e.g., "AI tools for content marketing"]
— Number of Items: [NUMBER]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS READING THIS]
— Selection Criteria: [HOW ITEMS WERE CHOSEN — e.g., "tested by our team," "based on user reviews"]

Landing Page Prompts (Prompts 17–20)

Landing pages are where traffic turns into revenue. Every ad, email, and social post you create eventually sends people to a landing page — and if that page does not convert, everything upstream was wasted effort. These four prompts cover the sections that make or break a landing page: the hero section that determines whether visitors stay or bounce, the benefit stack that translates features into outcomes, the FAQ section that handles objections, and the final CTA block that closes the deal.

Prompt 17: Hero Section — Headline, Subheadline, Bullets, CTA

The hero section is the most important 600 pixels on any landing page. It is what visitors see before they scroll — and 60% of them never scroll. This prompt generates a complete above-the-fold hero section with a benefit-driven headline, a supporting subheadline that adds specificity, 3–4 bullet points that preview the value, and a CTA button with action-oriented copy.

Prompt WP-17: Landing Page Hero
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in landing page hero sections. Write a complete above-the-fold hero section for the product/offer below.

Structure:
— Headline: 6–12 words. Lead with the primary benefit, not the product name. Must be specific enough to convey value and broad enough to apply to the full target audience.
— Subheadline: 15–25 words. Add specificity to the headline: who it is for, what it includes, or how it works.
— 3–4 bullet points: Each starts with a tangible outcome (not a feature). Format: "[Outcome] — [brief how]."
— CTA button text: 3–5 words, action-oriented. Not "Submit" or "Learn More."
— Supporting text under CTA: 1 line addressing the biggest objection (risk reversal, guarantee, or social proof).

Also provide 2 alternative headlines using different angles (curiosity, social proof).

Variables:
— Product/Offer: [PRODUCT NAME + WHAT IT IS]
— Target Audience: [WHO THIS IS FOR]
— Primary Benefit: [THE #1 OUTCOME]
— Price/Offer: [PRICE OR OFFER DETAILS]
— Social Proof: [USER COUNT, RATING, OR TESTIMONIAL SNIPPET]

Prompt 18: Benefit Stack — Feature-to-Benefit Translation

Features tell people what your product does. Benefits tell people what your product does for them. Most landing pages list features and hope the visitor connects the dots. This prompt takes your feature list and translates each one into a benefit statement with emotional resonance, then organizes them into a scannable benefit stack section.

Prompt WP-18: Benefit Stack
Act as a conversion copywriter who translates product features into benefit-driven landing page copy. Create a benefit stack section for the product below.

Process: For each feature provided, generate:
— Benefit headline: 5–8 words starting with a verb or outcome (not the feature name)
— Supporting copy: 2–3 sentences explaining HOW the feature delivers the benefit. Be specific. Include one concrete example or scenario.

Output format: Organize as a scrollable section with 4–6 benefit blocks. Each block should work independently (visitors scan, they do not read top to bottom).

Rules: Never start a benefit headline with the feature name. Always lead with what the user gets, not what the product has. Avoid superlatives (best, fastest, most powerful). Use specific outcomes instead.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Feature List: [LIST 4–6 KEY FEATURES WITH BRIEF DESCRIPTIONS]
— Target Audience: [WHO IS BUYING]
— Primary Use Case: [HOW MOST CUSTOMERS USE THE PRODUCT]

Prompt 19: FAQ/Objection Handler — Buyer-Objection FAQs

The FAQ section on a landing page is not really about answering questions — it is about handling objections. Every question a potential buyer has is a reason they might not buy. "Does it work with my existing tools?" means "I am worried about compatibility." "What if it does not work for me?" means "I am worried about wasting money." This prompt generates FAQ pairs that address the real objection behind each question.

Prompt WP-19: FAQ/Objection Handler
Act as a conversion copywriter who writes FAQ sections designed to overcome buying objections. Generate 6–8 FAQ pairs for the landing page described below.

Process:
1. Identify the most common objections for this type of product (price, fit, trust, timing, competition)
2. Frame each objection as a natural question a prospect would ask
3. Write an answer that addresses the objection directly, provides evidence, and ends with a confidence-building statement

Rules:
— Questions should sound like a real person asking, not a marketing team planting questions
— Answers should be 2–4 sentences: acknowledge, answer, reassure
— Include at least one answer with a specific number or stat
— Include at least one answer that mentions the guarantee or refund policy
— Never use "We are glad you asked" or similar filler

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Price: [PRICE]
— Guarantee: [GUARANTEE DETAILS]
— Top 3 Objections: [OBJECTION 1, OBJECTION 2, OBJECTION 3]
— Target Buyer: [WHO IS BUYING AND WHAT THEY NEED]

Prompt 20: Final CTA Block — Urgency-Driven Closing Section

The final CTA block is the last chance to convert a visitor who has read your entire page. They have seen the hero, scanned the benefits, read the FAQ — and they are still here. That means they are interested but need one more push. This prompt generates a closing section that combines a summary of value, urgency (real or scarcity-based), risk reversal, and a final CTA that makes the decision feel easy.

Prompt WP-20: Final CTA Block
Act as a landing page copywriter writing the final CTA section at the bottom of a sales page. This is for visitors who have read the full page and need one final push to convert.

Structure:
— Section headline: Restate the primary benefit in a new way. Not "Ready to get started?" — something that reminds them what they are getting.
— Value recap: 2–3 sentences summarizing what is included and the total value.
— Urgency element: 1–2 sentences with a real deadline, limited availability, or price increase. Only use urgency that is actually true.
— Risk reversal: 1 sentence stating the guarantee clearly.
— CTA button text: action-oriented, 3–5 words.
— Sub-CTA text: 1 line addressing the final hesitation (e.g., "One-time payment. No subscription. Lifetime access.").

Output: 100–150 words total. Tone: confident, clear, zero pressure.

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— What is Included: [BRIEF SUMMARY OF WHAT THEY GET]
— Price: [CURRENT PRICE]
— Urgency: [DEADLINE, SCARCITY, OR PRICE INCREASE — must be real]
— Guarantee: [GUARANTEE DETAILS]

FAQ — AI Writing Prompts for Marketing

Are these AI writing prompts free to use commercially? +
Yes. Every prompt in this article is free to copy, modify, and use for commercial purposes. The prompts are templates — you fill in your own brand details, product information, and audience data. The output you generate belongs to you. Whether you use it for client work, your own business, or as part of a service you sell, there are no licensing restrictions on any prompt published here. The AI-generated content you produce with these prompts is yours to use however you see fit.
Which AI tool produces the best marketing copy? +
The quality of marketing copy depends more on the prompt than the tool. ChatGPT (GPT-4o), Claude, and Gemini all produce professional-grade output when given structured prompts with role assignments, frameworks, and variables. ChatGPT tends to be more creative and varied with ad copy. Claude excels at longer-form content, nuanced tone matching, and following complex instructions precisely. Gemini integrates well with Google Workspace for workflow efficiency. Test the same prompt across two tools and pick the one that consistently matches your brand voice best.
How do I maintain brand consistency across AI-generated content? +
Create a brand voice document that includes your tone descriptors (e.g., "conversational, confident, zero jargon"), 3–5 example sentences that capture your voice, words you always use, and words you never use. Paste this document into every prompt as a variable or in the role assignment. The prompts in this article include tone parameters, but adding your specific brand voice guide ensures consistency across ads, emails, social posts, blog content, and landing pages. Update the document quarterly as your brand evolves.
Can AI writing prompts replace a marketing team? +
No — and that is not the goal. AI writing prompts replace the blank page, not the marketer. They generate structured first drafts in minutes instead of hours, but a human still needs to add brand-specific details, verify claims, inject personality, and make strategic decisions about positioning and targeting. Think of these prompts as a 10x multiplier for an existing marketer, not a replacement for one. A solo marketer with a good prompt library can produce the output volume of a small team — but the strategic thinking, brand judgment, and creative direction still require a human brain.
What is the difference between a prompt and a template? +
A template is a fill-in-the-blank document where you replace placeholder text with your own words — the structure is fixed, and you provide the content. A prompt is an instruction set for an AI model that generates original content based on your inputs — the structure is guided, but the output is dynamic. Templates give you a fixed structure with your words inserted. Prompts give you a dynamic output that adapts to your variables, audience, and context. The prompts in this article are "prompt templates" — they combine the repeatability and consistency of templates with the generative, adaptive power of AI.
