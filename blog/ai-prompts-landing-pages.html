7 AI Prompts for Landing Pages That Double Conversions

ContentKit AI•11 min read

AI prompts for landing pages are conversion-focused instructions that generate hero headlines, benefit sections, social proof blocks, FAQ copy, and CTAs — every section a high-converting landing page needs. This guide delivers 7 copy-paste prompts that cover a complete landing page from top to bottom, each built on proven conversion copywriting frameworks.

Most landing pages fail. Not because the product is bad, not because the traffic is wrong, but because the copy does not do its job. The average landing page converts at 2.35%. The top 10% convert at 11.45% or higher. The difference between those two numbers is not design or traffic source — it is copy. Specifically, it is whether each section of the page does its psychological job: the headline hooks attention, the problem section creates urgency, the benefits section builds desire, the social proof section eliminates doubt, and the CTA section drives action.

When most people use AI to write landing page copy, they type something like "write me a landing page for my product" and get back a wall of generic text that sounds like it was assembled from a brochure template. No structure. No conversion psychology. No section-by-section strategy. The output reads like AI because the input gave the AI nothing meaningful to work with.

Structured prompts fix this entirely. Instead of asking AI for "a landing page," you give it a specific section, a specific conversion framework, a specific role, and specific variables about your product and audience. The result is copy that follows the same psychological patterns professional conversion copywriters charge $3,000-$8,000 to produce — hero sections that hook in under 3 seconds, benefit stacks that translate features into outcomes, social proof layouts that build real credibility, and CTAs that create genuine urgency.

These 7 AI prompts for landing pages cover every section of a complete, high-converting page. Use all 7 for cold traffic and higher-price offers. Use prompts 1, 3, and 7 for warm-traffic short-form pages. Each prompt includes the full template with variables, the conversion psychology behind the section, and example output you can reference.

Why Landing Page Copy Fails (and How Structured Prompts Fix It)

Landing page copy fails for three predictable reasons, and understanding them explains why structured AI prompts produce dramatically better results than freeform writing or generic AI instructions.

Reason 1: The headline talks about the product instead of the outcome. Visitors do not care what your product is. They care what it does for them. "AI-Powered Marketing Suite" tells a visitor nothing. "Cut your content creation time by 70%" tells them exactly what they gain. Most AI-generated headlines default to product descriptions because that is how most people describe their products in the prompt. The hero section prompt below forces outcome-focused language by constraining the headline to a transformation statement under 12 words.

Reason 2: The page lists features instead of benefits. Features are what the product does. Benefits are what the customer gains. "500+ prompt templates" is a feature. "Never stare at a blank page again" is a benefit. When you ask AI to "write about my product features," it lists features. When you give AI the benefit stack prompt below — with explicit instructions to translate each feature into a bolded micro-outcome — it produces the kind of benefit-driven copy that makes visitors feel the value.

Reason 3: There is no objection handling. Every visitor arrives with objections: "Is this worth the price?" "Will it work for my industry?" "How long until I see results?" "What if it does not work?" A landing page without an FAQ or objection-handling section leaves those questions unanswered, and unanswered objections kill conversions. The FAQ prompt below generates 8-10 objection-based questions with direct answers — not deflections, not vague reassurances, but specific, transparent responses that eliminate hesitation.

Structured prompts solve all three problems because they encode conversion copywriting principles directly into the instruction. The AI is not guessing what to write — it is following a proven framework with your specific product details plugged into the variables. That is the difference between a 2% conversion rate and a 10%+ conversion rate.

7 AI Prompts for Every Landing Page Section

Each prompt below covers one section of a complete landing page. They are ordered from top to bottom — the same order a visitor experiences the page. You can use them individually to rewrite a specific section or run all 7 in sequence to build a complete landing page from scratch.

Prompt 1: Hero Section (Headline + Subheadline + Bullets + CTA)

The hero section is the single most valuable piece of real estate on any landing page. Research from Nielsen Norman Group confirms that visitors form a judgment about your page in less than 3 seconds. If your headline does not communicate a clear, desirable outcome in that window, the visitor bounces — and no amount of brilliant copy below the fold will save you. The hero section must accomplish four things simultaneously: hook attention with an outcome-focused headline, clarify who this is for and what makes it different with a subheadline, reinforce value with three scannable benefit bullets, and drive action with a CTA button that tells visitors exactly what to do next. This prompt produces all four elements in one output, optimized for cold traffic conversion.

The hero section must include: (1) a primary headline — one sentence, outcome-focused, under 12 words. The headline must communicate the transformation or result, NOT describe the product. No product names in the headline; (2) a subheadline — one to two sentences that clarify who this is for and what makes it different from alternatives, under 30 words; (3) three benefit bullets — each starting with a bolded micro-benefit (3-4 words describing the outcome), followed by a single explanatory sentence that connects the benefit to a specific pain point the audience has; (4) a CTA button label — action-oriented, under 5 words, using a verb that implies getting a result (not "Learn More" or "Submit"). Prompt 2: Pain/Problem Section (PAS Problem Amplification)

After the hero hooks attention, the pain section makes the visitor feel the cost of their current situation. This is where the Problem-Agitate-Solve framework earns its reputation as the most reliable conversion copywriting structure in direct response. The psychology is straightforward: people are more motivated to escape pain than to pursue pleasure. A visitor who intellectually knows they "should" improve their landing page will not act. A visitor who feels the frustration of watching competitors win clients with better pages, who calculates the revenue they have already lost to low conversion rates, who recognizes the pattern of trying and failing with generic AI tools — that visitor is ready to buy. The PAS prompt below produces three paragraphs that systematically amplify the problem before your solution section resolves it.

Prompt — LP-02: Pain/Problem Section (PAS Framework)
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in the Problem-Agitate-Solve framework for landing pages. Write a 3-paragraph pain/problem section for the landing page described below. Structure: Paragraph 1 — PROBLEM: Describe the specific, daily frustration your target audience faces. Use concrete details (time wasted, money lost, opportunities missed). Make it feel personally familiar. Paragraph 2 — AGITATE: Amplify the cost of inaction. What happens if they do not solve this problem? Include emotional cost (frustration, self-doubt), financial cost (revenue lost, money wasted on bad solutions), and practical cost (time, missed opportunities). Do not be dramatic — be accurate and specific. Paragraph 3 — BRIDGE: Transition from the problem to the solution. Acknowledge that the problem is not their fault — the tools and approaches they have tried were not built for their situation. End with a single sentence that positions the product as the specific solution without yet describing it. Do not mention the product name until the last sentence. Tone: empathetic, direct, specific. Total length: 200-250 words.

Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
Target Audience: [TARGET AUDIENCE]
Core Pain Point: [CORE PAIN POINT — the daily frustration]
Failed Alternatives: [FAILED ALTERNATIVES — what they have tried that did not work]
Cost of Inaction: [COST OF INACTION — what happens if they do nothing]

The PAS framework works on landing pages because it mirrors the internal dialogue your visitor is already having. They arrived at your page because they have a problem. The pain section confirms that you understand their problem better than they do — and that specificity builds trust before you ever ask for a sale. When filling in the variables, be as specific as possible about the daily frustration. "Spending too much time on marketing" is weak. "Spending 3 hours every Monday writing social media posts that get 12 likes and zero leads" is specific enough to make someone nod.

Prompt 3: Benefit Stack (6 Feature-to-Benefit Bullets)

The benefit stack is where most landing pages either win or lose the conversion. The mistake nearly every business makes is listing features — what the product includes or does — instead of benefits — what the customer gains or avoids. Features speak to logic. Benefits speak to desire. A visitor who reads "500+ marketing prompt templates" processes that as information. A visitor who reads "Never stare at a blank page again — every prompt is pre-written and ready to customize" processes that as relief from a frustration they feel every day. This prompt forces the AI to translate every feature into a bolded micro-outcome followed by an explanatory sentence that connects the feature to a specific pain point. The result is a benefit stack that makes visitors feel the value instead of just understanding it.

Format for each bullet: (1) a bolded micro-outcome headline (3-5 words that describe the result the user experiences, NOT the feature itself), followed by (2) one explanatory sentence that connects the feature to a specific pain point or desire the target audience has. Rules: Never start the headline with the feature name. Never use the word "feature." Every headline must answer the visitor's unspoken question: "What does this do for ME?" Tone: confident, specific, outcome-focused.

Prompt 4: Social Proof Section (Testimonial Layout + Customer Outreach)

Social proof is the most powerful conversion element on a landing page because it transfers the persuasion burden from you to your customers. When you say your product is great, that is marketing. When a customer says your product is great, that is evidence. The challenge is that most businesses display testimonials poorly — a generic "What Our Customers Say" headline followed by vague quotes like "Great product! Would recommend." That kind of social proof actually hurts credibility because it looks fabricated. This prompt solves two problems at once: it generates the section layout with a conversion-optimized headline and formatted testimonial slots, and it produces the exact questions you should send to real customers to elicit the kind of specific, results-driven quotes that actually move the conversion needle.

Prompt — LP-04: Social Proof / Testimonial Section
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in social proof sections for high-converting landing pages. Generate a complete testimonial section for the landing page described below. Include: (1) A section headline that frames the testimonials around a specific outcome or result — NOT "What Our Customers Say" or "Testimonials." The headline should reinforce the main benefit of the product. (2) Three testimonial placeholder slots. For each slot, provide: (a) a description of the ideal customer profile for that testimonial (e.g., "small business owner, 6 months using the product, specific revenue result"), (b) a formatting template showing Name, Role/Title, Company, and a one-sentence context line, (c) the exact email or survey question you would send to a real customer to get a usable, specific quote for that slot. (3) A brief note on testimonial display best practices (photo, star rating, bold the key result sentence). The goal is a section you can build immediately and populate with real quotes using the outreach questions provided.

Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
Main Outcome: [MAIN OUTCOME]

The outreach questions are the most valuable part of this prompt's output. Most businesses ask customers "Can you write us a testimonial?" and get back "Great product!" because the customer does not know what to say. Specific questions like "What were you doing before you found us, and what changed in the first 30 days?" produce specific answers that other visitors can relate to. Use the questions the prompt generates, send them to 5-10 real customers, and populate the section with genuine quotes.

Prompt 5: How It Works Section (3-Step Process Breakdown)

The "How It Works" section exists for one reason: to reduce perceived complexity. Even after a visitor is interested in your product, a nagging question remains — "But how does it actually work?" If the answer feels complicated, they hesitate. If the answer is a simple 3-step process, they think "I can do that" and keep scrolling toward the buy button. The psychology here is cognitive fluency — the easier something is to understand, the more likely people are to trust it and act on it. Three steps is the sweet spot. Two feels oversimplified. Four or more starts to feel like work. This prompt produces a clean 3-step breakdown with a step label, a short description, and a micro-outcome for each step that shows the visitor exactly what happens between "buy" and "result."

Prompt — LP-05: How It Works Section (3-Step Process)
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in "How It Works" sections for SaaS and digital product landing pages. Write a 3-step process breakdown for the product described below. Format for each step: (1) Step number and label (e.g., "Step 1: [Action Verb + Object]") — the label should be 3-5 words starting with an action verb; (2) A 2-3 sentence description that explains what the user does in this step and what happens as a result; (3) A bolded micro-outcome (one sentence) that tells the user what they have achieved after completing this step. The overall arc should be: Step 1 = getting started (low effort), Step 2 = using the product (core action), Step 3 = experiencing the result (outcome). Also write a section headline that is NOT "How It Works" — make it outcome-oriented. Tone: clear, simple, confidence-building.

Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
What the User Does First: [FIRST ACTION — e.g., sign up, download, choose a template]
Core Product Action: [CORE ACTION — the main thing the user does with the product]
End Result: [END RESULT — the outcome or deliverable the user walks away with]
Time to First Result: [TIME TO FIRST RESULT]

When filling in the variables, focus on making Step 1 feel effortless. The biggest conversion barrier at this stage is perceived effort. If Step 1 is "Schedule a 30-minute onboarding call," you have introduced friction. If Step 1 is "Pick a template and fill in 3 fields," you have made it feel easy. The prompt is structured to guide the AI toward a low-friction first step, but your variable input determines how easy it actually sounds.

Prompt 6: FAQ/Objection Handler (8-10 Buyer-Objection FAQs)

The FAQ section is the most underrated conversion tool on a landing page. Most businesses treat it as an afterthought — a dumping ground for generic questions like "How do I contact support?" But the best-converting landing pages use the FAQ section as a systematic objection handler. Every question represents a reason someone might not buy. Every answer is a chance to remove that barrier. The conversion psychology is simple: unanswered objections kill sales. A visitor who thinks "I wonder if this works for my industry" and does not find an answer will leave and never come back. A visitor who finds that exact question with a specific, confident answer keeps scrolling toward the CTA. This prompt generates 8-10 FAQs based on the most common buying objections for your product type, with each answer written to address the concern directly — not deflect it, not hedge, not redirect to a sales call.

Prompt — LP-06: FAQ / Objection Handler Section
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in FAQ sections that function as objection handlers on landing pages. Generate 8-10 frequently asked questions for the product described below. Rules: (1) Every question must represent a real objection or concern a potential buyer would have — NOT a generic support question. Include objections about: price/value, results timeline, technical difficulty, comparison to alternatives, refund/guarantee, industry fit, and time commitment. (2) Every answer must be 2-3 sentences that address the objection directly. No hedging, no "it depends," no redirecting to a sales call. Be specific and transparent. If there is a limitation, acknowledge it honestly and reframe it. (3) The first FAQ should address the #1 objection for this product type. The last FAQ should address the guarantee or risk-reversal. Tone: confident, transparent, helpful.

Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
Product Type: [PRODUCT TYPE]
Price: [PRICE]
Guarantee: [GUARANTEE — e.g., 30-day money-back, no questions asked]
Target Audience: [TARGET AUDIENCE]
Top Objection: [TOP OBJECTION — the #1 reason people hesitate to buy]
Competitors/Alternatives: [COMPETITORS OR ALTERNATIVES they might be considering]

The key to a high-converting FAQ section is ordering the questions by objection severity. Put the #1 objection first — the one that stops the most people from buying. For a $147 digital product, that is usually "Is this worth the price?" For a $49/month subscription, it is usually "How long until I see results?" For a service-based offer, it is usually "Will this work for my specific situation?" The prompt is structured to address the top objection first and the guarantee last, which mirrors the psychological journey from "I am skeptical" to "I feel safe trying this."

Prompt 7: Final CTA Block (Restated Headline + Value Summary + Urgency + CTA)

The final CTA block is the most important conversion element for visitors who have read the entire page. These are your most engaged prospects — they have consumed every section, processed every benefit, read every FAQ answer, and they are now at the bottom of the page making a decision. The final CTA block must do four things: restate the main outcome (in different language from the hero headline, so it feels fresh rather than repetitive), summarize the core value in one sentence (a compression of everything above into a single reason to buy), introduce urgency (a legitimate reason to act now rather than "think about it"), and present a clear, action-oriented CTA button. This is the section where "I will think about it later" either converts to "I am buying this now" or dies forever. Most "I will think about it" visitors never come back.

Prompt — LP-07: Final CTA Block
Act as a conversion copywriter who specializes in closing CTA blocks for landing pages. Write a final CTA section for the bottom of the landing page described below. Structure: (1) A restated headline that reinforces the main outcome — use DIFFERENT phrasing and angle from the hero headline. If the hero was outcome-focused, make this one pain-avoidance focused, or vice versa. Under 12 words. (2) One sentence that summarizes the entire value proposition — what the buyer gets, who it is for, and why it is worth the price. This is the "if you read nothing else on this page" sentence. (3) An urgency element — a specific, legitimate reason to act now. Use one of: limited-time price, bonus expiring, limited availability, or price increase date. Do NOT use fake urgency or countdown timers with no real deadline. (4) A CTA button label — action-oriented, under 5 words, using a verb that implies getting a result. (5) A one-line risk reversal statement below the button (e.g., "30-day money-back guarantee. No questions asked."). Tone: confident, urgent without being desperate, direct.

How to Assemble a Complete Landing Page From These 7 Prompts

These 7 prompts are designed to work as a system. Running them in sequence produces a complete landing page with every section a cold-traffic visitor needs to go from "I do not know what this is" to "I am buying this." Here is the assembly process.

Step 1: Fill in your product variables once. Before you run any prompts, write down your product name, target audience, main outcome, key features (6 of them), price, guarantee, and urgency element. You will reuse these variables across all 7 prompts, which creates messaging consistency automatically. When the hero section promises "cut your content creation time by 70%" and the final CTA restates that same outcome from a different angle, the page feels cohesive — because it is built on the same core variables.

Step 2: Run the prompts in order (1 through 7). The sequence matters. Prompt 1 (hero) establishes your headline and core outcome. Prompt 2 (pain) uses that outcome as the implied solution. Prompt 3 (benefits) expands on the specific value. Prompt 4 (social proof) reinforces the claims with customer evidence. Prompt 5 (how it works) reduces friction. Prompt 6 (FAQ) handles remaining objections. Prompt 7 (final CTA) closes the deal. This mirrors the psychological journey of a landing page visitor.

Step 3: Edit for voice and accuracy. AI produces the structure and the language patterns. You add your brand voice, real customer data, specific numbers, and any claims that need factual verification. Plan to spend 30-45 minutes editing the full output. The prompts give you a strong first draft that is 80-90% of the way there — your editing pass handles the last 10-20% that makes it genuinely yours.

Step 4: Paste into your builder section by section. Each prompt's output maps directly to a section block in any landing page builder. In Carrd, Unbounce, Leadpages, Instapage, or WordPress with Elementor, add a section for each prompt output: hero section, text block for pain, bullet list for benefits, testimonial block for social proof, steps/columns for how it works, accordion for FAQ, and a CTA section for the closing block. The copy drives the design, not the other way around.

Step 5: Run a pre-launch conversion checklist. Before publishing, verify these five elements: (1) The hero headline communicates a clear outcome in under 12 words. (2) The benefit stack uses bolded outcomes, not feature names. (3) The FAQ addresses your #1 buying objection. (4) The final CTA has a real urgency element. (5) Every CTA button on the page uses the same label for consistency. If all five check out, your page is ready to receive traffic.

The entire process — running all 7 prompts, editing the output, and pasting into a builder — takes 60-90 minutes. A professional conversion copywriter would charge $3,000-$8,000 and take 1-2 weeks to produce the same page structure. The prompts do not replace the copywriter's strategic expertise, but they eliminate the blank-page problem and give you a production-ready first draft that is structurally sound from the start.

Pro Tips for Getting Better Output From Landing Page Prompts

The prompts above work as written. But the quality of the output scales directly with the quality of your variable inputs. Here are four techniques that make a measurable difference in output quality.

Be absurdly specific in your variables. "Target Audience: marketers" produces generic copy. "Target Audience: solo freelance marketers handling 3-5 clients, spending 10+ hours per week on content creation, charging under $2K/month per client, using ChatGPT but getting bland output" produces copy that makes your ideal customer think "this is exactly my situation." The more specific the input, the more personalized the output.

Run the hero prompt twice with different angles. The first time, fill in Main Outcome with an achievement-focused statement ("produce a week of content in one afternoon"). The second time, fill it in with a pain-avoidance statement ("stop wasting 10+ hours every week on content that does not convert"). Compare the two hero sections and pick the stronger one — or A/B test them against each other for real data.

Chain the social proof prompt with real customer responses. After you run Prompt 4 and get the outreach questions, send those questions to 5-10 real customers. Then run a follow-up prompt: "Format the following raw customer responses into polished testimonial quotes. Keep the customer's voice and specific details. Bold the key result sentence in each quote. Do not add claims the customer did not make." This gives you genuine social proof that sounds real because it is real.

Use the FAQ output as your ad copy source material. The objections surfaced by Prompt 6 are the same objections your ad audience has. Turn each FAQ into an ad angle: "Worried it will not work for your industry? Here is why [Product] works for SaaS, e-commerce, coaching, and local services." Objection-based ads consistently outperform benefit-based ads for retargeting campaigns because they address the exact hesitation that prevented the first conversion.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Landing Pages

Can AI really write landing page copy that converts? +
Yes, when given structured prompts with conversion frameworks. AI produces roughly 80-90% of what a professional conversion copywriter delivers. The remaining 10-20% — brand-specific voice, real customer data, and strategic positioning — requires human editing. These prompts handle the heavy structural work (headlines, benefit stacks, objection handling, CTAs) so you focus only on refinement. Businesses using structured AI prompts for landing pages report 2-3x faster page launches and comparable conversion rates to professionally written pages after the editing pass.
What landing page builder should I use with these prompts? +
Any builder works. These prompts generate the copy — headlines, body text, bullet points, CTAs, and FAQ answers — not the design. Paste the output into Carrd, Unbounce, Leadpages, Instapage, Webflow, WordPress with Elementor, or a simple HTML page. The prompts are builder-agnostic. If your builder has a drag-and-drop editor, each prompt maps to a specific section block you can add to your page.
How long should a landing page be? +
Length depends on traffic temperature and price point. For warm traffic (email subscribers, retargeting) and low-price offers (under $50), a short page with hero + benefits + CTA converts well — roughly 500-800 words. For cold traffic and higher-price offers ($100+), you need the full stack: hero, problem, benefits, social proof, how it works, FAQ, and final CTA — roughly 1,500-2,500 words. The 7 prompts in this guide produce a complete long-form page. Use all 7 for cold traffic. Use prompts 1, 3, and 7 for a short-form warm-traffic page.
Should I write the landing page copy before or after the design? +
Copy first. Always. The copy determines the structure of the page — how many sections you need, how long each section is, and what visual elements support the message. Designing first forces you to fit copy into arbitrary containers, which almost always weakens the message. Use these 7 prompts to generate the full copy stack, then hand the copy to your designer (or paste it into your builder) and let the design serve the words.
How do I test which version of my landing page converts better? +
Run A/B tests on one element at a time. Start with the headline — it has the highest impact on conversion rate. Use the hero section prompt twice with different angles (outcome-focused vs. pain-focused) and split traffic 50/50. Most landing page builders (Unbounce, Leadpages, Instapage) have built-in A/B testing. If you use a simple builder like Carrd, use Google Optimize or create two page URLs and split traffic manually. Test for at least 100 conversions per variation before declaring a winner.
