10 AI Prompts for LinkedIn Ads That Generate B2B Leads

ContentKit AI•13 min read

AI prompts for LinkedIn ads are structured instructions that generate high-converting B2B sponsored content, InMail campaigns, lead gen forms, and conversation ads — written for professional audiences and ready to paste directly into LinkedIn Campaign Manager. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every LinkedIn ad format, each built on proven B2B direct-response frameworks that lower cost-per-lead and increase pipeline quality.

LinkedIn is the only advertising platform where you can target by job title, seniority level, company size, industry, and function simultaneously — and reach that audience while they are in a professional mindset. That targeting precision is why LinkedIn ads consistently produce the highest-quality B2B leads of any paid channel, even when cost-per-click is 3–5x higher than Google or Meta. The platform delivers decision-makers: CFOs evaluating software, Directors of HR sourcing vendors, VPs of Sales researching tools. When you reach the right person with the right message, a $15 click becomes a $50,000 pipeline opportunity.

But LinkedIn's audience has a high tolerance threshold. Professionals on LinkedIn are bombarded with vendor outreach every day — in their inbox, in their feed, in their notifications. Generic ad copy that does not immediately speak to a specific role, a specific pain point, and a specific business outcome gets ignored. The window to earn attention is the introductory text above the fold in the feed, and most advertisers waste it with corporate platitudes that sound like every other ad.

This is where structured ai prompts for linkedin ads change the outcome. Instead of writing generic copy hoping it resonates, you use prompts that force specificity: the exact job title being targeted, the exact pain point they face in that role, the exact business outcome your product delivers, and the exact framework that earns a click from a time-constrained professional. The AI handles the drafting. You handle the strategy.

The 10 prompts below cover every major LinkedIn ad format: single-image sponsored content for feed visibility, carousel ads for multi-point storytelling, video ads for higher-engagement campaigns, Message Ads (InMail) for direct outreach, Conversation Ads for branching dialogue, Lead Gen Form ads for frictionless lead capture, Text Ads for sidebar presence, Dynamic Ads for personalized follower growth, Event Ads for webinar and event promotion, and a full B2B campaign strategy prompt that ties it all together. Each prompt includes LinkedIn-specific character limits, audience-targeting guidance, and B2B copywriting frameworks.

Why AI Prompts Produce Better LinkedIn Ad Copy

LinkedIn ad copy fails for one of three reasons: it is too generic to earn attention from a specific professional audience, it is too feature-focused to communicate business value, or it uses the wrong tone for a platform where the audience is in work mode. Structured AI prompts solve all three problems by forcing the inputs that drive specificity.

Audience Specificity Beats Broad Appeal

The single biggest mistake in LinkedIn advertising is writing copy for everyone. "Boost your team's productivity" means nothing to a VP of Operations who is trying to reduce headcount costs by 20% before the board meeting. "How operations leaders cut headcount costs 20% without layoffs" speaks directly to that person's actual problem. LinkedIn's targeting can put your ad in front of exactly that VP — but only your copy can make them stop scrolling. Prompts that require you to input the exact job title, the exact business pain, and the exact outcome create the specificity that earns that stop.

B2B Buying Has Multiple Stakeholders

B2B purchases involve an average of 6.8 decision-makers. Your LinkedIn ad might reach the end user, but the champion who pushes the purchase internally, the economic buyer who approves the budget, and the IT stakeholder who evaluates security all need different messages. Structured prompts let you generate separate creative for each audience segment — same product, different angle — so your campaign speaks to every stakeholder in the buying committee simultaneously.

Professional Tone Is Not Corporate Jargon

There is a difference between professional tone and corporate jargon. Professional tone is direct, specific, and treats the reader as an intelligent peer. Corporate jargon uses buzzwords ("synergies," "best-in-class," "holistic solutions") that signal nothing and communicate less. AI prompts that specify "write in a direct, peer-to-peer professional tone — no jargon, no buzzwords" consistently produce copy that sounds like a trusted colleague sharing a relevant insight, not a vendor pitch. That distinction matters enormously on LinkedIn, where the audience has developed a strong filter for sales language.

The bottom line: ai prompts for linkedin ads do not replace your B2B marketing strategy. They execute it with greater precision, in every format, with the specificity that LinkedIn's professional audience demands. Here are the 10 prompts.

10 AI Prompts for LinkedIn Ads

Prompt 1: Sponsored Content Ad (Single Image)

Single-image sponsored content is the most common LinkedIn ad format, appearing natively in the feed alongside organic posts. The key distinction from other social ad platforms is the introductory text: LinkedIn shows the first 150 characters before a "see more" truncation, so those first two lines must earn the click. Most LinkedIn advertisers bury their hook deep in the copy. This prompt generates single-image sponsored content that leads with the most compelling insight or challenge for the target audience, with the value proposition delivered within the visible preview.

Prompt LI-01: Sponsored Content Ad (Single Image)
Act as a B2B LinkedIn advertising specialist with 10+ years of experience running sponsored content campaigns. Write a single-image sponsored content ad for the business below.

Provide:
— Introductory Text (600 characters max, but front-load the hook in the first 150 characters before truncation): Open with a specific insight, statistic, challenge, or contrarian statement that speaks directly to the target job title. Do not open with the company name or product pitch.
— Headline (70 characters max): Benefit-driven headline that appears below the image. Make it specific to the outcome, not the product.
— Description (100 characters max, optional): Supporting line that reinforces the headline with proof or a secondary benefit.
— CTA Button: Select the most appropriate from LinkedIn's options: Apply, Download, Get Quote, Learn More, Register, Sign Up, Subscribe, Visit Website, Request Demo.

Write 3 variations, each testing a different angle:
1. Insight/stat angle: Open with a data point that reveals the problem
2. Challenge angle: Open with a pain point phrased as a question the audience asks themselves
3. Outcome angle: Open with the result the audience wants and work backwards to the offer

Rules: LinkedIn audience is professional and time-constrained. No corporate jargon. No vague claims. Every sentence must earn its place. Speak peer-to-peer, not vendor-to-prospect.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Job Title: [EXACT JOB TITLE — e.g., "VP of Sales," "Head of HR," "Director of Engineering"]
— Target Industry: [INDUSTRY — e.g., "SaaS," "financial services," "manufacturing"]
— Company Size: [TARGET COMPANY SIZE — e.g., "50–500 employees," "enterprise 1,000+"]
— Core Pain Point: [SPECIFIC BUSINESS PROBLEM — e.g., "sales reps spending 40% of time on non-selling activities," "high employee turnover costing $15K per hire"]
— Key Outcome: [MEASURABLE RESULT — e.g., "close 30% more deals," "reduce time-to-hire by 3 weeks," "cut infrastructure costs by 40%"]
— Offer/CTA: [WHAT YOU ARE OFFERING — e.g., "free ROI calculator," "15-minute demo," "gated industry report"]

Variation 1 — Insight/Stat Angle:
Sales reps spend 67% of their time on tasks that have nothing to do with selling. Prospecting, data entry, follow-up scheduling, internal reporting. Only 33% of the work week actually moves deals forward. If your team is hitting 70% of quota, the problem probably is not your reps — it is their workflow. [Company Name] automates the 67% so your team can focus on the 33% that closes.

Headline: How Top Sales Teams Close 30% More Without Hiring
CTA: Request Demo

Variation 2 — Challenge Angle:
Are your sales reps actually selling — or are they your most expensive data entry team? VPs of Sales at [Company Size] companies tell us the same thing: the tools meant to help their reps are adding friction, not removing it. Three CRMs. Two outreach platforms. One spreadsheet that everyone secretly relies on. There is a better way.

Headline: One Platform. Your Entire Sales Workflow.
CTA: Get Quote

Notice how both variations speak directly to the VP of Sales without mentioning the product name in the first sentence. The hook is the audience's problem, not the advertiser's solution. That is the LinkedIn feed psychology: earn the "see more" click with relevance, then deliver the pitch.

Prompt 2: Sponsored Content Ad (Carousel)

LinkedIn carousel ads let you include up to 10 swipeable cards in a single ad unit. In a B2B context, carousels work best as micro-content pieces — a quick framework, a step-by-step process, a comparison table, or a "before and after" narrative. Each card must deliver standalone value while creating momentum to swipe to the next. This prompt generates carousel copy with a narrative arc that builds across all cards and ends with a strong CTA on the final card.

Prompt LI-02: Sponsored Content Ad (Carousel)
Act as a B2B LinkedIn advertising specialist who creates high-engagement carousel ads. Write a carousel sponsored content ad for the business below.

Structure:
— Introductory Text (600 characters max, hook in first 150): Tease the value of the carousel — what will the reader learn or gain by swiping through all the cards?
— Card 1 (Cover Card): Headline (45 characters max) that states the promise of the carousel. This is the hook that earns the first swipe.
— Cards 2–9: Each card needs a Headline (45 characters max) and Body Text (255 characters max). Each card should deliver one concrete insight, step, stat, or example. Build narrative momentum — each card should reference or build on the previous one.
— Final Card (CTA Card): Headline (45 characters max) + Body Text (255 characters max) + CTA button. This card should summarize the takeaway and make the CTA feel like the logical next step after consuming the carousel.

Carousel formats that perform best on LinkedIn:
1. Step-by-step process (e.g., "The 5-Step Framework We Use To...")
2. Myth vs. reality (e.g., "5 Things Most [Job Title]s Get Wrong About...")
3. Before and after (e.g., "What [Job Title] Life Looks Like With vs. Without...")
4. Data story (e.g., "What We Learned From Analyzing 1,000 [Relevant Data Points]")

Choose the format that best fits the offer and write a complete carousel using that structure.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Job Title: [EXACT JOB TITLE]
— Core Topic: [SUBJECT OF THE CAROUSEL — e.g., "how to build a sales pipeline," "5 hiring mistakes," "cloud cost optimization"]
— Number of Cards: [4–10]
— Offer on Final Card: [WHAT YOU ARE PROMOTING — e.g., "download the full guide," "book a strategy session," "start a free trial"]

Carousels are LinkedIn's most shareable ad format because they resemble the native educational content that performs best organically on the platform. When you design carousel ads around genuine professional insights — frameworks, processes, and data — rather than product features, they earn organic engagement on top of paid reach. That secondary engagement means your ad reaches beyond your paid audience at no additional cost.

Prompt 3: Sponsored Content Ad (Video)

LinkedIn video ads autoplay silently in the feed, which means the first three seconds of your video must communicate value without sound. Most B2B video ads fail because they open with a brand logo animation or a vague value statement that takes 10 seconds to get to the point. By then, the viewer has scrolled past. This prompt generates video ad scripts optimized for the silent autoplay environment, with on-screen text guidance and a hook that earns audio-on engagement within the first three seconds.

Prompt LI-03: Sponsored Content Ad (Video)
Act as a B2B video advertising strategist for LinkedIn. Write a video ad script for a sponsored content campaign. LinkedIn video ads autoplay silently — the first 3 seconds must communicate value without audio so the viewer decides to turn on sound.

Provide:
— Introductory Text (150 characters visible before truncation): Reinforce the video hook in text so the combined intro + video opening delivers the hook twice.
— Video Script with timing markers (30–90 seconds total):
  — 0–3 seconds: Hook (on-screen text, no voiceover reliance). Must answer "why should I watch this?" in one line.
  — 3–15 seconds: Problem statement. Describe the pain without offering the solution yet. Build tension.
  — 15–45 seconds: Solution reveal and key proof point. Introduce the product as the answer. One specific stat or case result.
  — 45–75 seconds: How it works (if relevant). 2–3 key features explained as outcomes, not functions.
  — Final 15 seconds: CTA. What to do next, why now, and what they will get.
— On-screen text for each section (what text appears on screen during each segment)
— Headline (70 characters max): Appears below the video.
— CTA Button: Best LinkedIn CTA option for this video's goal.

Rules: Write the voiceover and on-screen text separately. The on-screen text must tell the complete story for viewers watching without sound. The voiceover should add depth, not repeat the on-screen text verbatim.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Audience: [JOB TITLE AND INDUSTRY]
— Video Type: [TYPE — e.g., "product demo," "customer testimonial," "thought leadership," "explainer"]
— Core Problem: [BUSINESS PAIN BEING SOLVED]
— Key Proof Point: [STAT OR CASE STUDY RESULT]
— CTA Goal: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "book demo," "download report," "start trial"]

LinkedIn video ads under 30 seconds outperform longer formats for most B2B objectives because professionals on the platform allocate a few seconds, not a few minutes. If your product genuinely requires a longer explanation, use the first 30 seconds as a trailer that earns the click to a longer form video on your landing page. The prompt above handles this by specifying "if relevant" for the how-it-works section — an optional module you include only when the product complexity demands it.

Prompt 4: Message Ad (InMail)

LinkedIn Message Ads (formerly Sponsored InMail) land directly in a member's LinkedIn inbox and are delivered only when the member is active — guaranteeing 100% deliverability with no spam filters. The open rate benchmark is 30–50%, significantly higher than email. But the flip side is that a poorly written InMail feels intrusive in a way that a feed ad does not. The subject line, the opening sentence, and the brevity of the message must all signal that you have done the work to make this relevant — or the recipient hits "Not Interested" and your campaign scores suffer. This prompt generates InMail copy that feels like a warm outreach from a relevant peer, not a cold sales blast.

Prompt LI-04: Message Ad (InMail)
Act as a B2B LinkedIn outreach specialist experienced with Message Ads (Sponsored InMail). Write a LinkedIn Message Ad that feels like a warm, relevant outreach from a peer — not a cold sales pitch.

Provide:
— Subject Line (60 characters max): The make-or-break element. Must create curiosity or relevance without being clickbait. Avoid "RE:" or "Follow Up" tactics — LinkedIn penalizes these. Best formats: question, insight, or direct benefit statement personalized to the role.
— Message Body (500 characters recommended, 1,000 max): Structure:
  — Opening (1 sentence): Acknowledge who they are and why you are reaching out to them specifically (job title, industry, or a recent trigger event). Make it feel individually researched even at scale.
  — Problem statement (1–2 sentences): Name the specific challenge this job title faces. Do not pitch your product yet.
  — Value statement (1–2 sentences): Introduce what you offer as the answer to that challenge. Lead with the outcome, not the feature.
  — Offer (1 sentence): Make a low-friction ask — a resource, an insight, a short call. Not "buy now."
  — CTA (1 sentence): One specific next step. Do not give them options.
— CTA Button Text (20 characters max): The button text below the message.
— Sender Profile Guidance: What should the sender's LinkedIn profile headline say to maximize credibility for this message?

Rules: InMail lands in the inbox alongside personal messages. It must feel earned. No superlatives ("amazing," "revolutionary"). No passive voice. Do not mention competitors. Every sentence must do work.

Variables:
— Sender Name/Role: [SENDER — e.g., "Alex Chen, VP of Sales at Acme"]
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Job Title: [EXACT JOB TITLE]
— Target Industry: [INDUSTRY]
— Specific Pain Point: [ROLE-SPECIFIC CHALLENGE]
— Key Outcome: [MEASURABLE RESULT YOU DELIVER]
— Offer: [LOW-FRICTION ASK — e.g., "a 10-page benchmark report," "a 15-minute strategy call," "a personalized audit"]
— Urgency/Context: [WHY NOW — e.g., "Q1 planning season," "new regulation taking effect," "recent industry shift"]

Subject Line: How HR Directors at [Company Size] firms are cutting time-to-hire in half

Message Body:
Hi [First Name],

I reach out to HR Directors at [target company size] companies specifically — because Q1 hiring pressure tends to hit your function harder than any other.

Most HR leaders we speak with are managing a backlog of 20+ open roles while their ATS gives them a fire hose of unqualified applications and zero prioritization signal.

HireFlow surfaces the top 10% of applicants within 48 hours of posting, using the specific role criteria your team defines — not generic AI scoring. Our clients cut average time-to-hire from 6 weeks to 3.

I put together a quick benchmark report showing time-to-hire data across 200 companies in your industry. Worth a look before Q1 ramp-up hits full speed.

Can I send it over?

Alex

CTA Button: Send Me the Report

The example above is 497 characters — under the recommended 500. Every sentence is load-bearing. The opening acknowledges the specific role and timing without being sycophantic. The problem is named without sounding like a sales pitch. The solution is introduced through the outcome (time-to-hire cut in half), not the feature (AI scoring). And the ask is a free resource, not a demo request — the lowest-friction next step possible.

Prompt 5: Conversation Ad (Multi-CTA)

Conversation Ads are LinkedIn's most interactive format: they deliver a message with multiple CTA buttons, and each button triggers a different follow-up message, creating a branching dialogue within the inbox. This format is ideal for segmenting your audience by intent level — someone who clicks "I want to see pricing" is further down the funnel than someone who clicks "send me a free resource" — and delivering the right follow-up to each. This prompt generates a complete Conversation Ad tree with opening message, button options, and tailored follow-up messages for each branch.

Prompt LI-05: Conversation Ad (Multi-CTA)
Act as a LinkedIn advertising strategist experienced with Conversation Ads. Write a complete Conversation Ad flow that segments recipients by intent level and delivers the right follow-up message to each.

Structure:
— Opening Message (500 characters max): Introduce the value proposition and present 2–4 CTA buttons. Each button should represent a different intent level or interest area. The opening must create enough curiosity or value to earn a button click.
— For each CTA button, write:
  — Button Label (25 characters max): Clear, benefit-oriented label
  — Follow-up Message (500 characters max): Tailored response that delivers exactly what the button promised + a next-step CTA
  — Final CTA Button (25 characters max) + destination URL guidance

Design the branching flow around these intent levels:
— High intent: Ready to talk to sales / see pricing / book demo
— Medium intent: Wants more information / case studies / comparison
— Low intent: Wants a free resource / report / checklist
— Not interested: Polite off-ramp that builds goodwill (do not dead-end this branch)

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Audience: [JOB TITLE AND INDUSTRY]
— Core Value Proposition: [PRIMARY BENEFIT IN ONE SENTENCE]
— High-Intent Offer: [WHAT HIGH-INTENT RECIPIENTS GET — e.g., "personalized demo," "custom pricing proposal"]
— Medium-Intent Offer: [WHAT MEDIUM-INTENT RECIPIENTS GET — e.g., "customer case study," "product comparison guide"]
— Low-Intent Offer: [FREE RESOURCE — e.g., "benchmark report," "ROI calculator," "checklist"]

Conversation Ads outperform standard Message Ads for audiences earlier in the buying journey because they remove the pressure of a single-path response. When a recipient sees four buttons instead of one CTA, the message shifts from "we want you to do this" to "tell us where you are." That self-selection signals respect for the recipient's time and intent — and it delivers far more actionable data to your sales team about who is ready to buy.

Prompt 6: Lead Gen Form Ad

LinkedIn Lead Gen Forms are the highest-converting lead capture mechanism on the platform. When a member clicks the CTA, a form opens natively within LinkedIn, pre-filled with their profile data: name, email, job title, company name, company size, and LinkedIn profile URL. The member submits in two taps without leaving LinkedIn. Conversion rates for Lead Gen Forms average 13%, compared to 2–5% for forms on external landing pages. The key is writing the form headline and confirmation message to frame the exchange as a value transfer, not a data collection exercise. This prompt generates the complete Lead Gen Form copy — the sponsoring ad and the form itself.

Prompt LI-06: Lead Gen Form Ad
Act as a B2B LinkedIn advertising specialist who optimizes Lead Gen Form campaigns for the lowest cost-per-lead. Write the complete copy package for a Lead Gen Form ad — the sponsored content that drives the click, and the form that captures the lead.

Part 1 — Sponsored Content (drives the click to the form):
— Introductory Text (150 characters visible): The gated asset must be compelling enough to justify sharing contact details. Open with why this resource is valuable, not what it is.
— Headline (70 characters max): Benefit-driven headline for the ad card.
— CTA Button: Download, Get Quote, Learn More, Register, or Sign Up

Part 2 — Lead Gen Form:
— Form Headline (60 characters max): Should feel like the fulfillment of the ad promise — "You're almost there. Here's what you're getting:" framing.
— Form Details / Privacy Policy Text (160 characters max): What the lead gets in return for their information. Be specific about the asset and when they will receive it.
— Recommended Fields to Collect (choose 3–5 maximum — fewer fields = higher conversion): Justify each field you include. If you would not use it in your CRM segmentation, do not ask for it.
— Thank You Message (160 characters max): Confirmation that the submission worked + immediate next step (check email, download link, what to expect).
— Thank You Page URL (optional): Where to send leads after form submission for higher-intent follow-up.

Rules: Lead Gen Forms convert at 3–6x the rate of external landing pages because they eliminate friction. Do not waste that conversion advantage with a 10-field form. Every field you add drops conversion rate by 10–15%.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Gated Asset: [WHAT YOU ARE GIVING AWAY — e.g., "2026 SaaS Benchmark Report," "ROI Calculator," "30-Point Security Checklist"]
— Asset Value Proposition: [WHY THIS ASSET IS VALUABLE — e.g., "data from 500 SaaS companies on CAC, churn, and NRR benchmarks"]
— Target Audience: [JOB TITLE AND INDUSTRY]
— Delivery Method: [HOW THEY RECEIVE THE ASSET — e.g., "emailed within 5 minutes," "instant download link," "sales rep follows up with report"]

Sponsored Content Introductory Text:
We analyzed 500 SaaS companies to benchmark CAC, churn rate, NRR, and sales cycle length by company stage — because "industry average" is not useful when you are trying to know if your numbers are healthy for your stage. The 2026 SaaS Metrics Benchmark Report is available now.

Headline: 2026 SaaS Metrics Benchmark — Free Download
CTA: Download

Form Headline: Get Your Free Copy of the 2026 SaaS Benchmark
Form Details: Download the 42-page report with CAC, churn, and NRR benchmarks for 500 SaaS companies, segmented by ARR tier. Sent to your email in 5 minutes.
Fields: First Name, Last Name, Work Email, Job Title, Company Size
Thank You: You're in. Check your inbox in 5 minutes for the 2026 SaaS Benchmark Report. Questions? Reply to that email.

Five fields is the maximum for a Lead Gen Form targeting a high-quality B2B audience. The fields above (name, email, title, company size) are all used in lead routing: name for personalization, email for delivery, title for sales rep assignment, company size for segment qualification. If you cannot articulate how you will use a field in your first 24 hours with the lead, remove it from the form.

Prompt 7: Text Ad

LinkedIn Text Ads appear in the right-column sidebar on desktop and at the top of the feed on mobile. They are small: a 50x50 pixel image, a 25-character headline, and a 75-character description. Most advertisers treat Text Ads as an afterthought because they look small. That is a mistake — Text Ads have lower CPCs than sponsored content (often 50–70% lower), and they create brand frequency with the target audience even when they are not actively scrolling the feed. This prompt generates Text Ad copy that maximizes the limited space with specificity and a clear value proposition.

Prompt LI-07: Text Ad
Act as a LinkedIn Text Ad specialist. Write Text Ad copy for a sidebar placement campaign targeting a specific B2B audience. Text Ads have extremely limited space, so every character must earn its place.

Provide 5 variations (A/B test materials):
— Headline (25 characters max each): The most important real estate. Lead with the benefit or the specific audience call-out. Avoid generic statements.
— Description (75 characters max each): Expand the headline with one specific outcome, stat, or differentiator. Include the CTA in the description since there is no separate CTA button.

Angles to test across 5 variations:
1. Audience call-out + benefit: "[Job Title]s: achieve [outcome] faster"
2. Stat/proof: Lead with a specific number that creates credibility
3. Question: Pose the problem the audience is trying to solve
4. Offer-first: Lead with the free resource or trial offer
5. Competitor contrast: Position as the better alternative without naming competitors

Rules: Text Ad headlines at 25 characters are shorter than a tweet. Write with the precision of a billboard copywriter. No wasted words. Every word must either name the audience, identify the pain, or promise the outcome.

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Job Title: [EXACT JOB TITLE]
— Key Benefit: [PRIMARY OUTCOME IN ONE PHRASE]
— Key Stat: [PROOF POINT — e.g., "40% faster," "2x pipeline," "saves 5 hrs/week"]
— Offer: [FREE RESOURCE OR TRIAL]

Text Ads are LinkedIn's most cost-efficient format for building audience frequency. Run them alongside your sponsored content campaigns targeting the same audience. By the time a prospect sees your InMail or your feed ad, they have already seen your Text Ad a dozen times — and familiarity reduces the friction on the higher-cost formats. This multi-format strategy stretches your LinkedIn budget further than spending everything on sponsored content alone.

Prompt 8: Dynamic Ad (Follower Ad)

LinkedIn Dynamic Ads personalize the ad creative with the viewer's own profile photo and name — automatically, at scale. The two most common types are Follower Ads (grow your LinkedIn Company Page audience) and Spotlight Ads (drive traffic to a landing page or document). Follower Ads display the viewer's photo alongside your company logo with a "Follow [Company]" CTA. The personalization effect is significant: seeing your own face in an ad creates a self-referential moment that drives higher engagement than static creative. This prompt generates the Dynamic Ad copy and the Company Page value proposition that drives follow conversions.

Prompt LI-08: Dynamic Ad (Follower Ad)
Act as a LinkedIn Company Page growth specialist. Write copy for a LinkedIn Dynamic Follower Ad campaign designed to grow a B2B company's LinkedIn following with the right professional audience.

Dynamic Ads auto-personalize with the member's profile photo and name. Your copy must pair with that personalization to feel relevant, not gimmicky.

Provide:
— Ad Headline (50 characters max): Appears above the personalized creative. The best-performing format is a direct value statement: "Follow [Company] for [specific content type]." Avoid generic "Follow us!" instructions.
— Company Description (70 characters max): Appears below the company name in the ad. Must communicate what followers get — what kind of content, how often, why it is worth following.
— CTA Button: "Follow" (standard for Follower Ads) or "Visit" for Spotlight Ads
— Company Page Content Strategy (3–5 bullet points): What content will you actually publish to justify the follow? These bullets inform both the ad copy and the content calendar. If you cannot commit to specific content, do not run follower ads.
— Spotlight Ad Variation: Write an alternative Spotlight Ad version that drives traffic to a specific landing page rather than a Company Page follow.
  — Spotlight Headline (50 characters max)
  — Spotlight Body Text (70 characters max)
  — CTA Button: Visit, Download, Apply, Get Quote, Learn More

Variables:
— Company Name: [COMPANY NAME]
— Industry/Category: [YOUR INDUSTRY]
— Content Pillars: [WHAT YOU PUBLISH — e.g., "B2B sales frameworks, LinkedIn growth tactics, SaaS metrics breakdowns"]
— Posting Frequency: [HOW OFTEN YOU PUBLISH]
— Target Follower: [WHO YOU WANT FOLLOWING — job title and why they benefit from following]
— Spotlight Destination: [LANDING PAGE URL AND OFFER FOR SPOTLIGHT VERSION]

Follower Ads are a long-game play. The followers you acquire through this campaign become a warm organic audience for every future post you publish — which means the CPL from your organic content drops with each new follower. The ROI of LinkedIn follower growth compounds over time in a way that most ad formats do not. For B2B companies with a content-driven go-to-market motion, building a LinkedIn following through Dynamic Ads is one of the highest-leverage investments in the platform.

Prompt 9: Event Ad

LinkedIn Event Ads promote LinkedIn Events — webinars, virtual conferences, live sessions, and in-person events — directly in the feed. When a member registers for a LinkedIn Event, they receive a reminder notification when the event starts, creating a second touchpoint with no additional ad spend. This prompt generates Event Ad copy for both the sponsored content that drives registrations and the LinkedIn Event page description that converts browsers into attendees.

Prompt LI-09: Event Ad
Act as a B2B event marketing specialist running LinkedIn Event Ad campaigns. Write copy for a LinkedIn Event Ad campaign promoting a webinar, virtual event, or live session. LinkedIn Event Ads promote a LinkedIn Event and drive registrations directly on the platform.

Provide:
— Introductory Text for the Sponsored Content (150 characters visible, 600 max): Open with the problem the event solves or the outcome attendees will leave with. Do not lead with the event name.
— Event Page Headline (100 characters max): The name/title of the LinkedIn Event. Should communicate what attendees learn, not just the event category.
— Event Page Description (2,000 characters max): Structure:
  — Opening hook (2–3 sentences): The problem this event addresses and why it matters now
  — What you will learn (3–5 bullet points): Specific takeaways, not vague topics

5 Tips for Getting the Most from LinkedIn Ad Prompts

The 10 prompts above deliver the raw copy. These five principles ensure that copy performs inside LinkedIn Campaign Manager.

1. Always Name the Job Title in Your Copy

LinkedIn's targeting puts your ad in front of the right job title. Your copy should confirm that targeting by naming the role explicitly. "Are you a VP of Sales managing a team of 10+ reps?" outperforms "Attention sales leaders" by 20–40% CTR in most B2B categories because it creates an immediate relevance signal. The prompts above build job-title personalization into the structure — make sure you fill in the exact job title variable rather than a generic description. "Head of Talent Acquisition at Series B–D companies" beats "HR professional" every time.

2. Test Images as Aggressively as Copy

On LinkedIn, the image in your sponsored content drives 70% of the CTR decision before anyone reads a word of your copy. The best-performing LinkedIn ad images in B2B are: real faces of real people (not stock photography), text-on-image that reinforces the hook, document previews that show the first page of a gated asset, and data visualizations that tease the insight inside the report. Use the prompts in this article to generate copy variations, then pair each copy variation with a different image type and test them as separate ads. The winner is almost always determined more by image than by copy.

3. Exclude Irrelevant Audiences Before You Launch

LinkedIn's targeting is powerful, but the default audience includes members who match your targeting criteria but are irrelevant to your offer: students studying for the role they will hold in five years, consultants who match the title but are freelancers, employees at your own company who inflate your impression share. Before any campaign launches, add exclusions: company names (your own company, direct competitors you do not want seeing your strategy), member groups (students), and industries that are definitively not your market. Exclusions are the fastest way to lower CPL without changing a word of your ad copy.

4. Set a Minimum Audience Size of 50,000

LinkedIn campaigns with audiences under 50,000 members tend to exhaust quickly, leading to high frequency and declining CTR. More importantly, LinkedIn's Campaign Manager starts showing frequency warnings that reduce your bid competitiveness. Build your audiences to 50,000–300,000 members by combining job title targeting with job function targeting (broader reach), layering in relevant skills, and expanding to related seniority levels. If your core ICP audience is too small, run two campaigns: one to the exact ICP targeting, and one to the expanded adjacent audience with different creative that speaks to that broader group.

5. Let Campaigns Run for 14 Days Before Optimizing

LinkedIn's algorithm takes 7–14 days to exit the learning phase for most campaign objectives. Making significant changes (budget, targeting, creative) before the learning phase completes resets the algorithm and delays optimization. The only changes worth making in the first 14 days are pausing ads with zero impressions (which signals a bid or targeting issue) and fixing any LinkedIn policy disapprovals. After day 14, use actual performance data to pause underperforming ads, increase budgets on winners, and rotate in fresh creative to combat frequency fatigue. Patience in the first two weeks is the most common differentiator between advertisers who see consistent LinkedIn results and those who give up after a week.
