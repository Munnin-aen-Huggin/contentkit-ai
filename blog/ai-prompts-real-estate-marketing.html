Why AI Prompts Work Exceptionally Well for Real Estate Marketing

Real estate marketing has a structural advantage that makes AI prompts unusually effective: every property has a fixed set of facts. Bedrooms, bathrooms, square footage, year built, lot size, school district, neighborhood — these details are knowable and specific. The AI does not need to invent them. It needs to take what you give it and craft those facts into emotionally compelling copy that helps a buyer see themselves living in the home.

This is the opposite of the problem that plagues generic AI content. When there are no specific facts to work with, AI produces generic output. When you give it a 4-bedroom craftsman bungalow in Decatur, Georgia, built in 1928, with a restored original hardwood floors, a wraparound porch, a detached garage converted to a home office, and a backyard with a 100-year-old oak tree — the AI has everything it needs to produce copy that sells.

Three Structural Advantages of Real Estate AI Prompts

Repeatable formats with variable inputs. Every listing description follows a similar structure: hook, features, lifestyle, location, CTA. Every just-sold post follows a similar arc: announcement, key details, social proof, next step. AI prompts codify the best structure for each format and let you swap in the property-specific details. You get the same quality output for listing 47 as you did for listing 1.

Buyer persona targeting built into the prompt. The most effective listing copy does not describe the house to everyone — it speaks directly to the most likely buyer. A 4-bedroom home near a top elementary school sells differently to a young family than a downtown loft sells to a remote-working professional couple. Each prompt below includes a buyer persona variable that lets the AI adjust tone, emphasis, and lifestyle framing to speak to the right audience.

Volume without burnout. A solo agent producing 2–3 social posts per week, one email per week, and a listing description for every new property will write more than 200 pieces of marketing content per year. At 30–60 minutes per piece, that is 100–200 hours of writing annually — the equivalent of 2.5–5 full work weeks. With structured AI prompts, the same output takes 15–30 hours. The freed-up time goes directly into income-producing activities.

The bottom line: ai prompts for real estate marketing do not replace your market knowledge, client relationships, or negotiation skills. They replace the blank page and the hours spent staring at it. Here are the 12 prompts.

12 AI Prompts for Real Estate Marketing

Prompt 1: Property Listing Description (MLS-Optimized)

The MLS listing description is the most read piece of real estate marketing copy for any property. Buyers searching on Zillow, Realtor.com, and the MLS see this description within seconds of viewing the photos. Most agents write descriptions that list features in the order they think of them — "3 beds, 2 baths, updated kitchen, nice yard" — which tells the buyer what the house has but does not help them feel what it is like to live there. This prompt generates a description built around the buyer's lifestyle, leading with the emotional hook and weaving in the features as evidence for why this home delivers on the promise.

Prompt RE-01: Property Listing Description (MLS-Optimized)
Act as a top-producing real estate copywriter with 10+ years of experience writing MLS listings that sell above asking price. Write a compelling property listing description for the home below.

Structure:
— Opening hook (1–2 sentences): Lead with the lifestyle or the single most compelling feature. Do NOT start with the address or bedroom count.
— Feature narrative (3–4 sentences): Weave the key features into a story of how the buyer will live in this home. Use sensory language. Avoid bullet-point recitations.
— Location and neighborhood (1–2 sentences): Specific proximity to desirable landmarks, schools, or lifestyle destinations. No generic "great location" language.
— Closing CTA (1 sentence): Create urgency or exclusivity without cliche phrases like "don't miss out."

Rules: 200–250 words total. No passive voice. No agent-speak ("boasts," "features," "nestled"). Write like a lifestyle magazine, not a property disclosure. Every sentence must earn its place by adding information the buyer cannot get from the photos alone.

Variables:
— Property Type: [PROPERTY TYPE — e.g., "craftsman bungalow," "modern townhome," "ranch-style single family"]
— Bedrooms / Bathrooms: [BED/BATH COUNT]
— Square Footage: [SQ FT]
— Standout Features (top 3): [SPECIFIC FEATURES — e.g., "chef's kitchen with Wolf range," "primary suite with soaking tub," "detached ADU with full kitchen"]
— Location / Neighborhood: [NEIGHBORHOOD + KEY PROXIMITY — e.g., "Inman Park, 3 blocks from the BeltLine"]
— Target Buyer Persona: [WHO IS BUYING THIS — e.g., "young professional couple," "growing family with school-age kids," "empty nester downsizing"]
— Asking Price: [PRICE]
— One Unique Detail: [SOMETHING THAT CANNOT BE SEEN IN PHOTOS — e.g., "the kitchen faces east for morning light," "the backyard is completely private from neighbors," "original 1940s tile work in both bathrooms"]

Example Output (Inman Park Bungalow):

Morning light pours through the east-facing kitchen windows while coffee brews — this is the daily rhythm that 412 Sinclair Ave has been offering since 1928. Meticulously restored over the past three years, this 3-bedroom craftsman bungalow blends original character — think wide-plank heart pine floors, picture-rail moldings, and claw-foot tub — with a kitchen that a professional cook would actually use: Carrara marble countertops, a 36-inch gas range, and custom walnut cabinetry that goes to the ceiling. The detached garage is finished as a home office or creative studio, completely separate from the main house, with its own mini-split and fiber connection. Step outside and the wraparound porch overlooks a fenced backyard anchored by a 90-year-old magnolia whose canopy creates complete privacy from the street. Three blocks to the BeltLine trail, four to Krog Street Market. Homes with this combination of original integrity and modern livability do not appear on the market twice. Offered at $875,000.

Notice what this description does not say: "boasts," "nestled," "don't miss," or "move-in ready." It earns the buyer's imagination through specific, sensory details that photographs cannot communicate — the east-facing morning light, the privacy of the magnolia, the fiber connection in the studio. That specificity is what converts a browser into a showing request.

Prompt 2: Luxury Property Description

Luxury listing copy operates by entirely different rules than standard MLS descriptions. The buyers are sophisticated. They have seen dozens of properties and can spot generic copy instantly. Luxury descriptions must convey exclusivity, craftsmanship, and provenance — the story of the home, the caliber of the finishes, and the specific lifestyle it enables for people who can afford any home they want. Price is rarely mentioned until the end, if at all. This prompt generates luxury property copy that positions the home as a rare opportunity rather than a property for sale.

Prompt RE-02: Luxury Property Description
Act as a luxury real estate copywriter whose work appears in Architectural Digest, The Wall Street Journal real estate section, and Christie's International Real Estate. Write a property description for the luxury home below.

Luxury copy principles:
— Provenance and rarity: Establish why this property is singular — what cannot be replicated about it (location, architecture, history, views)
— Craftsmanship language: Name the artisans, the materials, the countries of origin. "European oak" not "hardwood floors." "Waterworks unlacquered brass fixtures" not "updated bathrooms."
— Lifestyle aspiration: Describe the life the buyer will live, not the rooms they will occupy
— Restraint: Avoid hyperbole and exclamation points. Confidence does not shout.
— Length: 300–400 words. Paragraphs of 3–5 sentences. No bullet points.

Variables:
— Property Type and Architecture: [STYLE — e.g., "contemporary estate," "Georgian revival," "oceanfront compound"]
— Square Footage / Acreage: [SIZE]
— Bedrooms / Bathrooms: [BED/BATH]
— Signature Features (top 5): [SPECIFIC HIGH-END DETAILS — e.g., "Boffi kitchen," "motorized glass wall system," "resort-caliber pool and spa by Claffey Pools"]
— Location and Setting: [WHERE AND WHY IT MATTERS — e.g., "1.4 private acres in Preston Hollow, 200 feet of lake frontage"]
— Provenance or Story: [HISTORY, ARCHITECT, OR NOTABLE DETAIL — e.g., "designed by Lake|Flato Architects," "original 1920 carriage house preserved on-site"]
— Price: [ASKING PRICE — present at the end or omit entirely for ultra-luxury]

The single most important instruction in this prompt is "name the artisans, the materials, the countries of origin." Generic luxury copy says "beautiful kitchen." Effective luxury copy says "Boffi cabinetry in lacquered silk finish, Calacatta Borghini marble sourced from the same quarry that supplied the Vatican." That level of specificity signals to sophisticated buyers that this agent knows the property intimately — and that the property deserves that level of attention.

Prompt 3: Open House Invitation (Email + Social)

Open house marketing is a two-channel problem: you need an email to your buyer database and a social post for Instagram and Facebook, each optimized for its format. Email can be longer and more descriptive, with a subject line that earns the open. Social needs to stop the scroll in the first two lines before the "more" cutoff. This prompt generates both in a single pass, so you have everything you need to promote the open house across channels without writing the same content twice in different tones.

Prompt RE-03: Open House Invitation (Email + Social)
Act as a real estate marketing specialist. Write both a marketing email AND a social media post to promote an upcoming open house. Each piece should be optimized for its format but carry the same brand voice and property highlights.

Email requirements:
— Subject line (5 options): Create urgency and curiosity. Avoid generic "Open House This Weekend" subjects.
— Preview text (1 option, 90 characters max): Complements the subject line
— Body (150–200 words): Hook, top 3 property highlights, open house details (date, time, address), and a "RSVP / add to calendar" CTA

Social media post requirements (Instagram / Facebook):
— First 2 lines (before the "more" cutoff): Must stop the scroll and communicate the property's single most compelling feature
— Body (3–5 sentences): Key details, open house date/time, call to action
— Hashtags (10–15): Mix of hyperlocal (#[NeighborhoodName]RealEstate), market-level (#[CityName]Homes), and broad (#JustListed #OpenHouse)
— Caption total: Under 300 words

Variables:
— Property Address: [ADDRESS]
— Property Type and Quick Stats: [E.g., "4-bed, 3-bath craftsman, 2,800 sq ft"]
— Top 3 Features: [SPECIFIC HIGHLIGHTS]
— Open House Date and Time: [DATE, START TIME – END TIME]
— Asking Price: [PRICE]
— Neighborhood: [NEIGHBORHOOD NAME]
— Agent Name and Contact: [AGENT NAME, PHONE / DM]
— Tone: [E.g., "warm and inviting," "upscale and exclusive," "energetic and conversational"]

The key to effective open house marketing is treating the email and the social post as complementary — not identical — pieces. The email goes to people who already know the agent and the market; it can be more detailed and conversational. The social post reaches people who are scrolling past it cold; it has two lines to earn their attention before the "more" button. This prompt generates both formats from a single set of inputs, so you spend 3 minutes instead of 30.

Prompt 4: Just Listed / Just Sold Social Post

Just listed and just sold announcements are the highest-engagement content type in real estate social media. They are simultaneously proof of production, social proof of results, and free advertising for the agent's market knowledge. But most agents produce identical posts — a photo, the address, and "Just Listed!" or "Just Sold!" — that generate polite engagement from family members and no actual leads. This prompt generates announcement posts that use the listing or sale as a storytelling vehicle, turning each transaction into a showcase of the agent's expertise and market insight.

Prompt RE-04: Just Listed / Just Sold Social Post
Act as a real estate social media strategist. Write a high-engagement Instagram/Facebook post announcing either a just listed or just sold property. The post should do three things: generate engagement from potential clients, demonstrate the agent's expertise, and subtly invite people to work with the agent.

Structure for Just Listed post:
— Hook (first line): The single most compelling thing about this property. Not "Just Listed!" — lead with what makes it special.
— Property story (2–3 sentences): Paint a picture of living in this home. Include one detail that photographs cannot capture.
— Key stats (1 line): Beds, baths, sq ft, price — formatted concisely
— Soft CTA: Invite comments, DMs, or attendance at open house
— Hashtags (10–15)

Structure for Just Sold post:
— Hook (first line): The result (above asking, multiple offers, sold in X days) — lead with the win
— Brief story: What made this property sell fast / for the price it did. What the agent did to achieve the result.
— Social proof CTA: Invite buyers or sellers in the same neighborhood/price range to reach out
— Hashtags (10–15)

Variables:
— Post Type: [JUST LISTED or JUST SOLD]
— Property Quick Stats: [BEDS, BATHS, SQ FT, PRICE / SALE PRICE]
— Neighborhood: [NEIGHBORHOOD NAME]
— Key Property Feature: [ONE MEMORABLE DETAIL]
— Result (for Just Sold): [E.g., "$45K above asking, 11 offers, sold in 4 days"]
— Agent Name: [AGENT NAME]
— Tone: [E.g., "confident and celebratory," "warm and storytelling," "market-expert and data-driven"]

Example Just Sold Output:

11 offers. $52,000 above asking. Closed in 21 days.

This Decatur ranch hit the market last Tuesday and never sat still. We priced it to create competition, launched with professional photography and a twilight shoot on Friday, and by Sunday evening had 11 offers on the table. The sellers accepted a clean cash offer at $52K over list price with a 21-day close.

This is what happens when pricing strategy, timing, and marketing align. If you are thinking about selling in the Decatur/Avondale corridor in the next 90 days, I would love to show you what this market is doing for sellers right now. DM me or drop a comment below.

#DecaturRealEstate #JustSold #AtlantaRealEstate #DecaturHomes #SoldAboveAsking #AtlantaAgent #RealEstateSold #HomesSold #AtlantaHomeSales #SellerAgent

The just sold post above works because it leads with the result (not "Excited to announce..."), tells the story of how the result happened (pricing strategy, photography, timing), and converts the announcement into a direct seller lead invitation. Every transaction is an opportunity to attract the next client — if the post is framed correctly.

Prompt 5: Neighborhood Guide Blog Post

Neighborhood guide blog posts are the single highest-value SEO content type for real estate agents. When a buyer relocates to a new city or explores a new neighborhood, they search for "[neighborhood name] neighborhood guide" or "what is it like to live in [neighborhood]" — and the agent whose content answers that question becomes the trusted expert before the buyer ever makes a call. This prompt generates a comprehensive neighborhood guide optimized for organic search, structured to rank for hyperlocal queries and convert readers into buyer consultations.

Prompt RE-05: Neighborhood Guide Blog Post
Act as a local real estate expert and content strategist. Write a comprehensive neighborhood guide blog post that establishes an agent as the go-to authority for this area. The guide should rank for "[neighborhood] neighborhood guide" and related searches while genuinely informing buyers who are researching the area.

Structure (800–1,200 words):
— Introduction (100 words): Who lives here and why they love it. Lead with the neighborhood's defining character, not its geography.
— Housing Overview (150 words): Types of homes, typical price ranges, what buyers get for their budget. Include specific examples.
— Lifestyle and Vibe (150 words): Coffee shops, restaurants, nightlife, parks, fitness — specific names and what makes them worth mentioning
— Schools (100 words): Public schools (with ratings if known), notable private options, proximity to major universities if relevant
— Transportation and Commute (100 words): Walkability, bike-friendliness, transit options, typical commute times to downtown and major employment centers
— Market Snapshot (150 words): Current median price, year-over-year appreciation, average days on market, competition level. Frame as agent expertise, not a data dump.
— Who Moves Here (100 words): Buyer personas — who is buying in this neighborhood and why
— Agent CTA (100 words): Natural invitation to connect, without hard-sell language

Variables:
— Neighborhood Name: [NEIGHBORHOOD]
— City: [CITY]
— Notable Spots (5+): [SPECIFIC RESTAURANTS, PARKS, SHOPS, LANDMARKS]
— Housing Price Range: [LOW – HIGH END PRICES]
— School Names: [PUBLIC ELEMENTARY / MIDDLE / HIGH SCHOOL NAMES]
— Market Data: [MEDIAN PRICE, YOY CHANGE, AVG DAYS ON MARKET]
— Agent Name and Specialty: [AGENT NAME, HOW LONG THEY'VE WORKED THIS AREA]

The neighborhood guide is a long-term lead generation asset. A well-written guide targeting "[neighborhood] real estate" or "living in [neighborhood]" can generate organic buyer leads for years from a single piece of content. The key is specificity: name the coffee shop on the corner, the park where dog owners gather, the restaurant that has a 2-hour wait on Saturday nights. Generic content ranks nowhere and converts no one. Specific content earns trust and ranks locally.

Prompt 6: Real Estate Email Drip Campaign

Most real estate CRMs include a generic email drip sequence that gets sent to every lead regardless of whether they are a first-time buyer, a relocating executive, or a seller preparing to downsize. These sequences have open rates in the low single digits because they are obviously automated and irrelevant to the recipient's actual situation. This prompt generates a targeted 5-email drip sequence for a specific lead type, with each email building on the previous one and moving the lead toward a consultation call.

Prompt RE-06: Real Estate Email Drip Campaign
Act as a real estate marketing strategist specializing in lead nurture email sequences. Write a 5-email drip campaign for a specific real estate lead type. Each email should feel personal, add genuine value, and naturally move the lead toward booking a consultation call — without feeling like a sales sequence.

Email sequence structure:
— Email 1 (Day 1): Warm welcome + immediate value. Deliver the thing they signed up for or searched for. Establish who you are without overselling. No hard CTA — just a soft "reply if you have questions."
— Email 2 (Day 3): Local market insight. Share one specific, surprising piece of market data for their target neighborhood. Frame yourself as the expert who knows things they do not.
— Email 3 (Day 7): Address their #1 fear or objection. For buyers: affordability, competition, timing. For sellers: pricing, agent selection, timing the sale.
— Email 4 (Day 14): Social proof. Share a brief story about a client in a similar situation and how you helped them. First-person narrative, specific details.
— Email 5 (Day 21): Soft CTA to book a call. Offer a specific, low-commitment next step (15-minute market call, free seller consult, buyer strategy session).

Rules: Subject lines must earn the open. Every email under 200 words. No "just checking in" emails. Every email delivers a reason the reader is glad they opened it.

Variables:
— Lead Type: [E.g., "first-time buyer," "relocating buyer," "homeowner considering selling," "expired listing owner"]
— Market Area: [CITY / NEIGHBORHOOD]
— Agent Name: [AGENT NAME]
— Agent Specialty: [NICHE — e.g., "buyer's agent," "listing specialist," "luxury market," "investment properties"]
— Key Market Insight: [ONE SPECIFIC LOCAL DATA POINT — e.g., "inventory is down 34% YOY," "median DOM is 8 days," "63% of homes selling above list"]
— Social Proof Story: [BRIEF CLIENT STORY SUMMARY — e.g., "first-time buyer won in competitive offer after two failed offers with previous agent"]
— Booking Link: [CALENDLY OR BOOKING URL]

The "no checking in" rule is the most important constraint in this prompt. "Just wanted to check in and see where you are in your search" is the lowest-value email in existence. It signals that the agent has nothing to offer and is reaching out purely for their own pipeline management. Every email in this sequence delivers information the lead can use — market data, objection handling, social proof, or a specific next step with a clear benefit. That is why leads stay subscribed and eventually convert.

Prompt 7: Real Estate Facebook Ad

Facebook and Instagram ads are the primary paid channel for real estate lead generation at the top and middle of the funnel. They reach buyers and sellers before they are actively searching, building awareness and desire before the Google search happens. Effective real estate Facebook ads target a specific audience segment — "homeowners 45–65 in zip code 30307 who match the profile of a seller" or "renters 28–40 earning $80K+ who are likely to buy in the next 12 months" — and serve copy that speaks directly to that segment's specific situation. This prompt generates Facebook ad copy for two real estate use cases: seller lead generation and buyer lead generation.

Prompt RE-07: Real Estate Facebook Ad
Act as a real estate Facebook ads specialist. Write Facebook/Instagram ad copy for a real estate lead generation campaign. Write two complete ad variations: one for seller lead generation and one for buyer lead generation.

For each variation provide:
— Primary text (150–200 words): Starts with a pattern-interrupt hook for the feed, not a generic opener. Uses PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) or BAB (Before-After-Bridge) framework.
— Headline (5 options, 40 characters max each): Directly addresses the audience's specific situation
— Description (2 options, 25 characters max each): Supports the headline
— CTA button recommendation: Choose from Facebook's options (Learn More, Sign Up, Get Quote, Contact Us, Book Now)

Audience targeting notes (include as a note below each ad): Who to target this ad to in Meta Ads Manager (age, interests, behaviors, zip codes)

Variables:
— Agent Name and Brokerage: [AGENT / BROKERAGE NAME]
— Market Area: [CITY + KEY ZIP CODES OR NEIGHBORHOODS]
— Seller Offer: [LEAD MAGNET — e.g., "free home valuation," "seller's guide," "market report"]
— Buyer Offer: [LEAD MAGNET — e.g., "free buyer's guide," "list of off-market homes," "first-time buyer workshop"]
— Key Market Stat: [A COMPELLING LOCAL MARKET DATA POINT]
— Agent Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES THIS AGENT DIFFERENT — e.g., "average sale price 4.2% above market," "former appraiser," "10 years exclusively in this zip code"]

The targeting note section is as important as the ad copy itself. A brilliant Facebook ad shown to the wrong audience generates zero leads. For seller lead generation, the most effective Meta audiences are homeowners (property ownership behavior) in specific zip codes, age 35–65, combined with life event signals like "likely to move." For buyer lead generation, target renters in adjacent zip codes, age 25–45, with income indicators that suggest purchasing power. This prompt includes audience guidance built into the output so you have everything you need to launch the campaign.

Prompt 8: Real Estate Google Ad

Google Ads captures real estate intent at its highest peak: someone actively searching "homes for sale in [neighborhood]," "sell my house [city]," or "real estate agent near me." Unlike Facebook ads that interrupt, Google ads answer. The searcher has a need; your ad is the answer. But most real estate Google ads are generic — "Find Your Dream Home | Expert Local Agent | Call Today" — and compete on price rather than differentiation. This prompt generates Google search ad copy that differentiates the agent and matches the specific intent of high-value real estate searches.

Prompt RE-08: Real Estate Google Ad
Act as a Google Ads specialist with expertise in real estate campaigns. Write a complete responsive search ad (RSA) for a real estate agent or team. Provide 10 headlines and 4 descriptions, each within Google's character limits.

Headline requirements (30 characters max each):
— Headlines 1–3: Include the target keyword or service + location
— Headlines 4–6: Agent differentiator (what competitors do not offer)
— Headlines 7–8: Social proof (reviews, homes sold, years in market)
— Headlines 9–10: CTA (call, book, search, start)

Description requirements (90 characters max each):
— Description 1: Lead benefit for the buyer or seller (what they get)
— Description 2: Trust / proof signal
— Description 3: Specific offer or free resource
— Description 4: Urgency + CTA

Also provide 2 sitelink extensions (title 25 chars, 2 description lines 35 chars each) for the agent's most valuable lead generation pages.

Variables:
— Agent / Team Name: [NAME]
— Campaign Type: [BUYER LEADS, SELLER LEADS, or BOTH]
— Target Keyword: [E.g., "real estate agent austin," "sell my home dallas," "homes for sale inman park"]
— Location: [CITY / NEIGHBORHOOD]
— Agent Differentiator: [UNIQUE ADVANTAGE]
— Social Proof: [HOMES SOLD, YEARS EXPERIENCE, REVIEW RATING]
— Free Offer: [FREE VALUATION, BUYER CONSULT, MARKET REPORT]

For real estate Google Ads, the differentiator headlines are the ones that do the most work. "Real Estate Agent Atlanta" describes what you do. "Avg. 4.1% Above List Price" and "Sold 200+ Homes in Decatur" describe what you deliver. High-intent real estate searchers are evaluating multiple agents simultaneously; the agent whose ad copy signals a specific, provable advantage wins the click. Fill the differentiator variable with your most concrete, verifiable claim — not a soft attribute like "local expertise."

Prompt 9: Agent Bio / About Page

The agent bio is the most-read page on any real estate website and the most commonly written poorly. Most agent bios read like a corporate resume: years licensed, designations earned, awards received, areas served. They communicate credentials but not character — and character is what clients are buying when they choose an agent. This prompt generates an agent bio that leads with story, communicates values and work ethic through specific anecdotes, and positions the agent's experience as directly relevant to the client's goal.

Prompt RE-09: Agent Bio / About Page
Act as a real estate brand copywriter who specializes in agent positioning and bio pages. Write a compelling agent bio for the real estate professional below. The bio should make potential clients feel they already know and trust this agent before they have spoken a word.

Structure (400–500 words):
— Opening story hook (75 words): Begin with a moment, not a credential. Start with a scene from the agent's work or life that reveals their character and why they do what they do. No "I have been in real estate for X years" openings.
— What they do and who they serve (75 words): Clear, specific positioning. What type of clients, what market area, what price range, what specialty. Speak directly to the target client.
— Approach and differentiator (100 words): How they work differently from other agents. Specific behaviors and practices — not "I go above and beyond" but the specific things they actually do differently.
— Results and social proof (75 words): Concrete numbers. Homes sold, average sale-to-list ratio, client satisfaction stats, awards — but framed as proof of client outcomes, not agent ego.
— Personal (75 words): Life outside real estate — family, neighborhood, interests — that makes the agent feel like a real person. This is where clients find the "like me" signal that makes them reach out.
— CTA (50 words): A specific, low-barrier invitation to connect — not "contact me" but a specific next step with a clear benefit.

Write in third person for website use AND first person for email/social use. Provide both versions.

Variables:
— Agent Name: [NAME]
— Years Licensed: [YEARS]
— Market Specialty: [AREA + PROPERTY TYPE SPECIALTY]
— Client Type Focus: [E.g., "first-time buyers," "luxury sellers," "investor clients," "relocating families"]
— Key Results: [HOMES SOLD, SALE/LIST RATIO, NOTABLE ACHIEVEMENT]
— Approach Differentiator: [SPECIFIC THING YOU DO DIFFERENTLY — e.g., "pre-listing home staging consult at no cost," "weekly written market reports to every client," "available 7 days a week during active transactions"]
— Personal Details: [NEIGHBORHOOD YOU LIVE IN, FAMILY, INTERESTS]
— Origin Story: [WHY YOU GOT INTO REAL ESTATE — a specific moment or experience]

The "opening story hook" instruction is the most important element of this prompt. Starting an agent bio with a specific scene — the moment you handed keys to a first-time buyer who had been searching for 18 months, the client who called you at midnight in a panic during a contingency deadline — does more to build trust in 75 words than two paragraphs of credentials. Clients are not hiring a resume; they are hiring a person they will trust with the largest financial decision of their life.

Prompt 10: Real Estate Video Script (Property Tour)

Video property tours have become a standard expectation for buyers, and the agents who do them well — with a clear script, strategic flow, and compelling narration — generate more showing requests and higher engagement than those who walk through a property saying "and here is the kitchen." This prompt generates a complete video tour script that guides the camera flow, provides specific narration for each room, and ends with a CTA that converts viewers into showing requests. It works for both agent-hosted tours and voiceover-only video walkthroughs.

Prompt RE-10: Real Estate Video Script (Property Tour)
Act as a real estate video production specialist and scriptwriter. Write a complete property tour video script for the listing below. The script should guide the filming sequence, provide natural narration for each space, and convert viewers into showing requests.

Script format:
— [CAMERA DIRECTION]: Brief note on what to show and how (wide shot, close-up of feature, pan right, etc.)
— [NARRATION]: What the agent says or the voiceover text

Structure (3–4 minutes of finished video):
— Opening exterior / curb appeal (30 seconds): Hook the viewer with the best exterior shot. Narration leads with lifestyle or neighborhood context — not address or price.
— Main living areas (60 seconds): Flow naturally through living room, dining, kitchen. Each room gets 1–2 sentences of narration focused on what it feels like to live there, not just what it looks like.
— Primary bedroom suite (30 seconds): Narrate the details that photographs do not communicate (morning light, quiet location, closet size, bathroom flow)
— Secondary bedrooms / flex spaces (20 seconds): Quick walk-through with one specific detail per room
— Outdoor spaces (30 seconds): Backyard, patio, pool, or garage — whatever the standout outdoor feature is
— Neighborhood context (20 seconds): Film from the street or a nearby landmark. Name-drop 2–3 neighborhood amenities.
— Closing CTA (30 seconds): Agent on camera. Specific invitation to book a showing, with contact info.

Variables:
— Property Address and Type: [ADDRESS + PROPERTY TYPE]
— Key Features to Highlight: [TOP 3–5 FEATURES IN PRIORITY ORDER]
— Neighborhood Amenities: [WALKABLE DESTINATIONS, PARKS, RESTAURANTS]
— Target Buyer: [WHO IS THIS VIDEO FOR — e.g., "young family," "luxury buyer," "work-from-home professional"]
— Agent Name and Contact: [NAME, PHONE, WEBSITE]
— Tone: [E.g., "warm and conversational," "polished and aspirational," "upbeat and energetic"]

The camera direction instructions in this prompt are what make it genuinely useful versus a generic script. Real estate video fails most often not because of bad narration but because of poor filming decisions — too much time on the hallway, not enough on the primary suite, no close-ups of the hardware and fixtures that justify the price point. The script format with alternating camera direction and narration gives whoever is filming a clear shot list and the agent a clear path through the property.

Prompt 11: Market Update Newsletter

A monthly market update newsletter is the highest-retention content type in real estate email marketing. When done well, it positions the agent as an indispensable local market analyst — someone whose insights are worth reading regardless of whether the subscriber is actively buying or selling. Most market update emails are data dumps: a table of stats with no interpretation and no story. This prompt generates a market update that tells the story of what the data means, frames it for the reader's situation, and naturally positions the agent as the person to call when they are ready to act.

Prompt RE-11: Market Update Newsletter
Act as a real estate market analyst and newsletter strategist. Write a monthly market update email that positions the agent as a local market expert and keeps their database engaged, regardless of where each subscriber is in their buying or selling timeline.

Structure (350–450 words):
— Subject line (5 options): Curiosity + local specificity. Avoid "March Market Update" as the entire subject line. Include a data point or tension in the subject.
— Opening hook (50 words): One surprising or counter-intuitive thing the market is doing. Do not start with "Happy [month]!"
— Market snapshot (150 words): 4–5 key metrics with plain-English interpretation of what each means for buyers and sellers. What does this data mean for someone's decision right now?
— What to watch (75 words): One forward-looking trend or signal the agent is watching. This is what makes the newsletter feel like insider knowledge, not a public data report.
— Agent take (75 words): First person. What is the agent advising clients to do given this data? Makes the newsletter feel human, not automated.
— Soft CTA (50 words): Natural invitation to reply, book a call, or forward to someone who is thinking about the market

Variables:
— Market Area: [CITY / NEIGHBORHOODS COVERED]
— Month / Year: [MONTH, YEAR]
— Key Metrics: [MEDIAN PRICE, YOY CHANGE, ACTIVE INVENTORY, AVG DAYS ON MARKET, SALE-TO-LIST RATIO, MONTHS OF SUPPLY]
— Surprising Data Point: [THE MOST COUNTER-INTUITIVE THING IN THIS MONTH'S DATA]
— Forward-Looking Signal: [TREND YOU ARE WATCHING — e.g., "new listings up 18% in January, first time in 6 months," "rate locks increasing among jumbo buyers"]
— Agent Name: [NAME]
— Agent Advice for This Market: [WHAT YOU ARE TELLING YOUR CLIENTS RIGHT NOW]

The "Agent Take" section is the element most agents skip because they are uncertain about expressing a market opinion in writing. Do not skip it. It is the section that transforms a data report into a newsletter worth reading. Your subscribers can get market statistics from Zillow. They cannot get your specific interpretation, your advice for this specific market, and your read on what comes next. That interpretation is your competitive advantage in print — use it.

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts in Real Estate Marketing

The 12 prompts above give you the tools. These five practices ensure you use them to produce content that actually wins listings, attracts buyers, and builds your brand in your market.

1. Fill Every Variable With Maximum Specificity

The most common mistake when using AI prompts is providing vague inputs. "Nice kitchen" produces generic output. "Chef's kitchen with 10-foot quartz waterfall island, 48-inch Wolf dual-fuel range, and a Thermador column refrigerator" produces output that stops buyers mid-scroll. The AI cannot invent specificity you do not give it. For every property, spend 5 minutes before running the prompt writing down the three most memorable, concrete details — the things a visitor would tell their spouse about later. Those are your variable inputs.

2. Specify the Buyer Persona in Every Listing Prompt

Every property has a most-likely buyer. A 4-bedroom home near the top-rated elementary school sells primarily to families with school-age children. A downtown loft with no parking sells primarily to urban professionals who walk or use transit. A golf community home sells primarily to retirees or semi-retired couples. When you tell the AI which persona to write for, the output shifts from describing the home to describing the life that home enables for that specific person. That shift is the difference between copy that gets saved and copy that gets ignored.

3. Regenerate Listing Descriptions After Price Reductions

When a listing has been on the market for 30+ days and requires a price reduction, the existing description has already failed to convert browsers into buyers. Run the listing description prompt again with new variables: a different hook, different featured details, and a different buyer persona angle. Fresh copy signals a refreshed listing to buyers who saw it before and passed. Pair the new description with new photography angles (twilight shots, detail shots, staging adjustments) and the price reduction becomes a full relaunch, not just a number change on Zillow.

4. Build a Prompt Library for Your Market

After running these prompts a few times, you will discover which variable inputs produce the best output for your specific market and property types. Save those successful prompt versions — with your most effective variable examples — in a shared document or Notion database. Over time, this becomes your personal real estate marketing prompt library, tuned to your brand voice, your market area, and your client types. New team members can onboard to your marketing standards in a single session instead of six months of training.

5. Use the Content Calendar Prompt to Plan Quarterly, Not Monthly

The content calendar prompt generates a month of content in a single session. Run it at the beginning of each month, or more efficiently, run it for 3 months at a time at the start of each quarter. Quarterly planning lets you align content with seasonal market trends (spring selling season, summer slowdown, fall inventory surge), local events, and your own listing pipeline. With three months planned in advance, daily posting becomes a 5-minute task — pulling from the calendar and customizing the hook for the day's specific news or listing update.
