15 AI Prompts for Taglines and Slogans That Stick

ContentKit AI•14 min read

AI prompts for taglines and slogans are structured instructions that generate memorable brand phrases, campaign lines, and product taglines — using proven rhetorical frameworks like rhyme, alliteration, contrast, and emotional resonance — in seconds instead of weeks. This guide delivers 15 copy-paste prompts covering every tagline type, from timeless brand lines and time-bound campaign slogans to minimalist three-word phrases and A/B-testable variations, each built on the creative techniques behind the world's most remembered marketing copy.

A great tagline is one of the highest-leverage assets in all of marketing. "Just Do It." "Think Different." "Because You're Worth It." These four-to-five-word phrases have generated billions of dollars in brand equity. They appear on every ad, every product, every piece of packaging. They live in culture for decades. And they are extraordinarily difficult to write — which is why most brands never have one, most campaigns settle for a forgettable line, and most product launches go to market without a phrase worth remembering.

The traditional process for developing a tagline involves a creative brief, a brainstorming session, weeks of agency rounds, and a final selection from a shortlist that often feels like choosing the least bad option. That process is expensive (ad agencies charge $5,000 to $50,000 for tagline development), slow (four to eight weeks minimum), and often produces generic output because everyone in the room is anchored to the same obvious ideas.

Structured ai prompts for taglines and slogans break that model. Instead of a single brainstorm with ten people in a room, you run fifteen prompts, each targeting a different rhetorical angle — rhyme, alliteration, emotion, wit, minimalism, benefit-focus — and collect fifty to one hundred candidates in under an hour. The AI is not a replacement for creative judgment. It is a volume machine that exhausts the obvious options fast and surfaces unexpected angles that the room would never have reached before breaking for lunch.

The 15 prompts below cover every tagline and slogan scenario: a permanent brand tagline built to last decades, a campaign slogan engineered for a specific promotion, a product tagline that sells a single SKU, a service tagline that sells an intangible outcome, and specialized generators for rhyme, alliteration, emotion, humor, and ultra-brevity. The final two prompts — A/B test variations and an evaluation framework — help you select and test the best candidate before committing it to your brand.

For prompts that go beyond taglines into the broader territory of brand voice, positioning, and identity, see our guide on AI prompts for brand voice.

What Makes a Tagline Stick

Before using any of these prompts, it helps to understand what separates a tagline that sticks from one that disappears. Researchers who study advertising memory have identified five characteristics that predict tagline retention. The prompts in this article are built around these characteristics.

Brevity

The most remembered taglines in advertising history average three to five words. "Just Do It" (three words). "I'm Lovin' It" (three words). "Got Milk?" (two words). "Think Different" (two words). Brevity is not a constraint — it is a feature. Short taglines are easier to say aloud, easier to put on a billboard, easier to fit on a business card, and significantly more likely to be remembered. Every prompt in this article asks the AI to prioritize concision, with several prompts explicitly capping the output at three words or fewer.

Rhythm and Sound

Taglines that rhyme, alliterate, or have a satisfying cadence are remembered 30–40% better than taglines with the same semantic content but no sonic structure. "Snap, Crackle, Pop." "Have It Your Way." "Betcha Can't Eat Just One." The rhythm makes the phrase feel complete — like it belongs together — and that sense of completeness makes it stick in memory. Prompts 5 and 6 specifically target rhyme and alliteration, and Prompt 9 uses humor and wordplay as a sonic device.

Emotional Truth

The most durable taglines do not describe products. They describe how using the product makes you feel or who you become when you use it. "Because You're Worth It" (L'Oréal) is not about hair dye. It is about self-worth. "The Ultimate Driving Machine" (BMW) is not about horsepower. It is about the driver's identity. Prompt 8 is dedicated entirely to this emotional and aspirational register, and it is often the prompt that produces the most powerful candidates.

Distinctiveness

A tagline that could belong to any brand in your category is worthless. "Quality You Can Trust" could be on a plumbing company, a law firm, or a sock manufacturer. Distinctive taglines are specific to the brand's positioning, personality, and competitive context. The brand tagline prompt (Prompt 1) and the industry-specific prompt (Prompt 11) both explicitly instruct the AI to avoid generic phrasing and generate options tied to the brand's unique differentiator.

Implicit Promise

The best taglines contain a promise without stating it literally. "Just Do It" promises you will overcome hesitation. "Think Different" promises you will see what others miss. "The Few. The Proud. The Marines." promises you will become extraordinary. This implicit promise structure is more powerful than an explicit claim because it invites the reader to complete the thought themselves. When someone completes the thought, they own it — and they remember it. Prompt 7 focuses specifically on benefit-first taglines that carry this implicit promise.

15 AI Prompts for Taglines and Slogans

Prompt 1: Brand Tagline (Timeless)

A brand tagline is the permanent line attached to your company name. It appears under your logo, in your email signature, on your website header, and in every piece of advertising you run for years or decades. It must be versatile enough to work across every context, distinctive enough to own a position in the market, and resonant enough to accumulate meaning over time. This prompt generates brand tagline candidates built for longevity — not campaign-specific, not trendy, not dependent on current events. Just a phrase that could live alongside your brand for the next ten years.

Prompt TS-01: Brand Tagline (Timeless)
Act as a senior brand strategist and award-winning copywriter who specializes in developing brand taglines for global companies. Generate 15 brand tagline candidates for the business below.

Requirements:
— Maximum 6 words per tagline (shorter is better)
— Each tagline must work without seeing the logo, product image, or any visual context
— Avoid generic language: no "quality," "excellence," "trusted," "innovative," or "solutions"
— Each tagline must be distinctive to this brand's specific positioning — not transferable to competitors
— Vary the approach across the 15 options: include lines that are aspirational, functional, emotional, bold, quiet, witty, and contrarian
— For each tagline, provide a one-sentence rationale explaining the strategic idea behind it

Evaluation criteria (note these for your own assessment, do not output as headers):
— Could this phrase belong to any other brand in this category? (If yes, reject it)
— Does it contain a promise, even implicitly?
— Is it sayable in one breath?
— Would it still make sense in 10 years?

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry / Category: [INDUSTRY — e.g., "sustainable footwear," "B2B project management software," "craft cold brew coffee"]
— Target Customer: [WHO BUYS FROM YOU — e.g., "design-conscious professionals aged 28-40," "operations leaders at mid-market companies"]
— Core Positioning: [YOUR UNIQUE MARKET POSITION — e.g., "the only CRM built for solo consultants," "coffee roasted within 48 hours of shipping"]
— Brand Personality: [3 ADJECTIVES — e.g., "bold, irreverent, honest" or "calm, precise, expert"]
— Competitor Brands: [2-3 MAIN COMPETITORS — so the tagline does not accidentally sound like theirs]

Example output for a B2B project management tool targeting solo consultants:

1. Your pipeline, your pace. — Positions freedom and autonomy as the core benefit for independent workers.
2. One person. Full stack. — Celebrates the solo operator's range without making it feel overwhelming.
3. Built for one. Scaled for when. — Implies growth readiness without overselling enterprise features.
4. Stop managing. Start closing. — Uses contrast to capture the CRM's role in shifting focus to revenue.
5. The CRM that follows your lead. — Personification that communicates adaptability and service.

Notice how each candidate takes a genuinely different angle on the same brand. This variety is the point — the goal is to identify which strategic territory resonates before you commit to any single direction. Never evaluate taglines in isolation. Compare the full list and ask which direction the brand should own.

Prompt 2: Campaign Slogan (Time-Bound)

A campaign slogan is different from a brand tagline in one critical way: it has an expiration date. Campaign slogans support a specific promotion, season, product launch, or marketing push. They can be more specific, more topical, and more aggressive than a brand tagline because they will be retired when the campaign ends. This prompt generates campaign slogans engineered for a defined goal — driving purchases during a sale, registrations for an event, downloads of a new product, or attendance at a launch.

Prompt TS-02: Campaign Slogan (Time-Bound)
Act as a direct-response copywriter and campaign strategist. Generate 12 campaign slogans for the marketing campaign described below. Campaign slogans are time-bound and campaign-specific — they can be more urgent, specific, and promotional than a permanent brand tagline.

Requirements:
— Each slogan must support the specific campaign goal (not be a generic brand line)
— Include urgency or specificity where appropriate (dates, quantities, limited offers)
— Maximum 8 words per slogan
— Generate three tonal variations: (A) bold and direct, (B) emotional and aspirational, (C) witty and light
— Each slogan should work as a headline for an ad, an email subject line, and a social post
— For each slogan, note which channel it is strongest for (paid social, email, outdoor, event signage)

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Campaign Name / Theme: [CAMPAIGN NAME — e.g., "Summer Sale," "Product Launch," "Annual Conference," "Back to School"]
— Campaign Goal: [WHAT YOU WANT PEOPLE TO DO — e.g., "purchase during the sale," "register for the event," "download the new app"]
— Campaign Dates: [START AND END DATE]
— Target Audience: [WHO THE CAMPAIGN TARGETS]
— Key Offer or Hook: [THE COMPELLING REASON TO ACT — e.g., "40% off everything," "early access before the public," "founders pricing for 48 hours"]
— Brand Tone: [BRAND PERSONALITY — e.g., "professional but warm," "edgy and irreverent," "friendly and approachable"]

The three-tonal-variation structure is the most useful part of this prompt for campaign planning. Running all three tones in parallel lets you match the slogan to different audiences and placements — the bold version for paid social, the emotional version for email, the witty version for social media organic — without creating three entirely separate campaigns.

Prompt 3: Product Tagline

A product tagline is separate from your brand tagline — it attaches to a specific SKU, product line, or offering within your broader brand portfolio. Product taglines appear on packaging, product pages, and product-specific advertising. They must communicate the product's specific benefit or personality without contradicting the parent brand's positioning. This prompt generates product taglines that stand alone while remaining consistent with the brand's identity.

Prompt TS-03: Product Tagline
Act as a product marketing copywriter who specializes in packaging copy and product launches. Generate 15 product tagline candidates for the product described below. The tagline will appear on the product packaging, product page hero section, and product-specific advertising.

Requirements:
— Maximum 7 words
— Must communicate the product's primary benefit or differentiator, not just describe what it is
— Must feel consistent with the parent brand's personality without copying the brand tagline
— Vary the approach: include lines that lead with the outcome, the feeling, the speed, the simplicity, and the transformation
— Avoid feature-listing — sell the benefit of the feature, not the feature itself
— For each tagline, note which product page section it is strongest for: hero headline, under the product name, or as a packaging callout

Variables:
— Product Name: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Parent Brand: [BRAND NAME]
— Product Category: [WHAT TYPE OF PRODUCT — e.g., "single-origin espresso blend," "project tracking software," "wireless noise-cancelling headphones"]
— Primary Benefit: [THE #1 THING THE PRODUCT DOES FOR THE USER — e.g., "cuts meeting time in half," "makes focus effortless," "brews in under 60 seconds"]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES THIS PRODUCT DIFFERENT FROM DIRECT COMPETITORS]
— Target User: [WHO THIS PRODUCT IS FOR]
— Brand Personality: [3 ADJECTIVES DESCRIBING BRAND TONE]

The most common mistake in product tagline writing is describing the product instead of selling it. "A wireless noise-cancelling headphone" is a description. "Silence on demand" is a tagline. "Focus, whenever you need it" is a tagline. The prompt explicitly instructs the AI to lead with the benefit or transformation — not the feature. That single constraint produces dramatically better output.

Prompt 4: Service Tagline

Service businesses face a unique tagline challenge: you cannot photograph your product. You are selling an experience, an outcome, a transformation — all of which are intangible. The most effective service taglines sell the result the client achieves, not the process the business uses to get them there. "We help you grow" is a process description. "Grow faster than your competition" is a result. This prompt is built specifically for service businesses whose tagline must make an invisible offering feel real and desirable.

Prompt TS-04: Service Tagline
Act as a brand strategist specializing in professional services and service-based businesses. Generate 15 tagline candidates for the service business described below. Service taglines must sell an outcome or transformation, not a process or method — because the service itself is invisible.

Requirements:
— Maximum 7 words
— Lead with the client's result, not the service provider's method
— Avoid professional services clichés: no "partnership," "solutions," "full-service," "comprehensive," "tailored," or "trusted"
— Each tagline should pass the "so what?" test — a potential client reading it should immediately understand why it matters to them
— Include at least 3 taglines that speak to a specific pain point the client has before hiring this service
— For each tagline, note the emotional state it addresses (relief, ambition, confidence, urgency, pride)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Service Type: [WHAT YOU DO — e.g., "financial planning for freelancers," "recruitment for tech startups," "brand identity design for emerging CPG brands"]
— Ideal Client: [WHO YOUR BEST CLIENT IS — be specific, e.g., "Series A founders who raised money but have no HR structure"]
— Primary Outcome: [THE #1 RESULT A CLIENT GETS — e.g., "a fully staffed engineering team in 60 days," "a brand identity that commands premium pricing"]
— Client Pain Point Before Hiring You: [WHAT THEY STRUGGLE WITH BEFORE YOU — e.g., "spending 30% of their time on recruiting instead of building," "looking like a startup when they want to be taken seriously"]
— Your Differentiator: [WHAT YOU DO THAT OTHERS DO NOT]

Prompt 5: Rhyming Tagline Generator

Rhyme is the oldest memory device in human language, and it remains one of the most reliable tools in tagline writing. "Snap, Crackle, Pop." "Betcha Can't Eat Just One." "Every Kiss Begins with Kay." Rhyming taglines are remembered more reliably, repeated more often, and shared more naturally than their non-rhyming equivalents. But forced rhyme — where the rhyme is awkward or obvious — makes a brand seem cheap. This prompt generates rhyming taglines that feel earned, not forced, by instructing the AI to generate only rhymes that serve the meaning rather than distort it.

Prompt TS-05: Rhyming Tagline Generator
Act as a copywriter who specializes in sonic branding and mnemonic advertising phrases. Generate 15 rhyming tagline candidates for the brand or product below. Rhyme is a powerful memory device when it feels natural — and a brand killer when it feels forced.

Rhyme types to explore across your 15 options:
— End rhyme: "You win more when you [brand]"
— Internal rhyme: "Better days, better ways"
— Near rhyme / slant rhyme: words that sound similar but do not perfectly rhyme
— Repetition as rhythm: "More [X], more [Y]"
— Rule of three with rhyme: three-beat phrases like "Snap, Crackle, Pop"

Discard any rhyme where:
— The rhyme word was chosen at the expense of meaning
— The phrase sounds forced or sing-song
— A 7-year-old would roll their eyes at it

For each tagline, note the rhyme type used and give it a memorability score from 1–10.

Variables:
— Brand / Product Name: [BRAND OR PRODUCT NAME]
— Core Benefit: [PRIMARY BENEFIT OR PROMISE — e.g., "faster shipping," "clearer skin," "smarter investing"]
— Key Words to Rhyme Around: [WORDS THAT ARE CENTRAL TO THE BRAND — e.g., "grow," "clear," "move," "build," "more"]
— Brand Tone: [TONE — e.g., "playful," "professional," "bold," "warm"]
— Audience: [WHO THIS IS FOR]

Example output for a fitness app called "Forma":

1. Train more, gain more. (end rhyme, 9/10) — Simple, direct, works as both a tagline and an ad headline.
2. Feel the form, find the norm. (internal near rhyme, 7/10) — More abstract; better for a campaign than permanent brand use.
3. Better body, better day. (near rhyme, 8/10) — Conversational and aspirational without sounding clinical.
4. Stronger. Leaner. Every evening. (repetition + rhythm, 8/10) — The rule-of-three cadence makes this highly sayable.
5. You shape it. Forma makes it. (repetition, 7/10) — Plays on the brand name and the user's agency.

Prompt 6: Alliterative Tagline Generator

Alliteration — the repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of adjacent or closely connected words — creates a sonic glue that makes phrases feel like they belong together and stick in memory. "Designed for Doers." "Precision. Passion. Performance." "Bold. Better. Built to Last." Alliterative taglines feel deliberate and crafted, which subtly signals brand quality. This prompt generates alliterative tagline candidates across multiple consonant sounds to find the combination that best matches the brand's personality.

Prompt TS-06: Alliterative Tagline Generator
Act as a linguistic copywriter specializing in brand mnemonics and alliterative branding. Generate 20 alliterative tagline candidates for the brand below. Alliteration should feel intentional and elegant — not like a tongue twister.

Alliteration approaches to explore:
— Full alliteration: every word starts with the same sound ("Crisp. Clear. ContentKit.")
— Partial alliteration: the two most prominent words share a sound ("Precision performance")
— Brand name alliteration: the tagline's first word starts with the same letter as the brand name
— Double alliteration: two pairs of words that each share a sound ("Fast, focused. Smart, scalable.")

Consonant sounds to prioritize (choose based on brand personality):
— Hard sounds (B, D, G, K, P, T): convey strength, confidence, boldness
— Soft sounds (F, L, M, N, S, W): convey warmth, ease, elegance
— Fricative sounds (F, V, Z): convey speed, energy, modernity

For each tagline, note: the alliteration type, the dominant sound, and the brand personality it reinforces.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Brand Personality: [3 ADJECTIVES]
— Core Benefit: [PRIMARY BENEFIT]
— Industry: [INDUSTRY / CATEGORY]
— Sounds to Prioritize: [OPTIONAL — "prefer soft S and L sounds" or "prefer hard B and P sounds"]

The sound-personality mapping in this prompt is the key insight. Brands with authoritative, serious positioning should gravitate toward hard consonants (P, B, T, K) that feel decisive. Brands with warm, approachable positioning should favor soft consonants (S, L, M, N) that feel smooth. If you find yourself choosing between two equally strong alliterative options, let the consonant sound tip the scale in favor of the brand's personality.

Prompt 7: Benefit-Focused Tagline

Benefit-focused taglines work by making the customer's outcome the center of the phrase rather than the brand's process or features. Where a feature tagline says "Clinically proven ingredients," a benefit tagline says "Clearer skin in 30 days." The difference is fundamental: features describe what the product does; benefits describe what the customer gets. This prompt generates benefit-first taglines using the Before-After-Bridge framework, the "so that" technique, and the outcome-first sentence structure that the most effective direct-response taglines share.

Prompt TS-07: Benefit-Focused Tagline
Act as a direct-response copywriter who specializes in benefit-first messaging. Generate 15 benefit-focused tagline candidates for the brand or product below. Every tagline must lead with or imply the customer's outcome — not the brand's method, features, or process.

Benefit-first techniques to use across your 15 options:
— Outcome statement: "[Result] in [timeframe]" — "Debt-free in 18 months"
— Before-After contrast: "[Old way] is over. [New result]." — "No more spreadsheets. Just results."
— Second-person transformation: "You [achieve outcome]" — "You close more deals"
— Implicit promise: phrases where the benefit is understood without being stated literally
— Superlative result: the most extreme, specific, or dramatic version of the benefit

Test each tagline against this filter: Would a customer's eyes light up if they read this? If yes, keep it. If they would shrug, cut it.

Variables:
— Brand / Product Name: [BRAND OR PRODUCT NAME]
— Primary Benefit: [THE #1 RESULT THE CUSTOMER GETS]
— Secondary Benefit: [SUPPORTING BENEFIT]
— Specific Outcome (if measurable): [QUANTIFIABLE RESULT — e.g., "saves 5 hours per week," "reduces churn by 30%," "ships 2x faster"]
— Target Customer: [WHO THIS IS FOR]
— Their Biggest Pain Before Using You: [THE FRUSTRATION OR PROBLEM THEY HAVE NOW]

Prompt 8: Emotional / Aspirational Tagline

The most powerful brand taglines in history do not describe products. They describe identity. "Because You're Worth It" (L'Oréal) tells women they deserve to invest in themselves. "Think Different" (Apple) tells buyers they are not ordinary. "The Few. The Proud. The Marines." tells recruits they can become extraordinary. These taglines work by positioning the brand as a vehicle for a deeper aspiration — the person the customer wants to become, the life they want to live, the statement they want to make. This prompt generates aspirational taglines that sell identity and transformation rather than products and features.

Prompt TS-08: Emotional / Aspirational Tagline
Act as a brand strategist and emotional copywriter. Generate 15 emotionally resonant and aspirational tagline candidates. These taglines should make the customer feel something — not explain something. They sell identity, transformation, and belonging, not products or services.

Emotional territory to explore across your 15 options:
— Identity: who the customer becomes by using this brand
— Belonging: the community or tribe the brand represents
— Aspiration: the life or outcome the customer is working toward
— Liberation: freedom from a constraint, fear, or limitation
— Pride: the feeling of having made a smart, bold, or meaningful choice

Style notes:
— Avoid sentimental clichés: "chase your dreams," "be the best," "live your best life"
— Write with restraint — the most emotionally powerful lines are often the shortest
— The tagline should create a feeling in one read, not require interpretation
— Include at least 3 taglines that would work well spoken aloud in a TV or video ad

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Brand Category: [INDUSTRY / CATEGORY]
— Who the Customer Wants to Become: [IDENTITY ASPIRATION — e.g., "a person who has their finances under control," "someone who looks effortlessly put-together," "a leader people follow willingly"]
— What They Are Leaving Behind: [THE VERSION OF THEMSELVES THEY WANT TO ESCAPE — e.g., "the overwhelmed founder," "the person who always says they'll start Monday"]
— Brand Values: [2-3 CORE VALUES]
— Brand Tone: [EMOTIONAL REGISTER — e.g., "quiet confidence," "bold defiance," "warm encouragement"]

Example output for a personal finance app targeting stressed professionals:

1. Finally, money that works for you. — Liberation from financial anxiety; implies reversal of a frustrating dynamic.
2. Stop worrying. Start living. — Four words that capture the core emotional transformation in one breath.
3. Calm is a financial plan. — Reframes financial planning as an emotional state, not a task.
4. Wealth isn't a number. It's a feeling. — Challenges a common belief and positions emotional security as the real goal.
5. Built for people who think about money too much. — Speaks directly to the audience's self-awareness; highly targeted and empathetic.

Prompt 9: Humorous / Witty Tagline

Humor is one of the most underused tools in brand tagline development. Most marketers avoid it because it is difficult to write well and easy to get wrong — a joke that lands creates instant brand affinity, but a joke that falls flat can make the brand look amateurish. When the product, the audience, and the brand personality align, a witty tagline does something no amount of serious positioning can achieve: it makes people genuinely like the brand. This prompt generates witty and humorous taglines using wordplay, self-awareness, subversion, and absurdism — the four main comedy registers that work in advertising.

Prompt TS-09: Humorous / Witty Tagline
Act as a comedic copywriter who has worked on campaigns for brands known for their wit and irreverence (think: Dollar Shave Club, Oatly, Liquid Death, Cards Against Humanity). Generate 15 humorous or witty tagline candidates for the brand below. The humor must serve the brand — not just be a joke for its own sake.

Humor techniques to explore across your 15 options:
— Wordplay / pun: double meanings, homophones, or words that work two ways
— Self-aware irony: the brand acknowledges something true that competitors would never admit
— Subverted expectation: start a sentence going one direction, end it somewhere surprising
— Absurdism: take the brand's benefit to an absurd extreme
— Anti-marketing voice: copy that sounds like it actively resists being an ad
— Deadpan: say something outrageous in a completely flat, matter-of-fact tone

Rules:
— Each line must still communicate something true about the brand or product
— Avoid humor that punches down, alienates a group, or ages poorly
— The joke should land in one read — no setup required
— Rate each tagline: Funny (would make someone actually laugh), Witty (makes someone smile or nod), Clever (earns a quiet appreciation)

Variables:
— Brand / Product Name: [BRAND OR PRODUCT NAME]
— What You Sell: [CATEGORY / PRODUCT]
— The Boring Truth About Your Category: [THE THING EVERYONE IN YOUR INDUSTRY TAKES TOO SERIOUSLY — e.g., "accounting software is not exciting," "vitamins all taste terrible," "most B2B tools are ugly"]
— Brand Personality: [HOW EDGY CAN THE HUMOR GO? — e.g., "PG, family-friendly," "dry and deadpan," "irreverent but not offensive"]
— Audience: [WHO WILL READ THIS — so the humor lands for them specifically]

The category-truth variable is the most generative field in this prompt. The funniest taglines come from acknowledging something true that everyone in the category knows but no one says out loud. An accounting software that says "Finally, taxes that don't make you want to quit your job" is funnier and more memorable than any earnest value proposition — because it is speaking the customer's actual internal monologue.

Prompt 10: Minimalist Tagline (3 Words or Fewer)

Constraints produce creativity. The requirement to communicate everything in three words or fewer forces a level of precision that longer taglines never demand. "Just Do It." "Think Different." "Got Milk?" "Impossible is Nothing." These are the taglines everyone knows because they have been repeated ten thousand times — and they have been repeated ten thousand times because they are short enough to say, write, and remember effortlessly. This prompt generates ultra-short tagline candidates using the constraint of three words maximum as the creative brief itself.

Prompt TS-10: Minimalist Tagline (3 Words or Fewer)
Act as a minimalist copywriter inspired by the brevity traditions of haiku, aphorism, and the best one-line advertising in history. Generate 20 tagline candidates of 3 words or fewer for the brand below. Every word must earn its place — if a word can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.

Requirements:
— Hard limit: 3 words maximum. Two-word and single-word taglines are acceptable and often superior.
— Every candidate must be a complete thought, not a fragment
— Avoid articles (a, an, the) where possible — they eat word count without adding meaning
— Each tagline must be immediately understandable without context or explanation
— Test each tagline: read it aloud without showing the brand name. Does it conjure a clear feeling or idea? If yes, keep it.

Structural approaches to try:
— Imperative verb: "Build more." / "Go further." / "Stay sharp."
— Noun + verb: "Confidence works." / "Results speak."
— Two-word contrast: "Bold. Precise." / "Fast. Focused."
— Question: "Why not more?" / "Ready yet?"
— Single powerful noun: "Clarity." / "Flow." / "Edge."

For each tagline, note: the word count, the structural type, and which brand placement it is strongest for (logo lockup, product label, billboard, social bio).

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Brand Positioning: [ONE SENTENCE — e.g., "the fastest way for solo consultants to track client projects"]
— Core Feeling: [THE FEELING THE BRAND SHOULD EVOKE — e.g., "confidence," "ease," "momentum," "clarity"]
— Brand Personality: [3 ADJECTIVES]

Prompt 11: Industry-Specific Tagline

Every industry has its own language, cultural references, insider jokes, and shared pain points. A tagline that speaks that specific language signals to the audience that this brand actually understands them — not just their category, but their specific world. A tagline for a B2B SaaS company targeting DevOps engineers can use terminology that would confuse a general audience but signal deep credibility to the exact right buyer. This prompt generates industry-specific taglines that speak the target audience's native language rather than defaulting to generic marketing vocabulary.

Prompt TS-11: Industry-Specific Tagline
Act as a brand strategist with deep expertise in the industry described below. Generate 15 tagline candidates that use industry-specific language, cultural references, and insider understanding to signal credibility and belonging to the target audience. A non-specialist should find these taglines slightly opaque — that is fine and intentional. The specialist should feel immediately seen.

Requirements:
— Use at least 3 taglines that incorporate industry-specific vocabulary or jargon (used correctly and naturally)
— Use at least 3 taglines that reference a shared pain point or inside joke within the industry
— Include taglines that range from highly technical to emotionally resonant within the same industry context
— Maximum 8 words per tagline
— For each tagline, note the insider knowledge it assumes and who would most strongly connect with it

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry: [SPECIFIC INDUSTRY — e.g., "enterprise cybersecurity," "independent restaurant ownership," "independent financial advisory," "mechanical engineering manufacturing"]
— Target Role / Title: [SPECIFIC JOB ROLE — e.g., "CISO," "restaurant owner-operator," "RIA founder," "plant floor supervisor"]
— Industry Pain Point: [THE DOMINANT FRUSTRATION IN THIS ROLE — be specific and insider-level]
— Industry Aspiration: [WHAT THIS ROLE GENUINELY WANTS TO ACHIEVE — again, be specific]

Prompt 14: Tagline A/B Test Variations

Generating tagline candidates is the first half of the work. Testing them is the second. Most brands skip the testing step and choose a tagline based on internal preference — which is how you end up with a tagline that the founder loves and the customer ignores. This prompt generates structured A/B test variations of a single tagline concept, each varying one element at a time so you can run clean tests and understand precisely why one version outperforms another. Use these variations in paid social A/B tests, email subject line tests, or audience polling tools like PickFu.

Prompt TS-14: Tagline A/B Test Variations
Act as a conversion copywriter and A/B testing specialist. Take the tagline concept below and generate structured A/B test variations that each isolate a single variable. The goal is to run clean tests that reveal WHY one version outperforms another — not just which one wins.

For the tagline concept provided, generate variations that test each of these variables independently:
— Voice: second person ("you") vs. first person ("we") vs. third person (no pronoun)
— Length: original vs. 50% shorter vs. 50% longer
— Tone: emotional vs. rational vs. humorous
— Specificity: abstract/aspirational vs. concrete/specific (with numbers or timeframes)
— Verb tense: present ("you get") vs. future ("you will get") vs. imperative ("get")
— Structure: statement vs. question vs. contrast (two opposing ideas)

Output format:
— List each variation with a label: e.g., "Version A: Second Person," "Version B: First Person"
— For each variation, note: the variable being tested, the hypothesis (why this might outperform the original), and the best testing channel (paid social, email subject, audience poll)
— Total: 12–15 test variations

Variables:
— Original Tagline Concept: [THE TAGLINE IDEA OR DRAFT YOU WANT TO TEST — e.g., "Financial clarity for people who hate budgeting"]
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Target Audience: [WHO WILL SEE THE TEST]
— Primary Goal: [WHAT YOU ARE OPTIMIZING FOR — e.g., "click-through rate on paid social," "email open rate," "audience poll memorability score"]

Example A/B variations for "Financial clarity for people who hate budgeting":

Version A (Original): Financial clarity for people who hate budgeting. — Baseline.
Version B (Second Person): Get financial clarity, even if you hate budgeting. — Tests whether direct address increases click-through.
Version C (Shorter): Clarity without the spreadsheets. — Tests whether removing "people who hate budgeting" loses resonance or gains brevity.
Version D (Specific): Clear finances in 15 minutes a month. — Tests whether a specific timeframe beats the emotional "hate budgeting" hook.
Version E (Question): Hate budgeting? You'll love this. — Tests question format + curiosity gap against statement format.
Version F (Aspirational): Money confidence, minus the misery. — Tests whether aspirational language ("confidence") outperforms problem-language ("hate").

Prompt 15: Tagline Evaluation Framework

After running the previous 14 prompts, you may have 50, 100, or even 150 tagline candidates. The final prompt provides a structured evaluation framework to score each candidate objectively across the dimensions that predict long-term tagline success. This is not a subjective "which one feels right" exercise. It is a scored rubric that forces you to evaluate each tagline against the same criteria, then rank the results to identify the top three to five candidates for real-world testing.

Prompt TS-15: Tagline Evaluation Framework
Act as a brand strategy consultant who evaluates tagline candidates for global consumer and B2B brands. Apply the evaluation framework below to the tagline candidates I provide. Score each tagline across all five dimensions, calculate the total score, and rank the candidates from highest to lowest. After ranking, write a one-paragraph recommendation identifying the top three and explaining the strategic rationale for choosing each as a finalist for audience testing.

Evaluation dimensions (score each 1–10):

1. Memorability (1–10): Would someone remember this phrase 24 hours after reading it once? Does it have a sonic quality (rhyme, rhythm, alliteration) or structural hook (surprise, contrast, brevity) that aids recall?

2. Distinctiveness (1–10): Could this tagline appear on a competitor's brand without modification? If yes, score below 5. The tagline should be ownable by exactly one brand in the category.

3. Relevance (1–10): Does this tagline speak directly to something the target audience genuinely cares about — a desire, a pain, an aspiration, or an identity? Score based on specificity of audience fit.

4. Longevity (1–10): Will this tagline still be true and resonant in 5–10 years? Deduct points for trend-dependent language, cultural references that date quickly, or specificity tied to a product feature that may change.

5. Verbal Economy (1–10): Is every word earning its place? Could any word be removed without losing meaning? Score higher for shorter taglines that carry more meaning per word.

Total score: sum of all five dimensions (max: 50)

Variables:
— Tagline Candidates to Evaluate: [PASTE YOUR LIST OF TAGLINE CANDIDATES — numbered, one per line]
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Target Audience: [WHO THE TAGLINE MUST RESONATE WITH]
— Brand Positioning: [ONE SENTENCE ON YOUR MARKET POSITION]
— Primary Use Case: [WHERE THE TAGLINE WILL APPEAR MOST — e.g., "logo lockup," "TV campaign," "social media bio," "product packaging"]

The evaluation framework works best when you paste in the output from multiple earlier prompts. Generate 20 candidates from Prompt 1, 15 from Prompt 7, and 10 from Prompt 8, combine them into a single numbered list, and run them all through Prompt 15. The scoring process often surfaces candidates that seemed unremarkable in isolation but score exceptionally well across all five dimensions — and eliminates candidates that feel intuitively appealing but are actually generic or dated.

Tips for Writing AI Taglines That Actually Stick

The 15 prompts above give you the raw material. These five tips ensure you extract the best possible candidates and use them effectively.

1. Run Every Prompt at Least Twice

AI models produce different output on every run, even with identical inputs. The first pass generates the most obvious options — the phrases everyone would think of. The second and third passes, with no other changes, begin surfacing more unexpected combinations. For high-stakes tagline work — a brand identity, a product launch, a major campaign — run each relevant prompt two or three times, combine all outputs into a single list, and evaluate the full set using Prompt 15. The best tagline is rarely in the first batch.

2. Vary One Variable at a Time

If you are not satisfied with the output from a prompt, do not rewrite the entire prompt. Change one variable and regenerate. Replace a vague target audience description with a hyper-specific one. Swap a generic benefit for a measurable outcome. Change the brand personality from "professional" to "bold and irreverent." Single-variable changes reveal exactly which input drives the most useful output — and teach you how to use these prompts more effectively over time.

3. Never Choose Without an Outside Opinion

Internal teams are the worst judges of taglines. Everyone in the room is too close to the brand, too aware of the internal debates that led to each option, and too likely to choose what sounds smartest to them rather than what resonates with a cold audience. Before committing to any tagline, test your top three to five candidates with five to ten people who represent your target customer. A five-minute PickFu poll or a simple email survey to existing customers is enough to reveal which option your audience actually connects with.

4. Say Every Candidate Aloud

Taglines live in language, and language is heard before it is read. A tagline that reads well on a screen but sounds awkward when spoken fails the most basic test. After generating each batch of candidates, read every one of them aloud — ideally to another person, but at minimum to yourself. Does it feel natural to say? Does the rhythm work spoken as well as written? Would you feel comfortable saying it in an elevator pitch, on a podcast, or in a sales call? If not, it is not the right tagline, regardless of how good it looks on paper.

5. Check for Trademark Conflicts Before Committing

Once you have identified your top one to three candidates, run a trademark search before investing in brand assets, packaging, or advertising. In the United States, search the USPTO TESS database. In the EU, search EUIPO. Globally, consider a trademark attorney search for any tagline you plan to use commercially. A tagline is only an asset if you own it — and a tagline that belongs to another company in your category is a legal and reputational liability, not a brand asset.

FAQ — AI Prompts for Taglines and Slogans

What is the difference between a tagline and a slogan? +
A tagline is a permanent phrase attached to a brand or product — it appears on your logo, website header, and packaging for years or even decades. Think "Just Do It" (Nike) or "Think Different" (Apple). A slogan is campaign-specific and time-bound — it supports a single marketing push, product launch, or seasonal promotion and is replaced when the campaign ends. The prompts in this article cover both: prompts 1, 3, and 4 generate taglines designed to last, while prompt 2 generates slogans engineered for specific campaigns. Understanding the distinction helps you use the right prompt for the right purpose.
How many tagline options should I generate before choosing one? +
Generate at least 20 to 30 options before narrowing down. The first five taglines any AI produces are often the most obvious — the phrases everyone would think of. Genuinely memorable taglines tend to emerge after the obvious options are exhausted. Each prompt in this article asks for 10 to 20 variations. Run the prompt two or three times with slightly different variables, collect 30 to 50 options, then apply the evaluation framework from Prompt 15 to score them objectively before selecting a winner. Testing the top three with your audience before committing is also strongly recommended.
Can AI generate taglines that are legally safe to trademark? +
AI can generate tagline candidates, but trademark clearance requires a legal professional. Before investing in a tagline — putting it on signage, packaging, or advertising — run it through a trademark search (USPTO TESS in the United States, or your country's equivalent) and consult a trademark attorney. Common phrases, purely descriptive taglines ("The Best Coffee"), and geographic terms are generally not trademarkable. Distinctive, coined, or suggestive taglines have the strongest trademark protection. Use AI to generate creative options in volume, then apply trademark diligence to the finalists.
Do these prompts work for service businesses as well as product brands? +
Yes. Every prompt in this article includes variables for both product brands and service businesses. The product tagline prompt (Prompt 3) focuses on a single product's specific benefit, while the service tagline prompt (Prompt 4) emphasizes the transformation or outcome a client experiences. Service businesses often find the emotional and aspirational prompt (Prompt 8) particularly effective because their offering is intangible — the tagline must sell a feeling or result rather than a physical object. Fill in the variables with your service type, target client, and the key outcome you deliver, and the prompts adapt accordingly.
How do I test a tagline before committing to it? +
The most reliable way to test a tagline is to run a simple A/B poll with your target audience. Tools like PickFu, Wynter, or even a Google Form shared with existing customers let you present three to five tagline candidates and ask which one they find most memorable, most believable, and most relevant to what they need. You can also run a short paid social campaign using each tagline as ad copy and measure click-through rate — the tagline that earns the most clicks tends to be the one that resonates. Prompt 14 in this article generates multiple A/B-ready variations of any tagline specifically to support this kind of structured testing.
