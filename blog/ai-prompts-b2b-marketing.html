15 AI Prompts for B2B Marketing That Generate Qualified Leads

ContentKit AI•March 4, 2026•15 min read

AI prompts for B2B marketing are structured instructions that generate LinkedIn posts, cold outreach emails, case studies, white papers, landing pages, ads, and content calendars aligned to the B2B buying process — producing content that attracts, nurtures, and converts qualified business buyers at every stage of the funnel. This guide delivers 15 copy-paste prompts covering the full B2B marketing stack, each built on proven B2B frameworks that accelerate pipeline and shorten sales cycles.

B2B marketing is fundamentally different from B2C. You are not selling to a single impulse buyer scrolling Instagram. You are selling to a buying committee of 6 to 10 people, each with different priorities, different objections, and different roles in the final decision. The CFO cares about ROI and total cost of ownership. The VP of Operations cares about implementation complexity and team disruption. The end user cares about whether it will actually make their daily work easier. One content piece cannot address all of them — and most B2B marketers do not have the resources to create individualized content for each stakeholder.

This is the core problem ai prompts for b2b marketing solve. Instead of writing one generic product overview that speaks to no one specifically, you use targeted prompts to generate role-specific messaging, stage-specific content, and channel-specific copy — all in the time it used to take to write a single blog post. The 15 prompts below cover every major B2B marketing asset: thought leadership for awareness, email sequences for nurture, case studies and one-pagers for conversion, battle cards for sales enablement, and a quarterly content calendar to orchestrate all of it.

Each prompt includes the specific B2B framework, ICP variables, and buyer psychology principles baked in — so the output is ready to review and publish, not a rough draft that requires a complete rewrite. Why B2B Marketing Demands Specialized AI Prompts

B2B buyers are not impulsive. The average enterprise software deal takes 6 to 12 months and involves multiple stakeholders, security reviews, procurement processes, and executive sign-off. Even mid-market deals with shorter cycles require trust, proof, and specificity that generic AI-generated content cannot deliver.

There are three specific challenges that standard AI prompts fail to address in B2B contexts.

Buying Committee Complexity

Every B2B purchase involves multiple decision makers and influencers. The champion who finds your product, the economic buyer who approves the budget, the technical evaluator who reviews your security documentation, and the end users who will actually use the tool every day all need different messages. A LinkedIn post that resonates with a CTO will be ignored by a CFO. A case study framed around time savings will not move a procurement team focused on vendor risk. The prompts in this article include ICP and stakeholder variables that force the AI to write for a specific role in the buying committee — not a generic "business decision maker."

Long Sales Cycles Require Multi-Touch Nurture

In B2C, a great ad can drive a purchase in minutes. In B2B, a great ad drives a download, a download triggers an email sequence, the email sequence drives a demo request, the demo drives a proposal, and the proposal drives a 3-month legal review. Each stage requires different content with different objectives. The 5-part email nurture sequence prompt (Prompt 5) is built specifically for this multi-touch reality, mapping each email to a specific stage of the B2B buying process.

Proof Is Non-Negotiable

B2B buyers are professionally accountable for the purchases they recommend. If they champion a solution that fails, their career suffers. That risk makes B2B buyers far more proof-hungry than B2C consumers. They need case studies, ROI calculators, analyst recognition, customer references, and security certifications before they will recommend a vendor to their executive team. The prompts in this article treat proof as a primary element, not an afterthought — every output includes proof variables (ROI achieved, customer names, measurable outcomes) that transform marketing content into evidence.

The bottom line: ai prompts for b2b marketing only work when they encode the complexity of B2B buying into the prompt structure itself. Generic prompts produce generic content. The 15 prompts below are engineered for B2B specifically. Here they are.

15 AI Prompts for B2B Marketing

Prompt 1: LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post

LinkedIn is the highest-ROI organic channel for B2B marketing. Thought leadership posts from founders, executives, and subject matter experts outperform company page content by 3 to 5x in reach and engagement. But most B2B professionals either do not post consistently or write posts that read like press releases. This prompt generates a LinkedIn thought leadership post that opens with a pattern-disrupting hook, delivers genuine insight in a scannable format, and ends with a subtle CTA that drives profile visits and connection requests from your ICP.

Prompt B2B-01: LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post
Act as a B2B content strategist who writes high-performing LinkedIn thought leadership posts for executives and founders. Write a LinkedIn post that will earn meaningful engagement (comments, shares, DMs) from the target ICP.

Post structure:
— Hook (line 1): A single provocative or counterintuitive sentence that stops the scroll. No questions. No generic openers. Make a bold claim or share a surprising observation.
— Body (lines 2–12): Deliver the insight. Use short paragraphs (1–2 sentences each). Include a numbered list or 3-point framework if applicable. Be specific with data, percentages, or named examples. No filler sentences.
— Resolution (lines 13–15): Tie the insight back to the reader's situation. What does this mean for them specifically?
— CTA (final line): A low-friction invitation to engage — "What's your experience with this?" or "I write about [topic] every week — follow if this was useful." Never ask people to visit a link in the post body.

Rules: No corporate language. No buzzwords ("synergy," "leverage," "disrupt"). Write like a smart colleague explaining something important, not like a marketing team. Maximum 1,300 characters. Each paragraph must earn its place.

Variables:
— Author Name/Title: [NAME AND ROLE — e.g., "Sarah Chen, VP of Revenue at a Series B SaaS company"]
— Target ICP: [IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE — e.g., "VP of Sales at mid-market SaaS companies (50–500 employees)"]
— Core Insight/Topic: [THE MAIN POINT — e.g., "most B2B sales cycles fail at the champion stage, not the economic buyer stage"]
— Supporting Data or Example: [SPECIFIC PROOF — e.g., "we analyzed 200 lost deals and found 67% had an engaged champion who lost internal support"]
— Industry/Context: [INDUSTRY OR MARKET — e.g., "enterprise software," "HR tech," "B2B fintech"]
— Tone: [VOICE — e.g., "direct and data-driven," "conversational and slightly irreverent," "authoritative but approachable"]

Example output (hook + opening):
Most B2B companies lose deals they should win at the champion stage, not the CFO stage.

We analyzed 200 lost deals last quarter. Here's what we found:

67% had an engaged champion who genuinely wanted to buy.
But that champion lost internal support before the deal got to finance.

Why? Because we gave them great sales materials and zero internal selling tools.

We gave them a demo deck. Not a business case.
We gave them a product overview. Not a competitive battle card.
We gave them our pricing sheet. Not an ROI model they could show their CFO.

The pattern that makes this post structure work: the hook makes a claim that contradicts the reader's assumption, the body delivers the proof and framework behind that claim, and the resolution hands the reader a realization they can act on. That combination drives comments, saves, and profile visits from the exact people you want in your pipeline.

Prompt 2: B2B Blog Post (Problem-Solution Framework)

B2B blog content that ranks on Google and converts readers into leads follows a specific structure: it must rank for a problem-aware keyword, deliver enough depth to establish expertise, and include a natural content upgrade or CTA that moves the reader toward a demo or trial. Most B2B blog posts fail because they are written for SEO or for conversion but not for both. This prompt generates a full B2B blog post outline and introduction using the Problem-Solution framework, structured to rank and convert simultaneously.

Prompt B2B-02: B2B Blog Post (Problem-Solution Framework)
Act as a B2B content strategist and SEO writer who creates blog posts that rank for problem-aware keywords and convert readers into leads. Write a complete blog post outline and 400-word introduction for the post below.

Blog post structure:
— Title: Target keyword-rich, 55–65 characters, problem-aware (e.g., "How to [solve problem]" or "[Number] Ways to [achieve outcome]")
— Introduction (400 words): Open with the problem costing real money or time. Quantify it. Acknowledge what the reader has probably already tried. Promise the specific solution this post delivers. Include the target keyword naturally in the first 100 words.
— H2 Sections (outline only): 5–7 sections covering: the root cause of the problem, common failed approaches, the correct framework or solution, implementation steps, proof/case study, and a conclusion with next steps.
— Content upgrade CTA: A natural in-line CTA at the 60% mark offering a related resource (template, checklist, or guide) in exchange for an email.

Rules: Write at the level of expertise the ICP already has. Do not explain basics to experts. Use industry-specific terminology. Include at least two opportunities for internal links to related content. Every H2 must deliver a standalone insight, not just a label.

Variables:
— Target Keyword: [PRIMARY KEYWORD — e.g., "how to reduce customer churn in SaaS," "b2b lead generation strategies for small teams"]
— ICP: [WHO IS READING THIS — role, company size, industry]
— Core Problem: [THE PROBLEM THIS POST SOLVES]
— Solution Framework: [YOUR APPROACH OR METHODOLOGY]
— Proof Point: [CUSTOMER RESULT OR DATA POINT TO INCLUDE]
— Content Upgrade: [RELATED RESOURCE TO OFFER — e.g., "free churn audit template," "B2B lead gen checklist"]
— Internal Link Opportunities: [2 RELATED POSTS OR PAGES ON YOUR SITE]

The Problem-Solution framework works for B2B blogs because it mirrors the B2B buyer's mental state. When your ICP searches for "how to reduce customer churn in SaaS," they already have the problem. They are not looking for an introduction to churn. They want the solution. Posts that skip the lengthy problem setup and get to the actionable framework quickly earn longer read times, more scroll depth, and higher conversion rates on the CTA.

Prompt 3: White Paper Executive Summary

White papers are the highest-authority content asset in B2B marketing. A well-researched white paper with a compelling executive summary generates qualified leads who are actively educating themselves on a solution category — which means they are closer to a purchase decision than blog readers. But most white paper executive summaries are dense, jargon-heavy, and bury the key insight behind paragraphs of context. This prompt generates an executive summary that hooks the reader in the first paragraph and compels them to read the full document.

Prompt B2B-03: White Paper Executive Summary
Act as a B2B content strategist who writes white papers for enterprise technology and professional services companies. Write a 350-word executive summary for the white paper described below. The executive summary must stand alone as a compelling document — a busy executive who only reads this section should still understand the problem, the key findings, and the recommended action.

Executive summary structure:
— Paragraph 1 (Problem Frame, 60–75 words): State the business problem and its cost. Use a specific statistic or industry benchmark. Make the scale of the problem undeniable.
— Paragraph 2 (Research/Findings, 75–90 words): Summarize the 3 most important findings from the white paper. Use numbered sub-points if needed. Each finding should be a specific, non-obvious insight — not a generic observation.
— Paragraph 3 (Framework/Solution, 75–90 words): Outline the recommended approach or framework. Explain why it works and what makes it different from how organizations currently address the problem.
— Paragraph 4 (Business Impact, 60–75 words): Quantify what organizations that implement this framework achieve. Use specific metrics: time saved, revenue impact, cost reduction, or risk mitigation.
— Call to Action (1–2 sentences): Tell the reader the most important next step — read the full report, schedule a briefing, or download the implementation guide.

Tone: Authoritative but not academic. Written for a C-suite or VP-level reader who has 3 minutes and a full inbox. No buzzwords. No passive voice. Direct.

Variables:
— White Paper Title: [TITLE]
— Industry/Market: [INDUSTRY — e.g., "enterprise healthcare IT," "mid-market financial services"]
— Core Problem: [THE BUSINESS PROBLEM THIS PAPER ADDRESSES]
— Key Statistic: [A COMPELLING DATA POINT — e.g., "organizations lose an average of $1.3M annually to manual data reconciliation errors"]
— 3 Key Findings: [YOUR TOP 3 RESEARCH FINDINGS OR INSIGHTS]
— Recommended Framework: [YOUR SOLUTION APPROACH OR METHODOLOGY NAME]
— Proof of Impact: [CUSTOMER RESULT OR BENCHMARK — e.g., "organizations using this framework reduced reconciliation time by 73%"]

The executive summary is the most undervalued section of any white paper. Most are written last, as an afterthought, by someone who summarizes the table of contents. The most effective executive summaries are written first — as a hypothesis for what the full paper will prove — and function as standalone selling documents. When a VP reads your executive summary and immediately forwards it to two colleagues, you have generated 3 engaged leads from a single document. That is the standard this prompt targets.

Prompt 4: B2B Case Study

Case studies are the most conversion-effective content asset in B2B marketing. They answer the question every serious buyer asks: "Has someone like me actually achieved results with this?" A case study that names a recognizable customer, quantifies the result with specific metrics, and tells the story of how the transformation happened accelerates deals more than any other content type. This prompt generates a structured case study that follows the Situation-Complication-Resolution narrative used by top consulting firms.

Prompt B2B-04: B2B Case Study
Act as a B2B case study writer who creates customer success stories for technology and professional services companies. Write a 600-word case study following the Situation-Complication-Resolution framework. The case study must be specific enough to be credible and outcome-focused enough to be compelling.

Case study structure:
— Headline: "[Customer Name] Achieved [Specific Metric] in [Timeframe] Using [Product/Service]" (or a close variant)
— Situation (100–120 words): Describe the customer — company size, industry, team structure, and the specific context before they became a customer. Include a quote from the customer contact establishing the stakes.
— Complication (120–150 words): The specific problem or challenge they faced. Be precise: what was breaking, how often, what it cost them in time or money, what they had already tried. This section builds empathy with readers in the same situation.
— Resolution (150–180 words): How they implemented the solution. Include the timeline, key milestones, and what made the difference. Include a second customer quote describing the experience of getting to results.
— Results (100–120 words): The specific, quantified outcomes. Primary metric (revenue, cost, time), secondary metrics (team satisfaction, retention, etc.), and a comparison to the before state. Include a final customer quote endorsing the outcome.
— What's Next (60–80 words): What the customer is doing next, and a CTA for the reader to get the same result.

Variables:
— Customer Name/Company: [CUSTOMER NAME AND COMPANY — or use "a mid-market SaaS company" if anonymized]
— Customer Industry/Size: [INDUSTRY AND EMPLOYEE COUNT OR REVENUE]
— Customer Contact: [NAME AND TITLE OF QUOTED CUSTOMER]
— The Problem: [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE BEFORE USING YOUR SOLUTION]
— The Solution: [YOUR PRODUCT/SERVICE AND HOW IT WAS IMPLEMENTED]
— Primary Result: [KEY METRIC — e.g., "reduced onboarding time from 3 weeks to 4 days," "increased qualified pipeline by 210%"]
— Secondary Results: [2–3 ADDITIONAL OUTCOMES]
— Timeframe to Results: [HOW LONG IT TOOK TO SEE RESULTS]

Example headline + situation:
Meridian Software Cut Sales Onboarding from 21 Days to 5 Days in 60 Days

Meridian Software is a 280-person revenue intelligence platform serving mid-market financial services companies. When the sales team grew from 12 to 28 reps in a single quarter following a Series B round, Head of Revenue Enablement James Park faced a problem that threatened to derail the company's growth targets: "We had a 21-day ramp timeline built for a world where we hired 3 or 4 reps a year. We were suddenly onboarding 5 reps a month. The process was falling apart."

The Situation-Complication-Resolution framework works because it mirrors how the human brain processes and retains information: context first, problem second, solution third. Readers recognize themselves in the Situation, feel the Complication viscerally, and embrace the Resolution as the answer they were looking for. A case study written with this structure does not feel like marketing — it feels like a story a colleague told you about something that actually worked.

Prompt 5: B2B Email Nurture Sequence (5-Part)

B2B leads who are not ready to buy today represent the majority of your pipeline. Research consistently shows that 80% of B2B leads who are not sales-ready will buy from someone within 24 months — and the company that nurtures them wins the deal. A 5-part email nurture sequence converts top-of-funnel content downloads into bottom-of-funnel demo requests by educating, building trust, and timing the sales conversation for when the buyer is ready. This prompt generates a complete 5-email nurture sequence mapped to the B2B awareness-consideration-decision journey.

Prompt B2B-05: B2B Email Nurture Sequence (5-Part)
Act as a B2B email marketing strategist who builds nurture sequences that move leads from awareness to demo request. Write a 5-part email nurture sequence for the buyer journey below. Each email must have a clear objective, a subject line, and a single CTA aligned to the buyer's stage.

Email sequence map:
— Email 1 (Day 0 — Welcome + Value Delivery): Deliver the promised lead magnet. Introduce your POV. CTA: read the resource they downloaded.
— Email 2 (Day 3 — Education): Teach one specific insight related to their problem. No pitch. CTA: read a related blog post or case study.
— Email 3 (Day 7 — Social Proof): Share a customer story directly relevant to their situation. Include a specific metric. CTA: read the full case study.
— Email 4 (Day 14 — Objection Handling): Address the most common objection buyers have at this stage. Be direct. CTA: answer a specific question or watch a short demo video.
— Email 5 (Day 21 — Soft CTA): Make the first direct ask. Low friction — not "buy now" but "see how it works for teams like yours." CTA: book a 20-minute call or start a free trial.

For each email provide:
— Subject line (3 options per email)
— Preview text (90 characters max)
— Email body (150–200 words)
— Primary CTA (button text + destination)

Variables:
— ICP: [ROLE, COMPANY SIZE, INDUSTRY]
— Lead Magnet They Downloaded: [WHAT TRIGGERED THE SEQUENCE — e.g., "B2B lead generation checklist," "SaaS churn audit template"]
— Core Problem You Solve: [THE BUSINESS PROBLEM YOUR PRODUCT ADDRESSES]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Key Benefit: [PRIMARY OUTCOME YOUR CUSTOMERS ACHIEVE]
— Customer Success Story: [CUSTOMER NAME, PROBLEM, RESULT — e.g., "Acme Corp reduced churn from 8% to 2.1% in 90 days"]
— Most Common Objection: [THE #1 REASON BUYERS HESITATE — e.g., "implementation complexity," "team adoption," "pricing vs. incumbent"]
— Demo/Trial CTA: [YOUR CONVERSION CTA — e.g., "book a 20-minute demo," "start a 14-day free trial"]

The 21-day sequence with the soft CTA on Email 5 is calibrated to B2B buying psychology. Asking for a demo on Day 1 alienates leads who are not ready. Waiting 90 days to make the ask lets competitors get there first. The 21-day cadence with increasing specificity and social proof mirrors the natural arc of B2B consideration: awareness, education, trust-building, objection resolution, and decision readiness. When the CTA arrives on Day 21, the reader has already received enough value to see you as an expert — not a vendor trying to sell them something.

Prompt 6: Cold Outreach Email

Cold email is the most scalable outbound channel in B2B marketing when done with personalization and relevance. But most cold emails fail for the same reasons: they open with "I hope this email finds you well," spend three sentences describing the sender's company, and close with a calendar link for a 45-minute discovery call. This prompt generates cold outreach emails using the BASHO framework (personalization + problem + proof + ask) that earn responses from busy B2B executives.

Prompt B2B-06: Cold Outreach Email
Act as a B2B outbound sales specialist who writes cold emails that get replies from VP and C-suite buyers. Write a cold outreach email using the BASHO framework: personalization hook, problem statement, proof point, and a low-friction ask.

Email structure:
— Subject line (5 options): Under 7 words. Must not look like marketing. Personalized or curiosity-driven.
— Opening sentence: A specific, researched observation about the recipient's company or role. Not a compliment. A real observation that demonstrates you did your homework. (e.g., "Saw your team is expanding into the mid-market — congratulations on the Series B.")
— Problem bridge (1–2 sentences): Connect your observation to a problem their company likely faces at their stage or in their context. Be specific to their situation, not generic to their industry.
— Proof (1–2 sentences): One customer example with a specific result. Name the company if possible. Quantify the outcome.
— Ask (1 sentence): The lowest-friction possible ask. Not a 45-minute demo. Something they can say yes to in 30 seconds: "Worth a 15-minute call?" or "Happy to send a one-page summary of how we did this — would that be useful?"

Rules: The entire email must be under 150 words. No attachments. No "I hope this email finds you well." No bullet lists in the first cold email. Write like a person, not like marketing software.

Variables:
— Sender Name/Title: [YOUR NAME AND ROLE]
— Recipient Name/Title: [RECIPIENT NAME AND TITLE]
— Recipient Company: [COMPANY NAME]
— Personalization Hook: [SOMETHING SPECIFIC AND RECENT ABOUT THE RECIPIENT OR THEIR COMPANY — e.g., "recent funding round," "new product launch," "LinkedIn post about their challenge," "job posting for a specific role"]
— Problem You Solve: [THE SPECIFIC PAIN THIS PERSON LIKELY HAS]
— Proof Point: [CUSTOMER RESULT RELEVANT TO THIS RECIPIENT]
— Ask: [THE SPECIFIC LOW-FRICTION REQUEST]

Example cold email output:
Subject: Series B growth pains

Hi Marcus,

Saw Vertex just closed a $22M Series B — congrats. Growing from 15 to 50 reps in 12 months is exciting and brutal at the same time.

Most teams at that stage see onboarding time balloon from 2 weeks to 6 because the playbooks that worked for 15 reps break at 50.

We helped Meridian Software cut rep ramp time from 21 days to 5 in their first 60 days post-Series A. Same growth stage, same problem.

Worth a 15-minute call to see if it's relevant for Vertex?

— Sarah

The cold email above is 97 words. It demonstrates specific research (the Series B), identifies a real pain point specific to their situation (scaling onboarding), provides proof with a named company and specific metrics, and asks for 15 minutes — not 45. This is the formula that gets replies from people who delete 95% of the cold emails they receive every day.

Prompt 7: Webinar Promotion Copy

Webinars are one of the most effective B2B lead generation tactics because they require active registration and attendance commitment, which pre-qualifies attendees as genuinely interested. But most webinar promotion copy fails to answer the attendee's core question: "Why should I spend an hour of my limited time on this, and what specific thing will I be able to do afterward that I cannot do now?" This prompt generates webinar promotion copy across all channels — email invite, LinkedIn post, and landing page headline — that drives registrations from your ICP.

Prompt B2B-07: Webinar Promotion Copy
Act as a B2B demand generation marketer who runs webinar campaigns that consistently hit 200+ registrations. Write a complete webinar promotion copy set for the webinar described below.

Provide:
— Webinar title (3 options): Outcome-focused. Include a specific deliverable. Bad: "B2B Lead Generation Strategies." Good: "How to Generate 50 Qualified B2B Leads Per Month Without Increasing Your Ad Budget."
— Email invitation (300 words): Subject line + preview text + email body. Opening: the cost of NOT knowing what you will learn. Middle: the 3 specific outcomes attendees will leave with. Close: the speaker's credibility in 2 sentences. CTA: register button.
— LinkedIn event post (150 words): Hook + 3 bullet outcomes + speaker name + date/time + registration link. Designed to earn shares from the target ICP.
— Registration page headline + subheadline (2 options each): Outcome-driven, specific, with a benefit for each ICP stakeholder who might attend.
— Reminder email (Day-before, 100 words): Short and direct. What they will learn. What they need to do to attend. One-line reason to show up live vs. watch the recording.

Variables:
— Webinar Topic: [WHAT THE WEBINAR IS ABOUT]
— Specific Outcome 1: [WHAT ATTENDEES WILL LEARN/DO #1]
— Specific Outcome 2: [WHAT ATTENDEES WILL LEARN/DO #2]
— Specific Outcome 3: [WHAT ATTENDEES WILL LEARN/DO #3]
— Speaker Name/Title/Credibility: [SPEAKER BIO — e.g., "Jane Kim, VP of Marketing at Growthly, who generated $4M in pipeline from webinars last year"]
— Date/Time/Platform: [WEBINAR LOGISTICS]
— Target ICP: [WHO SHOULD ATTEND]
— Registration URL: [YOUR REGISTRATION LINK]

The difference between a webinar that gets 40 registrations and one that gets 400 is almost entirely in the promotion copy. Specifically, it comes down to outcome specificity: "join us for a discussion on B2B marketing" gets 40 registrations, while "leave with a 13-week content calendar template you can fill in during the live session" gets 400. The prompt forces outcome specificity by requiring three concrete deliverables in every piece of promotion copy — so every potential attendee can evaluate whether the 60 minutes is worth their time.

Prompt 8: B2B Landing Page

B2B landing pages for lead generation live and die by their above-the-fold copy. The headline must communicate what the visitor gets, who it is for, and why they should act now — all in 10 words or fewer. Most B2B landing pages fail because they lead with what the company does ("We provide enterprise-grade revenue intelligence solutions") instead of what the buyer gets ("See exactly which deals will close this quarter"). This prompt generates a complete B2B landing page using the Value-Proof-Action framework.

Prompt B2B-08: B2B Landing Page
Act as a B2B conversion copywriter who designs landing pages that convert at 15%+ for qualified B2B traffic. Write complete landing page copy for a B2B product or service using the Value-Proof-Action framework.

Landing page sections:
— Hero headline (3 options): 8–12 words. What the buyer gets, not what you do. Outcome-focused.
— Hero subheadline (3 options): 20–30 words. Who it is for, how it works, and one proof point. One sentence.
— Hero CTA (2 options): The action. Low friction. "See [Product] in Action" beats "Request a Demo."
— Problem statement (100 words): The cost of the status quo. Written for the buyer, in their language. No jargon.
— How it works (3 steps, 40 words each): Simple. What the buyer does, not what the technology does. Step 1: setup/connect. Step 2: the main workflow. Step 3: the outcome they achieve.
— Social proof section: 3 customer quotes (30–40 words each) + 3 customer logos + 1 key metric ("Trusted by 1,200+ revenue teams" or "4.8 stars on G2").
— Objection handler (3 FAQs): The 3 most common reasons qualified buyers do not convert. Direct, honest answers. 50–75 words each.
— Final CTA section: Headline + subheadline + CTA button. Create urgency without fake scarcity.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— ICP: [WHO THE PAGE IS FOR]
— Primary Outcome: [THE #1 RESULT BUYERS ACHIEVE]
— Secondary Outcome: [SECOND KEY BENEFIT]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT COMPETITORS DO NOT OFFER]
— Social Proof: [CUSTOMER QUOTES, LOGOS, AND METRICS]
— Top 3 Objections: [WHY QUALIFIED BUYERS HESITATE]
— Conversion CTA: [YOUR DESIRED ACTION]

The Value-Proof-Action framework works for B2B landing pages because it respects the professional skepticism of B2B buyers. They arrive at your landing page already aware that you want their contact information. The framework earns that information by delivering value first (the outcome statement), proving it with evidence second (customer proof), and asking for action third (the CTA). Pages that lead with the form field ask first convert at 2 to 4%. Pages that follow Value-Proof-Action convert at 12 to 20% from qualified traffic.

Prompt 9: B2B Google Ad

B2B Google search ads compete for the attention of buyers who are actively researching solutions — which means they are already solution-aware and comparing options. Unlike B2C ads that can rely on impulse, B2B Google ads must communicate value, credibility, and differentiation in 30-character headlines and 90-character descriptions. This prompt generates a complete B2B Google ad set with responsive search ad headlines and descriptions that convert high-intent B2B search traffic into demo requests and trial signups.

B2B ads must establish credibility and communicate ROI quickly — B2B buyers click on ads that look like they were written by someone who understands their business.

Provide:

Prompt 11: Product Demo Script

The product demo is the highest-stakes moment in the B2B sales cycle. It is where deals are won or lost. Yet most demo scripts are built around the product's features rather than the buyer's problems. A feature-led demo shows off what the product can do. A problem-led demo shows the buyer exactly what their life looks like after they use the product to solve their specific problem. This prompt generates a 20-minute product demo script structured around the buyer's pain points, not the product's feature list.

Prompt B2B-11: Product Demo Script
Act as a B2B sales consultant who trains SaaS and professional services teams on high-conversion product demos. Write a 20-minute demo script that follows the problem-led demo framework. The script should make the buyer see themselves in the demo, not watch a product tour.

Demo script structure:
— Opening (2 minutes): Confirm the discovery call findings. "Based on our last conversation, the three things you wanted to see today were [X, Y, Z]. Is that still right?" This confirms agenda and re-establishes pain context.
— Problem validation (3 minutes): Before opening the product, verbalize the buyer's current pain state. Use their exact words from discovery. Get them nodding before you share your screen.
— Demonstration flow (12 minutes): 3 use-case scenarios, 4 minutes each. Each scenario: state the problem scenario in the buyer's language, show the product solving it, pause for their reaction before moving on. Never demo features you have not connected to a stated problem.
— Proof moment (2 minutes): Share one customer story (similar company, similar problem) with specific results. "A company like yours — [size, industry, same pain] — was able to [result] in [timeframe]."
— Next step close (1 minute): Do not close on a vague "I'll send you some information." Ask for a specific next step: technical review, stakeholder presentation, or trial kickoff.

Write the full script with:
— Speaker notes for the AE (what to say)
— Transition phrases between sections
— Questions to ask the buyer at each stage
— Objection responses for the 3 most common demo objections

Variables:
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU ARE DEMOING]
— ICP: [BUYER ROLE AND COMPANY TYPE]
— Pain Point 1: [FIRST CORE PROBLEM THE DEMO ADDRESSES]
— Pain Point 2: [SECOND CORE PROBLEM]
— Pain Point 3: [THIRD CORE PROBLEM]
— Key Feature 1: [PRODUCT CAPABILITY THAT SOLVES PAIN 1]
— Key Feature 2: [PRODUCT CAPABILITY THAT SOLVES PAIN 2]
— Key Feature 3: [PRODUCT CAPABILITY THAT SOLVES PAIN 3]
— Reference Customer: [CUSTOMER STORY TO USE IN PROOF MOMENT]
— Desired Next Step: [WHAT YOU WANT THE BUYER TO COMMIT TO AT THE END]

The confirmation opener is the single most underused technique in B2B demos. By starting with "Based on our last conversation, the three things you wanted to see today were X, Y, and Z — is that still right?" you do three things simultaneously: you demonstrate that you listened during discovery, you set a clear agenda that prevents scope creep, and you get the buyer actively engaged before you share your screen. Demos that start with an agenda confirmation close at 2x the rate of demos that open with "Let me show you our platform."

Prompt 13: Sales Enablement One-Pager

Sales enablement content is the bridge between marketing and revenue. A one-pager that a sales rep can send before a demo, leave after a meeting, or share in a multi-stakeholder email thread serves as a silent salesperson that works while your rep is sleeping. Most one-pagers are too long, too feature-focused, and too complex for a busy executive to process in 90 seconds. This prompt generates a one-pager that communicates the value proposition, proof, and next step in a format that executives actually read.

Prompt B2B-13: Sales Enablement One-Pager
Act as a B2B sales enablement specialist who creates materials that help sales reps close deals faster. Write a one-pager (maximum one printed page, approximately 400 words of copy) that a sales rep can send before a demo, share in a stakeholder email, or leave after an in-person meeting. The one-pager must communicate value, prove it, and drive a next step — all in under 90 seconds of reading time.

One-pager sections:
— Headline (1 option): The outcome your customer achieves. Not your product name. "How [Customer Type] Achieve [Specific Result] in [Timeframe]."
— The problem (60–80 words): Two short paragraphs. The cost of the status quo in concrete terms. Written for the economic buyer (revenue, cost, risk) not the end user (time, frustration).
— Our solution (60–80 words): What you do and why it works. One sentence on the mechanism. Two bullet points on the primary outcomes. No feature lists. No technical specifications unless the audience is technical.
— Proof (80–100 words): One customer case study summary (3–4 sentences). Include company type, the specific result, and a direct quote. If you have multiple customer types, include 2 micro-case studies of 50 words each.
— Why us (40–60 words): Three bullet points. The three things you do that your top two competitors do not. One sentence each. No superlatives ("best," "leading," "world-class"). Specific advantages only.
— Next step (40–60 words): Clear, direct, low-friction. "The next step is [specific action] — it takes [time commitment] and you will walk away with [deliverable]."
— Contact/CTA bar: Name, title, email, calendar link, and company URL.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— ICP: [THE PRIMARY READER — e.g., "CFO or VP of Finance at 200–1,000 employee companies"]
— Primary Outcome: [KEY RESULT IN SPECIFIC NUMBERS]
— Customer Story 1: [COMPANY, PROBLEM, RESULT, QUOTE]
— Top 3 Differentiators: [THE THREE THINGS COMPETITORS DO NOT OFFER]
— Next Step: [THE SPECIFIC CALL TO ACTION]

The one-pager prompt enforces the 90-second read rule, which is the real constraint any sales enablement asset faces. An executive forwarded your one-pager by their VP has exactly 90 seconds before they move to the next item in their inbox. If your one-pager has not communicated the problem, the proof, and the next step in that window, it has failed. The structure above — problem in 80 words, proof in 100 words, differentiators in 3 bullets — is calibrated to that constraint.

Prompt 14: Competitive Battle Card

Competitive battle cards are sales enablement assets that give your reps the knowledge and language they need to win deals when a competitor is in the evaluation. Without a battle card, your rep either avoids the competitive conversation (which cedes credibility) or attacks the competitor directly (which backfires). A well-crafted battle card arms your rep with respectful, factual positioning that steers the conversation to your genuine strengths. This prompt generates a complete competitive battle card for head-to-head situations.

Prompt B2B-14: Competitive Battle Card
Act as a B2B product marketing manager who creates competitive intelligence materials for sales teams. Write a complete competitive battle card for the head-to-head situation described below. The battle card must be honest (no false claims), tactical (specific phrases the rep can use), and outcome-focused (steering toward your genuine strengths).

Battle card sections:
— One-line summary: "Win when [your product] is the right choice; lose gracefully when it is not." (Honest competitive positioning builds rep credibility.)
— When you win: 3 specific situations where your product is clearly superior. Include the customer profile, use case, and the proof point that demonstrates superiority.
— When they win: 2 honest situations where the competitor's product is a better fit. (Knowing when to disqualify a prospect saves time and protects rep credibility.)
— The 5 competitor objections and responses: The exact objections buyers raise when they prefer the competitor, and the exact language the rep should use to respond. Format: "When the buyer says [exact objection]..." / "Your rep says [exact response]..."
— Landmine questions: 3 questions your rep can ask that surface the competitor's weaknesses without attacking them directly. Questions that make the buyer discover the problem themselves.
— Proof points: 3 customer success stories where your product won against this competitor. Company type, the deciding factor, the result. Real names preferred.
— Do's and Don'ts: 5 things to do and 5 things to avoid in competitive situations.

Variables:
— Your Product: [YOUR PRODUCT NAME AND CATEGORY]
— Competitor: [COMPETITOR NAME]
— Your Top 3 Strengths vs. Competitor: [WHERE YOU ARE GENUINELY BETTER]
— Competitor's Top 3 Strengths: [WHERE THEY ARE GENUINELY BETTER — be honest]
— Common Objections When Competitor Is In: [WHAT BUYERS SAY WHEN THEY PREFER THE COMPETITOR]
— Win Stories vs. Competitor: [3 DEALS YOU WON WHERE THE COMPETITOR WAS EVALUATED]

The "when they win" section is what makes this battle card framework different from most. Most competitive materials are pure attack — the competitor is bad at everything. Experienced buyers see through this immediately and lose trust in the rep. When a rep voluntarily acknowledges "there are two situations where their product would actually be a better fit for you," the buyer's trust in everything else the rep says increases dramatically. Honest competitive positioning is the counterintuitive competitive advantage this prompt is designed to produce.

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts in B2B Marketing

The 15 prompts above give you the raw material. These five tips ensure you use that material to build a B2B marketing program that generates qualified pipeline.

1. Always Specify the Buying Committee Role

Every B2B marketing asset should be written for a specific role in the buying committee, not a generic "decision maker." The ICP variable in each prompt above is not optional. Before running any prompt, decide whether this piece is for the economic buyer (CFO, VP of Finance), the technical evaluator (CTO, IT Director), the champion (the person who found you and wants to buy), or the end user (the team that will actually use the product). A LinkedIn post written for a VP of Sales and a LinkedIn post written for a Head of Revenue Operations are completely different pieces of content, even if they cover the same topic. Specificity in the ICP variable produces specificity in the output.

2. Use Proof Variables with Specific Numbers

The most common mistake when running B2B marketing prompts is entering vague proof variables. "We helped a customer improve their results" produces vague content. "We helped Meridian Software reduce sales onboarding from 21 days to 5 days in 60 days" produces compelling content. Before running any of the 15 prompts above, gather your top 3 to 5 customer success stories with specific, quantified outcomes. Use those same specific stories in every prompt that includes a proof or social proof variable. The AI will weave the specifics into the content naturally, producing output that reads like evidence rather than marketing.

3. Layer Prompts Across the Sales Funnel

No single prompt generates a complete B2B marketing program. The prompts above are designed to layer. A LinkedIn thought leadership post (Prompt 1) generates awareness. A blog post (Prompt 2) captures the searcher who clicks on a LinkedIn post about that topic. A content upgrade within that blog post triggers the email nurture sequence (Prompt 5). The email nurture sequence drives a demo request. The demo script (Prompt 11) converts the demo into a next step. The one-pager (Prompt 13) and battle card (Prompt 14) support the multi-stakeholder evaluation. Use the quarterly content calendar (Prompt 15) to orchestrate all 14 other prompts into a coordinated program.

4. Test One Variable Change at a Time

When running multiple variations of the same prompt — two versions of a cold email, three subject line options for a nurture email — change only one variable between runs. If you change the ICP, the pain point, and the proof point simultaneously, you cannot tell which change drove the performance difference. Change the pain point variable while keeping everything else constant. Once you find the pain point that generates the highest response rate, test the proof point. This A/B testing discipline applied to AI prompt outputs is how you build institutional knowledge about what resonates with your specific ICP.

5. Refresh Quarterly with Updated Proof and Market Context

B2B markets move faster than quarterly blog posts. A competitive landscape that was accurate in January may be significantly different by March. Run your most important prompts — the LinkedIn thought leadership post, the cold email, the battle card — at the start of every quarter with updated variables: new customer success stories, updated competitive intelligence, and current market trends. The AI output is only as current as the variables you provide. Quarterly refreshes ensure your content reflects the current reality of your market, not a snapshot from six months ago.
