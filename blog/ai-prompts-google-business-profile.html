10 AI Prompts for Google Business Profile Posts

ContentKit AI•March 4, 2026•12 min read

AI prompts for Google Business Profile give local businesses a faster way to write What's New updates, Offer posts, Event announcements, Product highlights, Q&A responses, and review replies — all optimized with local keywords that improve visibility in Google Maps and the local search pack. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every major GBP content type, each built to work within Google's character limits and post format requirements.

Google Business Profile is the most underused free marketing channel available to local businesses. When someone searches "coffee shop near me," "dentist in Austin," or "best HVAC repair Dallas," the businesses that appear in the local pack — the three listings with maps, photos, and star ratings that dominate the search results page — win the click at a rate of 44% compared to 8% for the first organic result below. That is the local pack advantage: higher visibility, higher trust, and higher click intent than any paid ad or organic ranking below it.

But the local pack is not static. Google's algorithm evaluates dozens of signals to determine which three businesses appear for any given local query. Among those signals: how recently the business posted, how keyword-rich the Business Description is, how complete the profile is, how many Q&A questions have been answered, and how consistently the business responds to reviews. In other words, the businesses that win the local pack are not just the ones with the most reviews — they are the ones with the most actively managed profiles.

This is where structured ai prompts for google business profile solve a real problem. Most local business owners know they should be posting to their GBP weekly, responding to every review, and keeping their Q&A section populated. Almost none of them actually do it consistently, because writing fresh content every week feels like a second job. A well-structured AI prompt turns that weekly task into a two-minute copy-paste operation — and it generates copy that is more keyword-rich and format-correct than what most businesses write manually.

The 10 prompts below cover every content type on Google Business Profile: What's New update posts, Offer/Promotion posts, Event posts, Product Highlight posts, Q&A responses, the 750-character Business Description, individual Service Descriptions, Photo captions and alt text, five-star Review Response templates, and a full Monthly Post Calendar. Each prompt is built with GBP's exact character limits and post field requirements baked in, so the output is ready to paste directly into your profile.

Why Consistent GBP Posting Lifts Local Rankings

Google's local ranking algorithm weighs three primary factors: relevance (does this business match what the searcher is looking for?), distance (how far is the business from the searcher?), and prominence (how well-known and trusted is the business online?). GBP posts, descriptions, and Q&A responses directly improve two of those three factors: relevance and prominence.

When your Business Description includes the services you offer, the neighborhoods you serve, and the specific keywords your customers search for, Google can more accurately match your listing to relevant local queries. When your profile shows fresh posts, recent review responses, and answered Q&A questions, it signals to Google that your business is active and engaged — a component of prominence. Businesses with consistently updated profiles appear in 35% more local searches than profiles that have not been updated in the past 30 days.

The Compounding Effect of Keyword-Rich GBP Content

Every piece of content you publish to your Google Business Profile — posts, descriptions, review responses, Q&A answers — is indexed by Google. That means the keywords you include in your GBP content become part of Google's understanding of what your business does, where it does it, and who it serves. A plumber who writes GBP posts mentioning "emergency pipe repair," "water heater installation," and "Austin TX" builds a keyword footprint that reinforces their local relevance for each of those queries. The AI prompts in this article incorporate your specific services, location, and target keywords into every piece of content — so each post compounds your local keyword footprint rather than wasting the space on generic copy.

Post Freshness as a Ranking Signal

Standard GBP posts (What's New) expire after seven days. This is not a bug — it is Google's way of rewarding businesses that post consistently rather than once and forget. Businesses that post at least once per week maintain a continuous freshness signal that tells Google their profile is actively managed. The Monthly Post Calendar prompt at the end of this article generates a full four-week schedule in a single AI session, so you can plan your entire month of GBP content in ten minutes and schedule it using Google's built-in scheduling tool or a third-party platform like Semrush Local or BrightLocal.

10 AI Prompts for Google Business Profile

Prompt 1: What's New Update Post

The What's New post is the most flexible GBP post type — it can cover anything happening at your business that is not a specific offer or event. New team members, updated hours, seasonal service launches, a new piece of equipment, a community sponsorship, or a change in your process. The key is writing What's New posts that feel genuinely informative rather than promotional, since Google gives this post type lower visual prominence than Offer or Event posts. The content compensates for lower visual prominence by being more keyword-rich and more naturally integrated into Google's understanding of your business.

Prompt GBP-01: What's New Update Post
Act as a local SEO content specialist who writes Google Business Profile posts for small and medium-sized businesses. Write a What's New update post for the business below.

Format requirements:
— Length: 150–300 characters (Google's sweet spot for engagement before the "more" truncation)
— Tone: Informative, local, conversational — not corporate or salesy
— First sentence: The most important information. Write it to stand alone if only the first 100 characters are read.
— Include: The business's primary service or product keyword, the city or neighborhood, and a clear next step (call, visit, book, learn more)
— Do NOT include: promotional language ("best," "#1," "amazing deal") unless it appears in a quote or award name

Write 3 variations testing different angles:
— Variation A: Announce the update itself (what changed and why it matters)
— Variation B: Frame the update as a customer benefit (what this means for them)
— Variation C: Story angle (brief narrative about why the business made this change)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [TYPE OF BUSINESS — e.g., "family dentistry," "HVAC repair," "Italian restaurant"]
— City/Neighborhood: [LOCATION]
— Update Topic: [WHAT IS NEW — e.g., "we added a new hygienist," "we now offer same-day AC repair," "new weekend brunch menu"]
— Primary Keyword: [TARGET LOCAL KEYWORD — e.g., "teeth whitening Austin," "AC repair Dallas," "brunch near downtown"]
— Call to Action: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "call to book," "visit us on Main St," "book online at [URL]"]

Variation A (Announce the update):
Bright Smile Dental in Austin now offers same-day teeth whitening appointments. No waiting weeks — call us at (512) 555-0142 and we will fit you in this week.

Variation B (Customer benefit):
Austin patients: you can now get professional teeth whitening in a single visit at Bright Smile Dental. Walk in Monday–Saturday or book online in 60 seconds.

Variation C (Story angle):
We kept hearing "I wish I could get this done faster." So we added same-day teeth whitening at our Austin office. Because your schedule should not wait on your smile.

Notice how each variation stays under 300 characters, leads with the most important information, and weaves in the location and service keyword naturally. Variation C demonstrates how a story frame can generate more engagement even in a very short format — it creates a "why" that makes the update feel meaningful rather than transactional.

Prompt 2: Offer / Promotion Post

Offer posts are the highest-click-through post type on Google Business Profile because they display a prominent "View Offer" button in both the local pack and the full profile view. When someone searches for a local service and sees a listing with an active offer, they are 2–3x more likely to click through than a listing with no offer displayed. Offer posts include additional fields beyond the text — a start date, an end date, an optional coupon code, and an optional redemption link — so the prompt below generates content for all of those fields simultaneously.

Prompt GBP-02: Offer / Promotion Post
Act as a local marketing specialist who writes high-converting Google Business Profile Offer posts. Write an Offer post for the business below that maximizes the "View Offer" click-through rate.

Provide all required Offer post fields:
— Offer Title (58 characters max): The headline that appears in bold. Make it specific — include the offer amount or percentage and the service/product.
— Offer Details (1,500 characters max, aim for 150–250): Expand on the offer, explain the value, include any conditions, and add a local keyword and CTA.
— Coupon Code (optional): A simple, memorable code if applicable.
— Offer Start Date: [START DATE]
— Offer End Date: [END DATE]
— Redemption Link (optional): [URL TO BOOKING OR OFFER PAGE]

Write 2 variations:
— Variation A: Percentage-off or dollar-off framing
— Variation B: Value-added framing (what they get for free or extra)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [CATEGORY]
— City: [CITY]
— Offer: [DESCRIBE THE PROMOTION — e.g., "20% off first cleaning," "free tire rotation with oil change," "buy one get one on all smoothies"]
— Conditions: [ANY RESTRICTIONS — e.g., "new patients only," "with purchase of $50 or more," "in-store only"]
— Primary Keyword: [LOCAL SEARCH KEYWORD]

The offer title is the most important field because it is the largest text element in the "View Offer" button label. The difference between "Spring Special" and "20% Off Roof Inspection — March Only" is significant: the specific title tells the searcher exactly what they are getting before they click, which increases qualified clicks and reduces wasted interactions from people who would not have converted anyway.

Prompt 3: Event Post

Event posts on Google Business Profile have a dual advantage: they appear on your business profile and they surface in Google's dedicated event search results when someone searches for events in your area. A hardware store hosting a DIY workshop, a salon hosting a styling class, a restaurant hosting a wine tasting, a law firm hosting a free legal clinic — each of these events becomes a separate search-visible content asset when published as a GBP Event post. The prompt below generates event post copy for all required fields, including the event title, description, start time, end time, and registration link.

— Event Description (1,500 characters max, aim for 200–350): Cover the who, what, where, when, and why someone should attend. Include the city/neighborhood keyword, what attendees will learn or experience, and a clear CTA to register or RSVP.
— Event Start Date and Time: [DATE AND TIME]
— Event End Date and Time: [DATE AND TIME]

Tone: Enthusiastic but informative. This post appears in event search results alongside community and entertainment events — it must compete for attention with concerts, markets, and festivals. Make your event sound worth attending.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [CATEGORY]
— City/Address: [LOCATION]

Our licensed contractor will walk you through surface prep, adhesive application, grouting, and finishing — the exact steps that separate a DIY tile job that lasts from one that fails in six months. Reserve your seat at the link below.

The example above works because it answers every question a potential attendee has before they click: what they will learn, who is teaching it, how long it takes, what is provided, how many seats are available, and where to go. Event posts that answer all five Ws consistently outperform vague "join us for an event" posts by a wide margin.

Prompt 4: Product Highlight Post

Product posts on Google Business Profile display in a dedicated "Products" section on your listing and can also appear as image-forward posts in the post feed. For retail businesses, restaurants, and service businesses with discrete service packages, Product posts are one of the most underused GBP features. A well-written Product post for your highest-margin item or most popular service package creates a persistent, visual entry point directly on your Google listing — separate from your regular post feed — that stays visible indefinitely until you remove it.

Prompt GBP-04: Product Highlight Post
Act as a local e-commerce and retail content specialist writing Google Business Profile Product posts that drive clicks and conversions from the Google local listing. Write a Product post for the item or service package below.

Provide all Product post fields:
— Product Name (58 characters max): Clear, specific, and keyword-rich. Include the key attribute (size, flavor, type, tier) in the name.
— Product Description (1,000 characters max, aim for 150–250): Lead with the primary benefit, include 2–3 specific features or details, add social proof if available, and close with a CTA.
— Price (if applicable): [PRICE OR PRICE RANGE]
— Product Category: [GBP CATEGORY — e.g., "Services," "Food & Drink," "Products"]
— CTA Button Type: Choose from "Order Online," "Buy," "Learn More," "Get Offer," or "Book." Recommend the best option for this product.
— CTA Link: [URL TO PRODUCT PAGE OR BOOKING]

Write 2 variations:
— Variation A: Feature-forward (lead with what the product is and does)
— Variation B: Outcome-forward (lead with what the customer achieves or experiences)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Product/Service Name: [WHAT YOU ARE POSTING]
— Key Features: [3–5 SPECIFIC FEATURES OR ATTRIBUTES]
— Primary Benefit: [WHAT THE CUSTOMER GETS]
— Social Proof: [OPTIONAL: REVIEWS, BESTSELLER STATUS, OR AWARDS]
— Local Keyword: [SERVICE + CITY]

The two-variation approach here is deliberate. Feature-forward copy works better for high-consideration purchases where the customer needs to evaluate specifications before buying. Outcome-forward copy works better for impulse or lifestyle purchases where the customer's emotional motivation drives the decision. Testing both reveals which your specific audience responds to — and that insight carries over to every other content channel you use.

Prompt 5: Q&A Response Generator

The Questions and Answers section of Google Business Profile is one of the most powerful and most neglected local SEO tools available. Anyone can ask a question on your GBP listing, and anyone can answer it — including your competitors or random members of the public. Business owners who proactively populate their own Q&A section with keyword-rich questions and detailed answers accomplish two things: they prevent misinformation from appearing on their listing, and they create indexed content that helps Google understand exactly what services, products, and locations their business covers. The Q&A prompt below generates both the questions and the answers so you can seed your GBP with high-value local keyword content from day one.

Prompt GBP-05: Q&A Response Generator
Act as a local SEO specialist who optimizes the Questions and Answers section of Google Business Profile listings. Generate 5 high-value Q&A pairs for the business below.

Requirements:
— Questions: Write the questions the way a real customer would type them into Google Search — natural language, often location-specific
— Answers: 100–200 characters each. Informative and complete. Include the business name, primary service keyword, and location naturally in at least 3 of the 5 answers.
— Question types to cover: service availability, pricing (if appropriate), location/hours, comparison ("do you offer X or Y?"), and a trust/credential question ("are you licensed?" or "how long have you been in business?")
— Write answers in the first person as the business owner responding

Also provide:
— 2 "planted question" suggestions: Questions you should ask yourself on GBP (using a personal Google account) to seed the most important topics before customers or competitors ask them

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [CATEGORY — e.g., "family dentistry," "roofing contractor," "yoga studio"]
— City/Service Area: [LOCATION]
— Top Services: [LIST 3–5 KEY SERVICES OR PRODUCTS]
— Hours: [BUSINESS HOURS]
— Key Credentials: [LICENSES, CERTIFICATIONS, YEARS IN BUSINESS]
— Pricing Range (if public): [OPTIONAL: STARTING PRICES OR RANGES]

Q: Does River Oaks Dental accept new patients in Houston?
A: Yes! River Oaks Dental is welcoming new patients in Houston. We accept most PPO insurance plans and offer flexible scheduling including Saturday appointments. Call (713) 555-0199 to get started.

Q: How much does a teeth cleaning cost in Houston without insurance?
A: At River Oaks Dental in Houston, a routine cleaning starts at $89 for uninsured patients. We also offer an in-house membership plan that covers two cleanings per year — ask about it when you call.

Planted Question Suggestion:
"Do you offer emergency dental appointments in Houston?" — This seeds a high-intent local keyword that drives searchers looking for urgent care directly to your listing.

The planted question strategy is one of the highest-leverage tactics in local SEO. By seeding your own Q&A section with questions that match high-intent local searches — "do you offer emergency [service] in [city]?" — you create GBP content that targets specific keywords before a competitor or an unhelpful random user answers that question instead of you.

Prompt 6: Business Description (750 Characters)

The Google Business Profile Business Description is your most permanent and most keyword-influential piece of GBP content. Unlike posts that expire or rotate, the Business Description appears on your full profile at all times and is crawled by Google as a primary signal for what your business does and where it does it. Google gives you 750 characters — roughly 100–120 words — to describe your business. Most businesses waste this space with generic copy ("We are dedicated to providing excellent service to our valued customers") that tells Google nothing specific and earns no keyword credit. The prompt below generates a Business Description that maximizes keyword density, geographic specificity, and trust signals within the 750-character limit.

Prompt GBP-06: Business Description (750 Characters)
Act as a local SEO content specialist who writes Google Business Profile Business Descriptions optimized for local keyword ranking. Write a Business Description for the business below that maximizes relevance for local search queries within Google's 750-character limit.

Requirements:
— Length: 700–750 characters (use the full limit — every character is an SEO opportunity)
— First sentence: Include the primary keyword and city within the first 100 characters (this appears in the preview on mobile before "more")
— Include naturally: primary service keywords (3–5), city and at least one neighborhood or surrounding area, years in business, key credentials or certifications, and a brief statement of the customer outcome
— Tone: Professional but approachable. Written as if a trusted neighbor is describing your business.
— Do NOT include: website URLs, phone numbers (Google has dedicated fields for these), or promotional language like "best" or "#1" without a citation

Write 2 variations:
— Variation A: Service-first (lead with what you do and who you serve)
— Variation B: Story-first (lead with why the business was founded or what drives it)

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]

The 750-character limit feels tight until you realize most businesses use fewer than 200 characters. Filling the description with specific, relevant content — every service you want to rank for, every neighborhood you serve, your key credentials — is one of the highest-leverage single edits you can make to your GBP. Do it once, do it right, and it compounds indefinitely.

Prompt 7: Service Description

Beyond the Business Description, Google Business Profile allows you to list individual services under your primary category, each with its own name and description. These Service Descriptions appear in the Services tab of your profile and, critically, they are indexed content that Google uses to match your listing to specific service-level queries. A plumbing company that adds individual service listings for "water heater installation," "sewer line inspection," "bathroom fixture repair," and "emergency pipe repair" — each with a keyword-rich description — creates far more indexed content than a plumber whose listing only says "plumbing services."

5 Tips for Getting the Most from Your GBP AI Prompts

The 10 prompts above generate the raw content. These five practices ensure that content is deployed in a way that maximizes your local search impact.

1. Always Include Your City and Service in the First Sentence

Google truncates GBP post previews at roughly 100 characters in the local pack and at 250 characters in Maps before showing a "more" link. That means the first sentence of every post is the only part many searchers will ever read. More importantly, it is the part of the post that Google weights most heavily for keyword relevance. Every prompt in this article instructs the AI to front-load the location and service keyword — but verify this in every output before publishing. If the AI buries your city name in the third sentence, manually move it forward.

2. Add UTM Parameters to Every GBP Link

Google Business Profile posts can include a "Learn More," "Book," "Order," or "Get Offer" button linking to your website. Add UTM parameters to every link you include in GBP posts so you can track traffic, engagement, and conversions in Google Analytics: ?utm_source=google&utm_medium=gbp&utm_campaign=march-whitening-offer. Without UTM tracking, GBP-generated traffic appears as "direct" in your analytics and you cannot measure the ROI of your posting activity.

3. Respond to Every Review Within 24 Hours

Google's research shows that businesses that respond to reviews are 1.7x more likely to be considered trustworthy by consumers. Response time also matters: reviews that receive a response within 24 hours generate more engagement than those responded to days later.
