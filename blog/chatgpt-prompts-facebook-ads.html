Facebook ad spend hit $164 billion globally in 2025, and most of it was wasted. Not because of targeting, not because of budget, not because of creative fatigue — but because the copy was bad. Generic headlines. Vague primary text. CTAs that say "Learn More" when they should say something specific. The average Facebook user scrolls 300 feet of content per day. Your ad gets 1.7 seconds to earn attention. Bad copy does not survive that filter.

The problem is not that marketers cannot write. The problem is that Facebook ads have a unique set of constraints that make them harder to write than almost any other format. You have a 125-character headline limit, a 125-character description limit, and while primary text can run longer, only the first 125 characters show above the fold before the "See more" truncation. Every word has to earn its place. One wasted sentence means one fewer person reads your CTA.

On top of the character constraints, Meta's ad policies are strict and getting stricter. Make a health claim without evidence? Rejected. Use a before-and-after image for a supplement? Rejected. Call out a personal attribute like "Are you overweight?" Rejected. The copy has to sell without crossing lines that get your ad account flagged — or worse, banned.

Why Facebook Ad Copy Needs Structured Prompts

Before we get to the prompts, here is why generic AI instructions fail for Facebook ads specifically — and what each prompt does to fix the problem.

1. Facebook Ads Have a Multi-Field Format

A Facebook ad is not one block of text. It is four distinct fields: primary text (the main copy above the image), headline (bold text below the image), description (smaller text below the headline), and CTA button. Each field has a different job. Primary text does the selling. The headline reinforces the core benefit. The description adds a secondary proof point or urgency element. The CTA drives the click. When you tell ChatGPT to "write a Facebook ad," it does not know this structure exists. It gives you a paragraph. The prompts below specify the exact output format so every field is covered.

2. Cold Traffic and Warm Traffic Need Different Copy

An ad targeting someone who has never heard of your brand needs a completely different message than an ad targeting someone who visited your pricing page yesterday. Cold traffic copy must establish credibility, create awareness, and earn the click — all in 125 characters above the fold. Retargeting copy can skip the awareness step and go straight to the objection, the offer, or the urgency play. Using the same prompt for both audiences is like using the same script for a first date and a marriage proposal. The prompts are organized by funnel stage so the copy matches the audience's awareness level.

3. Compliance Is Not Optional

Meta rejects approximately 2.2 billion ads per year for policy violations. Common triggers include income guarantees ("Make $10K/month"), unsubstantiated health claims, before-and-after comparisons, and personal attribute callouts. Each prompt includes tone constraints and guardrails that steer the AI away from flagged language patterns. You still need to review every ad, but the prompts give you a compliant starting point instead of a liability.

Now let us get into the prompts. Each one is copy-paste ready with variables you fill in for your specific business, product, and audience.

Cold Traffic Ad Prompts (Prompts 1–5)

Cold traffic ads target people who have never interacted with your brand. These are the hardest ads to write because you are interrupting someone who did not ask to hear from you. The copy has to earn attention, build just enough trust, and drive a click — all within a thumb-scroll. These five prompts use proven direct-response frameworks designed for exactly that challenge.

Prompt 1: PAS Framework Cold Traffic Ad

Problem-Agitate-Solve is the most reliable direct-response framework for cold audiences. It works because it mirrors how people think about their own problems: they know something is wrong (Problem), they feel the frustration of it (Agitate), and they want relief (Solve). Use this when your audience has a clearly defined pain point and your product is the direct solution.

Prompt FB-01: PAS Framework Cold Traffic Ad
Act as a direct-response Facebook ad copywriter who specializes in cold traffic campaigns for [INDUSTRY] brands. Write a complete Facebook ad using the Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS) framework for the product below.

Structure:
— Primary Text (3–5 sentences): Open with a specific, relatable problem your target audience faces daily. Agitate by describing the emotional and practical consequences of leaving the problem unsolved. Present the product as the solve with one concrete proof point.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Benefit-driven. No hype words.
— Description (under 30 characters): Supporting proof point or urgency element.
— CTA Button: Choose from [Shop Now / Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer / Download]

Tone: Conversational, specific, zero corporate speak. Write like a smart friend recommending something — not a brand broadcasting a message.

Compliance: No income guarantees, no unsubstantiated health claims, no personal attribute callouts, no before-and-after promises without evidence.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE — demographics, psychographics, behaviors]
— Core Problem: [THE MAIN PROBLEM YOUR PRODUCT SOLVES]
— Key Proof Point: [STAT, TESTIMONIAL, OR RESULT THAT PROVES THE SOLVE]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER — discount, free trial, free shipping, etc.]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]

Prompt 2: AIDA Framework Ad

AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action — works when your audience needs to be educated about the solution category before they can want your specific product. The framework guides the reader through a logical progression: get their attention with a bold opening, build interest with a surprising fact or insight, create desire by painting the outcome, and close with a clear action step. Use this for products that solve problems people do not yet know they have.

Prompt FB-02: AIDA Framework Ad
Act as a Facebook advertising copywriter who writes high-converting cold traffic ads. Write a complete Facebook ad using the AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action) framework.

Structure:
— Primary Text:
• Attention (1 sentence): Bold statement, surprising stat, or provocative question that stops the scroll.
• Interest (1–2 sentences): Expand on the attention hook with a specific insight or data point that educates the reader.
• Desire (1–2 sentences): Paint the specific outcome the reader will achieve. Use sensory or emotional language.
• Action (1 sentence): Clear, specific CTA with what happens after the click.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Outcome-focused.
— Description (under 30 characters): Credibility element.
— CTA Button: [Shop Now / Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer / Download]

Tone: Confident but not arrogant. Specific but not jargon-heavy.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Attention Hook Angle: [SURPRISING STAT, BOLD CLAIM, OR QUESTION]
— Key Benefit: [THE PRIMARY OUTCOME YOUR PRODUCT DELIVERS]
— Credibility Element: [NUMBER OF CUSTOMERS, RATING, MEDIA MENTION, OR AWARD]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER]

Prompt 3: Before-After-Bridge Ad

Before-After-Bridge shows the reader their current reality (Before), paints the desired future state (After), and positions your product as the bridge between the two. This framework works best for transformation-focused products — courses, coaching, SaaS tools, and wellness products. The key is making the "Before" specific enough that the reader thinks "that is me" and the "After" vivid enough that they want to get there.

Prompt FB-03: Before-After-Bridge Ad
Act as a direct-response copywriter who specializes in transformation-focused Facebook ads. Write a complete Facebook ad using the Before-After-Bridge framework.

Structure:
— Primary Text:
• Before (1–2 sentences): Describe the reader's current frustrating situation with specific, relatable details. Use "you" language.
• After (1–2 sentences): Paint the desired outcome in concrete terms. What does their day/week/business look like after the transformation?
• Bridge (1–2 sentences): Introduce the product as the bridge. Explain how it works in one simple sentence. Include one proof point.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Transformation-focused.
— Description (under 30 characters): Proof or urgency.
— CTA Button: [Shop Now / Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer]

Tone: Empathetic, not preachy. Show you understand the "before" without condescending.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Before State: [CURRENT FRUSTRATION — be specific and emotional]
— After State: [DESIRED OUTCOME — be concrete and visual]
— How It Works: [ONE-SENTENCE EXPLANATION OF THE MECHANISM]
— Proof Point: [STAT, TESTIMONIAL, OR RESULT]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER]

Prompt 4: Curiosity-Gap Ad

The curiosity-gap ad creates an information gap that the reader can only close by clicking. This is the most effective framework for high scroll-stop rates because it leverages a psychological principle: people are more motivated to close an information gap than to gain new information. The trick is creating genuine curiosity without being clickbait. Use this when you have a counterintuitive insight, a surprising method, or a contrarian take on a common practice in your industry.

Prompt FB-04: Curiosity-Gap Ad
Act as a Facebook ad copywriter who specializes in hook-driven, high-CTR ads. Write a complete Facebook ad using the Curiosity-Gap framework.

Structure:
— Primary Text:
• Hook (1 sentence): Open with a surprising statement, counterintuitive fact, or unexpected claim that creates an information gap. The reader must want to know more.
• Tease (2–3 sentences): Build the curiosity without resolving it. Reference the result or discovery without revealing the method. Use specific numbers or details to add credibility to the tease.
• CTA (1 sentence): Direct the reader to click for the full answer/method/reveal.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Amplify the curiosity gap.
— Description (under 30 characters): Add credibility.
— CTA Button: [Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer]

Tone: Intriguing but honest. Create real curiosity, not clickbait. No misleading claims.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Counterintuitive Insight: [THE SURPRISING THING YOUR AUDIENCE DOES NOT KNOW]
— Result/Proof: [THE OUTCOME THAT PROVES THE INSIGHT WORKS]
— Landing Page Content: [WHAT THE READER WILL FIND WHEN THEY CLICK]

Prompt 5: Social Proof Ad

Social proof ads lead with evidence that other people — ideally people like your target audience — have already bought and benefited from your product. This framework works best when you have strong testimonials, impressive customer counts, notable case studies, or third-party validation. It is particularly effective for higher-priced products where the buying decision carries more risk.

Prompt FB-05: Social Proof Ad
Act as a direct-response Facebook ad copywriter who builds ads around social proof and customer results. Write a complete Facebook ad using the Social Proof framework.

Structure:
— Primary Text:
• Lead with proof (1 sentence): Open with a specific customer result, testimonial quote, or aggregate stat (e.g., "12,000+ marketers use this daily").
• Context (1–2 sentences): Explain who these customers are and what they were doing before. Make the reader see themselves in the customer story.
• Product bridge (1 sentence): Connect the proof to the product in plain language.
• CTA (1 sentence): Invite the reader to join/start/try with a specific next step.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Include a number or result.
— Description (under 30 characters): Trust element (rating, guarantee, or count).
— CTA Button: [Shop Now / Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer]

Tone: Credible and grounded. Let the results speak. No hype.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Social Proof Type: [TESTIMONIAL QUOTE / CUSTOMER COUNT / CASE STUDY / RATING / MEDIA MENTION]
— Specific Result: [THE MEASURABLE OUTCOME A CUSTOMER ACHIEVED]
— Customer Description: [WHO THE CUSTOMER IS — role, industry, company size]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER]

Retargeting Ad Prompts (Prompts 6–9)

Retargeting ads speak to people who already know your brand. They visited your website, added something to their cart, watched your video, or engaged with a previous ad. These audiences do not need to be introduced to you — they need to be reminded, reassured, or given a reason to act now. The copy strategy is fundamentally different from cold traffic: shorter, more direct, and focused on overcoming the specific objection that stopped them from converting the first time.

Prompt 6: Cart Abandonment Retargeting

Cart abandonment retargeting targets the warmest audience in your funnel — people who were one click away from buying. They chose your product, added it to their cart, and left. The copy should acknowledge that they were interested, address the most likely objection (price, timing, trust), and give them a reason to complete the purchase. This single ad type often produces the highest ROAS of any campaign.

Prompt FB-06: Cart Abandonment Retargeting Ad
Act as a Facebook retargeting ad specialist. Write a complete Facebook ad targeting users who added a product to their cart but did not complete the purchase.

Structure:
— Primary Text (3–4 sentences): Acknowledge that the reader was interested (without being creepy about tracking). Address the #1 likely objection for this product. Provide a reason to come back now (incentive, guarantee, social proof, or scarcity).
— Headline (under 40 characters): Reference the incomplete action.
— Description (under 30 characters): Incentive or guarantee.
— CTA Button: [Shop Now / Get Offer]

Tone: Friendly nudge, not aggressive push. Casual and understanding, like a helpful reminder.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Price Point: [PRICE]
— #1 Likely Objection: [PRICE / TIMING / TRUST / NEED MORE INFO]
— Incentive to Return: [DISCOUNT CODE / FREE SHIPPING / MONEY-BACK GUARANTEE / BONUS]
— Social Proof: [CUSTOMER COUNT, RATING, OR TESTIMONIAL]
— Cart Recovery URL: [URL]

Prompt 7: Page Visitor Retargeting

Page visitor retargeting targets people who visited a specific page on your website — typically a product page, pricing page, or landing page — but did not take the next step. They showed intent but did not convert. The copy should reference what they were looking at, introduce a new angle or benefit they may have missed, and make the next step crystal clear. This works especially well when paired with a custom audience of pricing page visitors.

Prompt FB-07: Page Visitor Retargeting Ad
Act as a Facebook retargeting specialist. Write a complete Facebook ad targeting users who visited a specific product or pricing page but did not convert.

Structure:
— Primary Text (3–4 sentences): Reference the type of page they visited without naming the exact URL (e.g., "You were checking out [product category] recently"). Introduce a benefit or angle they might not have considered during their first visit. Include one piece of social proof. Close with a direct CTA.
— Headline (under 40 characters): New angle or benefit they missed.
— Description (under 30 characters): Trust signal.
— CTA Button: [Learn More / Shop Now / Get Offer]

Tone: Helpful, not salesy. Assume they are interested but need one more reason.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Page Type: [PRODUCT PAGE / PRICING PAGE / LANDING PAGE / CATEGORY PAGE]
— Missed Benefit: [A BENEFIT OR FEATURE THAT MAY NOT HAVE BEEN OBVIOUS]
— Social Proof: [CUSTOMER COUNT, TESTIMONIAL, OR RATING]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER OR INCENTIVE]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]

Prompt 8: Engagement Retargeting

Engagement retargeting targets people who interacted with your previous ads or organic content — liked, commented, shared, watched a video, or clicked but did not convert. These people showed interest but were not ready to buy. The copy should build on the engagement by introducing a deeper value proposition or addressing a common objection. Think of it as the second conversation in a relationship — you do not re-introduce yourself, you go deeper.

Prompt FB-08: Engagement Retargeting Ad
Act as a Facebook ad copywriter specializing in engagement retargeting. Write a complete Facebook ad targeting people who engaged with previous ads or content (likes, comments, shares, video views) but have not yet visited the website or purchased.

Go deeper than the original content — share a more specific benefit, a case study detail, or answer the most common question people ask. End with a clear next step and what they will find on the landing page.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Value-forward, action-oriented.
— Description (under 30 characters): Specificity or proof.
— CTA Button: [Learn More / Sign Up / Shop Now]

Tone: Warm and familiar. They already know you — act like it.

Variables:

Prompt 9: Time-Sensitive Retargeting

Time-sensitive retargeting uses urgency to convert people who are interested but procrastinating. This works for limited-time offers, seasonal promotions, expiring discounts, or product launches with deadlines. The urgency must be real — fake countdown timers and manufactured scarcity destroy trust. Use this when you have a genuine deadline, a real inventory constraint, or a price increase with a set date.

Prompt FB-09: Time-Sensitive Retargeting Ad
Act as a Facebook ad copywriter who specializes in urgency-driven retargeting campaigns. Write a complete Facebook ad targeting warm audiences with a time-sensitive offer.

Structure:
— Primary Text (3–4 sentences): Lead with the deadline or time constraint. State what the reader will miss if they do not act (specific benefit or savings). Include one proof point to reinforce the value. Close with a specific, urgent CTA that includes what happens after the click.
— Headline (under 40 characters): Include the time element.
— Description (under 30 characters): What they get or save.
— CTA Button: [Get Offer / Shop Now]

Tone: Urgent but not desperate. Confident, not pushy. The offer is valuable — you are doing them a favor by reminding them.

Compliance: Only use real deadlines. No fake scarcity. No manufactured urgency.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME]
— Deadline: [SPECIFIC DATE/TIME THE OFFER EXPIRES]
— Offer Details: [DISCOUNT AMOUNT, BONUS, OR SPECIAL TERMS]
— What They Miss: [SPECIFIC BENEFIT OR SAVINGS THEY LOSE AFTER THE DEADLINE]
— Proof Point: [HOW MANY PEOPLE HAVE ALREADY BOUGHT, RATING, OR RESULT]
— Landing Page URL: [URL]

Ad Variation Prompts (Prompts 10–12)

Writing one good ad is not enough. Facebook's algorithm needs variations to test and optimize. But most marketers struggle to write more than one version because they run out of angles after the first draft. These three prompts solve that problem by systematically generating headline variations, hook variations, and CTA variations from a single core message. Use them after you have written your primary ad with Prompts 1–9.

Prompt 10: Headline A/B Test Generator

Headlines are the highest-leverage element to test in any Facebook ad. A headline change can swing CTR by 30–50% without touching the primary text or creative. This prompt generates 10 headline variations from a single angle, each using a different copywriting approach — benefit-led, curiosity-driven, number-based, social proof, urgency, question, how-to, and more. Use it to build a testing pipeline you can run for weeks.

Prompt FB-10: Headline A/B Test Generator
Act as a Facebook ad copywriter and A/B testing strategist. Generate 10 headline variations for the Facebook ad below. Each headline must be under 40 characters and use a different copywriting angle.

Angles to cover (one headline per angle):
1. Benefit-Led (state the primary outcome)
2. Curiosity-Driven (create an information gap)
3. Number/Stat-Based (lead with a specific number)
4. Social Proof (reference customer count or results)
5. Urgency (time element without being pushy)
6. Question (ask something the reader would answer "yes" to)
7. How-To (promise a method or process)
8. Contrarian (challenge conventional wisdom)
9. Direct Command (tell the reader what to do)
10. Emotional Trigger (tap into a specific feeling)

Output format: Numbered list with the angle label in parentheses after each headline.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Primary Benefit: [THE MAIN OUTCOME YOUR PRODUCT DELIVERS]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Key Stat: [YOUR STRONGEST NUMBER — customers, results, savings]

Prompt 11: Hook Variation Generator

The hook — the first line of your primary text — determines whether anyone reads the rest. On Facebook, only the first 125 characters show above the fold in mobile feed. If the hook does not earn the "See more" click, the rest of your ad is invisible. This prompt generates 5 different hooks for the same ad, each designed to stop the scroll from a different psychological angle.

Variables:

Prompt 12: CTA Variation Generator

Most Facebook ads default to "Learn More" or "Shop Now" for the CTA button and "Click here to get started" for the CTA text. But CTA copy is another high-leverage testing variable. The best CTAs tell the reader exactly what happens after the click and reinforce the value of taking action. This prompt generates multiple CTA approaches so you can find the one that drives the most conversions for your specific offer.

7. Simplicity Close: Emphasize how easy the next step is.

Output format: Numbered list. For each variation, provide the closing primary text sentence, CTA button choice, and supporting headline.

Variables:

Special Format Ad Prompts (Prompts 13–15)

Standard single-image ads are the workhorse of most Facebook campaigns, but special formats — carousel ads, video ads, and lead form ads — often outperform them when done right. The challenge is that each format has its own structural requirements and copy considerations. A carousel ad needs sequential card copy that tells a story across 3–10 cards. A video ad script needs a hook in the first 3 seconds, visual direction, and a spoken CTA. A lead form ad needs copy that pre-qualifies leads and form questions that capture useful data. These three prompts handle each format.

Prompt 13: Carousel Ad Copy

Carousel ads let you tell a sequential story across multiple cards. Each card has its own image, headline, description, and link. The best carousels build a narrative where each card adds a new reason to buy, and the final card delivers the CTA. This prompt generates a 5-card carousel with copy that works as a progressive story — not just five random benefit statements.

Prompt FB-13: Carousel Ad Copy
Act as a Facebook ad copywriter who specializes in carousel ad formats. Write complete copy for a 5-card carousel ad that tells a sequential story.

Structure:
— Primary Text (2–3 sentences above the carousel): Set up the story. Create a reason for the reader to swipe through all 5 cards.
— Card 1 — The Problem: Headline (under 40 chars) + Description (under 20 words) that names the pain point.
— Card 2 — The Cost: Headline + Description that quantifies what the problem costs (time, money, stress, opportunity).
— Card 3 — The Solution: Headline + Description that introduces the product as the answer.
— Card 4 — The Proof: Headline + Description with a specific result, testimonial, or stat.
— Card 5 — The CTA: Headline + Description with the offer and a direct call to action.
— CTA Button (all cards): [Shop Now / Learn More / Sign Up / Get Offer]

Tone: Each card should feel like a natural continuation of the one before it. The reader should want to swipe.

Variables:
— Product/Service: [PRODUCT NAME AND WHAT IT DOES]
— Target Audience: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE]
— Problem: [THE CORE PROBLEM]
— Cost of Problem: [WHAT THE PROBLEM COSTS IN TIME/MONEY/STRESS]
— How It Works: [ONE-SENTENCE EXPLANATION]
— Proof Point: [STAT, TESTIMONIAL, OR CASE STUDY]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER]
— Image Direction (optional): [BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF WHAT EACH CARD IMAGE SHOULD SHOW]

Prompt 14: Video Ad Script (15–30 sec)

Short-form video ads are the highest-performing format on Facebook and Instagram in 2026. But the script structure is different from written ad copy. You need a visual hook in the first 3 seconds (before the viewer scrolls), a problem-solution narrative in the body, and a spoken CTA with on-screen text reinforcement. This prompt produces a complete script with scene directions, spoken copy, and on-screen text overlays.

Prompt FB-14: Video Ad Script (15–30 sec)

Facebook Ad Best Practices When Using AI

The prompts above will give you 80% of the work. The other 20% — the part that separates good ads from great ads — comes from how you use the output. Here are the best practices that make AI-generated Facebook ads actually convert.

Always Edit the First Draft

AI output is a first draft, not a final draft. Read every ad out loud. If it sounds like a press release or a LinkedIn post, rewrite the opening. The most important edit is removing filler phrases that AI defaults to: "In today's fast-paced world," "Are you ready to take your business to the next level," and "Don't miss this incredible opportunity." Delete all of them. Replace them with specific details about your product, your audience, and your offer.

Test One Variable at a Time

Do not test a new headline, new primary text, and new creative simultaneously. You will not know which change caused the performance difference. Use Prompt 10 to generate headline variations while keeping everything else the same. Once you find the winning headline, use Prompt 11 to test hooks. Then use Prompt 12 to test CTAs. This sequential testing approach compounds — each winning variation builds on the last.

Match the Framework to the Funnel Stage

Cold traffic needs education and credibility. Retargeting needs objection handling and urgency. Do not use a PAS ad (Prompt 1) for retargeting — the audience already knows the problem. Use a cart abandonment ad (Prompt 6) or time-sensitive ad (Prompt 9) instead. The framework-funnel match is the single biggest determinant of ad performance after targeting.

Respect Character Limits

Facebook truncates primary text after approximately 125 characters on mobile. Your hook — the first sentence — must work within that limit. If the most compelling part of your ad is in sentence three, your ad will underperform because most people never see sentence three. Use Prompt 11 specifically to craft hooks that sell the "See more" click.

Include Specific Numbers

Ads with specific numbers outperform ads with vague claims. "Save 4 hours per week" beats "Save time." "Join 2,347 marketers" beats "Join thousands." "47% increase in conversions" beats "Boost your conversions." When you fill in the prompt variables, use the most specific, defensible number you have. If you do not have a number, get one before running the ad.

Write for Sound Off

85% of Facebook video is watched without sound. If you use Prompt 14 (Video Ad Script), make sure every key message appears as on-screen text. The prompt includes an on-screen text column for this reason. Review the script with the "spoken copy" column covered — if the story still makes sense from the on-screen text alone, the video will work.

Review Against Meta's Policies Before Publishing

The prompts include compliance guardrails, but they are not a substitute for human review. Before publishing any ad, check it against Meta's advertising standards. Pay special attention to personal attributes (do not imply you know the reader's health, financial, or relationship status), unsubstantiated claims (do not promise specific results without evidence), and prohibited content (no misleading buttons, no non-functional elements, no clickbait).

FAQ — ChatGPT Prompts for Facebook Ads

Do these prompts comply with Facebook's ad policies? +
The prompts are designed to produce compliant ad copy, but you must always review the output against Meta's current advertising standards. Common violations include making income guarantees, using before-and-after claims without evidence, and calling out personal attributes (age, race, health conditions). Each prompt includes tone constraints that steer the AI away from hype and unsubstantiated claims, but final compliance responsibility is yours. Run every ad through Facebook's Ad Library search to compare against approved ads in your niche.
What Facebook ad objective should I use with these prompts? +
Match the objective to the prompt type. Cold traffic prompts (1–5) work best with Traffic, Engagement, or Conversions objectives depending on your funnel stage. Retargeting prompts (6–9) pair with Conversions or Catalog Sales. The Lead Form Ad prompt (15) requires the Lead Generation objective. Carousel prompts work with any objective. The ad copy these prompts generate is optimized for the corresponding objective — the PAS and AIDA prompts drive clicks, while the retargeting prompts drive conversions from warm audiences.
How many ad variations should I test? +
Start with 3–5 variations per ad set. Use one prompt framework for your primary text, then use the Headline A/B Test Generator (Prompt 10) and Hook Variation Generator (Prompt 11) to create variations. Test one variable at a time — headlines first, then hooks, then CTAs. Facebook's algorithm needs 50 conversions per ad set per week to optimize properly, so do not split your budget across too many variations. For most budgets under $100/day, 3 variations is the sweet spot.
Can ChatGPT write Facebook ads for any industry? +
Yes, but regulated industries need extra care. Healthcare, finance, real estate, alcohol, and political ads have additional Meta policies and legal requirements. The prompts include variables for industry-specific details, but you must add compliance constraints for regulated verticals. For example, add "Do not make specific health claims or reference medical conditions" for supplement ads, or "Include required disclosures and disclaimers" for financial service ads. The more specific your variables, the better the output for any industry.
How do I know which prompt framework to use? +
Match the framework to your audience's awareness level. PAS (Prompt 1) works best when your audience knows they have a problem but has not found a solution. AIDA (Prompt 2) works for audiences who need to be educated about the solution. Before-After-Bridge (Prompt 3) is strongest when you can show a clear transformation. Curiosity-Gap (Prompt 4) works for scroll-stopping hooks in crowded feeds. Social Proof (Prompt 5) is best when you have strong testimonials or data. When in doubt, start with PAS — it is the most versatile direct-response framework.
