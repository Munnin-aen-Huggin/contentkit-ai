8 AI Prompts for Brand Guidelines and Style Guides

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for brand guidelines are structured instructions that generate complete brand documentation — voice and tone guides, visual identity standards, content rules, and channel-specific playbooks — ready to be compiled into a professional style guide your entire team can follow. This guide delivers 8 copy-paste prompts covering every section of a complete brand guidelines document, each built on proven branding frameworks that produce consistent, usable standards from day one.

Brand inconsistency is one of the most expensive silent problems in marketing. When your social media team writes in a casual, emoji-heavy tone, your email team sends formal corporate copy, and your sales team pitches with language that sounds like a different company entirely, you are not building a brand — you are building confusion. Customers who encounter multiple versions of your brand take longer to trust you, convert at lower rates, and are harder to retain. Consistent brands, by contrast, earn 20% more revenue on average than inconsistent ones across their marketing channels.

The problem is that most brands do not have documented guidelines. A founder or creative director carries the brand in their head. New hires guess at the tone. Agencies interpret the brief differently every engagement. And because writing proper brand guidelines from scratch requires weeks of strategic work, a design system, and often a $15,000 to $50,000 agency engagement, most small and mid-size businesses simply go without.

This is exactly where ai prompts for brand guidelines change the equation. Instead of waiting for a brand agency, you can generate a comprehensive first draft of your entire brand guidelines document — voice, tone, visual direction, content standards, messaging framework, channel playbooks, and audit checklist — in an afternoon. The AI handles the structure and the writing. You handle the strategic decisions about what fits your brand and what to refine.

The 8 prompts below cover every core section of a professional brand guidelines document: the overview and positioning, voice and tone guide, content standards and style rules, social media brand standards, email brand standards, brand messaging framework, photography and visual direction, and a brand audit checklist. Each prompt is built around the specific decisions a brand strategist makes when building guidelines for a client — so the output is document-ready, not generic.

Why AI Prompts Produce Usable Brand Guidelines

Brand guidelines fail for two reasons: they are either too vague to be useful ("be authentic, be clear, be human") or too prescriptive to be practical (42-page documents nobody reads). The best brand guidelines make clear decisions, document them in plain language, and give teams enough context to apply the standards independently without asking for approval on every piece of content.

Here is why structured AI prompts consistently produce guidelines that actually get used.

Frameworks Replace Blank-Page Paralysis

The hardest part of writing brand guidelines is knowing what decisions to make. Most brand managers know their brand intuitively but struggle to articulate why it sounds the way it does, what makes a piece of content on-brand versus off-brand, or how to explain their visual aesthetic to a new designer without showing examples. The prompts in this article are built on established branding frameworks — brand archetypes, voice spectrum mapping, the messaging hierarchy, the brand personality scale — so the AI generates guidelines grounded in real brand strategy, not generic advice.

Variables Capture Your Specific Context

Generic brand guidelines are worse than no guidelines because they give teams false confidence that the standards are documented when they are actually just boilerplate. The prompts below use detailed variable fields — your specific audience, your positioning relative to competitors, your founding story, your category conventions, your product's personality — to generate guidelines that are specific to your brand. The more detail you put into the variables, the more precise and useful the output.

Structure Creates Usable Documents

Each prompt specifies the exact sections, headings, and format the output should take, producing guidelines structured the same way professional brand agencies deliver them. That means the output can be pasted directly into Notion, Google Docs, or a brand guidelines tool like Frontify or Brandfolder with minimal reformatting. The difference between a wall of text and a usable brand document is structure — and the prompts enforce it.

The bottom line: ai prompts for brand guidelines do not replace brand strategy. They execute it faster, with more structure, using frameworks that ensure every important decision gets documented. Here are the 8 prompts.

8 AI Prompts for Brand Guidelines

Prompt 1: Brand Guidelines Overview Document

The brand overview section is the foundation of your entire guidelines document. It establishes why your brand exists, who it serves, what it believes, and how it wants to be perceived — the strategic context that every other section of the guidelines flows from. Most brand guidelines documents skip this section or include a cursory mission statement. That is a mistake: without strategic context, the voice and tone rules feel arbitrary and the visual standards feel disconnected. This prompt generates a complete brand overview section with mission, vision, values, positioning statement, brand personality, and audience definition.

Prompt BG-01: Brand Guidelines Overview Document
Act as a senior brand strategist with experience building brand guidelines for consumer, B2B, and DTC companies. Write the Brand Overview section of a complete brand guidelines document for the brand below.

Include the following subsections:
— Brand Story (3–4 sentences): Why the brand exists and the founding insight that drives it
— Mission Statement (1 sentence): What the brand does and for whom
— Vision Statement (1 sentence): The future the brand is working toward
— Core Values (4–5 values): Each with a name and a 2–3 sentence explanation of what it means in practice, not just in principle
— Brand Positioning Statement: Using the structure "For [audience], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]"
— Brand Personality (5 adjectives): With a brief description of how each one shows up in content and communication
— Target Audience Profile (primary and secondary): Demographics, psychographics, goals, frustrations, and what they believe before discovering this brand
— Competitive Landscape Note: How this brand sits differently from its 2–3 nearest competitors

Format as a structured document section with clear headings. Write in plain, direct language. Values descriptions must be specific to this brand, not generic corporate values.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry/Category: [INDUSTRY OR CATEGORY — e.g., "B2B SaaS project management," "DTC skincare," "professional services consulting"]
— Founding Story: [WHY THE BRAND WAS STARTED — the insight, problem, or frustration that led to its creation]
— Primary Audience: [WHO THE BRAND PRIMARILY SERVES — e.g., "marketing directors at mid-market B2B companies," "women 28–45 who care about clean beauty"]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES THIS BRAND DIFFERENT FROM COMPETITORS]
— Top 2–3 Competitors: [COMPETITOR NAMES OR DESCRIPTIONS]
— Brand Personality Words (if you have a starting point): [ANY ADJECTIVES YOU ALREADY USE TO DESCRIBE THE BRAND]

Brand Positioning Statement Example Output:
For marketing directors at mid-market SaaS companies, Clearwave is the content analytics platform that turns publishing decisions into measurable pipeline because it connects content engagement directly to CRM deal data — without requiring a data team to run the analysis.

Brand Personality Example Output:
1. Direct — We say what we mean without corporate hedging. Our content makes a point, takes a side, and explains why. We do not write "it depends" when we have a clear answer.
2. Rigorous — We back every claim with data. When we say something works, we show the numbers. Our credibility is built on specificity, not assertion.
3. Warm — We treat marketers as smart professionals, not as recipients of our expertise. Our tone is collegial, not authoritative. We are a peer, not a professor.

The brand overview section is worth spending extra time refining because every other section of your guidelines flows from it. The voice and tone prompt will ask you to describe your brand personality — use the output from this prompt. The messaging framework prompt will ask for your positioning — it is already here. Build the foundation right, and the rest of the guidelines document takes far less effort.

Prompt 2: Brand Voice and Tone Guide

Brand voice is who your brand is in writing. Brand tone is how that voice adjusts for different contexts. Voice stays consistent across everything you publish — it is the personality that makes your content recognizable whether it is a tweet, a legal disclaimer, or a product launch email. Tone adapts — the same brand is warmer in a customer service response than in a sales email, more authoritative in a white paper than in an Instagram caption. Most brands document voice but ignore tone, which leads to content that feels off in context even when the individual pieces seem fine. This prompt generates a complete voice and tone guide with usage examples and application rules for different content contexts.

Prompt BG-02: Brand Voice and Tone Guide
Act as a senior brand copywriter who specializes in brand voice development for growth-stage companies. Write a complete Brand Voice and Tone Guide for the brand below.

Include the following sections:
— Voice Overview (1 paragraph): The brand's fundamental communication personality in plain language
— Voice Attributes (4–5 attributes): For each, provide: the attribute name, a one-line definition, 3 "we are / we are not" pairs that define the boundaries (e.g., "Direct, not blunt"), and a before/after example showing off-brand vs. on-brand copy
— Tone by Context (6 contexts): Show how the voice adapts for: customer service/support, social media, sales/marketing copy, long-form content (blog/reports), product UI copy, and crisis/sensitive communications
— Words We Use / Words We Avoid: A reference list of brand-appropriate vocabulary and terms to avoid (jargon, competitor language, industry clichés)
— Punctuation and Grammar Style Notes: 5–8 specific rules relevant to this brand's style (e.g., "We use em dashes, not parentheses," "We do not use exclamation points in B2B content")
— Reading Level Target: The appropriate complexity level for this brand's primary audience and why

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry: [INDUSTRY]
— Brand Personality Adjectives: [4–6 ADJECTIVES THAT DESCRIBE YOUR BRAND'S PERSONALITY]
— Primary Audience: [WHO READS YOUR CONTENT]
— Content Channels: [WHERE YOU PUBLISH — e.g., "website, LinkedIn, email newsletter, product UI"]
— Brands With Voice You Admire: [2–3 BRANDS WHOSE COMMUNICATION STYLE YOU LIKE — for calibration, not copying]
— Specific Words or Phrases You Avoid: [ANY TERMS YOUR BRAND HAS ALREADY DECIDED NOT TO USE]

The "we are / we are not" framework is the most useful tool in a voice guide because it defines the edges. "Direct" alone is too vague — it can mean brutally blunt or warmly clear. "Direct, not blunt" and "Direct, not diplomatic to the point of being unclear" tells a writer exactly where the line is. The before/after examples reinforce that line with copy a writer can pattern-match against their own work. These two elements alone save hours of revision cycles on brand copy.

Prompt 3: Content Standards and Style Rules

A voice and tone guide tells your team how to sound. A content standards and style guide tells them how to write — the specific rules for grammar, formatting, terminology, and structure that create consistency across every document your brand publishes. Without a style guide, every writer makes independent decisions about Oxford commas, capitalization, number formatting, and brand terminology. The result is a patchwork of inconsistencies that signals an unprofessional brand even when the ideas are strong. This prompt generates a practical style guide tailored to your brand, built on an established style framework (AP, Chicago, or a custom ruleset).

Prompt BG-03: Content Standards and Style Rules
Act as an editorial director who has written style guides for media companies, tech brands, and professional services firms. Write a Content Standards and Style Guide for the brand below. This guide will be used by writers, editors, social media managers, and AI content tools to ensure consistent formatting and language across all brand content.

Include the following sections:
— Base Style Reference: State the primary style guide this brand follows (AP, Chicago, or other) and list specific departures from that guide
— Grammar and Punctuation Rules (10–15 specific rules): Cover: Oxford comma policy, em dash vs. en dash usage, serial lists, parentheses policy, exclamation point policy, contractions policy, passive vs. active voice preference, sentence length guidance
— Capitalization Rules: Brand name, product names, job titles, industry terms, heading style (title case vs. sentence case)
— Numbers and Data Formatting: When to spell out vs. use numerals, percentage formatting, currency formatting, date formatting, time formatting
— Brand Terminology Glossary: 10–15 brand-specific terms with the correct usage, incorrect usage, and why the distinction matters
— Content Formatting Standards: Preferred heading hierarchy, paragraph length guidance, use of bullet lists vs. numbered lists vs. prose, image caption style
— Inclusive Language Guidelines: 5–8 specific rules for inclusive, non-biased language relevant to this brand's audience and category

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry: [INDUSTRY]
— Primary Content Types: [WHAT YOU PUBLISH — e.g., "blog posts, white papers, email newsletters, social media, UI copy"]
— Preferred Style Base: [AP STYLE, CHICAGO STYLE, OR "CREATE A CUSTOM RULESET"]
— Brand/Product Names That Require Special Formatting: [ANY NAMES WITH UNIQUE CAPITALIZATION OR STYLING]
— Industry Terms That Are Commonly Misused: [TERMS WHERE PRECISION MATTERS IN YOUR CATEGORY]
— Audience Sensitivity Considerations: [ANY LANGUAGE SENSITIVITIES SPECIFIC TO YOUR AUDIENCE OR CATEGORY]

The brand terminology glossary is one of the highest-value outputs from this prompt. Every brand has five to fifteen terms that writers consistently get wrong: the product name capitalization, the correct way to describe the core use case, the industry term your brand prefers over the generic one. Document these once, correctly, with examples of right and wrong usage, and you eliminate an entire category of revision feedback across every piece of content your team produces.

Prompt 4: Social Media Brand Standards

Social media is where brand inconsistency is most visible. Your brand might have a coherent website and polished email program, but if your Instagram sounds like a different company than your LinkedIn, the brand falls apart for anyone who follows you on multiple channels. Social media brand standards go beyond "be conversational on Instagram" — they define the specific creative and copy conventions for each platform: caption length and structure, hashtag strategy, emoji usage, response tone, content mix ratios, and formatting rules. This prompt generates channel-specific social media standards that give your team a clear playbook for every platform you use.

Prompt BG-04: Social Media Brand Standards
Act as a social media brand strategist who has built platform-specific brand playbooks for DTC, B2B, and creator brands. Write the Social Media Brand Standards section of a complete brand guidelines document for the brand below.

For each social platform the brand uses, provide:
— Platform Role: What this platform does for the brand (awareness, community, conversion, thought leadership, etc.)
— Audience on This Platform: Who the brand is talking to specifically on this channel (may differ from the overall audience)
— Tone Adjustment: How the brand voice adapts for this specific platform's culture and format
— Caption Structure: Standard format for posts on this platform (hook, body, CTA, hashtags — specify length and structure)
— Content Mix: Recommended ratio of content types (educational, promotional, community, behind-the-scenes, etc.)
— Hashtag Strategy: Branded hashtags, category hashtags, count per platform
— Emoji Usage Policy: Whether emojis are used, how many, and in what context
— Response and Engagement Standards: Tone for comments and DMs, response time expectations, escalation policy for negative comments
— Things to Never Post: Platform-specific content guardrails

Also include a Cross-Platform Consistency section: rules that apply everywhere regardless of platform, including profile bio standards, link-in-bio strategy, and how to adapt a single piece of content for multiple platforms without losing brand integrity.

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Social Platforms Used: [LIST PLATFORMS — e.g., "Instagram, LinkedIn, X (Twitter), TikTok, Facebook"]
— Brand Personality: [YOUR BRAND'S CORE VOICE ADJECTIVES]
— Primary Audience: [WHO THE BRAND SERVES]
— Business Goals for Social: [WHAT SOCIAL IS SUPPOSED TO DO — e.g., "drive newsletter signups," "generate leads," "build community," "drive e-commerce sales"]
— Branded Hashtag(s): [ANY EXISTING BRANDED HASHTAGS]
— Content Pillars: [3–5 TOPIC AREAS THE BRAND PUBLISHES ABOUT]

LinkedIn Caption Structure (Example Output):
Hook (1–2 lines, no more than 200 characters before the "see more" break): State an insight, counterintuitive claim, or specific data point. Do not open with "I" or "We."
Body (3–8 short paragraphs): Expand the insight with examples, data, or a short story. One idea per paragraph. Max 3 sentences per paragraph.
CTA (1 line): Ask a specific question or direct to a specific resource. Do not use "link in bio" on LinkedIn — post the URL directly in the comments.
Hashtags: 3–5 only. Mix of branded (1), category (1–2), and topic-specific (1–2). Never at the end of the post — put them in the first comment.

The channel-specific approach matters because what works on LinkedIn fails on Instagram, and what works on Instagram looks amateurish on LinkedIn. A brand that treats every platform the same ends up performing poorly everywhere. Platform-specific standards give your social team clear guardrails for each channel so they can create natively for each platform without drifting off-brand in the process.

Prompt 5: Email Brand Standards

Email is the highest-ROI marketing channel for most brands, and it is where brand inconsistency is most costly because you are in someone's inbox — a personal space where they already have high expectations. An email from your brand that does not match the voice and structure of previous emails erodes trust, increases unsubscribes, and signals an unprofessional operation even if the content itself is strong. Email brand standards cover everything from subject line formulas and preheader conventions to sender name and from-address policy, email structure, footer requirements, and unsubscribe management. This prompt generates a complete email brand standard covering all email types in your program.

Prompt BG-05: Email Brand Standards
Act as an email marketing strategist and brand copywriter who has built email brand standards for subscription businesses, e-commerce brands, and B2B SaaS companies. Write the Email Brand Standards section of a complete brand guidelines document for the brand below.

Include the following subsections:
— Sender Identity Standards: From name format, from email address format, reply-to policy, when to use a person's name vs. the brand name as the sender
— Subject Line Standards: Formula, character count target, personalization usage, emoji usage policy, A/B test methodology
— Preheader Standards: Length, relationship to subject line, common mistakes to avoid
— Email Structure by Type: Provide the standard structure for each email type the brand sends (e.g., newsletter, promotional, transactional, onboarding, re-engagement) — including word count ranges, section order, CTA placement, and image-to-text ratio
— CTA Standards: Button copy formulas, link text conventions, number of CTAs per email, primary vs. secondary CTA hierarchy
— Tone by Email Type: How the brand voice adapts for transactional vs. marketing vs. support emails
— Footer and Legal Requirements: Required elements, unsubscribe language, physical address, social links
— Accessibility Standards: Plain text version requirements, alt text for images, minimum font size, color contrast
— Things to Never Include: Content, language, or design patterns this brand explicitly avoids in email

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Email Types Sent: [LIST EMAIL TYPES — e.g., "weekly newsletter, promotional campaigns, onboarding sequence, transactional, re-engagement"]
— Email Platform: [ESP — e.g., "Klaviyo," "ConvertKit," "HubSpot," "Mailchimp"]
— Brand Voice Adjectives: [CORE PERSONALITY TRAITS]
— Primary Audience: [WHO RECEIVES YOUR EMAILS]
— Sender Name Policy: [DO YOU SEND FROM A PERSON'S NAME, THE BRAND NAME, OR BOTH — e.g., "Sarah at Clearwave" or "Clearwave Team"]
— Specific Rules You Already Have: [ANY EXISTING EMAIL CONVENTIONS YOUR TEAM FOLLOWS]

Sender identity is the most underrated element of email brand standards. Research consistently shows that emails sent from a person's name ("[First Name] at [Brand]") outperform emails sent from a brand name alone in open rate by 15 to 30 percent — but many brands default to "The [Brand] Team" without a policy reason. Email brand standards force that decision to be made intentionally, documented, and applied consistently across every email your team sends.

Prompt 6: Brand Messaging Framework

A brand messaging framework is the source of truth for how your brand talks about itself in marketing and sales materials. It defines your tagline, your elevator pitch, your core value propositions, your proof points, and your key messages for each audience segment. Without a messaging framework, your website copy, sales deck, and case studies each tell a slightly different story about what your brand does and why it matters. With one, every piece of marketing content pulls from the same messaging hierarchy — different words, same substance. This prompt generates a complete messaging framework your marketing and sales teams can align on and use as a source of truth for all brand copy.

Prompt BG-06: Brand Messaging Framework
Act as a brand strategist and messaging consultant who has developed messaging frameworks for venture-backed startups, established consumer brands, and professional services firms. Write a Brand Messaging Framework for the brand below. This document will be used as the messaging source of truth for website copy, sales decks, investor pitches, case studies, and marketing campaigns.

Include the following sections:
— Brand Tagline Options (3–5 variations): Short, memorable statements of the brand's core promise. Include a recommended primary choice with rationale.
— One-Sentence Brand Description: The simplest possible explanation of what the brand does, for whom, and why it is different. Usable in any context.
— Elevator Pitch (50 words): For verbal use in networking, sales calls, and media. Clear, specific, no jargon.
— About Us Paragraph (150 words): For website About pages, press kits, and boilerplate. Sets context, communicates positioning, tells the story concisely.
— Core Value Propositions (3–4): Each with: the proposition headline, a supporting paragraph (50 words), and 2–3 proof points (data, testimonials, examples)
— Audience-Specific Messages (per segment): For each key audience segment, the primary message, primary pain point addressed, primary proof point, and preferred CTA
— Objection-Response Matrix: The 5 most common objections the brand faces, with a recommended response for each
— Messages to Avoid: Claims, comparisons, or framings the brand explicitly does not use in marketing

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Primary Audience Segments: [LIST KEY AUDIENCE SEGMENTS]
— Core Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT FROM ALTERNATIVES]
— Top Proof Points: [YOUR STRONGEST DATA, TESTIMONIALS, OR CASE STUDY RESULTS]
— Most Common Sales Objections: [THE TOP 3–5 REASONS PROSPECTS DO NOT BUY]
— Competitor Positioning (how competitors describe themselves): [HOW YOUR TOP 2–3 COMPETITORS POSITION THEMSELVES — so your messaging can differentiate]

Tagline Options (Example Output):
1. Content That Converts. (Primary recommendation — clear, benefit-first, memorable)
2. Your Brand's Voice, Documented. (Works for positioning against agencies)
3. From Strategy to Style Guide. (Process-focused, good for B2B sales context)
4. Marketing Consistency at Scale. (Enterprise audience — speaks to the problem)
5. One Voice. Every Channel. (Visual simplicity, works well in design contexts)

Objection-Response Example:
Objection: "We already have brand guidelines."
Response: "Great — then you know how long it took to build them and how often people do not use them. [Brand] helps you enforce those guidelines in every piece of content your team produces, so the document you spent months creating actually has impact on your output."

The objection-response matrix is the highest-value output for sales-driven brands. Marketing and sales teams are often misaligned on how to respond to common objections — marketing creates aspirational messaging that sidesteps friction, while sales improvises in the moment. A documented objection-response matrix, drawn from the same messaging framework as all other brand content, gives both teams a consistent, practiced response to the same objections.

Prompt 7: Brand Photography and Visual Direction Guide

Brand photography direction is one of the most neglected sections of brand guidelines — not because it is unimportant, but because most brands assume it requires a creative director and a shoot to define. In reality, a written visual direction guide serves the same purpose: it tells designers, photographers, and content creators what kinds of images are on-brand without requiring a library of brand-approved photos to reference. Written descriptions of visual style — subject matter, composition, color treatment, lighting, mood, what to avoid — give any visual creator enough direction to produce on-brand images independently. This prompt generates a complete written visual direction guide that can accompany your brand guidelines without requiring visual assets.

Prompt BG-07: Brand Photography and Visual Direction Guide
Act as a brand creative director who writes visual identity documentation for brands that need to brief photographers, designers, and content creators without a physical brand photoshoot as reference. Write a Brand Photography and Visual Direction Guide for the brand below, using entirely written descriptions that communicate the visual aesthetic clearly enough for a creative professional to execute without seeing examples.

Include the following sections:
— Visual Brand Personality (3–4 sentences): The overall aesthetic and what it communicates about the brand's values and positioning
— Photography Style Description: Shot style (candid vs. staged vs. editorial), composition preferences, depth of field, angle conventions
— Subject Matter Guidelines: What appears in brand imagery (people, products, environments, abstracts) and how — with specifics on what types of people, what types of settings, what to emphasize
— Lighting Direction: Natural vs. artificial, hard vs. soft, specific moods (warm, clinical, dramatic, airy)
— Color Treatment: Whether photos are treated (filters, color grading), the color palette they pull from or complement, saturation and contrast preferences
— Imagery to Avoid (10–15 specific examples): Stock photo clichés, compositions, color treatments, or subject matter that is explicitly off-brand — be specific enough that a designer can identify the category
— Illustration and Graphic Style (if applicable): Whether the brand uses illustration, what style (flat, isometric, hand-drawn, etc.), and how it relates to the photography style
— Icon and Visual Asset Standards: Style, line weight, fill vs. outline preference, sizing conventions
— Video and Motion Considerations: Pacing, transitions, title card conventions, music direction if applicable

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Industry/Category: [INDUSTRY]
— Brand Personality Adjectives: [YOUR CORE BRAND PERSONALITY WORDS]
— Color Palette (if established): [PRIMARY AND SECONDARY BRAND COLORS]
— Primary Audience: [WHO THE BRAND SERVES]
— Brands With Visual Style You Admire: [2–3 BRANDS WHOSE VISUAL IDENTITY YOU LIKE — for calibration, not copying]
— What You Want to Avoid Visually: [ANY VISUAL CONVENTIONS YOU EXPLICITLY DO NOT WANT — e.g., "overly corporate stock photos," "dark moody aesthetics," "illustration-heavy"]

The "imagery to avoid" section is the most practically useful part of this guide for working with external creators. Designers and photographers respond more reliably to specific negative constraints than to positive aspirational descriptions. "Avoid smiling people pointing at laptops, handshake imagery, and aerial city skyline shots" tells a designer exactly what your brand is not, even before they understand exactly what it is. Specificity in the avoid list eliminates the most common category of visual brand drift.

Prompt 8: Brand Guidelines Checklist and Audit

Brand guidelines are only valuable if they are used — and used correctly. Most brands write their guidelines and let them sit in a Google Drive folder that nobody opens. The result is gradual brand drift: new team members adopt whatever style they see in existing content, agencies interpret the brief loosely, and after twelve months the brand sounds and looks noticeably different from what the guidelines specify. A brand audit checklist gives you a systematic way to measure adherence to your own standards across every content channel on a regular cadence. This prompt generates both a new-content review checklist for ongoing production and a brand audit template for quarterly review.

Prompt BG-08: Brand Guidelines Checklist and Audit
Act as a brand manager and editorial director who has conducted brand audits for companies undergoing rebrands, rapid growth, or team expansion. Write two complementary tools for the brand below:

1. New Content Review Checklist (to use before publishing any piece of content):
— Voice and Tone Check (8–10 items): Does this content reflect the brand personality? Are the tone adjustments appropriate for this channel and context?
— Style and Grammar Check (8–10 items): Does this content follow the brand style guide? Are terminology, capitalization, punctuation, and formatting correct?
— Messaging Check (5–7 items): Does this content use the approved messaging framework? Are claims consistent with documented proof points?
— Visual and Design Check (5–7 items): Does the visual treatment follow brand photography and design standards?
— Legal and Compliance Check (3–5 items): Are required disclosures, trademark symbols, and regulatory language included where required?

2. Quarterly Brand Audit Template:
— Channel-by-Channel Review: A scoring rubric (1–5) for each brand standard across each marketing channel
— Drift Identification Questions: 10 questions to identify where brand drift has occurred and what is causing it
— Team Alignment Assessment: Questions to gauge whether the team understands and can apply the brand guidelines
— Guidelines Update Triggers: Criteria for when the brand guidelines should be updated vs. when content should be brought into compliance with existing guidelines
— Audit Summary Template: A one-page format for documenting audit findings and prioritizing corrective actions

Variables:
— Brand Name: [BRAND NAME]
— Active Marketing Channels: [LIST ALL CHANNELS BEING AUDITED — e.g., "website, blog, email, Instagram, LinkedIn, paid ads, sales deck"]
— Team Size and Structure: [HOW MANY PEOPLE CREATE BRAND CONTENT — e.g., "3-person in-house team plus 2 freelancers and 1 agency"]
— Most Recent Brand Drift Issues: [ANY SPECIFIC BRAND CONSISTENCY PROBLEMS YOU HAVE NOTICED RECENTLY]
— Audit Frequency: [HOW OFTEN YOU PLAN TO CONDUCT AUDITS — quarterly, biannual, etc.]

The quarterly audit cadence is the single most important operational habit for brand consistency. Most brand problems are not caused by teams ignoring guidelines — they are caused by teams not realizing they have drifted. A structured audit every quarter, with a scored rubric against each standard, gives brand managers the visibility to catch drift early and correct it before it becomes entrenched. Build the audit into your team calendar the same way you schedule product reviews and financial reporting.

5 Tips for Using AI to Build Brand Guidelines That Actually Get Used

The 8 prompts above give you the complete framework. These five tips ensure the guidelines you build from that framework become living documents your team actually references.

1. Build in Order, Compile at the End

Run the prompts in sequence: start with the brand overview (Prompt 1), then voice and tone (Prompt 2), then content standards (Prompt 3), and so on. Each prompt builds on context established in the previous one — your voice guide assumes the brand personality from the overview, your messaging framework uses the positioning from the overview and the voice rules from the tone guide. Running them in order ensures consistency across sections. Once all eight are complete, compile them into a master document with a table of contents, version number, and date. This is your brand guidelines document.

2. Fill Variables With Maximum Specificity

The single biggest variable in output quality is how much detail you put into the variable fields. "Our audience is marketers" produces generic output. "Our primary audience is marketing directors at B2B SaaS companies with 50 to 500 employees who are frustrated that their content team produces volume but cannot attribute any of it to pipeline" produces guidelines that speak directly to that person's context. Take thirty minutes before running each prompt to fill in every variable with the most specific, accurate language you can. The prompts are structured to use that specificity — give them material to work with.

3. Use a Condensed Quick-Reference Card for Daily Use

A complete brand guidelines document serves as the source of truth. A one-page quick-reference card serves as the daily tool. After compiling your full guidelines, use AI to generate a condensed version: the five most important voice rules, the ten most important style rules, the key messaging phrases, and the visual direction in one page. Give every content creator, writer, and agency brief this card instead of the full document. Use the full document for onboarding and dispute resolution. The card is what creates day-to-day compliance.

4. Paste Your Guidelines Into Every AI Content Generation Session

Once your brand voice and tone section is complete, paste it as context at the beginning of every AI content generation prompt. This trains the AI to generate content that matches your standards without additional instruction. A system prompt that opens with "You are writing on behalf of [Brand]. Our brand voice is [paste voice guide]. Our style rules are [paste key rules]. Our approved messaging is [paste framework]" produces dramatically more on-brand output than a blank-context request. Your AI-generated brand guidelines become the instructions for all future AI-generated content.

5. Schedule a Quarterly Audit and Update the Guidelines

Brand guidelines that are never updated become obstacles. Your brand evolves — new products, new audiences, new channels, new competitive context. Guidelines written in year one should look different by year two. Use the audit checklist from Prompt 8 quarterly, and schedule an annual guidelines review to update sections that no longer reflect how the brand actually operates. Mark every change with a version number and date. Living guidelines that reflect the brand's current reality get used. Static documents from two years ago get ignored.
