10 Best AI Prompts for Small Business Marketing [Free Templates]

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for small business marketing are ready-to-use instructions that turn ChatGPT, Claude, or any AI into a local marketing team — generating Facebook ads, emails, Google Business posts, and social content tailored to your specific business. This guide delivers 10 free, copy-paste prompt templates covering every marketing channel a small business needs to grow.

Small business owners wear every hat. You are the accountant, the customer service rep, the operations manager, and the marketing department — all in one. Marketing is supposed to drive growth, but it gets squeezed into whatever time is left at the end of the day. And when you finally sit down to write a Facebook ad or email newsletter, you stare at a blank screen for 45 minutes, produce something mediocre, and wonder if it was worth the effort.

This is where AI changes the equation — but only if you use it correctly. Most small business owners open ChatGPT, type "write me a Facebook ad for my business," and get something so generic it could apply to any business in any city. No local flavor. No specificity. No reason for a customer to care. The problem is not the AI. The problem is the prompt.

Structured ai prompts for small business marketing solve this by giving the AI everything it needs to produce localized, specific, conversion-ready content. Each prompt in this article includes a role assignment (telling the AI who to be), a proven marketing framework (telling it how to structure the output), tone parameters (telling it how to sound), and variable fields (telling it exactly what business details to incorporate). The result is marketing content that sounds like it was written by a local marketing expert who knows your neighborhood, your customers, and your competitive landscape.

The 10 prompts below cover the core marketing channels for any small business: paid ads, email, Google Business, local SEO, social media, testimonial collection, seasonal campaigns, B2B outreach, search ads, and newsletters. Each one is free, copy-paste ready, and works with any AI tool. Fill in the variables, hit enter, and get a first draft in under two minutes.

Why Small Businesses Need Structured AI Prompts

A marketing agency charges $2,000–$5,000 per month. A freelance marketer costs $50–$150 per hour. A part-time marketing hire runs $25,000–$40,000 per year. For a small business doing $200K–$500K in annual revenue, those numbers are hard to justify — especially when you need marketing across multiple channels simultaneously.

AI closes this gap, but only with the right prompts. Here is why structure matters more than the AI model you choose.

Generic Prompts Produce Generic Output

When you type "write me a marketing email" into ChatGPT, the model has no context. It does not know your business, your location, your audience, or your offer. So it defaults to corporate-sounding, one-size-fits-all copy that could have been written for any business anywhere. Your customers can tell. It reads like spam because it was generated like spam — with no specificity and no local relevance.

Structured Prompts Act Like a Marketing Brief

A good marketing prompt works exactly like the creative brief a marketing agency would use. It specifies the target audience, the key message, the desired action, the tone, and the constraints. The only difference is that you are briefing an AI instead of a copywriter. The better the brief, the better the output — and the 10 prompts in this article are the briefs.

Local Businesses Need Local Content

National brands can get away with broad messaging. Small businesses cannot. Your competitive advantage is local: you know the neighborhood, you serve the community, you understand local problems in a way that chains and online competitors never will. Every prompt below includes location-specific variables — city name, neighborhood, local landmarks, regional pain points — so the AI output reflects your local expertise instead of sounding like a generic template.

The bottom line: a small business owner using structured AI prompts can produce marketing content that rivals what a $3,000/month agency delivers — in a fraction of the time and at zero ongoing cost. Here are the 10 prompts to make that happen.

Prompt 1: Local Facebook Ad (PAS Framework)

Facebook ads are the workhorse of local small business marketing. They let you target people within a specific radius of your business, by age, interest, and behavior. But most small business Facebook ads fail because they lead with features instead of feelings. The PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework fixes this by starting with the customer's pain, making them feel the cost of inaction, and then positioning your business as the solution. This prompt generates a complete Facebook ad — primary text, headline, and description — optimized for local targeting.

Prompt SB-01: Local Facebook Ad (PAS Framework)
Act as a direct-response copywriter specializing in local business Facebook ads. Write a Facebook ad using the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework for the small business below.

Structure:
— Primary Text (125 words max): Open with a local pain point that resonates with the target audience in the specific city/neighborhood. Agitate the problem with a specific consequence. Present the business as the local solution with one key differentiator. End with a clear call-to-action.
— Headline (40 characters max): Benefit-driven, location-specific
— Description (25 words max): Supporting detail with urgency or social proof

Tone: Conversational, neighborly, confident — like a trusted local recommendation, not a corporate ad.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Business Type: [BUSINESS TYPE — e.g., "plumbing company," "yoga studio," "bakery"]
— City/Neighborhood: [CITY OR NEIGHBORHOOD]
— Target Audience: [TARGET AUDIENCE — e.g., "homeowners aged 30-55 in Westlake"]
— Local Pain Point: [SPECIFIC LOCAL PROBLEM — e.g., "hard water damaging pipes," "no quality bakeries within 10 miles"]
— Key Differentiator: [WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT — e.g., "same-day service," "organic ingredients," "15 years in the community"]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER — e.g., "free estimate," "$20 off first visit," "free consultation"]
— CTA Link Destination: [WHERE THE AD LINKS — e.g., "booking page," "phone call," "website"]

Primary Text: Your AC unit is working overtime this Austin summer — and your energy bill proves it. Every week you wait for that tune-up is another $40-60 wasted on inefficiency. Coolridge HVAC has kept Westlake homes comfortable for 15 years. We will inspect your system, find the waste, and cut your cooling costs — guaranteed. Book your free diagnostic before the July rush fills our schedule.

Headline: Cut Your Austin Cooling Bill in Half

Description: Free AC diagnostic for Westlake homeowners. 15 years of local trust. Book today.

Notice how the ad names the city, the neighborhood, and a specific local pain point (Austin summer heat). That level of specificity is what separates a high-converting local ad from a generic template.

This prompt generates a welcome email that delivers value, sets expectations, and builds immediate trust.

Structure:

— Deliver the promised value (coupon, download, or confirmation) in the first 2 lines
— 2–3 sentences: who you are, why you started the business, and what makes you different locally
— Set expectations for future emails (frequency and content type)
— One helpful tip or resource related to your service (immediate value)
— Sign-off with owner name and business

Tone: Warm, personal, small-town friendly. Write as the business owner, not a corporation.
Length: Under 200 words.

Variables:

— Owner First Name: [OWNER NAME]
— Business Type: [BUSINESS TYPE]
— City: [CITY]

— Email Frequency: [FREQUENCY — e.g., "once a week," "twice a month"]

Prompt 6: Customer Testimonial Request Email

This prompt generates a testimonial request email that is polite, specific, and engineered to extract usable quotes — the kind that reference specific results, feelings, and before-and-after experiences.

Prompt SB-06: Customer Testimonial Request Email
Act as a customer success manager for a local small business. Write a testimonial request email that gets specific, usable quotes from happy customers — not generic "great service" responses.

Structure:
— Subject line: Personal, non-salesy, references their specific experience
— Opening: Thank them for their business and reference the specific service/product they purchased
— The ask: Explain that a short testimonial helps other locals find you, and frame it as helping the community (not helping your business)

  2. What was the result or experience?

Tone: Grateful, personal, zero pressure. Make it feel like a favor between neighbors, not a marketing tactic.
Length: Under 175 words.

Variables:

How to Customize These Prompts for Your Business

These 10 prompts are starting templates, not finished products. The businesses that get the best results from ai prompts for small business marketing customize them in three specific ways.

1. Add Your Brand Voice

Every business has a personality. A children's dentist sounds different from a criminal defense attorney. After generating the first draft, add a follow-up instruction: "Rewrite this in a tone that is [your tone]. Our brand voice is [describe it]."
