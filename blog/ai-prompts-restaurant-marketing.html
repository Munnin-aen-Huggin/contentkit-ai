10 AI Prompts for Restaurant Marketing and Social Media

ContentKit AI•2026-03-04•12 min read

AI prompts for restaurant marketing are structured instructions that generate appetizing menu descriptions, Instagram captions, Google Business posts, review responses, email newsletters, Facebook ads, and monthly content calendars — all tailored to your restaurant's cuisine, voice, and concept. This guide delivers 10 copy-paste prompts covering every major restaurant marketing channel, each built to produce copy that makes guests hungry, builds local reputation, and fills seats.

Restaurant marketing is one of the most emotionally demanding forms of copywriting. Every word has to do double duty: communicate information (what is in the dish, when the event is, what the deal is) while simultaneously making the reader feel something — hunger, excitement, warmth, FOMO. A menu description that lists ingredients is not the same as one that makes someone close their eyes and taste it before they have ordered. That gap between informative and evocative is where most restaurant marketing fails.

The practical challenge compounds this. The average independent restaurant needs to produce dozens of pieces of content every week: daily social media posts, weekly Google Business updates, monthly email newsletters, responses to every review (good and bad), seasonal menu rewrites, event promotions, ad copy for boosted posts, and an About page that makes a first-time visitor feel like they already know you. For a team of two or three people running a busy restaurant, creating all of that content from scratch — and making it consistently excellent — is effectively impossible.

This is where structured ai prompts for restaurant marketing change the equation. Instead of spending 45 minutes writing an Instagram caption for a new dish, you paste a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude, fill in the variables (dish name, key ingredients, flavor profile, mood), and get five caption options in under two minutes. You pick the best one, make a small edit, and post. The AI handles the drafting. You handle the taste.

The 10 prompts below cover the full restaurant marketing stack: menu descriptions, Instagram captions, Google Business posts, review responses (positive and negative), special event promotions, email newsletters, Facebook ads, happy hour posts, website About pages, and monthly content calendars. Each prompt includes the exact variables to fill in and the output format to expect — so you can go from prompt to published content in under five minutes.

For a broader set of AI prompts for small business marketing beyond the restaurant vertical — including landing pages, email sequences, and brand positioning — see our dedicated guide.

Why AI Prompts Work for Restaurant Marketing

Restaurant marketing operates on emotion and specificity. The best food copy is specific: it does not say "delicious pasta" — it says "hand-rolled pappardelle in a 48-hour Bolognese with San Marzano tomatoes and a finish of Pecorino Romano." AI models trained on culinary writing, food journalism, and restaurant copy can generate that level of specificity when the prompt gives them the right inputs.

Here is why structured prompts consistently outperform ad hoc AI requests for restaurant content.

Sensory Language Requires the Right Inputs

The difference between generic and appetizing restaurant copy is almost always in the sensory inputs. When you tell an AI "write a menu description for our salmon dish," you get generic output. When you tell it the cooking method (cedar-plank roasted), the flavor profile (smoky, citrus-forward, with a honey-miso glaze), the texture (crispy skin, flaky interior), and the garnish (microgreens, pickled cucumber, lemon oil), the AI has the raw material to write copy that makes someone's mouth water. The prompts in this article build those sensory inputs into the variable fields — so you are forced to provide the specifics that produce great output.

Consistency Across Channels Is Hard Without a Framework

A restaurant's voice should be consistent whether it appears on a menu, in an Instagram caption, in an email, or in a review response. That consistency is easy when you have one skilled writer. It is nearly impossible when content is produced ad hoc by whoever has a free moment. Structured prompts solve this by encoding your tone, concept, and voice into every prompt as a variable. Once you define "our tone is warm and neighborhood-focused, not precious or formal," that instruction travels into every piece of content you generate.

Volume Is the Real Advantage

Consistent restaurant social media means posting 5–7 times per week across platforms. That is 20–30 posts per month, plus reviews to respond to, emails to send, and ads to run. No small restaurant team can produce all of that at a high quality level without a system. AI prompts compress the drafting time from hours to minutes — which means a team of two can run a content operation that previously required a full-time marketing coordinator.

The bottom line: ai prompts for restaurant marketing do not replace your restaurant's personality and story. They give you a fast first draft that is already 80% of the way to excellent, so your editing time becomes five minutes of refinement instead of 45 minutes of blank-page terror. 10 AI Prompts for Restaurant Marketing

Prompt 1: Menu Item Description (Appetizing Copy)

A menu description has one job: make the guest choose that dish. The best menu descriptions engage multiple senses, tell a micro-story about origin or craft, and create desire without being so purple-prose that they feel overwrought. This prompt generates menu descriptions across three styles — concise (one sentence), narrative (two to three sentences), and evocative (immersive sensory storytelling) — so you can choose the style that matches your menu's voice and density.

Prompt RM-01: Menu Item Description
Act as a professional menu copywriter who has written for Michelin-starred restaurants and James Beard Award nominees. Write menu descriptions for the dish below that make the reader genuinely hungry and compelled to order.

Provide three versions:
— Concise (1 sentence, 25–35 words): For minimalist or upscale menus where brevity signals confidence
— Narrative (2–3 sentences, 60–80 words): For menus with space for story and context
— Evocative (3–4 sentences, 80–110 words): For experiential dining where the description is part of the experience

Rules:
— Lead with the most compelling sensory detail, not the protein or main ingredient name
— Use active, specific language: "slow-braised," not "cooked slowly"; "hand-rolled," not "homemade"
— Include at least two sensory dimensions (taste, texture, aroma, temperature, sound, visual)
— Mention the origin or sourcing if it adds credibility or story
— Never use the words "delicious," "fresh," "amazing," or "unique"

Variables:
— Dish Name: [DISH NAME]
— Protein / Main Ingredient: [MAIN INGREDIENT — e.g., "lamb shoulder," "wild mushrooms," "yellowfin tuna"]
— Cooking Method: [METHOD — e.g., "72-hour sous vide," "wood-fire grilled," "slow-braised," "stone oven baked"]
— Key Flavor Profile: [FLAVORS — e.g., "smoky, bright, umami-forward," "bright and acidic with herbaceous finish"]
— Textures: [TEXTURES — e.g., "crispy exterior, yielding center," "silky, melt-on-the-tongue"]
— Accompaniments / Garnishes: [WHAT IT COMES WITH — e.g., "saffron-scented risotto, crispy capers, lemon oil"]
— Origin / Sourcing Story: [ANY PROVENANCE — e.g., "heritage Berkshire pork from a family farm 40 miles north," "heirloom tomatoes from our rooftop garden"]
— Restaurant Concept / Tone: [TONE — e.g., "refined but approachable," "rustic Italian, warm and convivial," "modern Japanese, precise and spare"]

Concise: Cedar-plank salmon glazed with honey-miso, finished with pickled cucumber and citrus oil — smoky, bright, and gone before you know it.

Narrative: Our Pacific king salmon arrives on a cedar plank that has absorbed the smoke of the oak fire for an hour before service. The honey-miso glaze caramelizes into a lacquer-thin crust, cracking at the touch of a fork to reveal flaky, barely-cooked flesh beneath. Pickled cucumber and lemon oil cut through the richness with clean, cool acidity.

Evocative: The plank arrives still breathing faint woodsmoke, the salmon's skin crackled to a dark amber crisp that gives way to pale, silky flesh barely set from the heat. A honey-miso glaze — applied in three thin layers during the final minutes — has caramelized into something between lacquer and candy. A scatter of paper-thin pickled cucumber and a drizzle of cold-pressed lemon oil arrive last, cutting through the richness with the precision of a sharp knife. This is not a dish you eat. It is one you remember.

Notice the difference between the three versions. The concise version is confident and sharp — it trusts the diner. The narrative version earns its length with craft detail. The evocative version is a brief piece of food writing, appropriate for a menu that doubles as a dining experience. Choose the style that matches your concept and your menu real estate.

Prompt 2: Restaurant Instagram Post (Food Photography Caption)

A great food photo earns the attention. A great caption earns the follow, the save, and the reservation. Restaurant Instagram captions have to do multiple jobs simultaneously: describe what is in the frame, evoke the experience of eating it, communicate your restaurant's personality, and include a soft call to action (link in bio, reserve now, come in this weekend). This prompt generates captions in three formats: short and punchy, medium storytelling, and long editorial — each calibrated for different engagement goals.

Prompt RM-02: Restaurant Instagram Post (Food Photography Caption)
Act as a food and lifestyle copywriter who creates Instagram content for top-rated restaurants. Write Instagram captions for a food photography post that drives saves, comments, and reservation intent.

Provide three caption lengths:
— Short (under 80 characters): Hook-only — one line that stops the scroll
— Medium (100–200 characters): Hook + context + soft CTA
— Long (250–400 characters): Full storytelling caption with hook, sensory detail, brand voice, CTA, and line breaks for readability

Also provide:
— 5 relevant hashtag clusters (5 hashtags each, 25 total)
— A recommended Stories slide copy (2–3 lines) to pair with this post

Rules:
— Open with a hook that is NOT "Introducing our..." or "We are excited to..."
— Use line breaks in the long caption for visual breathing room
— Include one question or engagement prompt in the long caption
— CTA should be specific: "Reserve your table for this weekend" beats "Visit us"

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Dish or Moment Being Photographed: [WHAT IS IN THE PHOTO — e.g., "our new truffle pasta," "Saturday brunch flatlay," "the chef plating a tasting course"]
— Key Ingredient or Flavor Note: [STANDOUT DETAIL — e.g., "shaved black truffle," "blood orange reduction," "house-cured salmon"]
— Season / Occasion: [CONTEXT — e.g., "spring menu launch," "Valentine's weekend," "Tuesday night regular vibes"]
— Reservation / Order Link: [LINK IN BIO DESTINATION — e.g., "OpenTable link," "direct booking link," "online menu"]
— Restaurant Tone and Personality: [TONE — e.g., "warm neighborhood bistro, not fancy," "sleek downtown steakhouse, confident and bold," "playful taco spot, casual and funny"]
— City / Neighborhood: [LOCATION — e.g., "Austin's East Side," "the West Village," "downtown Nashville"]

The three-length approach matters because different posts serve different purposes. Short captions get shared in Stories and DMs where brevity wins. Medium captions work for Reels covers. Long captions drive the saves and comments that the Instagram algorithm rewards with wider reach. By generating all three versions in one prompt session, you choose the right length for each piece of content rather than defaulting to whatever length feels easiest to write.

Prompt 3: Google Business Profile Post

Google Business Profile posts are one of the most underused tools in local restaurant marketing. They appear directly in Google Search and Google Maps when someone searches for your restaurant, and they signal to Google's local algorithm that your listing is active and authoritative. Restaurants that post weekly to their GBP profile consistently rank higher in the local pack. This prompt generates GBP posts optimized for local SEO, formatted within Google's 1,500-character limit, and structured for maximum click-through on the "Order online" and "Book a table" buttons.

Prompt RM-03: Google Business Profile Post
Act as a local SEO specialist who manages Google Business Profile content for restaurant clients. Write a Google Business Profile post for the restaurant below. GBP posts appear in Google Search and Maps and influence local ranking signals — write accordingly.

Post requirements:
— Length: 150–300 words (well under the 1,500-character limit so the full post is visible without "Read more")
— Open with the most compelling information first (event, offer, or dish), not brand name
— Include local keywords naturally (neighborhood + cuisine type + occasion)
— Include a clear call to action tied to a GBP button (Order online, Book, Call, Get directions)
— Do not use excessive punctuation, emoji clusters, or all-caps

Provide three post types:
— What's New Post: Announce a new menu item or seasonal change
— Offer Post: Promote a time-limited deal or happy hour
— Event Post: Promote an upcoming special event or dining experience

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Cuisine Type: [CUISINE — e.g., "modern Italian," "Southern BBQ," "Japanese ramen"]
— Neighborhood / City: [LOCATION]
— New Item / Seasonal Feature: [WHAT IS NEW — e.g., "spring tasting menu," "weekend brunch launch," "new cocktail list"]
— Current Offer: [DEAL OR PROMOTION — e.g., "half-off appetizers 3–6pm daily," "$10 off tasting menu through March"]
— Upcoming Event: [EVENT — e.g., "Winemaker Dinner March 15," "Valentine's prix fixe," "live jazz every Friday"]
— Primary CTA Button: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Book a table," "Order online," "Call us"]

The most important GBP posting principle for restaurants is consistency, not perfection. Google's local algorithm rewards active listings. A restaurant that publishes one short, relevant GBP post per week will outrank a competitor with a more beautiful listing that has not been updated in three months. This prompt takes under three minutes to execute once a week — which is a realistic cadence even for a small team.

Prompt 4: Restaurant Review Response (Positive + Negative)

Review responses are public marketing. Every potential guest who reads a negative review also reads your response — and research consistently shows that a thoughtful, specific, human response to a negative review can actually increase booking intent compared to a restaurant with only positive reviews. Guests trust restaurants that show they care and handle criticism gracefully. This prompt generates responses to both positive and negative reviews, each calibrated to build reputation, include local keywords for SEO, and leave every reviewer feeling seen and heard.

Prompt RM-04: Restaurant Review Response (Positive + Negative)
Act as a hospitality reputation manager who writes review responses for award-winning restaurants. Write review responses that protect the restaurant's reputation, include local SEO keywords, and leave every reviewer — and every person who reads the response — with a better impression of the restaurant.

Provide two response types:

Positive Review Response:
— Length: 60–100 words
— Acknowledge a specific detail from the review (shows you actually read it)
— Thank the guest by first name if provided
— Mention the restaurant name and neighborhood once (local SEO)
— Invite them back with a specific seasonal hook or upcoming event
— Sign off warmly, not corporately

Negative Review Response (1–3 star):
— Length: 100–150 words
— Acknowledge the specific complaint without dismissing it
— Apologize sincerely without using "I'm sorry you feel that way"
— Take responsibility and explain what is being done or has been done
— Offer a specific recovery path (direct email, visit offer, manager name)
— Do NOT be defensive, combative, or generic
— Close by inviting them to give you another chance

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Reviewer Name: [FIRST NAME OR "Guest" if anonymous]
— Review Platform: [GOOGLE, YELP, TRIPADVISOR, ETC.]
— Positive Review Content: [PASTE THE POSITIVE REVIEW TEXT HERE]
— Negative Review Content: [PASTE THE NEGATIVE REVIEW TEXT HERE]
— Specific Complaint (for negative): [WHAT THEY COMPLAINED ABOUT — e.g., "long wait time," "overcooked steak," "server was inattentive"]
— What Has Been Done to Fix It: [INTERNAL CHANGE OR ACTION — e.g., "we have adjusted table pacing," "our kitchen team reviewed our temperature protocols"]
— Recovery Offer / Contact: [HOW TO MAKE IT RIGHT — e.g., "please email me directly at [GM name@restaurant.com]," "ask for [Manager Name] on your next visit"]
— Restaurant Neighborhood: [NEIGHBORHOOD + CITY]

Positive Response (example):
Sarah, reading your description of the tagliatelle made our entire kitchen team smile — that dish is one our chef has been perfecting for two years, so it means everything to hear it hit the mark. Thank you for choosing Osteria Fiora in the West Village for your anniversary. We would love to welcome you back for our spring tasting menu launching in April — we think you will love what is coming. Until then, thank you. — The Fiora Team

Negative Response (example):
Michael, thank you for being honest with us. A 40-minute wait for a table that was reserved is not acceptable, and I am genuinely sorry that it affected your evening. We had an unexpected staffing situation that night and handled the communication poorly — that is on us, not you. I would love the chance to make this right. Please reach out to me directly at sara@osteriafiora.com and I will personally ensure your next visit reflects the experience you deserved on Saturday. I hope you will give us that opportunity. — Sara Mancini, General Manager

The example negative response does several things right: it names the specific failure (40-minute wait for a reserved table), takes full responsibility without hedging, offers a specific and personal recovery path (direct GM email), and closes with genuine warmth rather than corporate apology language. That is the response that converts a one-star reviewer into a regular.

Prompt 5: Special Event / Prix Fixe Promotion

Special events are one of the highest-margin revenue opportunities a restaurant has — a Valentine's prix fixe, a New Year's Eve dinner, a winemaker dinner, a chef's tasting experience. But they only fill seats if the promotional copy creates genuine excitement and communicates the value of the experience clearly. Most restaurant event copy undersells by being too descriptive (listing the courses) and not evocative enough (making the reader feel what the evening will be like). This prompt generates event promotion copy across four channels: Instagram, email, Google Business, and a short ad headline — all in a single session.

Prompt RM-05: Special Event / Prix Fixe Promotion
Act as a hospitality marketing copywriter specializing in special dining events. Write promotional copy for the event below across four channels: Instagram caption, email subject line + body, Google Business Profile post, and a short Facebook ad headline.

Channel requirements:
— Instagram Caption (200–350 characters + 15 hashtags): Hook that sells the experience, not just the menu. Include date, price, and reservation CTA.
— Email Subject Line (5 variations, under 50 characters each): A/B testable options ranging from curiosity to urgency to direct
— Email Body (150–200 words): Opens with the experience, moves to the menu highlights, closes with urgency and clear booking CTA. Do not open with "We are pleased to announce."
— Google Business Post (100–150 words): Local-keyword-rich, direct, event-focused
— Facebook Ad Headline (3 variations, under 40 characters each): Scroll-stopping and benefit-led

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Event Name: [EVENT — e.g., "Spring Winemaker Dinner," "Valentine's Prix Fixe," "New Year's Eve Gala"]
— Date and Time: [DATE AND TIME — e.g., "Friday, March 15, 7pm seating only"]
— Price Per Person: [PRICE — e.g., "$125 per person, wine pairing $55 additional"]
— Menu Highlights (3–4 dishes or courses): [DISHES — e.g., "chilled oysters, duck confit agnolotti, 45-day dry-aged ribeye, chocolate soufflé"]
— Guest Experience Highlight: [WHAT MAKES IT SPECIAL — e.g., "chef joins each table for an amuse-bouche presentation," "winemaker from Sonoma leads the pairing conversation," "live pianist throughout the evening"]
— Seats Available: [SCARCITY — e.g., "only 40 seats," "two seatings of 24 guests each"]
— Reservation Link or Phone: [HOW TO BOOK]
— City / Neighborhood: [LOCATION]

The multi-channel approach is critical for event promotion. A single Instagram post to your existing followers will not fill 40 seats. You need the Instagram post to build excitement, the email to convert your list, the Google Business post to capture local search intent, and the Facebook ad to reach local diners who do not yet follow you. This prompt generates all four in a single session, so you can launch a complete event marketing campaign in under 30 minutes.

Prompt 6: Restaurant Email Newsletter

A restaurant's email list is its most direct and highest-converting marketing channel. Unlike social media, where your post reaches 5–10% of followers on a good day, an email lands in every subscriber's inbox. Restaurant email open rates average 35–45% — more than double the cross-industry average — because people who give you their email genuinely want to hear from you. The challenge is writing newsletters that feel like a letter from a friend, not a marketing blast. This prompt generates a complete monthly newsletter with the right balance of story, news, and offer.

Prompt RM-06: Restaurant Email Newsletter
Act as an email copywriter specializing in restaurant and hospitality brands. Write a monthly email newsletter for the restaurant below. The email should feel like a genuine update from someone who loves the restaurant — warm, specific, and personal — not a marketing blast.

Structure:
— Subject Line (5 variations, under 50 characters): Range from curiosity to direct announcement to personal hook
— Preview Text (under 90 characters): Pairs with the subject line to boost open rate
— Opening (50–75 words): A warm, story-led opening that is NOT "Happy [month]!" or "We have exciting news." Open with a specific scene, moment, or sensory detail from the restaurant this month.
— What's New Section (75–100 words): New menu items, seasonal changes, behind-the-scenes details that make readers feel like insiders
— Upcoming Events Section (50–75 words): Brief, exciting descriptions of 1–2 upcoming events with booking links
— The Offer (30–50 words): A clear, time-limited incentive for subscribers only (makes the list feel valuable)
— Closing (25–40 words): Personal and warm, signed by a real name

Rules: No exclamation mark overuse. No "We are thrilled." Specific beats general at every turn. Write like a human, not a marketing department.

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Month / Season: [CURRENT MONTH AND SEASON]
— New Menu Items (2–3): [DISHES — e.g., "spring pea risotto, strawberry shortcake with fromage blanc, new rosé program"]
— Behind-the-Scenes Story: [INSIDER DETAIL — e.g., "our chef spent a week in Bologna researching the new pasta program," "we planted an herb garden on the back patio"]
— Upcoming Events (1–2): [EVENTS WITH DATES]
— Subscriber-Only Offer: [OFFER — e.g., "complimentary dessert with dinner this week, mention this email," "15% off your next reservation through March 31"]
— Author / Sign-Off Name: [WHO SIGNS THE EMAIL — e.g., "Chef Marco," "Sarah, GM," "The Fiora Team"]
— Restaurant Tone: [TONE — e.g., "intimate and personal, like a letter from a friend," "energetic and fun, neighborhood pub," "refined but never stiff"]

The subscriber-only offer is the most important structural element of a restaurant newsletter. It makes the list feel exclusive, gives subscribers a reason to open every email, and directly drives revenue. Even a small offer — complimentary dessert, early access to a new menu — signals that being on the list has tangible value. That perceived value is why restaurant email lists with consistent subscriber-only offers have open rates 10–15 percentage points higher than lists that only broadcast news.

Prompt 7: Restaurant Facebook Ad (Local Targeting)

Facebook and Instagram ads for restaurants work best when they target local audiences with a specific, compelling offer tied to a time or occasion. The mistake most restaurants make with paid social is running "brand awareness" ads with beautiful food photos and no reason to act now. Effective restaurant Facebook ads do three things: target precisely (within 5–10 miles, specific demographics), offer something specific (not "great food" but "wood-fired pizza half-price tonight"), and make the action obvious (Book now, Order tonight, Claim this offer). This prompt generates a complete Facebook ad set for local restaurant targeting.

Prompt RM-07: Restaurant Facebook Ad (Local Targeting)
Act as a paid social specialist managing Facebook and Instagram ads for local restaurants. Write a complete Facebook ad campaign for the restaurant below. The ad will target a local audience within a specific radius with a specific offer or event.

Provide:
— 3 Primary Text variations (100–150 characters each): Hook + offer + CTA. Test curiosity vs. direct offer vs. social proof angle.
— 3 Headline variations (under 40 characters each): The bolded text below the image. Must stop the scroll and communicate the offer instantly.
— Description (under 30 characters): The smaller text below the headline. Usually the CTA or secondary benefit.
— Recommended CTA Button: Choose from Facebook's preset options (Book Now, Order Food, Get Offer, Learn More, Call Now)
— Audience Targeting Recommendation: Radius, age range, interests, and behavioral targeting for this specific offer
— Ad Image Copy Recommendation: What text overlay (if any) should appear on the creative to maximize click-through

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Cuisine Type: [CUISINE]
— City and Zip Code: [LOCATION]
— Offer / Event Being Promoted: [WHAT YOU ARE PROMOTING — e.g., "half-price wine Wednesdays," "new brunch menu," "Mother's Day reservation special"]
— Offer Details: [SPECIFICS — e.g., "50% off all bottles under $80, every Wednesday 4–10pm," "$45 three-course brunch, reservations required"]
— Offer Expiry or Event Date: [DEADLINE — e.g., "every Wednesday," "this Saturday only," "Mother's Day, May 11"]
— Target Audience: [WHO — e.g., "couples 25–45 within 5 miles," "families within 10 miles," "food enthusiasts 30–55"]
— Landing Page / Destination: [WHERE THE AD CLICKS TO — e.g., "OpenTable reservation page," "website menu page," "phone number"]

The targeting recommendation in this prompt is as valuable as the ad copy itself. Most restaurants either target too broadly (everyone in the city) or too narrowly (only existing followers). The sweet spot for local restaurant Facebook ads is typically a 5–8 mile radius, age-targeted to your core demographic, with interest targeting around food, dining, and local events. A $200–$300 monthly Facebook ad budget with tight local targeting can generate 20–40 incremental reservations per month for a well-positioned offer.

Prompt 8: Happy Hour / Daily Special Social Post

Happy hour and daily specials are the heartbeat of a restaurant's social media presence. They give you a genuine, time-sensitive reason to post every day — which is the most powerful driver of social media consistency. But most restaurants write the same post every week: "Happy Hour 3–6pm! Half-price apps!" That repetition trains followers to scroll past. This prompt generates happy hour and daily special posts with genuine variation — different hooks, different angles, different emotional tones — so that frequent posting actually builds engagement instead of eroding it.

Prompt RM-08: Happy Hour / Daily Special Social Post
Act as a social media content strategist for a busy restaurant. Write 7 unique social media posts for a happy hour or daily special — one for each day of the week — that use different hooks, angles, and emotional tones so the content feels fresh every day rather than repetitive.

Post structure for each:
— Hook line (first 125 characters, must appear before "more" cutoff): Varied daily — do NOT start any two posts with the same type of hook
— Body (50–120 characters): Specific offer details with time, price, and what is included
— CTA (1 line): Varied daily — rotate between "See you tonight," "Reserve your spot," "Walk-ins welcome," "Share with your crew," etc.

Hook angle rotation (one per day):
1. Monday: Empathy / workday survival ("You made it to Monday...")
2. Tuesday: Food photography tease ("Here is what the bar cart looks like at 4pm...")
3. Wednesday: Social proof / FOMO ("Our regulars have a secret...")
4. Thursday: Countdown / anticipation ("One more sleep until the weekend...")
5. Friday: Celebration / permission ("You have officially earned this...")
6. Saturday: Lifestyle / scene-setting ("This is what Saturday looks like at [Restaurant]...")
7. Sunday: Comfort / nostalgia ("Sundays were made for this...")

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Happy Hour / Special Details: [WHAT THE DEAL IS — e.g., "$5 cocktails and half-price wings, Mon–Fri 3–6pm"]
— Specific Items Featured: [FEATURED ITEMS — e.g., "our house margarita, crispy brussels, and loaded nachos"]
— Venue Vibe / Atmosphere: [WHAT IT FEELS LIKE — e.g., "lively neighborhood bar, great for groups," "quiet and cozy, best seats at the bar"]
— City / Neighborhood: [LOCATION]
— Walk-In or Reservation Policy: [POLICY — e.g., "walk-ins always welcome," "reservations recommended Thursday–Sunday"]

The seven-angle rotation is the key innovation in this prompt. By assigning a different emotional hook to each day of the week, you guarantee that no two posts feel the same — even though they are promoting the same offer. This is the difference between a happy hour social presence that trains followers to scroll past and one that trains them to check in daily to see what you have posted. Restaurants that use angle rotation on their daily specials consistently see 2–3x higher engagement rates than those that use a single format repeatedly.

Prompt 9: Restaurant Website About Page

A restaurant's About page is often its most visited page after the menu — and the most neglected. First-time guests read the About page to answer a question that menus cannot answer: what kind of place is this, really? Who are the people who made it? Why does it exist? A strong About page converts browsers into bookers by making the guest feel connected to the restaurant before they have ever walked through the door. This prompt generates a complete About page narrative with sections for the founding story, the kitchen philosophy, the team, and the neighborhood connection.

Prompt RM-09: Restaurant Website About Page
Act as a restaurant brand copywriter who specializes in writing About pages that turn website visitors into first-time guests. Write a complete restaurant About page that answers the question every potential guest is really asking: "Why should I care about this place and what will I feel when I am there?"

Page structure:
— Opening paragraph (60–90 words): A scene-setting intro that drops the reader into the restaurant experience. NOT "Welcome to [Restaurant Name], where..." and NOT a founding year summary. Open with a moment, a sensory detail, or a question.
— Our Story (100–150 words): The honest founding story. Who started this, why, and what was the vision? Include specific details that make it feel real, not generic.
— Our Kitchen (75–100 words): Culinary philosophy, sourcing approach, what drives the menu. Be specific about technique, region, relationships with producers.
— Our People (50–75 words): Brief, warm introduction to the team without feeling like a corporate org chart.
— Our Neighborhood (50–75 words): How the restaurant connects to its specific location and community.
— Closing (30–50 words): An invitation to visit — warm, specific, not generic.

Rules: Write for a human, not a food critic. Specific beats general. Story beats fact. Warmth beats formality. Every section should make the reader like the restaurant more, not just know more about it.

Variables:
— Restaurant Name: [RESTAURANT NAME]
— Cuisine / Concept: [CONCEPT — e.g., "wood-fired coastal Italian," "modern Korean BBQ," "New Orleans-inspired neighborhood bistro"]
— Founding Story: [THE REAL STORY — e.g., "two brothers who grew up cooking their grandmother's recipes," "a chef who left a Michelin kitchen to open a neighborhood place that felt like home"]

5 Tips for Using AI Prompts in Restaurant Marketing

The 10 prompts above give you the frameworks. These five tips ensure you use them to produce content that genuinely sounds like your restaurant — not generic AI copy.

1. Fill Every Variable Field Before Generating

The quality of AI-generated restaurant copy is almost entirely determined by the quality of your inputs. A prompt with vague variables ("our pasta," "Italian restaurant," "warm tone") will produce generic output. A prompt with specific variables ("48-hour hand-rolled pici with wild boar ragù, aged pecorino, and black pepper from Kampot," "modern Roman trattoria in Austin's East Side, approachable but serious about craft," "Chef Lorenzo, who spent four years cooking in Rome") will produce copy that is specific enough to be genuinely useful. Before you run any prompt, spend two minutes filling every variable field with the most specific information you have.

2. Always Edit for Your Voice

AI-generated restaurant copy is a first draft, not a final product. The AI does not know that your chef always says "cooked with love and obsession," that your regulars call the back corner table the "throne," or that your Instagram followers have come to expect a specific playful tone you have developed over three years. After generating, read the output out loud. Would a guest recognize this as coming from your restaurant? Edit the one or two lines that feel generic or off-brand. Five minutes of editing transforms good AI copy into copy that is unmistakably yours.

3. Use the Same Brand Voice Variable Across All Prompts

Define your restaurant's tone in one tight paragraph and save it. Then paste that same tone description into the tone variable of every prompt you run. Something like: "Warm, neighborhood-first, unpretentious. We take the food seriously but not ourselves. We write like a friend who happens to be an amazing cook — never fancy, never stiff, always specific." That consistent input produces consistent output across your menu, Instagram, email, and About page — which is how brand voice is built at scale.

4. Generate Variations and Test

The best way to improve your restaurant's social media engagement is to test different approaches systematically. Run the Instagram caption prompt and generate five variations with different hook styles. Post the top two over the next week and watch which one gets more saves, shares, and comments. After a month of testing, you will know whether your audience responds more to sensory storytelling or to humor, to direct offers or to lifestyle aspiration. AI makes generating variations fast — testing them is where the real learning happens.

5. Regenerate Seasonally with Updated Variables

Every time your menu changes, your specials rotate, or a new event is coming up, refresh your prompts with updated variables and regenerate. A restaurant's content should change as fast as its menu does. The menu description prompt is especially valuable to rerun with every seasonal menu update — what takes a skilled food writer 30–45 minutes per dish takes an AI prompt 90 seconds. Multiply that across a 20-dish seasonal menu and you recover an entire workday every time the menu changes.
