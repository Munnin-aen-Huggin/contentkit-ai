12 AI Prompts for Google Ads That Lower Your CPC

ContentKit AI•12 min read

AI prompts for Google Ads are engineered instructions that generate high-converting search ads, display ads, and Performance Max campaigns with optimized headlines, descriptions, and extensions — ready to paste into Google Ads Manager. This guide delivers 12 copy-paste prompts covering every Google Ads format, each built on proven direct-response frameworks that lower CPC and raise Quality Score.

Google Ads is the highest-intent advertising platform on the planet. When someone types "emergency plumber near me" or "best CRM for small business," they are not browsing — they are buying. That intent is why Google Ads delivers an average return of $2 for every $1 spent across industries, and significantly more in competitive verticals when the ad copy matches the searcher's need precisely.

But here is the problem most advertisers face: Google's ad formats are demanding. A responsive search ad requires up to 15 headlines (each under 30 characters) and 4 descriptions (each under 90 characters). Performance Max campaigns need a full asset group — headlines, long headlines, descriptions, images, and videos. Display ads demand punchy copy that communicates value in a handful of words on a banner someone sees for two seconds. Writing all of this copy manually is tedious, time-consuming, and often results in repetitive variations that fail to test meaningfully different angles.

This is where structured ai prompts for google ads change the equation. Instead of staring at Google Ads Manager trying to fill 15 headline slots, you paste a prompt into ChatGPT or Claude and get all 15 headlines — each within the character limit, each testing a different angle (keyword match, benefit, trust signal, CTA, numeric proof) — in under two minutes. The AI handles the volume. You handle the strategy.

The 12 prompts below cover every major Google Ads format: responsive search ads, search ads with extensions, dynamic search ads, display banners, Performance Max asset groups, shopping titles, YouTube discovery ads, call-only ads, app install ads, remarketing campaigns, competitor keyword ads, and local services ads. Each prompt includes the specific character limits, asset requirements, and copywriting framework for that format — so the output is ready to paste directly into your campaigns.

Why AI Prompts Produce Better Google Ads Copy

Google Ads rewards relevance. The entire auction system — Quality Score, Ad Rank, actual CPC — is designed to show the most relevant ad to each searcher. When your ad copy matches the keyword, answers the intent, and earns clicks, Google charges you less per click. When your ad copy is generic or misaligned, you pay more and show less.

Here is why structured AI prompts consistently produce ads that score higher on relevance.

Volume Creates Variation

Google's responsive search ads work by testing combinations. With 15 headlines and 4 descriptions, the system can generate over 43,000 unique ad combinations. But this only works if your headlines are genuinely different — testing different angles, benefits, proof points, and CTAs. Most advertisers write 5–6 similar headlines and call it done. AI prompts that specify "include 3 keyword-match headlines, 3 benefit headlines, 3 trust/proof headlines, 3 CTA headlines, and 3 numeric headlines" force genuine variation across all 15 slots. More variation means faster optimization and lower CPC.

Character Limits Become Constraints, Not Obstacles

When you tell an AI to write headlines "under 30 characters each," it treats the character limit as a hard constraint and works within it. No more writing a perfect headline, counting the characters, realizing it is 38 characters, and awkwardly trimming words until the meaning collapses. The AI generates within the limit from the start. Same for 90-character descriptions. Same for the 25-character display URL paths. Every Google Ads format has specific character limits, and the prompts in this article include all of them.

Frameworks Beat Guesswork

Each prompt below is built on a proven direct-response copywriting framework — PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution), AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action), BAB (Before-After-Bridge), or feature-benefit-proof. These frameworks have been tested across millions of ads over decades. When you tell the AI to "write the description using the PAS framework," the output follows a structure that has been validated at scale. Compare that to the typical approach of staring at the description field and typing whatever comes to mind.

The bottom line: ai prompts for google ads do not replace your advertising strategy. They execute it faster, with more variation, within every character limit, using frameworks that have been proven across millions of dollars in ad spend. Here are the 12 prompts.

Prompt 1: Responsive Search Ad (RSA) — 15 Headlines + 4 Descriptions

Responsive search ads are the default search ad format in Google Ads. You provide up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions, and Google's machine learning tests combinations to find the best performers. The more diverse your headlines, the more combinations Google can test, and the faster your campaign optimizes. This prompt generates a complete RSA asset set — all 15 headlines and all 4 descriptions — with strategic variation across keyword-match, benefit, trust, CTA, and numeric angles. Every headline stays within 30 characters and every description within 90 characters.

— Headlines 4–6: Benefit-driven (what the customer gets)
— Headlines 7–9: Trust and proof signals (ratings, years, awards, guarantees)
— Headlines 10–12: Call-to-action focused (book, call, get, start, try)
— Headlines 13–15: Numeric or specific (prices, percentages, timeframes)

Description requirements (90 characters max each):
— Description 1: Benefit-focused — what the customer achieves
— Description 2: Trust/proof-focused — why they should trust you
— Description 3: Offer-focused — current deal, pricing, or incentive
— Description 4: CTA-focused — specific next step with urgency

Rules: Every headline must be unique. No headline should start the same way as another. Count characters carefully — any headline over 30 characters is unusable.

— Trust Signal: [PROOF POINT — e.g., "4.9 stars 500+ reviews," "licensed since 2008," "$50M+ recovered"]
— Offer: [CURRENT OFFER — e.g., "free consultation," "$50 off first visit," "free trial"]
— CTA: [DESIRED ACTION — e.g., "Call Now," "Book Online," "Start Free Trial"]

Google reports that sitelinks increase average CTR by 10–20%. But most advertisers write sitelinks as an afterthought — generic labels like "About Us" and "Contact" that waste the opportunity. This prompt generates search ad copy with 4 strategically crafted sitelinks that function as mini-ads within your ad.

Variables:

The sitelink strategy matters more than most advertisers realize. When your four sitelinks each present a different reason to click — your best service, your reviews, your offer, and your credibility — you give the searcher four entry points instead of one. That is four chances to match their specific intent.

But you still control the descriptions — and the descriptions are where you differentiate from competitors whose DSAs all pull from similar page content. This prompt generates description variations optimized for DSA campaigns, where you cannot predict the exact headline but can craft descriptions that convert across a wide range of search intents.

The key to strong DSA descriptions is versatility. Since Google auto-generates the headline from your site content, your descriptions must pair well with any headline the system serves. Writing descriptions that are specific enough to be compelling but flexible enough to match any headline is the balance this prompt achieves.

Prompt 4: Display Ad Copy (Banner Text)

Display ads live in a different world from search ads. The user is not searching for you — they are reading an article, checking email, or watching a video. Your ad appears alongside that content and has roughly 2 seconds to earn attention. That means display ad copy must be brutally concise: a short headline that creates curiosity or urgency, a one-line value proposition, and a CTA button. This prompt generates copy for all three Google Display Ad text asset sizes.

Prompt GA-04: Display Ad Copy (Banner Text)

Provide copy for all three text asset sizes:
— Short Headline (25 characters max): Curiosity or benefit hook that stops the scroll
— Long Headline (90 characters max): Expanded value proposition with specificity
— Description (90 characters max): Supporting detail with social proof or urgency

Write 5 variations of each (15 total assets). Each variation should test a different angle:
1. Curiosity/intrigue angle
2. Direct benefit angle
3. Social proof angle
4. Fear of missing out / urgency angle
5. Problem/solution angle

Rules: Display ads interrupt — they do not answer a search query. Copy must earn attention in under 2 seconds. No jargon. No corporate language. Punchy, clear, direct.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Audience: [WHO SEES THIS AD — e.g., "marketing managers," "homeowners 35-55," "SaaS founders"]
— Key Benefit: [PRIMARY VALUE PROPOSITION]
— Social Proof: [STAT, REVIEW, OR CREDIBILITY POINT]
— Offer: [INCENTIVE — e.g., "free trial," "20% off," "free audit"]

The five-angle approach is critical for display campaigns. Display networks serve your ads across thousands of different placements, and what works on a tech blog will not work in a Gmail sidebar. By testing five genuinely different angles — curiosity, benefit, proof, urgency, and problem/solution — you give Google's algorithm the creative variation it needs to find winning combinations across different placements and audiences.

Google matches your product title to search queries, so a title like "Blue Running Shoes" will never show for "men's lightweight trail running shoes size 11." This prompt takes your existing product titles and rewrites them for maximum search query coverage, following Google's recommended title structure: Brand + Product Type + Key Attributes (color, size, material, gender).

Title structure: Brand + Product Type + Key Attributes (material, color, size, gender, use case)

Shopping title optimization is one of the highest-ROI activities in Google Ads. A single title rewrite can unlock dozens of new search queries you were previously invisible for — with no additional ad spend. If you run Shopping campaigns and have not optimized your titles, this prompt alone can meaningfully shift your performance.

Prompt 7: YouTube Discovery Ad Copy

YouTube discovery ads (formerly TrueView discovery) appear in YouTube search results, alongside related videos, and on the YouTube homepage. Unlike in-stream ads that interrupt, discovery ads are chosen by the viewer — they click your thumbnail and headline because it looks compelling. That means the copy must earn the click the way a YouTube video title earns a click: with curiosity, specificity, and a clear payoff. This prompt generates discovery ad copy with thumbnail text guidance, headlines, and descriptions optimized for voluntary engagement.

Prompt GA-07: YouTube Discovery Ad Copy
Act as a YouTube advertising specialist. Write copy for a YouTube discovery ad campaign (the ads that appear in YouTube search results and alongside related videos). The viewer must voluntarily click this ad, so the copy must be as compelling as an organic YouTube video title.

Provide:
— 5 Headline variations (100 characters max each): Written like a YouTube video title — curiosity-driven, specific, with a clear payoff. Use numbers, "how to," or unexpected angles.
— 5 Description variations (2 lines, 35 characters max per line): Supporting detail that reinforces the headline's promise and includes a reason to click now.
— Thumbnail text recommendation (3–5 words max): Bold, readable text overlay for the video thumbnail.

Rules: Discovery ads compete with organic YouTube content. If your headline would not earn a click as a video title, it will not work as an ad. Write for curiosity and specificity, not for corporate messaging.

Variables:
— Business/Product: [BUSINESS NAME AND WHAT YOU SELL]
— Target Audience: [WHO YOU ARE TARGETING]
— Video Content: [WHAT THE VIDEO COVERS — e.g., "product demo," "customer testimonial," "how-to tutorial"]
— Key Hook: [THE MOST INTERESTING OR SURPRISING THING IN THE VIDEO]
— Desired Action After Watching: [WHAT YOU WANT THE VIEWER TO DO — e.g., "visit site," "sign up for free trial," "book demo"]

The discovery ad mistake most advertisers make is writing ad copy instead of video titles. Your discovery ad sits next to organic YouTube videos, and viewers choose what to watch. If your ad headline reads like a corporate ad ("Introducing Our New Solution") while the video next to it says "I Tried 7 Project Management Tools — Here is the Only One Worth Paying For," the organic title wins every time. Write discovery ads like a YouTuber, not like an advertiser.

Prompt 8: Call-Only Ad Script

Call-only ads skip the website entirely — when someone taps the ad on mobile, it dials your phone number. This makes them ideal for emergency services (plumbers, locksmiths, tow trucks), appointment-based businesses (dentists, lawyers, clinics), and any business where a phone call is worth more than a website visit. The ad copy must create enough urgency and trust in a few lines to make someone pick up the phone immediately. This prompt generates call-only ad copy with a phone-first conversion strategy.

Prompt GA-08: Call-Only Ad Script
Act as a Google Ads specialist for service-based businesses that rely on phone calls for conversions. Write a call-only ad that makes mobile searchers tap to call immediately.

Structure:
— 2 Headlines (30 characters max each): First headline = service + location. Second headline = urgency or trust.
— 2 Descriptions (90 characters max each): First description = value proposition + differentiator. Second = trust signal + reason to call now.
— Display URL paths (2 paths, 15 characters max each): Reinforce the service and location.
— Call extension headline: A short phrase displayed next to the phone number.

Rules: Call-only ads must overcome the friction of making a phone call. Copy needs urgency (available now, open 24/7), trust (licensed, rated, insured), and specificity (what they will get when they call). Avoid vague language.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Service: [SERVICE TYPE — e.g., "emergency plumbing," "family dentistry," "personal injury law"]
— Location: [CITY]
— Hours/Availability: [AVAILABILITY — e.g., "24/7," "Mon-Sat 7am-9pm," "same-day appointments"]
— Key Differentiator: [UNIQUE SELLING POINT]
— Trust Signal: [PROOF POINT — e.g., "licensed & insured," "4.8 stars," "20 years experience"]
— What Happens When They Call: [WHAT THE CALLER GETS — e.g., "free quote in 2 minutes," "speak to an attorney immediately," "same-day booking"]

Call-only ads convert at 2–5x the rate of standard search ads for service businesses because they eliminate the website step entirely. The searcher has a problem, sees your ad, and calls. But the copy must do the work that a landing page normally does — establishing trust, communicating the offer, and creating urgency — all within 30-character headlines and 90-character descriptions.

Prompt 9: App Install Ad Copy

App install campaigns on Google Ads run across Search, Google Play, YouTube, Discover, and the Display Network. The challenge is writing copy that works across all these placements while communicating why someone should install your app in a few seconds of attention. Most app install ads fall into the "download our app" trap — telling people what to do without telling them why. This prompt generates app install ad copy that sells the outcome of using the app, not the act of downloading it.

— 1 Google Play Store short description recommendation (80 characters max): The first line users see on the store listing.

Rules: Never lead with "Download our app." Sell the transformation, not the transaction. Use specific numbers where possible (users, ratings, time saved). Copy must work across Search (text-only), YouTube (with video), and Display (with banner) — so it must stand alone without visual context.

Variables:
— App Name: [APP NAME]
— App Category: [CATEGORY — e.g., "fitness," "finance," "productivity," "food delivery"]
— Primary Benefit: [WHAT THE APP DOES FOR THE USER — e.g., "tracks spending automatically," "builds workout plans," "delivers meals in 30 min"]

— Differentiator: [WHAT COMPETITORS DO NOT OFFER]

The most effective app install ads read like product reviews, not advertisements. "I stopped missing deadlines after one week" beats "Task management app — download now" every time. This prompt forces outcome-first copy by explicitly instructing the AI to focus on what the user's life looks like after they install, not on the features of the app itself.

Prompt 10: Remarketing Search Ad

Someone visited your site, looked at your product or pricing page, and left without converting. They are your warmest audience — they already know who you are and what you offer. When they search again, a remarketing search ad (RLSA) that acknowledges their previous visit and gives them a reason to come back converts at 2–3x the rate of a cold search ad. This prompt generates remarketing search ad copy that feels personalized without being invasive, creating the "welcome back" effect that drives return visits and conversions.

Prompt GA-10: Remarketing Search Ad
Act as a Google Ads specialist experienced with remarketing lists for search ads (RLSA). Write search ad copy specifically for users who have visited the website before but did not convert. The ad should acknowledge their familiarity with the brand and give them a reason to return.

Structure:
— 5 Headlines (30 characters max each): Mix of "welcome back" signals, new incentives, and urgency. Do NOT use creepy language ("we saw you visited") — keep it natural.
— 4 Descriptions (90 characters max each): One addressing common objections, one with a new/exclusive offer, one with social proof, one with urgency/scarcity.

Remarketing copy principles:
— They already know the product. Do not re-explain it. Focus on the conversion barrier.
— Offer something new: a better deal, a limited-time incentive, or new information they did not see before.
— Reduce risk: free trial, money-back guarantee, no commitment.
— Create urgency: limited spots, price increase, expiring offer.

Variables:
— Business Name: [BUSINESS NAME]
— Product/Service: [WHAT YOU SELL]
— Audience Segment: [WHO ARE THESE VISITORS — e.g., "pricing page visitors," "cart abandoners," "free trial dropoffs"]
— Common Objection: [WHY THEY DID NOT CONVERT — e.g., "price," "not ready," "comparing options," "need approval"]
— Remarketing Offer: [INCENTIVE FOR RETURN VISITORS — e.g., "extra 10% off," "extended free trial," "free onboarding call"]
— Urgency Element: [SCARCITY OR DEADLINE — e.g., "offer expires Friday," "only 20 spots left," "price increases March 1"]

Headlines (30 chars max):
1. Still Comparing CRM Tools? (27)
2. Your Free Trial Is Waiting (26)
3. Extended Trial — 21 Days Free (29)
4. 4,200 Teams Made the Switch (27)
5. Lock In Current Pricing Now (27)

Descriptions (90 chars max):
1. Not sure if Flowbase fits your team? Start free for 21 days. No credit card. Cancel anytime. (90)
2. Come back for an exclusive extended trial — 21 days free instead of 14. Limited time only. (90)
3. 4,200+ teams switched to Flowbase last quarter. See why on our customer stories page. (87)
4. Pricing goes up April 1. Lock in current rates with a free trial today. No commitment. (87)

Notice how none of the remarketing copy says "we noticed you visited our site" or anything that feels surveillance-like. Instead, it offers value the visitor did not see before: an extended trial, a pricing deadline, and social proof. The ad feels helpful, not creepy. That is the line remarketing copy must walk, and this prompt ensures it.

Prompt 11: Competitor Keyword Ad

Bidding on competitor brand keywords is one of the most aggressive — and effective — Google Ads strategies. When someone searches for your competitor by name, they are in the market for exactly what you sell. A well-crafted competitor keyword ad does not attack the competitor. It positions your product as a worthy alternative and gives the searcher a reason to consider you. This prompt generates competitor keyword ad copy that is assertive without being negative — which is critical since Google can disapprove ads that misrepresent or disparage competitors.

Prompt GA-11: Competitor Keyword Ad
Act as a Google Ads strategist specializing in competitor conquest campaigns. Write search ad copy for a campaign bidding on competitor brand keywords. The ad must position the business as a strong alternative without mentioning the competitor by name (Google policy) or using negative language about the competitor.

Structure:
— 5 Headlines (30 characters max each): Position as the alternative. Use "switch," "compare," "alternative to," and specific advantages. Do NOT include the competitor's name in any headline.
— 4 Descriptions (90 characters max each): One highlighting a feature the competitor lacks, one with a switching incentive, one with social proof from switchers, one with a risk-free trial/guarantee.

Competitor keyword ad principles:
— The searcher already knows the competitor. Your job is to introduce doubt and present an option.
— Lead with your unique advantage — the thing the competitor does NOT offer.
— Include a switching incentive: free migration, data import, or trial extension.

Tips for Using AI to Optimize Your Google Ads

The 12 prompts above give you the raw material. These five tips ensure you use that material effectively inside Google Ads Manager.

1. Always Respect Character Limits

AI models occasionally miscounting characters is the number one issue with AI-generated Google Ads copy. After generating your ads, verify every headline and description against Google's exact limits: 30 characters for headlines, 90 characters for descriptions, 25 characters for sitelink titles, 15 characters for display URL paths. Paste each asset into a character counter before uploading. One over-limit headline will prevent the entire ad from running.

2. Pin Strategically, Not Excessively

Google lets you pin specific headlines to specific positions in responsive search ads. Most advertisers over-pin, which limits the combinations Google can test and defeats the purpose of RSAs. Pin only when necessary: your brand name to headline position 1, your strongest CTA to headline position 3, and your primary keyword to at least one description position. Let Google's machine learning optimize the rest. The prompts above generate headlines with enough variation that unpinned positions will perform well.

3. Test AI Variations Against Your Controls

Do not replace your existing ads with AI-generated versions. Create an experiment. Duplicate your current ad group, replace the copy with AI-generated assets, and run both versions simultaneously with a 50/50 traffic split. After 2–4 weeks (or 100+ conversions), compare CTR, CPC, conversion rate, and cost per conversion. This is how you prove whether AI-generated copy outperforms your current copy — and in most cases, the variation and specificity of AI-generated assets will win.

4. Use the AI Output as Seed Copy for Google's Suggestions

When you input your AI-generated headlines and descriptions into Google Ads Manager, the platform will suggest additional assets based on your inputs. These AI-on-AI suggestions can be surprisingly effective because Google's model is trained specifically on ad performance data. Use your structured prompts to create the initial asset set, then evaluate Google's suggestions as bonus variations. Accept the ones that add genuinely new angles and reject the ones that duplicate existing assets.

5. Regenerate Monthly for Fresh Creative

Ad fatigue is real, even for search ads. Run the same prompts monthly with updated variables — new offers, new trust signals (more reviews, new awards), new seasonal angles. Each regeneration gives you fresh asset variations to rotate into your campaigns. The advertisers who win on Google Ads are not the ones who write perfect ads once — they are the ones who test, refresh, and iterate continuously. Structured AI prompts make that cycle fast enough to sustain.
